Grok 4.5 undercut everyone on coding price, Fable's own maker may deprecate it, and the token bills are proving the spend is real

xAI shipped an Opus-tier coding model at $0.31 a task while OpenAI published mode-by-mode guidance for Sol and set July 23 to retire GPT-5.4. Anthropic reportedly earns under 5 percent of revenue from subscriptions, so Fable's days in the plan may be numbered. Two hard case studies show what the spending actually buys.

The model price war got another entrant and the workflow advice got specific, but the more interesting story is on the demand side. The token bills that look like waste are turning out to be real adoption, and two concrete case studies show where the money goes. Ranked, with the buildable stuff up top.

1. Grok 4.5 crashed the coding price again, and Sol now comes with a mode-by-mode manual. Per the Hermes PM list, xAI and Cursor shipped Grok 4.5 as a model built specifically for coding and agents, pitched as Opus-tier at a fraction of the cost, and it lands at $0.31 a task against $1.04 for GPT-5.6 and $2.75 for Claude Opus, ranking second on one real-world coding benchmark behind Fable, per the People AM list. The practical takeaway is that the cheap-and-good tier is now crowded, good news for anyone paying the bill.

The Sol guidance got concrete too. The operators on the Hermes list now run GPT-5.6 Sol on Medium as the daily driver, Extra High for hard problems, and Ultra only sparingly because it burns usage fast, and several report running Sol inside Claude Code where it outperforms Codex. OpenAI is retiring the older GPT-5.4 on July 23 and shipped a Skills feature that mirrors Claude's, per the Hermes AM list. The effort dial is the whole game, and the simplest way to hold it, per a Kris Torrington clip, is to treat Max like hiring a senior architect to rename a button, reserve it for when being wrong is genuinely expensive.

2. Fable's own economics now put its survival in question. The uncomfortable thread of the weekend, per the Hermes PM list: subscriptions reportedly make up under 5 percent of Anthropic's revenue, so the company may not have much reason beyond goodwill to keep Fable 5 in the consumer plan. The supporting evidence is the cost, Alex Finn burned about $500 of Fable credits in four hours, and the chatter is that an Opus 5 arrives within about two weeks as a cheaper replacement, per the Hermes AM list. If you have been leaning on Fable for planning-heavy work, treat its place in the subscription as temporary and get your routing off it.

3. The token bills that look insane are the actual product-market fit. The bill-shock numbers are real, a Fortune 500 firm overspending $500 million in a month, Meta burning 60 trillion tokens in 30 days, but the sharpest read is that the bills reflect real adoption rather than waste. Per Jordi Visser's long market note, SemiAnalysis's own AI bill went from under $100,000 to about $11 million annualized in six months, and the inflection was the moment Claude Code became genuinely usable after Opus 4.5. The enterprise data agrees, an a16z survey of technology chiefs has average large-language-model spend rising from $4.5 million to about $7 million and closed-source share climbing from 81 to 89 percent, per the Stocks AM list.

The counterweight keeps it honest. A PwC survey found most enterprises still report no measurable revenue or cost benefit, the pilot-purgatory problem, so the spend is running well ahead of the proven return. Both things are true, the usage is exploding and the payoff is unproven, and the next few quarters are where that gap either closes or bites.

4. Two case studies show exactly what the spend buys. The first is a receipt for the no-code path, per Kris Torrington: a company called Vulcan Technologies, run by non-technical founders, used Claude and Claude Code to attack regulatory complexity, won public-sector work in Virginia, and raised an $11 million seed round. His point is that the government customer using the product is a stronger proof than any revenue claim, and the win came from finding a painful bottleneck rather than from writing clever code.

The second is a full workflow you can copy, per Nate Herk: Claude Code as the orchestrator and Clay as the data source for cold outbound. You install the Clay plugin through the Claude Code terminal, which loads a Clay skill and connects its data server, then hand Claude a goal and let it run. His demo spun up six sub-agents across six cities, pulled 25 shops each, deduped and verified, and even had agents debate whether the subject lines were any good, producing 50 fully enriched and written leads for about $12 in roughly an hour. The waterfall enrichment (checking one data provider, falling through to the next on a miss) lifts the email hit rate from about 30 percent to 80 or 90.

5. AI-native teams ship faster because of org design more than the model. Nate B Jones's argument is that the labs move fast because they push repeatable human coordination into documents and code that agents can act on, so humans stop being the bottleneck. His rules are blunt, no meeting over an hour, product managers live in the terminal daily rather than making roadmaps, and documentation is treated as code because an ambiguous doc becomes agent slop. The warning is that partial adoption is worse than none, killing the roadmap without putting the product managers in the code just produces chaos, and he names Fable 5, a "5.6 Ultra" tier, and a multi-agent system called Ringer as the tooling behind the speed.

6. The model you pick now comes bundled with a silicon bet. Amazon's custom-silicon business (Trainium, Graviton, and Nitro) hit a $20 billion annualized run rate on triple-digit growth, with Trainium 3 nearly sold out and Anthropic committing up to 5 gigawatts of it, per Rick Orford. The AI-tools angle is that the compute a model runs on is quietly consolidating around a few in-house chip lines, so the model you pick increasingly comes bundled with a silicon and power bet you are not choosing directly. What to watch this week is whether Grok 4.5's price or Sol's benchmark lead moves the actual usage share, because the leaderboard has stopped predicting where the tokens go.
