The Chinese model the market sold AI stocks over just ran out of compute and had to pause signups, the clearest sign yet that open weights are not the cheap free lunch the panic assumed

Kimi K3 topped the coding leaderboard, overwhelmed its own GPUs, and is now being tested by Microsoft, all while a close reading says open weights get more expensive at the frontier rather than less. The other AI news is a reckoning about how little the tools actually sped you up.

The AI evening is a set of course corrections. The cheap model is not cheap, the productivity gains were smaller than advertised, and the browser turned out to be a robotics problem. Ranked.

1. Kimi K3 exposed the real economics of open weights, by running out of compute. The irony of the week is instructive. Per the Market list, Moonshot's Kimi K3 topped the coding arena, overwhelmed its own GPU capacity, and had to pause subscriptions over the weekend, and Microsoft is now testing it for Copilot by adding the open-weight model to Azure. The market sold every AI stock two weeks ago on the theory that a cheap Chinese model proved the buildout was overbuilt, and the model then proved the opposite by running out of the very compute the buildout provides.

The closer look confirms it. Per a detailed teardown, open weights do not get cheaper at the frontier, they scale up, with Kimi K3 needing 64 accelerator cores to run at full performance, pricing around $15 per million output tokens, and burning far more tokens per answer than the American models. The argument is that Chinese labs remain six to seven months behind and are not catching up, because the true frontier is the internal models the US labs have not released. The sharper warning is that a powerful open model with weak guardrails becomes a cyber tool, since K3 will reportedly fix security exploits that Codex and Claude refuse to touch.

2. The honest reckoning: the tools only made you a little faster. The most useful practitioner piece of the day is a splash of cold water. Per Dylan Davis, AI gave most people only small productivity gains because they bolted it onto their existing steps instead of redesigning the work, speeding up tasks they should have deleted entirely.

His fix is a discipline, not a tool. Sort every step into keep, cut, or change, keeping only the step that produces the result and the high-stakes human decision, and cutting the human tax of handoffs, practice drafts, and formatting that only existed because a person had to read the intermediate output. His worked example takes a proposal process from seven steps to four by letting the model read messy files, produce a full first draft instead of an outline, and pull from a hundred past proposals instead of two. The point that lands is that the gains come from process redesign, and the reason most people saw so little is that they never did it.

3. The browser is a robotics problem, and that reframes the whole agent race. The densest idea of the evening comes from a robotics researcher. Per Dhruv Batra, the former head of Meta's embodied-AI team now building Yutori, a web agent is a robot acting in a browser, taking pixels in and actions out, forced to explore and recover from errors because it cannot know what a button does until it clicks.

His reason for building digital agents before physical ones is the lesson worth keeping. There is no world where physical robots arrive before digital agents, because digital environments copy for free and their failures are not catastrophic, while hardware cycles are long and dangerous. His agent, Navigator N1, treats pixels as the source of truth rather than the page's underlying code, because most websites have no clean interface to read, the same bitter lesson self-driving learned, that the long tail of the real world never exposes a tidy API and the agent has to act like a human instead.

4. Machines now make more of the internet than people, and the agents will need their own money. The statistic that frames the future arrived quietly. Per Milk Road, for the first time ever machines generated more internet traffic than humans, with 57.4 percent of it coming from bots and AI systems in June, which turns building for agents from a thesis into a present-tense fact.

Those agents cannot transact on human plumbing. Per Jordi Visser, once AI agents become autonomous economic actors, they need crypto rails as financial guardrails, programmable wallets with spending limits, stablecoins for settlement, and escrow that releases on verified conditions, and that is why Stripe's reported bid for PayPal and its stablecoin is really a bet on agent commerce.

The cost warning underneath it is Chamath Palihapitiya's, per that clip, that enterprise token spending grew 21 times in a year and a finance chief is going to miss a quarter on it, because the right move for 95 percent of tasks is a model that costs a hundredth as much. The agents are already most of the traffic, and the open question is who controls the money and the meter they run on.
