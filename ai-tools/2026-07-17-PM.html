The Chinese model that wiped out three trillion dollars of chip stocks is not actually the cheap bomb the market thinks it is, and the best investor in the room said so

Kimi K3 topped the coding arena and triggered the selloff, but Gavin Baker and a hands-on benchmark both show it burns so many tokens it costs more per task than GPT-5.6. The real tooling story is a senior engineer's fully agentic setup, and the honest verdict on which assistant actually does your work.

The market panicked today over a model, and the people who actually measured it reached the opposite conclusion. That gap is the whole AI story tonight. Ranked.

1. Kimi K3 looks like a price bomb and behaves like a token hog, and that distinction moved three trillion dollars. The model that cracked the semis deserves the careful read the tape did not give it. Per Gavin Baker, Moonshot's Kimi K3 is 50 to 70 percent more expensive to run than GPT-5.6 by the measure of Artificial Analysis, because it is a token wastrel, so it ends up costing more per task than both GPT-5.6 and Grok 4.5. His point is that the real threat to the top labs would be a token-efficient open model, and K3 is not that.

The hands-on numbers back him up. Per a Chase AI benchmark, Kimi K3 does win the front-end coding arena and prices at $3 a million tokens against $10 for Fable and $5 for GPT-5.6, but measured by cost per finished task it runs about $95 against $14 for GPT-5.6, and it hallucinates more than either. On a real build it burned 21.5 million tokens over an hour and a half, where Fable finished in seventeen minutes. Baker's deeper argument is why Jensen Huang funds open models at all, that anything shrinking the model layer's fat margins helps every other layer, from power to chips to the app makers, so the panic that AI spending will collapse has the logic backwards.

2. The market got the Kimi story exactly wrong, and the reason is that quality and cost are now separate axes. The nuance the selloff missed is that Kimi K3 is genuinely good and genuinely not cheap at the same time. Per the Limitless read, K3 at 2.8 trillion parameters closes the roughly eight-month lead the West held, testing above GPT-5.6 on coding and near Fable on a good day, and that is the real headline, that China caught up on capability.

What it did not do is make intelligence free, and the hosts flag the softer claims honestly, that the model was likely trained partly on the frontier labs it now competes with and on hardware of uncertain origin. The takeaway for anyone building is that a model topping a leaderboard tells you nothing about what it costs to actually run, so the buy-the-selloff and sell-the-selloff crowds were both trading a benchmark rank as if it were a bill.

3. The most useful thing in AI today was one senior engineer's entire setup, laid bare. The signal under the noise is how the best practitioners actually work now. Per an interview with a former Meta and Atlassian principal engineer, his whole operation runs through a homemade orchestrator called First Mate, a supervisor agent that delegates across thirty repositories, driven mostly by talking to it in plain language, with GPT-5.6 on high reasoning as the default and Claude Fable reserved for the hardest design work.

Two ideas are worth stealing. The first is his adversarial review step, which caught a mistake in 63 percent of about a thousand changes he shipped in three months, because the agent that writes the code is not the one that should approve it. The second is his argument that agents want purpose-built command-line tools rather than heavy connectors, and that the official GitHub connector is the most wasteful one out there. For a lighter start, a separate rundown of seven Claude Code repositories names the ones worth installing, from Superpowers for a strict plan-and-review loop to Claude-Mem for memory across sessions.

4. The honest agentic-assistant verdict, and a deadline forcing everyone to reshuffle. The practical bake-off cut through the marketing. Per a real-work test of Claude Cowork, ChatGPT Work, and Gemini, run across meeting notes, inbox triage, and customer feedback, ChatGPT came out ahead for grounded day-to-day work while Claude won on voice and member-facing writing and Gemini's $100 tier was judged not worth it. The tester was careful to note the AI judge was itself an OpenAI model, then re-ran it through Claude, which reached the same ranking.

The forcing function is Anthropic moving Claude Fable to usage pricing on Monday, and that is why the field is scrambling. Per Alex Finn, the move is to spend scarce Fable budget on high-level thinking and hand execution to GPT-5.6 on medium, and per Nate B Jones, who let both agents pick their own problem, Codex reliably chose a safe bounded task while Fable reached for the higher-leverage one, so the play is Fable for strategy and Codex for cheap execution.

5. The quieter argument: building is easy now, so the whole game moved to maintaining and specializing. Two thinkers pointed past the model race. Per Craig Mod, anyone can one-shot a company in a weekend now, so building something is no longer the signal it once was, and the real test has shifted to whether you keep the thing alive and working over time. His line is that the metric of quality moved from can you build it at all to are you still maintaining it.

The companion argument is that general models are getting worse at the things generalists were supposed to master. Per AI Central, the safety training that makes a model a reliable assistant strips the variance that makes good fiction, and that is why specialized tools trained on narrow craft are pulling away for creative work. Both point at the same place the practitioners already live, that the edge is no longer having access to a smart model, it is the taste to aim it and the discipline to keep the result running.
