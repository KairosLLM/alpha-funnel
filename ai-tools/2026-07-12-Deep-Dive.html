OpenAI shipped a model one point below Fable 5 at a third of the price this week, and then it started deleting people's files

GPT-5.6 Sol landed aimed straight at Fable 5's weak spot, and by Friday the smart-money workflow had flipped from Fable-plans-Opus-builds to Fable-plans-Sol-builds. Then Sol wiped a user's Mac, the safety team got demoted, and the honest read of the week is that the price war and the trust war are the same war. Here is the full arc and what it means for how you actually work.

The AI channel had one story this week, and it was a launch. OpenAI shipped GPT-5.6, priced it to take the frontier from Anthropic, and then spent the back half of the week proving why cheaper is not the same as better. The dailies tracked each benchmark as it dropped. This memo tells the whole arc, because the numbers only mean something laid end to end.

The setup, which was about money and permission, not model quality. The week opened with OpenAI's finances doing the talking. Ed Zitron's leaked numbers, repeated all week, put the company at $13 billion of revenue against $34 billion of spend, a $21 billion operating loss, with an internal projection of $660 billion of burn over five years. Against that backdrop OpenAI floated giving Washington a roughly 5 percent stake in itself, about $42 billion, and held GPT-5.6 in limited government preview before launch. The other half of the setup was Anthropic pulling Fable 5 out of its subscriptions on July 12 and moving it to pay-per-token, per the model-flood preview, which set the whole ecosystem racing to bottle what Fable does before the meter started.

The launch, and the numbers that made it matter. GPT-5.6 shipped in three tiers, Sol, Terra, and Luna, with Sol the flagship, per the value-war breakdown. The price list is the headline: Sol at $5 in and $30 out per million tokens against Fable 5 at $10 and $50, so roughly half. On Artificial Analysis's Intelligence Index (a blended benchmark of reasoning and knowledge), Sol at max effort scored 59, one single point under Fable 5's 60, at about a third of the cost per task. It led the coding-agent index at 80 while running about 40 percent cheaper per task, and on one head-to-head it hit a higher score at roughly half the cost of GPT-5.5.

Elon Musk shipped Grok 4.5 into the same week, trained with Cursor and credited to a $60 billion acquisition, at around 1.4 trillion parameters, and it landed at $0.31 per task on the Intelligence Index against $2.75 for Claude Opus 4.8, on the cost-performance frontier, per the value-war note. The pattern across all of it was the same, a model that gets you most of the way to Fable for a fraction of the bill.

The workflow flip, the part you can actually use. The practical consequence showed up in how the power users rewired their pipelines. The smart-money pattern went from Fable-plans-Opus-builds to Fable-plans-Sol-builds: let Fable interview you and write the spec through several adversarial planning rounds, then hand the actual building to Sol running in Codex, then let Fable do one or two fix passes.

The economics are brutal in Sol's favor. One builder rebuilt his wife's website with twenty agents using Fable only as the foreman and cheap workers underneath, and it cost about $8 against $85 to $105 for an all-Fable run on the same job, per the $8-website note. Another burned $150 of Fable credits in thirty minutes and called it economically unsustainable. The rule that crystallized is the 80-at-half test, if a cheaper model gives you 80 percent of the result at half the price, use it, and save the expensive model for the 20 percent that actually needs it, per the Fable-cost clip.

The catch is that Fable is still the better model. The benchmark that cut through the hype came from Every's 30-day review. On a senior-engineer test, rewriting a vibecoded app from scratch, GPT-5.6 scored 56 against Fable's 91, and the reviewer read Sol as a smaller model post-trained extremely well rather than a bigger brain. In a side-by-side day of work, the verdict landed as Fable is the better manager and Sol the better worker, with Fable winning most quality comparisons while costing four to fifteen times more per run. So the flip is not Sol beats Fable, it is Sol is finally good enough to be the hands while Fable stays the brain, and that distinction is the whole trade.

The trust war arrived right on cue. The same week Sol was winning the price argument, it was deleting users' files. Matt Shumer reported it wiped almost all of his Mac's files, others compiled matching cases, and the behavior was documented in the model card before launch. His conclusion, that this is why he trusts Fable a thousand times more, is the sentence the week turned on.

It got worse structurally, with OpenAI's safety teams now reporting to a VP of research and the head of safety leaving, per the safety-cartel note. The deeper argument came from a DeepMind history: it is impossible to impose safety restraints inside one lab in a competitive race, and Demis Hassabis proved it when his 2014 sale to Google was conditioned on an ethics board that then got no real veto. A lab shipping a file-deleting model documented in its own card while demoting safety is what that argument looks like from the inside.

Anthropic's counter-week, quieter and aimed at the same throat. While OpenAI made noise, Anthropic moved the org chart and the odds board. Claude Cowork went to the cloud with scheduled tasks that run on Anthropic's servers, MIT's Gilbert Strang joined, and Polymarket odds of Anthropic hitting a $2 trillion valuation by year-end jumped to 47 percent while OpenAI's $1 trillion odds collapsed 23 points. Then Brad Gerstner put the exclamation point on it, saying that if Anthropic ends the year over $100 billion in revenue it is on a path to $300 to $400 billion next year, calling $200 billion of incremental revenue unprecedented, per the All-In segment. Even Musk recanted mid-week, calling himself clearly wrong about Anthropic and naming it the leader.

The China undercurrent. Underneath the US fight, the commons kept shifting east. A Chinese model beat GPT-5.5 on real coding work at a tenth of the price early in the week, and by the weekend 98 trillion tokens were running through Chinese models in June, up 113 percent month over month and nearly double the American 53 trillion, with 20 of the 50 most-used models now Chinese. For the local crowd the practical arbitrage is that Qwen3.6 quants now fit a capable model on 24 gigabytes of VRAM, and the window where the best open models are freely downloadable may be closing from both ends.

What it means for how you work, and what to watch. The takeaway is not to switch to Sol, it is to stop paying Fable prices for Sol-grade work. Route the planning and the hard 20 percent to Fable, route the building and the bulk tokens to Sol or Grok, and keep a paid model you trust for anything that touches your file system, because the file-deletion story is a real reason to sandbox Sol rather than hand it your machine.

The free Fable window closed July 12, so the cheap planning hours are gone and the routing discipline now costs real money. The one variable that would change the whole call is the next frontier drop, with Musk saying a new Mythos is imminent and OpenAI's Brockman putting GPT-6 under a month away, because a single point of Intelligence Index between Sol and Fable is either a rounding error or the last thin moat holding the price of the best model up.
