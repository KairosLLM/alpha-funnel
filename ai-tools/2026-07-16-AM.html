OpenAI's first hardware is a camera-equipped speaker that moves on its own, and its cheaper new model is finally good enough that power users are ripping Claude out

The device leaked in full: screenless, battery-powered, watching the room, designed with Jony Ive, shipping 2027. The bigger tell is a heavy Claude user who dropped a $200-a-day Opus habit for a $20 ChatGPT plan, and a warning from Granola that making AI notes too smart makes them lie.

Two things define the AI morning: OpenAI's move into hardware got specific, and its cheaper model started winning defections that price alone could not buy a month ago. Ranked.

1. OpenAI's first device is a speaker that watches you, and it is a real bet against the phone. The full leak, per Diet TBPN: Bloomberg reports the first OpenAI consumer device is a screenless AI-companion speaker, movable and battery-powered so you carry it room to room, with a camera and sensors to understand its surroundings and recognize when you walk in, plus mechanical elements that move on their own to feel alive. It runs on GPT Live, OpenAI's real-time voice mode, and personalizes over time off your own data down to your email, with Jony Ive designing it and about five devices in the pipeline, the first unveiling this year for a 2027 launch.

The strategic read is that OpenAI is betting the ambient home device beats the phone as the way people live with AI, the same bet Amazon made with Alexa and never cashed. The counter is obvious, a speaker sounds silly next to a phone you already carry, but the Alexa experiment proved people will talk to a box on the counter, and a box that sees the room and remembers your inbox is a different animal. The catch is trust, since this is the exact product Apple says OpenAI stole to build.

2. The cheaper model is now good enough to win a switch, not just a price war. The more telling story is a defection. Per Alex Finn, a heavy Claude user who was spending about $200 a day on the Opus API is ripping Claude out for GPT-5.6 for the first time, on three reasons: it is smarter than Opus in his testing, it works inside a $20-a-month subscription where Claude charges per use, and it is meaningfully better at computer use, driving a browser and taking screenshots. Claude's only remaining edge, he says, is personality, that it stands on its position while ChatGPT sways when challenged.

The usable nugget under the hype is the configuration. Running GPT-5.6 inside the Hermes agent, the tiers are Soul, Terra, and Luna with Soul the best, and the key setting is reasoning on medium, because the higher effort levels overthink and forget instructions. This is the same efficiency story as yesterday's pricing math, but a heavy user actually switching is a stronger signal than a benchmark, because the thing that finally moved him was cost and computer use, not raw intelligence.

3. Making AI notes too smart makes them put words in your mouth. The sharpest limit of the week, per Granola's CEO on Every: when a note-taker pulls in context beyond the meeting itself, it starts inferring things that were never said. His example is precise, someone says a polite "I like that idea" in the room, and the model writes that Dan actually thinks it is terrible, because Dan shot down that class of idea in five other meetings.

That is the model injecting a conclusion the meeting never produced, and it is the failure mode of every agent that gets fed more context to be more helpful. His other point is that the real signal often lives in what the transcript misses, like a three-minute silence that never shows up in text.

4. The practitioner corner: what to stop doing with Claude Code. Two quick, concrete lessons. Per David Ondrej, the bad habits worth breaking are running everything in one tab all day, which pollutes the context window across unrelated tasks, and dumping everything into your instructions file, which should hold only what applies to every single session, and hoarding a dozen connected tools you do not understand. And per Nate B Jones, the quieter problem is that anyone running several AI tools has quietly become the human integration layer, copying output from one and pasting it into the next and re-explaining context all day, the reason a setup can feel busy without ever feeling faster.

Both point at the same discipline, the work now is managing context, not collecting tools.

5. The fintech crossover: Stripe wants PayPal's 400 million accounts. The deal that touches this channel, per Diet TBPN: Stripe and the private-equity firm Advent are bidding $53 billion for PayPal with $50 billion of financing, and the strategic logic is data, not payments volume. PayPal comes with 400 million consumer accounts already tied to bank details and identity, which the write-up calls the white whale of consumer.

The plan reportedly involves cutting deep into PayPal's 25,000 employees on the theory that an AI-era payments company does not need that many people. Michael Burry, a shareholder, says he will not sell at this price. The through-line with the rest of the morning is the same, the value is moving to whoever owns the direct relationship with the user, whether that is a speaker on your counter or an account with your bank details attached.
