GPT-5.6's full price list is out and Sol lands one point under Fable on intelligence at a third of the cost, and OpenAI says Sol trained its own smaller sibling

The launch specs are hard now, three tiers from Sol to Luna, Sol at half Fable's per-token price, and Sol topping the coding-agent benchmark. On All-In, Cerebras CEO Andrew Feldman said AGI is already here, and Anthropic answered the launch by resetting everyone's limits while Musk conceded it is the leader. Here is the signal.

Yesterday we had the reviews. This morning we have the price list, the benchmarks, and a claim that the model trained its own smaller version. The center of gravity in AI shifted from which model is smartest to what each point of intelligence costs. Here is what changed.

1. The GPT-5.6 price list is public, and Sol undercuts Fable by half. The hard numbers landed. Per the Hermes list, GPT-5.6 ships in three tiers, Sol at $5 and $30 per million input and output tokens, Terra at $2.5 and $15, and Luna at $1 and $6, against Fable 5 at $10 and $50. That puts Sol at roughly half Fable's per-token price. These are also the first OpenAI models with cache-write pricing at 1.25 times input, matching Anthropic, and a new max reasoning tier that also mirrors Anthropic's ladder. The intelligence war became a price war overnight.

2. On the benchmarks, Sol is a hair behind on smarts and ahead on coding, at a fraction of the cost. The sweep is consistent. Per the Hermes list, on the Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index Sol on max scores 59, one point under Fable 5's 60, at about a third of the cost per task. Sol on max then leads the Coding Agent Index outright at 80 and comes in about 40 percent cheaper per task than Fable, and it takes the top spot on DeepSWE at 73 to 74 percent. Fable still holds the lead on the Briefcase rubric at 56 percent to 42 and on analytical reasoning. The one-line read making the rounds is that this is no longer an intelligence war, it is a value war.

3. OpenAI says Sol trained Luna by itself. The line worth stopping on. Per the Hermes list, Andrew Curran quoted the OpenAI livestream saying that GPT-5.6 Sol autonomously post-trained GPT-5.6 Luna, a frontier model building its smaller sibling with minimal human involvement. Whatever you make of the framing, it is the most concrete recursive-self-improvement claim a lab has put on the record, and it sets up the next story directly.

4. On All-In, Cerebras says AGI is already here. The claim came with a book of business behind it. Per All-In, Cerebras CEO Andrew Feldman said that by any definition used 20 years ago we have artificial general intelligence now, it just is not fully deployed. He put numbers on the company, a $25 billion backlog, a wafer chip that costs half a billion dollars to make, and a claim of 15 times faster inference than the GPU architecture he calls 20 years old, with named buyers including OpenAI, Anthropic, xAI, Google, Microsoft, and AWS. His open-source pitch was specific, that Cerebras already serves GLM, Kimi, and Qwen alongside closed models and builds sovereign models for GSK and G42, and that the US needs more domestic open source because the only real options today are a 12-billion-parameter model or Chinese ones. He also flagged the scale, that the data centers coming will use more power than the previous 50 years on Earth.

5. The safety debate split into an antitrust fight and a staged-rollout defense. Two separate arguments landed. Per AI Central, a piece called The Safety Cartel makes the case that OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind cooperate on safety in exactly the areas where they compete, raising a Sherman Act question, and it points at Anthropic's Project Glasswing and its exclusive Mythos preview access as the kind of arrangement antitrust law was built to scrutinize, all against crossholdings like Microsoft's roughly 27 percent of OpenAI. Per All-In, Feldman defended staged, red-teamed releases with a pharma analogy, and cited Palo Alto's Nikesh Arora running the new model against Palo Alto's own software, where it found bugs the company did not know about and forced a six-week patch cycle. One argument says the labs are too cozy, the other says the caution is earning its keep.

6. Anthropic answered the launch by opening the taps, and Musk conceded. The competitive response was immediate. Per the Hermes list, Anthropic reset every user's five-hour and weekly rate limits the same evening OpenAI shipped, with the team telling people to enjoy more Fable, on top of the free-through-July-12 access already in place. In the same window Elon Musk posted that he was clearly wrong about Anthropic, that it is obviously the current leader in AI, and that it will have Mythos 2 ready soon, the first mention of that model as imminent. OpenAI also launched ChatGPT Work, an agent powered by Codex and GPT-5.6 that acts across a user's apps and files for hours at a stretch.

7. The honest head-to-head keeps landing on the same verdict. The hands-on tests agree even when the benchmarks do not settle it. Per Nate Herk, a full day of Sol through Codex against Fable 5 through Claude Code had Fable winning two of three agentic builds on quality but costing three to twenty times more, $14 to $19 a run against Sol's $1 to $4.50, while on one-off API calls Sol won 24 to 3, mostly because Fable refused many requests outright on guardrails. His verdict is that Fable is the better manager and Sol is the better worker, the ideal being Fable orchestrating a fleet of Sol agents, and that the honest comparison is Sol against Opus 4.8, not against Fable. Per the Hermes list, Nous Research's Teknium made the same point from the other side, that Fable refused a common heavy task while Sol did it, cheaper and faster, and that is turning over-refusal into a real reason developers move workloads.

8. The moviemaking economics quietly broke. The most concrete real-world number came from film. Per All-In, Black Forest Labs founder Robin Rombach, the inventor of latent diffusion, described a single multimodal model spanning image, audio, video, and action prediction aimed at putting a brain on a robot, and said Martin Scorsese has sat with him multiple times using the models for storyboarding with a human in the loop. The number that lands is Gal Gadot shooting a Bitcoin movie on a soundstage with no green screens and all AI backgrounds for a $30 million budget against roughly $150 million to build it physically, a film she said would never have been green-lit otherwise. In the same breath, the Disney and OpenAI Sora licensing relationship is reportedly no longer happening.
