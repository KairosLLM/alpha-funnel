Mallaby puts 50/50 odds on OpenAI selling itself by next summer, and the rescue plan floating around Washington is a 5 percent government stake

The morning had the GPT-5.6 price list, the evening has the balance sheet behind it, a $21 billion operating loss and an IPO pushed to 2027. Grok 4.5 landed as a real coding option, GPT Live gave its first livestream interview, and Chinese models now carry 60 percent of developer traffic. Here is the evening signal.

This morning was launch-week scoreboard, prices and benchmarks. Tonight is the other ledger, what the launch is costing the company that shipped it, and who is quietly eating its developer base. Ranked, the money story first.

1. Mallaby's case that OpenAI goes broke has numbers attached, and a deadline. On Prof G Markets, Sebastian Mallaby, the CFR fellow who wrote The Power Law, stood by his January call that OpenAI runs out of money within 18 months and put 50/50 odds on a forced sale at a discount by next summer, to Amazon, to Microsoft, or piece by piece as the staff splinters. The core arithmetic comes from Ed Zitron's leaked financials: $13 billion of 2025 revenue against $34 billion of spend, a $21 billion operating loss, with internal documents projecting $660 billion of burn over five years.

The demand side is the trap: 900 million consumer users and only 5 percent pay, with India the number one market. Even the famous $122 billion raise was about two thirds IPO-conditional promises or payment-in-kind compute rather than cash.

The company is already behaving like the thesis is true. It pulled back from Stargate-scale data center builds, canceled money-losing Sora, stalled the shopping and ads push, and let the IPO slip to 2027, which Mallaby reads through the WeWork 2019 lens, the prospectus is where stories go to die. Secondaries already trade well below the $852 billion mark, and a down round near $600 billion would gut employee options exactly when retention matters most.

2. The fallback plan is making Washington a shareholder, and there is precedent now. Per the same interview, OpenAI has floated giving the US government a 5 percent stake, roughly $43 billion, and Mallaby's description is exact, recruiting the investment banker you cannot say no to. The template is Intel: the government took 10 percent last August, the stock is up about 400 percent against the SOX semiconductor index's 150, with Commerce Secretary Lutnick strong-arming partners along the way. CFR's Jonathan Hillman counts 30 US companies with government equity stakes since January 2025, prediction markets put better than one-in-three odds on OpenAI joining them, and Anthropic, Palantir, and Anduril get floated as next. State capitalism stopped being a China story.

3. Meanwhile the developer base is going Chinese, six months at a time. Mallaby's China numbers deserve their own line: Chinese models went from under a third of OpenRouter developer traffic in late 2025 to 60 percent by mid-2026, Zhipu's latest nearly matches Anthropic's Mythos, and Cursor, Airbnb, Shopify, and Uber all run Chinese models in production while Microsoft tests DeepSeek. The Limitless crew adds the silicon layer, DeepSeek and Zhipu both leaked custom inference-chip programs while OpenAI's own Jalapeno chip is about nine months out. Mallaby's policy answer is a Cold-War-style non-proliferation deal on open-weight frontier models, and his warning is that the current tools, the Malaysia cloud loophole and customer-by-customer sign-offs, gate nothing.

4. Grok 4.5 is the efficiency story of the week, and the Cursor acquisition just paid off. Per Limitless, SpaceX AI's first major release since going public lands around third place behind Fable 5 and GPT-5.5, at Opus 4.8-class quality for somewhere between half and a seventeenth of the cost depending on which host you believe. The remarkable part is the shape: 1.4 trillion parameters against Fable 5's estimated 10 to 15 trillion, and it solves SWE-bench tasks (the standard agentic coding benchmark) in about 16,000 tokens where Opus uses 67,000. The training data from the $60 billion Cursor acquisition gets the credit, AI Garage's hands-on agrees it is now a legitimate coding option, and it ships with X Premium+.

Musk promises a from-scratch foundation model every month through the end of 2026 on roughly 500,000 GPUs, and the same company unveiled Star Mine, a gigawatt-scale orbital AI-compute program starting with the solar-powered AI1 satellite (Lark's caveat for anyone eyeing the stock: tradable float goes from about 5 to 10 percent now to 60 percent by November, and he only buys under $100).

5. GPT-5.6 day two: the reviews hold up, and the voice model stole the show. AI Garage ran Sol as the daily driver and it fixed the Hyperliquid trading agent that 5.5 spent a week failing to debug, with answers that finally stopped rambling. Greg Brockman says GPT-6 arrives in under a month. The sleeper release is GPT Live, the full-duplex voice model (it listens while it talks, no turn-taking), which Limitless calls OpenAI's biggest release of the year, and which gave its first-ever livestream interview on Market Bubble, where it argued the internet's next battle is agency rather than attention, the algorithm that stops recommending your favorite movie and starts generating it.

Housekeeping for your own stack: the free Fable 5 window ends July 12, and Kris Torrington's rule is to spend the remaining days on your hardest unfinished work, then route by output-versus-cost after.

6. The agent reality check: 1.6 million registered, almost none of them did anything. Nate B Jones reports that at OpenClaw's peak 1.6 million agents signed up for the agent social network and most never ran a single task. Intelligence is metered and available, instincts about what to point it at are not. His evidence stack for what actually works:

  • A Stanford result: a cheap coding model goes from 15.9 percent solve rate on one attempt to 56 percent at 250 attempts, beating that year's single-shot state of the art, but without a mechanical checker the gains stall. Evals are the bottleneck, no longer the model.
  • Anthropic's multi-agent research: token spend explains 80 percent of run-quality variance, and an agent team beat the solo frontier model by 90 percent.
  • His own harness splits the labor, Fable 5 writes the specs and judges, cheap open-source workers burn the tokens, for roughly a tenth of the cost.
  • The Doomed from the Start paper attacks the waste from the other end, a probe reading the model's hidden state after the first action to abort doomed runs, saving up to 47 percent of tokens with about a tenth of would-be successes lost.
  • And the traffic backdrop from Limitless: 52 percent of Cloudflare crawler requests are now AI training, up from 22 percent last spring. Cloudflare's answer is x402, a micropayments API for charging the machines.

7. The lighter one: AI solved 3D printing, but only the fun half. Jordamøn's piece is a tidy case study in where generative tools actually stand. Meshy's text-to-3D has 10 million users and 100 million generated models with a 97 percent slicer pass rate on figurines, and Bambu Lab printers now run on-device failure detection. But the generators have no concept of scale, threads, or tolerances, so a functional bracket still means learning Fusion 360. One hobby now has two skill floors, and the gap between making art and making parts is exactly the gap between generation and engineering.
