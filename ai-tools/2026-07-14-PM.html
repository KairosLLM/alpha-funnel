Apple's lawsuit against OpenAI now has two names, a stolen supplier list, and a scheme where recruits studied confidential files as homework

The complaint reads like a heist, and Apple wants a courtroom instead of a settlement. Meta undercut the frontier again with a model 25 percent cheaper, Anthropic's Fable keeps disappearing from the subscription, and the plumbing under every agent just quietly changed.

The AI story today is a lawsuit with receipts, a price war getting cheaper, and a builder-level change most people will feel before they understand it. Ranked.

1. Apple's case against OpenAI got specific, and the details are damning. Per the Limitless breakdown, the complaint centers on two people. Chang Liu, an eight-year Apple electrical engineer, kept his company laptop after leaving and an authentication bug left his access to Apple's file storage and internal chat alive even after he joined OpenAI, where he allegedly downloaded more than 1,000 pages of confidential material on unreleased products, texting a colleague that he could still get into the network storage and it was so funny.

Tang Yu, a 24-year Apple veteran and former VP of design who is now OpenAI's chief hardware design officer, allegedly emailed himself Apple's entire supplier contact list on the way out and ran what the filing calls a homework pattern, having job candidates still at Apple screenshot and study confidential project files, then explain them in interviews to fill the gaps in his own knowledge.

The context makes it a war rather than a squabble. Roughly 400 Apple employees have gone to OpenAI, mostly in hardware, targeting the screenless AI device OpenAI wants to ship around February 2027 at a 100-million-unit scale.

Per Prof G Markets, Apple will not settle, because it wants a judge's ruling and painful discovery, and a court could in theory force OpenAI to unwind the hardware program to the beginning, a real threat to the IPO story. The through-line the guest drew is that OpenAI is now alienating every partner it has, Apple, Elon Musk, and even Microsoft's Satya Nadella, one at a time.

2. Meta undercut the frontier again, and the model itself keeps becoming a commodity. Per Prof G Markets, Meta's stock rose about 15 percent last week on a new model, Muse Spark 1.1, pitched at roughly 25 percent cheaper than the frontier and aimed straight at OpenAI and Anthropic pricing, though the guest thinks the real driver was Mark Zuckerberg confirming Meta may license out its excess compute at high margins.

The price war has more entrants behind it, with DeepSeek reportedly raising at a $71 billion valuation and heading for an IPO, per the Market AM list, and Samsung landing Anthropic as a custom-chip customer. The pattern is the same one that clipped IBM today, the model is turning into a commodity while nobody has yet cracked consumer AI, so the margin moves to whoever is cheapest or owns the compute.

3. Anthropic's Fable keeps vanishing from the plan, and ChatGPT Work has a file-deletion problem. The access story is getting concrete, per a builder's newsletter: Anthropic's Fable 5 launched on a Tuesday in June, was suspended globally by Friday with no notice, stayed gone for 19 days, then came back on usage credits rather than the flat subscription, with its removal date slipping from July 7 to July 12 to July 19. The writer's blunt read is that frontier access is narrowing on price and geography, and the subsidized $200 subscription is the part that stops scaling, so a model leaving your plan is an extraction deadline.

The rival product has its own trust gap. Per Kris Torrington, ChatGPT Work, which folds Codex directly into ChatGPT, is worth learning now but not trusting with your only copy of anything, because of unverified reports that the Sol model deleted user files during agentic runs. His rule is no backup, no agent, and his two green lights to actually adopt it are an official file-safety patch note and the feature reaching the Plus and Business tiers. Both stories point the same way, the tools are moving faster than their guarantees.

4. The plumbing under every agent just changed, and it explains the flakiness. The under-the-radar builder story, per AI Central, is that the Model Context Protocol, the standard that connects agents to tools, is going stateless in its biggest revision since launch. The recent rash of tools that load but will not fire and connections that silently drop traces back to the old session model breaking at scale, and the fix eliminates sessions so each request carries its context inline. The release candidate landed May 21 with the final spec due July 28, and the TypeScript software kit ships under new package names, so anyone building on it should expect a bumpy migration.

The payoff of all this agent plumbing is starting to show up in real money. Per 0xSammy, AI agents are becoming actual bank customers, with Visa's agent-payment rails doing about $87,000 in seven days across 12,000 wallets and Cloudflare reporting that automated traffic has crossed 60 percent of web requests for the first time. When agents transact on their own, the metric that matters shifts from how many accounts exist to how often the software spends.

5. The job the whole thing is creating pays $200,000 and did not exist last year. Per Nate Herk, the emerging role is the in-house AI consultant, the person who decides what to build rather than how, because the building is the part getting commoditized. The data behind it is real, an IBM survey of 2,000 chief executives found 76 percent now have some chief-AI-officer role, up from 26 percent a year ago, and workers with AI skills are paid about 56 percent more than the colleague beside them.

His playbook is to audit your own job for the tasks that eat hours and are low-risk if wrong, automate them, make the saved dollars visible, then escalate from annoyances to real business constraints and propose the formal role with a number attached. In a week where a legal startup and a voice company showed AI eating trillion-dollar industries, the safest seat is the one deciding where the AI points.
