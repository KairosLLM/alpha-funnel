Fable 5 is the best model on the board and it may leave in days, so the useful question is how to use it cheaply right now

The practitioners who actually ship agents say Fable 5 is a step change, its own team is publishing the playbook for cutting the cost 80% before it goes, and the China price war just turned into a distillation fight.

ModelsAgentsToolingComputeLocal/Open

The thread running through this morning is urgency. Fable 5 is drawing the strongest praise yet from the people who build agents for a living, and by their own account it is priced to leave via the API in a matter of days. So the practical move is not to debate whether it is good but to learn the cost playbook while you still can. Under that sits a China story that stopped being about price and became about who copied whom.

1. The people who ship agents for a living say Fable 5 is a genuine step change. This is not lab marketing, it is the practitioner tape. On the Hermes list, Shannon Sands and Uzi are calling Fable 5 the first model that makes long-horizon agents feel reliable, and Thariq published a "Field Guide" for driving it. The concrete example is Machina's deck-building agent reporting roughly 3x gains once it leaned on the model's memory instead of stuffing everything into the prompt.

The catch is the clock. Per Chase AI, the team behind it has signaled Fable 5 is "leaving via API pricing in a few days," so the window to build against it at current terms is short. If you have a real agent workload, this is the weekend to run it.

2. The cost playbook matters more than the benchmark, and the team published it. A frontier model you cannot afford to run in a loop is a demo. The useful Chase AI breakdown is that Fable 5's price swings enormously with its "effort" setting, the dial that controls how much reasoning it burns per task.

  • At max effort it runs about $22 a task at a 70% pass rate. That is real money once a background agent is looping on its own.
  • Dropped to low effort it falls to about $3.76 a task, more than 80% cheaper, and still beats Opus 4.8 on the same work.
  • The orchestration trick is to use the expensive setting as a planner or advisor that hands specs to cheaper models, rather than paying top rate for every keystroke. A "Ponytail" configuration reportedly trims another 20%.

The takeaway is that the cost of intelligence is now a tuning problem, not a fixed price. Most teams are overpaying because they run one setting for everything.

3. The killer use case is cloning software you already pay for. The demo that lands in Chase AI's use-case rundown is pointing the model's deep-research mode at an existing tool and having it rebuild a working version. The worked example pairs it with WhisperFlow, the dictation app, to reconstruct the feature set from scratch. The same loop covers auditing your own usage, standing up an agentic operating layer, and running code review. Treat these as proof the build-versus-buy line is moving, not as turnkey products yet.

4. Hermes shipped an agent release built entirely around not lying to you. Nous Research pushed Hermes Agent v0.18.0, the "Judgement Release," per the Hermes list. The design is a direct answer to the reliability complaint that has dogged autonomous agents. It adds completion contracts (the agent must satisfy an explicit checklist before it declares a task done), an evidence ledger that records why it believes each step succeeded, and a "goal_judge" that grades its own output. The interesting shift is that the frontier of agent work is moving from raw capability to verifiable honesty about what the agent actually did.

5. The China AI fight stopped being about price and became about distillation. For months the story was Chinese labs undercutting the frontier on cost. This week it inverted. Per the Financial Markets list, DeepSeek is raising its V4 Pro price to about 12 yuan (roughly $1.77) per million output tokens (the unit models are billed in), up from 6 yuan after a 75% cut in May, which reads as a company that thinks it is winning share and no longer needs to buy it.

At the same time Alibaba reportedly banned Anthropic's Claude Code over a "distillation scandal," distillation being the practice of training a cheaper model on a more expensive one's outputs. The accusation cuts both directions in this market, and it is now the sharp edge of the US-China model race. Separately, on the All-In show notes, Palantir and Nvidia are reported to be pairing on open Nemotron models, with Karp pushing government customers toward open weights. That is the same open-versus-closed argument playing out on the US side.
