The AI morning is about the floor falling out of the price of a token and what the labs are doing about it. The models keep getting cheaper and the fight over who profits keeps getting louder. Ranked.

1. Kimi K3 made intelligence cheap, and the American labs answered with a lobbying campaign. The number that matters is the cost per token. Per Limitless, Moonshot's Kimi K3 is a roughly 2.8-trillion-parameter open model that runs about $0.50 to $1 per million input tokens against roughly $50 for a US frontier model, and an OpenRouter chart shows close to 60 percent of US users' token spend has already flipped to open Chinese models. Moonshot was valued at $4.3 billion in January and is now raising at $30 billion, and it paused new signups because it ran out of GPUs, which tells you the demand is real and the constraint is compute.

The labs read the same chart and reached for regulation. Per Diet TBPN, OpenAI and Anthropic executives are warning that cheap Chinese open models create an unacceptable security future without new rules, and David Sacks calls it a regulatory-capture play by two companies preparing to go public that simply want to eliminate the competition. The policy machinery is already moving, with Treasury Secretary Bessent saying the administration can sanction overseas models that steal US intellectual property and the White House weighing an executive order aimed at open models, which would force even a router like OpenRouter to stop serving them. The through-line is that when you cannot win on price you try to win on the rulebook.

2. The most useful AI story today is a finance manager who taught Codex his own job. The practitioner lesson of the morning is not a model, it is a method. Per Every, OpenAI's Kyle Kober is not an engineer, and over a month of evenings he used Codex to build a custom app for the company's monthly compute close that compressed roughly five days of work into about five hours and now gets 95 to 98 percent of the way there before a human writes the commentary.

The way he did it is the part to steal. Per that interview, he took his hardest recurring monthly process, did it the old way while having Codex gather every file and Slack thread for context, then codified each step as a reusable skill, reverse-engineering his own job over time. He keeps a project folder with an AGENTS.md file so the agent orchestrates across projects, builds skills that improve other skills, and instructs it never to post to the CFO's channel before he reviews. This landed on the day OpenAI shipped GPT-5.6 Soul and merged Codex into ChatGPT, and the host's blunt framing is that Fable is the better raw programmer while GPT-5.6 is the more usable one, the model a non-coder can actually delegate to.

3. The technique behind the reliable agents has a name now, and it is graph engineering. The concept getting sharper this week is how you wire agents together. Per Chase AI, the field is moving from loop engineering, one agent running a trigger-task-check cycle, to graph engineering, where you decompose that single loop into many atomic single-task agents that each keep a clean context window and feed a synthesis agent above them.

The payoff is quality and diagnosis. Per that video, splitting a job across agents keeps each one out of the context rot that sets in at 300,000 to 500,000 tokens, lets them run in parallel, and localizes failure so you can tell a YouTube problem from a Reddit one. His third rule is the important one, that an agent grading its own high-stakes output is unreliable, so you add a separate review agent, perhaps not even the same model, to judge the work against the spec. That is exactly the mechanism Nate B Jones credits with catching one of his own agents after it hallucinated his wife's words while rebuilding her website, a second agent checking the first and shipping the fix without him lifting a finger.

4. And the frame to keep as the tools get cheaper, from Winston Churchill's writing machine. The steadying idea of the morning is a century old. Per Vox Day, Churchill did not write his roughly 15 million published words, he dictated them to relays of secretaries and researchers he called the Syndicate, a small industrial concern with a single editorial mind at the top, and the argument is that a textual AI model is that Syndicate automated for anyone.

The warning inside the essay is the part that matters for how you use these tools. Per that piece, what made the histories Churchillian is that he never let the apparatus touch the two things that carry a person's mark, the judgment and the voice, and he points the machine only at the drudgery. Hand AI the judgment and the voice as well and you get flattened, anonymous prose, the literary equivalent of a form letter. The line to keep as the models drop toward a dollar a million tokens is that the tool does not decide the nature of the output, the writer decides by choosing what he will control.
