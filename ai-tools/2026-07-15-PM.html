All three big labs shipped the same product in one week, the chatbot is dead, and the model that replaced it also deleted a user's production database

ChatGPT Work, Claude Cowork, and Copilot Cowork turned the AI into an agentic workspace, and 90 percent of what people do with it is not coding. OpenAI's new model does the same job in a sixth of the time for a fifth of the cost, but people are already using it to wipe their own machines and to train other models.

The AI story this week is a product category crystallizing, a price that keeps falling, and a safety record that keeps getting worse in the same breath. Ranked.

1. The chatbot became a command center, and every lab shipped it at once. The clearest read on where AI products are going, per AI Central: the agentic workspace has replaced the chat box as the default shape, and all three labs shipped one in the same July week, OpenAI's ChatGPT Work on GPT-5.6, Anthropic's Claude Cowork, and Microsoft's Copilot Cowork running on Anthropic's Opus. The tell is what people actually do with it. Across 1.2 million Cowork sessions, more than 90 percent were not software development, with business operations making up a third and content creation a sixth.

That reframes the whole competition. When the model is a commodity and the product is an agent that does your operations work, the moat moves to the connectors, the skills, and the admin controls, not the benchmark score. OpenAI is consolidating hard to win it, folding Codex into ChatGPT, adding hosted Sites, and killing its Atlas browser on August 9, while Anthropic added thirteen enterprise connectors and a Claude Science mode that a neuroscientist used to compress two years of literature review.

2. The new model is a sixth of the time and a fifth of the cost, and that is the actual release. Per Kris Torrington, the receipt on GPT-5.6 is not a smarter model, it is a cheaper one, with the Soul Pro tier producing a result in about one minute for 77 cents against six minutes and $4 for the prior generation. It sits just behind Claude's Fable on the harder knowledge-work benchmarks but wins decisively on cost and speed, so the honest way to judge it is cost per usable result rather than raw intelligence. The pricing war is the story, and it is squeezing everyone.

The catch is at the enterprise tier, per Ruben Hassid. OpenAI has no good middle plan, only a low-usage Business tier or an Enterprise contract with a 150-seat minimum, so a 150-person company runs roughly $3,000 a month in base fees plus $17,000 to $37,000 on top depending on the model. His conclusion is that the era of cheap unlimited tokens is ending, and that for a shop under 150 people Claude's flat plan still wins, with ChatGPT kept mainly for its image generation.

3. The same model that ships this fast is deleting people's machines. The dark side got named today. Per the Limitless podcast, Matt Schumer had a GPT-5.6 Sol subagent run a cleanup command that expanded the wrong directory and wiped his local machine unrecoverably, and a second user reported it deleted an entire production database. That is why one host will not give it desktop access at all, and it is the reason the speed and the price come with a trust discount the benchmarks do not show.

The more consequential wrinkle is what people are doing with the cheap capability. They are using GPT-5.6 to post-train and fine-tune other models, one OpenAI model teaching another, the exact capability Anthropic guardrailed Fable against, and Sam Altman is already saying GPT-6 arrives in under a month. The frontier is moving faster than the safety practices around it, and the file-deletion stories are what that looks like from a user's desk.

4. Two playbooks worth stealing, both about feeding the model less. The most useful practitioner idea this week is that more context makes the model dumber, not smarter. Per Dylan Davis, once you fill a context window past about half, quality drops like a rock, and the fix is a three-layer scaffold the AI builds for itself, a map file listing every document in a line each, a folder of one-page summaries, and the full sources read only when a specific section is needed. It is the same shape as a good filing system, and it makes the assistant get smarter as the folder grows instead of dumber.

The companion lesson comes from OpenAI's own new prompting guide, per the same channel: stop writing mega-prompts. The newest models find their own path, so spelling out every step forces an average process, and the better approach is to specify the destination and what good looks like, hold context to only what changes the answer, set three or four hard boundaries rather than twenty, and give a binary done-when test. Both playbooks point the same way, the skill is no longer stuffing the model, it is starving it of everything that does not matter.

5. The private numbers say the money is real, even as the pilots say it is not. Against the MIT finding that 95 percent of enterprise AI pilots show no return, the individual companies are printing revenue that is hard to argue with. Per All-In, the coding startup Lovable hit $500 million in revenue in 20 months, adding roughly $100 million every six months, with a million new projects a week and 700 million monthly visits to the apps it builds, and it is growing fastest in enterprise.

Granola, the note-taking app, is valued at $1.5 billion, per Every, and only started tracking its own token spend last week after a single Fable session cost a couple thousand dollars. The two facts coexist, most pilots do not pay back and a handful of companies are compounding revenue faster than almost anything in software history, exactly the shape of a real platform shift with a lot of waste around the edges.
