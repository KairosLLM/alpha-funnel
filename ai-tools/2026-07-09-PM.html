The 30-day Sol reviews landed and the verdict splits, it is the new daily driver and a better writer than Opus or Fable, but it still scored 56 to Fable's 91 on the hard rebuild

This morning was the launch specs. Tonight is the lived-in verdict from people who used Sol for a month, plus Codex the app is dead, Claude Cowork moved to the cloud, and Blue Origin took outside money for the first time in 25 years at a $130 billion tag. Here is the signal.

The morning gave us GPT-5.6 Sol's benchmarks. The evening gave us the reviews from people who actually ran it for a month, and they do not fully agree. The pattern under the disagreement is the important part, so here is what the day taught.

1. Sol is the new daily driver, and it is a genuinely better writer than the frontier. The reviews converge on a shape. Per Dan Shipper at Every, a month of internal use makes Sol plus its new harness the current gold standard for knowledge work, the Porsche of models, fast and precise and cheap enough to run all day, coding at an A tier with S still reserved for Fable. The surprise is the writing. Shipper says Sol is a better writer than both Opus 4.8 and Fable, because the two Claudes overexplain and Fable runs so long it invents its own private language. His read is that Sol is probably a smaller model than Fable that was post-trained extremely well, which means OpenAI and Anthropic are now deliberately building for different jobs.

  • The hard number keeps Fable on top where it counts. On Every's senior-engineer test, rewriting a vibecoded app from scratch, GPT-5.6 scored 56 out of 100 against Fable's 91, and Fable's code was simpler with fewer abstractions.

2. The routing fight got specific, and two power users disagree on where Sol goes. This is the real debate tonight. Per Alex Finn, 5.6 is the outright winner, Fable is still better at planning and holding a large codebase in its head but loses on execution, and Fable is dead in the water on price after he burned $500 of credits in a single afternoon. He expects Opus 5 to ship within a week as the affordable near-Fable answer. Where he breaks with the crowd is the sub-agent trick. Shipper endorses telling Fable to use Sol as a sub-agent, a match made in heaven because Fable burns credits the instant it spins up a hundred agents while Sol preserves the budget. Finn says do not do that, plan in Claude Code on high and hand the finished spec to ChatGPT to execute, because the harness that browses and signs up for sites is the actual edge now.

3. Codex the app is dead, and Claude Cowork moved to the cloud. Two quiet product shifts that change daily workflow. Per both Alex Finn and Every, the standalone Codex app is gone, folded into ChatGPT as a Work view that hides the code and a Codex view for developers, one engine underneath. On the Anthropic side, per one hands-on writeup, Claude Cowork shipped to web and mobile in beta for Max plans and scheduled tasks now run on Anthropic's servers with your laptop closed, though the tester found the catch, that a task tied to a local folder still only runs while your machine is awake, only the cloud connectors like Gmail and Drive run lid-closed. The tell in the launch data is that across 1.2 million Cowork sessions, 33 percent were business operations and over 90 percent were not coding at all.

4. Blue Origin took outside money for the first time in 25 years. The space race got a private round. Per Diet TBPN, Blue Origin is raising a roughly $10 billion round at about a $130 billion valuation, its first outside capital in 25 years, with Bezos putting in $2 billion directly and Coatue getting twice that at $4 billion. The company burns about $5 billion a year and has spent roughly $27 billion to date, and it is being valued on capability rather than revenue, second to orbit and reuse and ahead of China. The comps make the point, AST SpaceMobile near $27 billion and Rocket Lab near $50 billion, and the joke going around is that this makes SpaceX look underpriced.

5. Getty and Shutterstock called off their merger, and AI is why. A deal died on the same day two AI reviews shipped, and that is not a coincidence. Per Ben Thompson, Getty terminated its $3.7 billion merger with Shutterstock after UK regulators demanded it sell Shutterstock's editorial business. That headline price was set in early 2025, and both companies are now around $330 to $340 million in market cap, gutted by AI image generation from Meta Muse Image, Nano Banana Pro, and OpenAI Images 2. Thompson's read is that consolidation is the natural defense when an industry faces an external threat, and regulators reliably block the merger right when the industry needs it most.

6. The scarce skill is not the prompt, it is the loop. The through-line from this morning got a sharper name. Per swyx, the AI Engineer founder, you should stop writing prompts and start writing loops that create prompts, crediting Claude Code's Boris Cherny for the framing. A good agent loop is a specification, a verification step, and an explicit list of what not to do. The two things he says never to hand to the machine are your data structures and your user testing. The scale number underneath is loud, the token-billionaire program that expected 50 people to hit a billion tokens a week drew about 300 of 7,000 attendees, and Cursor developers are reportedly running 8 to 10 billion tokens a day.

7. The open-versus-closed fault line hardened. The week's model wave forced the question. Per the Hermes list, one builder says to just run Fable and GPT-5.6 Sol as your two main models because the gap over open or local models is now too big to justify, while Yann LeCun argues the biggest risk in AI is the concentration of power in a few proprietary providers and the only fix is open-source foundation models. swyx sided hard with the frontier, that the people preaching small models are coping because they do not have the GPUs, though he grants that agent labs will train their own vertical models rather than fine-tune. Both of these can be true, and the next Gemini release is the one that tests it.
