A cheap Chinese model matches the frontier and the incumbents keep growing anyway, because one engineer can burn $80,000 of tokens in a week

The gap between what open models can do and what enterprises will pay for is the whole AI trade right now, and a new MIT study says 95 percent of company AI pilots still show no profit. Demis Hassabis wants a federal gate that his rivals also have to pass, and Kalshi just made the price of compute itself a tradable thing.

The question hanging over every AI story this morning is the same one hanging over the memory stocks: the spending is enormous and the proven payoff is not here yet. Ranked.

1. Open models are as good and far cheaper, and it still is not moving the money. The puzzle, framed by Nate B Jones: a model like GLM 5.2 is genuinely excellent and a fraction of the price, yet OpenAI and Anthropic keep growing revenue at a tear, with no tipping point away from them. His evidence for why is a single data point that reframes the whole debate, one engineer reportedly spending $80,000 on tokens in a single week. At that scale the buyer does not chase the cheapest model, the buyer wants the one that fails least, because the expensive part is the engineer's time and the broken task, not the tokens.

The counterweight lands the same morning and cuts hard. An MIT study cited on the Hermes list found that 95 percent of enterprise generative-AI pilots show no profit-and-loss return at all. So the incumbents are growing on the spending of the few power users who genuinely get value, while the broad enterprise middle is still paying for pilots that do not pay back. That is a market held up by its top 5 percent, a strong position and a fragile one at the same time.

2. OpenAI cut Sol's price in half and shipped a live voice mode, and the pricing pressure is now constant. Per the Hermes list, Sam Altman says GPT-5.6 Sol runs at about half the price and twice the token efficiency of Anthropic's Fable, and the model shipped alongside a real-time voice product called GPT Live. That is the same move Meta made last week with a model priced a quarter cheaper than the frontier, and it points one way, the labs are competing on cost now because the capability gap between them has narrowed to something most users cannot feel.

The one wrinkle underneath it is reliability, with an overnight ChatGPT outage and the ongoing questions about Sol deleting files, so the cheaper model still carries a trust discount the price does not show.

3. Demis Hassabis wants a gate, and where you stand on it depends on whether you sell closed models or open ones. The head of Google DeepMind called for a US frontier-AI standards body, funded by the AI companies, that would test every frontier model up to 30 days before release and could coordinate an industry slowdown if a model proves dangerous, per Diet TBPN. Read as safety it is reasonable. Read as strategy it is sharper, because he wants the rules to cover foreign and open-source models too, and a 30-day review that a well-funded lab can absorb is a real burden on an open-source project that has no compliance budget.

The honest framing is that this could go either way for open models. A gate that slows the closed frontier labs buys the open ecosystem time to catch up, but a review requirement that only a big company can afford is the same dynamic that lets big pharma acquire small biotech before it ever ships. Either way, the man asking the government to build the gate runs one of the labs that would sail through it.

4. Compute itself just became a tradable price, and it says the buildout is not slowing. The quietly important launch, per the Kalshi list: forward-price curves for renting Nvidia's B200, H200, and A100 chips, so the cost of GPU time is now a market you can trade like oil or rates. The prices imply hyperscaler compute spending above $700 billion this year and a 97 percent chance that B200 compute stays above $7.97 an hour through year-end. For anyone building on these models, a liquid forward curve on compute is a real tool, a way to hedge or lock in the single most volatile input cost in the business, and its existence is its own signal that demand is expected to stay tight.

5. One funding note and one hardware note. Nous Research is raising at a $1.5 billion valuation, per the Hermes list, another open-model lab getting priced like a contender rather than a hobby. And on the consumer side, per TBPN, Sonos pushed out several long-tenured design and product leaders in a restructuring the company insists is not about AI, exactly the kind of denial that ages badly. The installed base of speakers in millions of homes is precisely the surface a genuinely capable voice assistant would want, and a hardware company thinning its design bench right as voice models get good is the sort of decision that looks obvious in hindsight, one way or the other.
