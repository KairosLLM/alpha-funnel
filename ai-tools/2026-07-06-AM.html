DeepSeek's big drop is a speed patch, not a smarter model, and the more interesting AI story this morning is who quietly owns the agent stack

DSpark turns out to be a serving-layer trick on the same V4 weights, the Nous Hermes tooling is maturing into a real platform, and a claim is circulating that Washington briefly cut Fable 5 and Mythos 5 off for everyone. Here is the signal.

ModelsAgentsToolingLocal/OpenCompute

The headline names today are DeepSeek and a policy scare, but the durable story is underneath. The value in AI keeps sliding from raw model capability toward the plumbing that runs agents cheaply and the judgment to point them at the right work. Here is what actually changed.

1. DeepSeek DSpark is a speed patch, and the number that matters is not the one being quoted. Read the model card before the hype. Per Kris Torrington, DSpark is not a new brain, it is the same DeepSeek V4 checkpoint with a speculative decoding module bolted on (a small draft model guesses the next tokens and the big model verifies them), so this is a serving upgrade, not a capability jump.

  • Reported speedups: V4 Flash runs about 60 to 85% faster per user, V4 Pro about 57 to 78% faster.
  • The specs underneath: V4 Flash is 284B parameters with 13B active, V4 Pro is 1.6 trillion with 49B active, both at a million-token context.
  • The catch: faster tokens do not mean your bill drops 85%. The only metric that pays you back is cost per completed workflow, and Torrington's own bar for switching is a 20 to 30% improvement on that, not a 5% one.

2. The Nous Hermes stack is turning into a real agent platform while nobody was looking. The open-agent side keeps shipping, and it is past toy stage. Per the Hermes list, two pieces landed that matter more than another benchmark tweet.

  • A Hermes browser extension hit v2: full session management, vision and screenshots, a side panel on every tab, and the ability to swap models mid-chat between Opus, Grok, GPT, and a local model. Eight-plus releases in a week.
  • A walkthrough of the Hermes Agent architecture showed the guts: 70-plus tools across roughly 28 toolsets, 18-plus providers, a 90-turn iteration budget, subagents capped at 50, and preflight context compression that kicks in at 50% full. It reads context files in priority order, checking its own `.hermes.md` before `AGENTS.md` before `CLAUDE.md`.

3. The local-model crowd is not a hobby anymore, it is a staffing model. The most interesting shift in the list is cultural. Per the Hermes list, Alex Finn described running four models across six computers 24/7 doing security scans, code optimization, scraping, and agent monitoring, and framed always-on local compute as unlimited Opus-level intelligence you do not meter. He still reaches for Fable 5 and Codex on the hard stuff, and that admission is the honest part. The takeaway from yesterday holds and sharpens: route the cheap and endless work to local swarms, and save the expensive frontier calls for the problems only you could have imagined.

4. Read the bayes near-future thread for the non-obvious bets, not the "AI will accelerate" part. A long forecast thread made the rounds, and most of it is the usual takeoff talk. Per bayes, the parts worth your attention are the specific ones.

  • The central wager: 4 to 7 more orders of magnitude of intelligence output per unit of scale are still on the table, which would make the current flat-looking frontier curve very misleading.
  • A compute-allocation squeeze that hits open source hardest: are your GB300s more valuable serving GLM 5.2 or building the next model inside a frontier lab? Capital does not fix it if the chips go to the frontier first.
  • A robotics ChatGPT-moment followed by an Opus-grade moment, with the gap between them measured in months, and physical scale-up toward roughly 100 million robots a year by 2030.

5. A policy scare to keep at arm's length, because the sourcing is thin. Handle this one carefully. Per Kris Torrington, the US government told Anthropic to restrict Fable 5 and Mythos 5 from foreign nationals, and because nationality could not be verified in real time, the models briefly went dark for everyone before access was restored. It advances yesterday's thread about Washington possibly pulling access to a major model, and it fits the new prediction markets on exactly that question. The honest note: this comes from a single creator with no named official, agency, or confirmation attached, so treat it as a claim to watch rather than an established fact.

The point that survives either way is the real one. If your whole stack sits on one frontier API, it can be working in the morning and cut off by policy that afternoon, and that is a dependency risk you can actually plan around. On a related thread, per the Market list, one investor is now betting against the standalone cybersecurity vendors on the theory that coding models are quietly eating their moat.
