A legal startup is growing 50 percent a quarter by eating firms that bill $4,000 an hour, and a voice company hit $600 million in revenue with zero product managers

Legora and ElevenLabs are the same story from two industries: narrow AI is swallowing trillion-dollar service businesses that are only a few percent software today. The practical build of the day is putting all your agents on one private network so a single agent can run the rest, plus the memory stack you can now assemble just by talking.

The theme this morning is AI eating professional services, and the two case studies are unusually concrete. Underneath them are two builds worth your time. Ranked.

1. The end of the billable hour is a real business now, and it has numbers. Per the All-In episode, the legal startup Legora is growing 50 percent a quarter for seven straight quarters, from $1 million to well over $100 million in revenue, by attacking a market that is $1 trillion a year in legal services but only $40 billion in software, so barely 4 percent digitized. The billing math is why it works, with partners charging up to $4,000 an hour and Kirkland and Ellis running roughly $10 billion a year at $5 to $10 million of profit per partner.

Legora embeds what it calls legal engineers, forward-deployed lawyers who sit with a firm's partners the way Palantir plants engineers with clients, and it closed four acquisitions this year with diligence done in-house in as little as 12 days.

The casualty is the incumbent. LexisNexis has a massive case-law moat but only makes a couple billion a year from it, and Legora and its rival Harvey combined are already near that, while the legacy players cannot pivot because of their talent, their hours, and their politics. The sharp aside for anyone building on Claude is that Legora's founder calls Anthropic's own legal offering a bundling of markdown skill files, a pipeline that funnels users toward specialist tools like his. The lesson is that in a data-is-everything field like law, where you need all the case law and not 80 percent of it, the narrow specialist beats the general model.

2. The same playbook is eating voice, and the company doing it runs without product managers. ElevenLabs is the voice version of the same thesis, per the same episode: $600 million in annual recurring revenue, up from $300 million at the end of last year, on 600 employees with zero product managers and no attrition among its first ten research and engineering hires. It structures around 5-to-10-person verticalized teams for telecom, finance, and healthcare, with engineers embedded in every function. The customers are named and real, Revolut and Klarna using it for payment collections because people disclose more to an AI, and it has paid more than $22 million back to voice talent, licensing Darth Vader through the James Earl Jones estate to run live in Fortnite.

The reason it earns a line of its own is the moat argument. OpenAI and Anthropic both want the voice market, and ElevenLabs' answer is that architecture matters more than scale, that a model-agnostic platform built specifically for voice with over 1,000 contractors labeling audio wins on the specific problem even as it sends tens of millions of dollars a year to the frontier labs it sits on top of. It is the same shape as Legora, a narrow player owning a vertical the giants cannot be bothered to specialize in.

3. The build of the day: put every agent you run on one private network. The most useful practical idea comes from David Ondrej's Tailscale walkthrough. The premise is that everyone now runs a dozen agents, Codex, Claude Code, Hermes, and others, scattered across local machines and rented servers, and managing them one at a time is a mess. Tailscale is a private mesh network that connects all of them peer to peer with no open ports, so a single personal agent can command, fix, and update every other agent at once, for instance pushing a new model to five agents on five servers with one prompt.

The security upgrade is the real sell. Identity-based access means a stolen laptop or a departed employee is revoked instantly, and a companion tool called Aperture holds your API keys centrally so they never live on individual machines. Ondrej's demo has one agent using Codex to log into a server, lock it down to allow only network traffic, install a Hermes agent, and swap its model from a Chinese open model to GPT-5.6 with a single instruction. His own model take is that Fable 5 is meaningfully better than GPT-5.6 for his work, and that both new-generation models now run their own tests and check for breaking changes unprompted, which the prior generation did not.

4. Two smaller tools, quickly. Per Nate B Jones, you can now build roughly 80 percent of your agent's memory stack just by talking to the agent already on your computer, a capability that did not exist in February, and the catch is the whole point: an agent this good at acting on your intent will act on the wrong version of your intent just as fast, so the memory stack is what keeps it pointed at what you meant. And per Chase AI, the open-source Scroll World skill that one-shots animated scrolling websites now runs on both Fable 5 and GPT-5.6, generating a still image, converting it to video, and stitching the scenes together through the Higgsfield pipeline, with a budget fork to keep it from burning through credits.

5. One sentiment shift worth a line. The AI-as-creative-tool case got an unlikely endorsement, per All-In: Martin Scorsese used AI image tools to pre-visualize a scene, iterating on generated images of an Eastern European village to get the picture out of his head, on the logic that language is a lossy medium and an image carries far more of the intent. When the director most associated with hand-crafted cinema is using generative models as a mood board, the tool has crossed from novelty into the workflow, the same quiet adoption curve every one of today's stories rides.
