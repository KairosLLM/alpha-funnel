Substack started scanning every post for whether a machine wrote it, one creator gamed the detector in three hours by swapping an em-dash for a colon, and the whole fight is really about who you trust to have thought

The AI-slop backlash turned into a product this week, and it exposed how flimsy detection actually is. Meanwhile the practitioners have moved past prompting entirely into agent loops, Anthropic made Fable 5 permanent with a catch, and David Sacks says the labs are laying the groundwork to ban open source.

The AI evening is about trust and taste in a world where the writing is free. The tools got good enough that the scarce thing is no longer generation, it is judgment. Ranked.

1. Substack shipped an AI detector, and a creator broke it before the ink dried. The slop backlash became a feature. Per a Substack CEO interview, Chris Best built a Pangram integration into the app that scans a long-form post for whether it went through a language model and pairs it with an optional writer statement, framing it as transparency rather than policing, and he cites a Pangram stat that about 40 percent of long-form LinkedIn writing is now fully machine-generated. His word for the betrayal is Claudefishing, and his point is that the offense is deceiving the reader, not using the tool.

The detector turned out to be easy to fool. Per Ruben Hassid, he spent three hours and $34 of Claude Code credits building a rewrite skill, found that a piece still scored 100 percent machine-written, and then flipped it to 100 percent human by swapping a single em-dash for a colon. Detectors are pattern-matchers, and that is why OpenAI disbanded its own detection team, and the tell they lean on hardest is the em-dash, the mark a model drops twenty times a page and a human uses two or three. The useful takeaway sits underneath both, that the fix for slop is not a scanner, it is a writer who keeps the judgment and the voice the machine cannot supply.

2. The practitioners have stopped prompting and started building loops. The frontier of how people actually use these tools moved this week. Per Alex Finn, he quotes Claude Code's creator saying he does not prompt anymore, he creates loops that do his whole job, and the concrete version is three skills named Spec, Build, and Review that turn a vague idea into filed issues, built code, and a tested pull request with almost no babysitting. The mechanics are a command like a five-minute loop that repeats a build or review step, using a tracker as the agent's second brain and a rocket emoji to approve a merge.

The counterweight is that more tooling is not automatically better. Per Dylan Davis, Claude actually gets worse when you install too many skills, because it silently scans every global skill's name before it acts and with thirty similarly named ones it picks the wrong tool, and the fix most people do not know is to bind a skill to a specific folder so only the relevant few ever load. His decision rule is the honest one, to ask whether you are the expert, and to build the skill yourself from a clean run about 80 percent of the time rather than downloading a bloated public one that carries someone else's context and not yours.

3. Anthropic made Fable 5 permanent, with the weekly limit as the catch. The model news of the week is an access change. Per Kris Torrington, Anthropic made Fable 5 permanent on all Max and Team Premium plans starting July 20, but capped it at half of your existing weekly usage allowance rather than giving it a separate bucket, while Pro and Team Standard get only a one-time $100 credit before metered pricing at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output.

The backstory is a reminder of how fragile a frontier launch is. Per that breakdown, Fable 5 launched June 9, got pulled globally three days later when export controls forced Anthropic to suspend it because it could not verify user nationality in real time, and the trigger was an Amazon researchers' report finding a way to bypass its safety features. Anthropic built a classifier that blocks the technique in more than 99 percent of cases and returned the model on July 1. That is the quiet story of the year, that the models now ship into a wall of export rules and jailbreak reports before anyone gets to use them.

4. The consultant economy is forming around these tools, and the open-source fight is heating up under it. The money is moving to the people who can deploy this. Per Nate Herk, the big four are on pace for about $725 billion of AI spend in 2026, PwC finds a 62 percent wage premium for AI skills, LinkedIn ranks AI consultant the second-fastest-growing US job, and yet an MIT study found 95 percent of generative-AI pilots had little bottom-line impact, and that gap is what the consultants fill. His playbook is to pick one painful recurring task, fix and document it in Claude with no code, prove the metric moved with a two-minute before-and-after, and sell that.

The policy fight over the models underneath is turning sharp. Per David Sacks, the White House AI lead argues Anthropic is laying the groundwork to ban open-source models in America, planting what he calls predicate facts in its own blog posts by taking a shot at open weights inside every threat it describes, so that a future push for a ban has a paper trail. Hold it next to the anti-slop story from the same day and the shape of the year is clear, that the value has moved off the raw model and onto the judgment around it, and that is exactly why the fights now are about who controls the models and who is trusted to have done the thinking.
