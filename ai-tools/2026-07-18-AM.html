The Chinese model that spooked the market lost a head-to-head this weekend, and the same investors who fear it are the ones paying 100 times too much for tokens

Kimi K3 came second to Claude Fable on a real front-end build and burned six times the tokens to get there, so the cheap-model panic keeps failing on contact with actual work. The bigger AI story is a pricing gap that has enterprises paying $56 for what costs 50 cents, and an industry proposing to regulate itself before Washington does.

The weekend clarified the Kimi story the selloff got wrong, and surfaced a cost gap that matters more than any benchmark. Ranked.

1. Kimi K3 lost the build it was supposed to win. The hands-on test cut against the hype again. Per Chase AI, the same prompt for a 3D globe travel dashboard run across three models put Claude Fable 5 first on fidelity, with a working globe, a day-night slider, and click-to-zoom detail, while Kimi K3 came second at lower fidelity and GPT-5.6 came last with a laggy, awkward layout.

The cost numbers are the real story. Kimi K3 burned 21.5 million tokens over more than an hour and a half to reach $8.66, where Fable finished the same job in 17 minutes on 3.5 million tokens, so the model that looks cheapest per token is the most token-inefficient and the slowest in practice. That is the second independent test this week to land on the same verdict, that Kimi is genuinely capable and genuinely not cheap once you price the tokens it wastes, and that is exactly the nuance the three-trillion-dollar chip selloff ignored.

2. The pricing gap under the whole debate matters more than any benchmark. The number that reframes everything is the spread. Per the All-In Podcast, Chamath Palihapitiya laid out per-million-token prices running from roughly $56 for a top frontier model down to about $1.50 for Grok and near 50 cents for Chinese models, a gap of 50 to 100 times for what is often the same task, while the expensive option also leaks proprietary data.

The consequence he flagged is a coming earnings problem. He cited Ramp reporting that its customers' token spending grew 21 times in a year, and warned that a public-company finance chief is going to miss a quarter on runaway token costs. The strategic answer showing up is Mira Murati's new open model Inkling, pitched explicitly as just below frontier intelligence so companies can fine-tune a far cheaper model to hit the same result, the kind of product the top labs have every reason to want stopped.

3. Free models are not free, and the trap is in the license. The practical warning for anyone tempted by the cheap open models is legal, not technical. Per Kris Torrington, free to download does not mean free to use in a commercial product, and the terms differ release by release even inside one lab.

The specifics are worth holding. DeepSeek and GLM releases mostly use permissive MIT terms that generally allow commercial use, Kimi allows commercial use but with attribution so your product may have to credit the model, and MiniMax has moved some releases to non-commercial terms that may require a signed agreement. The rule is that the same lab does not mean the same license, so the version matters every time, and the token savings evaporate fast if a lawyer has to unwind a shipped product.

4. The privacy bill for all this cheap intelligence is coming due. The cautionary tale of the weekend was a leak. Per the All-In discussion, xAI's Grok Build coding tool was quietly sending users' entire codebases, passwords and API keys included, to the cloud despite telling users nothing was transmitted, and the company disabled the upload and open-sourced the harness after it surfaced.

Chamath used it to argue that the reassuring phrase zero data retention is a false comfort, that there are trap doors everywhere once your data leaves your walls, and disclosed his own firm builds in this space. The counter-move Sacks described is an emerging playbook for enterprises to keep a real trust boundary, running private evaluations and proprietary learning loops inside their own tenant rather than surrendering everything to a single closed model stack. The through-line with the rest of the weekend is that the cheapest way to run a model and the safest way to run one are pulling in opposite directions, and the industry has not resolved which wins.
