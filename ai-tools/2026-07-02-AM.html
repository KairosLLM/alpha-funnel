Fable 5 is back, and the free clock runs out July 7

Anthropic redeployed its flagship with new guardrails that silently route sensitive work to Opus, and it published the exact habits to prompt it well.

ModelsAgentsToolingCompute

Two things are true this morning: the best model is back, and access to it is getting rationed and gated. Everything below is about using the open window well and not getting caught depending on a switch someone else controls.

1. Fable 5 is live again, and the window to use it cheap is short. Per AI Central, Claude Fable 5 returned to global availability on July 1 after an 18-day export ban (out June 9, banned June 12, back July 1), redeployed with new classifiers that block cybersecurity tasks and a routing change where routine coding can silently fall back to Opus 4.8. Here is the part that is actually actionable, from Nate Herk: pricing is $10 per million input and $50 per million output, which is 2x Opus, the promotional period ends July 7, and on Claude plans you can only spend up to 50% of your weekly limits on Fable before it flips to usage credits. His honest take is that you realistically need Fable 5 for maybe 5 to 15% of your work, so do not burn the free window running it on everything. Use it on the hard things this week.

2. The prompting playbook went public, so take it. Herk pulled six habits straight from Anthropic's own docs, and they are the most useful thing in the feed today.

  • Give it the why, not bare commands.
  • Negative-prompt explicitly: "report what you find and stop, do not fix, send, or delete until I say go."
  • Let it act once it has enough: "when you have enough information to act, then act," rather than babysitting plan mode.
  • Make it prove it: "before you say something is done, point to the result that proves it, and if it is not verified, say so." Bake that into your skills and CLAUDE.md.
  • Do not ask Fable to show its reasoning. This one is Fable-specific. A standing "explain your reasoning" line can trip a safety refusal and quietly route the task to Opus 4.8.
  • Say less. Short instructions now steer as well as long rule dumps.

On effort, default to high, step up to x-high for hard problems, and know that Fable on low roughly matches Opus 4.8 on x-high for less money. Requests touching hacking, dangerous bio, or revealing private reasoning get shunted to Opus, and the API shows the switch even though the chat UI does not.

3. Five things to actually build with it. Chase AI leaned hard on his own course, so discount the urgency, but the use cases are concrete. Clone paid software (he rebuilt WhisperFlow into a local dictation app on Ollama). Audit your own Claude Code usage by pointing subagents at your session transcripts to cluster what should become a skill versus an automation versus nothing, run across 39 sessions. Run a full code review that turned 45 raw findings from four parallel reviewers into 24 deduped and ranked by severity. And the long-horizon proof point, open-source dev Brapholk built a browser Three.js game from a single hand-written PRD, with Fable 5 writing 21,000 lines of TypeScript across more than 90 commits. The pattern worth copying: plan with Opus and deep research, verify with Codex, then hand the long agentic build to Fable.

4. The counter-narrative: own your alpha, and enterprises are voting with their wallets. The sharpest argument this cycle came from Palantir's Alex Karp, quoted via Ricardo: enterprises "are paying for tokens that create no value," and if the labs truly delivered the value they claim, they would take equity or 30% of the profit instead of selling compute by the token. His deeper point is the scary one for anyone piping confidential data through a frontier model, you are training your own replacement while paying for the privilege. Whatever the spin, the behavior backs him up. On the Hermes list, Kimi K2.7 and GLM 5.2 both landed on GitHub Copilot and Cursor this week, with developers openly switching to cheaper Chinese models on cost. NVIDIA Research shipped Nemotron-Labs-TwoTower, a diffusion LM that keeps 98.7% of quality at 2.42x faster generation, and Not Diamond claims 30%-plus savings on coding agents through pure model routing with no quality loss. The theme across all of it: the frontier is getting gated and expensive, and the market is routing around it.

5. Own your memory and your ownership. Nate B Jones made the governance point that pairs with the Fable relaunch: the fastest way to make an AI agent dangerous is to let everyone use it and nobody own it. Any agent that can read your files, draft messages, change code, or update records needs an actual operational owner, not an org-chart one. As the tooling gets more capable and more autonomous this week, that is the unglamorous discipline that keeps it from quietly going wrong.
