The independent Kimi K3 benchmarks are finally in, and the Chinese model that wiped out three trillion dollars of chips lost to Claude by less than half a point while costing twice the tokens to get there

Kimi K3 came second to Fable and ahead of GPT-5.6 on a neutral 38-model test, which confirms it is frontier-class and not the cheap replacement the selloff assumed. The weekend replaced the Kimi panic with numbers, and they tell a calmer story than the tape did. The more useful thread is what GPT-5.6 changes about how work actually gets done. Ranked.

1. The neutral Kimi K3 scorecard confirms the nuance the selloff ignored. The benchmarks that matter are the independent ones. Per a review of the Vals AI results, across 38 models Claude Fable 5 scored 75.14 percent, Kimi K3 came second at 74.7, and GPT-5.6 Sol third at 73.12, so Moonshot's open model lost to Claude by less than half a point while beating GPT-5.6, a result that is genuinely frontier-class rather than a clear number one.

The catch is the same one every hands-on test has found. Kimi K3 hit 95 percent on the SWE-bench coding measure and topped the front-end arena at 1,679 Elo, but it ran at about 62 output tokens a second against a peer average of 71 and emitted 130 million tokens in the evaluation against a median of 63 million, roughly double, so the cost balloons in practice. At $3 per million input tokens and $15 for output, with the weights not downloadable until July 27 and 2.8 trillion parameters to host, the verdict holds, that it is a real capability milestone and not the cheap bomb that moved three trillion dollars of chips.

2. The bigger shift is that GPT-5.6 turns knowledge work into system management. The most useful idea of the weekend is a change in what the job becomes. Per Every, GPT-5.6 is now smart, fast, and reliable enough to move knowledge work from doing tasks to managing a system that does them, the same pattern coders already live where you file an issue, an agent does the work, you review, and it ships.

The concrete version is a workflow, not a demo. The author built an app that turns incoming email into action cards that Codex pre-processes so he only approves or rejects, and runs the same system on meeting transcripts to flag decisions made after he left the room, with his role reduced to tuning the model on what to watch. The evidence it is ready came from a blunt test, per a second Every piece, where compiling 46 spreadsheets into one analysis broke GPT-5.5 entirely and even stumped Claude Fable, while GPT-5.6 found the files, scoped the work, and delivered, and that is why the tester made it her daily driver.

3. OpenAI rebuilt ChatGPT into the whole workbench, and it is copying Claude on purpose. The product context under the model is a consolidation. Per a full walkthrough, the new desktop app unifies chat, a Work agent, and Codex, with a three-tier model lineup of Sol for hard tasks, Terra for everyday, and Luna for fast cheap jobs, plus effort levels that run from light all the way to a parallel-splitting ultra mode.

The honest read from the guide is that OpenAI is tracing Claude's blueprint feature by feature. ChatGPT Work is the same idea as Claude's Cowork, ChatGPT Sites deploys a real hosted page the way Claude's artifacts do, and the app now carries skills, a goal command, plan mode, and connector support that all mirror the competitor. The reviewer's verdict is the useful one, that she would not leave Claude but is keeping both, because the two labs are now converging on the same shape of product and the choice is becoming preference rather than capability.

4. And the reminder under all of it: a model you depend on can vanish overnight. The cautionary note is a dependency risk. Per Kris Torrington, Claude Fable 5 launched on June 9 and was pulled offline days later amid what were described as export-control concerns before being restored, a reminder that a licensed model can go inaccessible without warning.

The lesson pairs with the private-AI thread from the morning. Building a product or a revenue stream on a single hosted model means accepting that a regulator, a policy change, or a pricing decision can take your foundation away for a while, the same argument for keeping a fallback that the local-model advocates make about keeping your data. The convenience of the frontier is real, and so is the fragility of renting it, and the practitioners who ship for a living are the ones already building for the day their default disappears.
