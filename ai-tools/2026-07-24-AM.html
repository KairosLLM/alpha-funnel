The AI morning is about cost. The models are close enough now that the winning move is no longer picking the smartest one, it is routing the cheapest one that can do the job. Ranked.

1. Kimi K3 drops its weights Monday, and the smart way to use it is as a planner. The model everyone is testing gets fully open this week. Per David Ondrej, Kimi K3 is a 2.8-trillion-parameter model from Moonshot that tops the front-end coding arena over any closed model, scores 26.7 percent on the Harvey legal benchmark against 14.2 percent for Fable, and runs about three times cheaper than Fable at comparable quality, with the weights releasing July 27 on Hugging Face and 20-plus inference providers expected within days.

The deeper lesson is how you deploy it. Per that video, the cost-per-task math says to route rather than pay one rate for everything, using a big slow model as the planner and a cheap fast model as the worker, a Kimi K3 planner with a smaller code model doing the typing, for roughly 9 to 10 times the savings of running a frontier model end to end. He walks through building a Blender web clone for $1.30 and notes you can self-host K3 for slow overnight use for around $16,000 to $22,000, and he advises grabbing a cheap drive to download the weights on Monday before any US ban lands. The through-line is that open weights plus routing is quietly rewriting the economics of building with AI.

2. AMD put its next chip behind Anthropic, and DeepSeek says the margins are extraordinary. The infrastructure race got two hard data points. Per Diet TBPN, AMD used its Advancing AI event to unveil the Helios rack system built on the MI450 accelerator and confirmed a roughly $5 billion deal to deploy up to 2 gigawatts of those chips for Anthropic, whose own models are reportedly speeding AMD's hardware bring-up in a software flywheel, while AMD also widened its Cerebras partnership on split-workload inference.

The economics underneath are the part worth holding onto. Per that show, DeepSeek's Liang Wenfeng told investors on a four-hour call that his inference margins run about 85 percent, that roughly $1 billion in API revenue is enough to be cash-flow positive, and that a GPU pays for itself in about 10 months against a three-to-five-year depreciation schedule. Set that next to OpenAI raising its compute budget to $750 billion through 2030, and the split in the market is stark, one camp spending like the payback is guaranteed and another proving you can be profitable on 20,000 chips if you stay disciplined. The buildout and the counterexample are running at the same time.

3. GPT-5.6 scored a 56 where Fable scored 90, and the reason is that it will not stop building. The benchmark of the day comes with a lesson about model temperament. Per Every, GPT-5.6 scored 56 on its senior-engineer benchmark against 90 for Fable, a task that asks a model to rewrite a messy vibecoded codebase, and the gap is real but the score undersells 5.6, which the reviewer would put closer to 70 to 80 by feel.

The why is the useful part. Per that breakdown, GPT-5.6 lost points because it did not delete enough code, leaving cruft standing and writing new code that was bigger and less clear than Fable's, a consistent tendency to overbuild that shows up especially in landing-page and website work. It pairs with the theme from the rest of the day, that the models are close enough on raw capability that their habits now matter more than their scores, and knowing that 5.6 overbuilds while Fable cuts is the kind of practical read that decides which one you reach for. Per Diet TBPN, OpenAI shipped voice control and a Health feature that connects Apple Health and medical records the same day, so the product keeps widening even where the coding score lags.

4. The practitioner discipline of the day is treating your instructions file as a router, not a brain. The most useful hands-on lesson is about context. Per Nate Herk, context fails in four distinct ways, poisoning when a false fact sits among true ones, bloat when there is so much data the model cannot find the needle, confusion when a needed fact is missing, and clash when a stale rule contradicts a new one, and the fix is to split what you load.

The move that makes it work is architectural. Per that video, he treats his instructions file as a routing table that points to a wiki, an index, a memory system, and tools rather than stuffing it all in as one giant system prompt, and he keeps always-on expertise context separate from just-in-time situational context so a lookup does not bloat every call.

He also runs an audit skill that fans out a subagent per check to flag stale data and broken routes, and his best tip is to make the agent retrace its own steps when it fails rather than just telling it not to fail again. It is the same insight as the routing story in models, that the win is no longer a bigger context or a smarter model, it is loading only what the task needs, when it needs it.
