The agents can now run for half an hour with nobody watching, and the whole skill is no longer prompting them, it is knowing how to put brakes on them

ChatGPT and Claude now grind unattended for 30 minutes to a couple of hours, so the new craft is defining where they stop and how you audit them. The weekend also sharpened the case that a smaller, faster model beats the giant for daily work, and put a real cost on the Kimi hype.

The weekend's AI story is about control, not capability. The models got autonomous enough that the hard part moved from telling them what to do to telling them when to stop. Ranked.

1. Agents now run unattended, and the skill is the brakes. The capability that changed the workflow is duration. Per Dylan Davis, desktop agents running on Codex and Claude Co-work now work for 30 minutes and sometimes one to three hours with no one watching, which means the failure mode is no longer a bad answer, it is an agent that quits too early, never stops, or breaks silently an hour into a job.

His fix is a discipline worth copying. First you make the model interview you with eight questions one at a time before it starts, then you give it four brakes, an exact destination where it drafts but never sends, five to eight yes-or-no passing checks rather than vague words like professional, a receipt trail you can audit afterward, and hard stopping conditions that cap the attempts and flag anything legal or financial for a human. The rule underneath it is that autonomy without a defined finish line burns tokens and trust, so the prompt now has to encode the guardrails, not just the goal.

2. The weekend sharpened the case that smaller beats bigger for real work. The model debate got a clean articulation. Per Dan Shipper at Every, Claude Fable has what he calls big model smell, chunky, slow, and expensive but capable of genius if you know how to wield it, while GPT-5.6 Sol takes the opposite bet, a smaller model that is well trained to be fast, ergonomic, and collaborative.

His verdict is that for most people most of the time, the smaller model you can actually work with beats the giant you have to manage, and he thinks Sol is that model. This is the same split the whole industry is living, the frontier tier reserved for the hardest fifth of tasks and a cheaper, faster default for everything else, and it is why the next fight is over the daily driver rather than the leaderboard.

3. ChatGPT Work is the productized version of that bet, and there is a setup trap. OpenAI shipped the consumer form of the agentic assistant. Per The AI Advantage, ChatGPT Work is a new mode that does the job for you rather than with you, building a multi-step plan, spinning up sub-agents like a designer that run in parallel, and delivering a finished output, including a live hosted website through its Sites tool.

The practical catch is worth knowing before you rely on it. You have to manually switch on the Sites tool before you send, or it improvises and hands you a zip file or raw HTML instead of a working site, and the feature sits behind the paid tier with a usage allowance you can burn through. The recommended prompt shape is a context sandwich, a couple of sentences on who you are, the task in the middle, and a clear picture of what good looks like at the end. The tell of where this is going is that a non-coder can now describe a business and get a deployed website back in minutes.

4. And the Kimi hype got a real price tag. The weekend's model story keeps failing the same test. Per Chase AI, Kimi K3 prices at $3 per million input tokens and $15 for output, which is 30 percent of Fable's price and half of GPT-5.6's, and on the front-end design arena it beat both.

The real-world number is the correction. On an actual build, Kimi K3 burned 21.5 million tokens over about an hour and a half to cost $8.66, more than the GPT-5.6 run and over an hour slower than both rivals, because it is a token hog and genuinely sluggish. That is now the third independent test this week landing on the same verdict, that the cheap-per-token headline is misleading once you pay for the tokens it wastes. It matters beyond the benchmark because open models like Kimi keep the frontier labs from cornering demand, and that is the reason, per the weekend roundup, Jensen Huang quietly roots for open source even as forecasters put full automation of AI research as close as early 2027.
