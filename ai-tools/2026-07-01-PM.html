Fable 5 is back, and the smart move is to own your memory, not rent it

Anthropic redeployed its flagship globally amid a caps-and-refusals backlash, the Skills-over-files shift went mainstream, and energy quietly became the AI bottleneck that matters.

ModelsAgentsToolingComputePKM

Fable 5 is back online, but the bigger story is architectural: the sharpest operators are all moving to own their memory and skills layer and just rent the model on top. Add two quiet Codex upgrades and a hard reality check on agent governance. Here is the rundown.

1. Fable 5 is live again, with guardrails and grumbling. Anthropic is redeploying Claude Fable 5 globally, this time wired with new cybersecurity classifiers, and routine coding requests fall back to Opus 4.8. The community reaction has been loud, with well-known accounts complaining about usage caps and over-eager refusals, and Anthropic reportedly drafting a jailbreak-severity framework alongside Amazon, Microsoft, and Google. Alex Finn posted an early playbook with a couple of real ideas buried in the hype (he leans hard on a paid deposit tool, so discount accordingly): use Fable to audit every pull request merged while it was gone, drive the Claude-for-Chrome extension to walk your app like a real user and write a UX report, and use the `/loop` command (for example `/loop 24h`) to repeatedly hammer your own API endpoints for vulnerabilities, since API routes are the biggest attack surface on vibe-coded apps. That last one is the genuinely useful idea in the video.

2. The real shift: own your memory, rent the intelligence. Two of the sharpest operators in this space are saying the same thing this week. Ruben Hassid, who has built a following on Claude Cowork guides, is now telling people to ditch files and folders entirely (they "leak," Claude reads output folders even when told not to and poisons its own context) in favor of Skills plus Projects only. A Skill is a capability you invoke with a slash command like `/linkedin-post` or `/contract-drafting`, and it is portable across Gemini and ChatGPT. His settings advice:

  • Turn almost everything off, keep only Connectors on.
  • Run Opus 4.8 on High, and step up to Max effort or Fable 5 when the task earns it.
  • Start a fresh session every twenty or so messages, because message thirty can cost thirty-one times message one in tokens. Nate B Jones makes the structural version of the argument: rent the model, own the memory. His stack is OpenBrain for memory, OpenSkills for portable methods, and OpenEngine to orchestrate across Claude, Codex, ChatGPT, Kimi, and Gemini, and his point is that the barrier to building this yourself has dropped roughly fivefold since February. If a flagship gets locked behind regulatory approval, the person who owns their memory and skills layer barely notices.

3. Codex quietly shipped two great features. From The AI Advantage: OpenAI's Codex now has record-and-replay. Hit record, perform any computer workflow for up to thirty minutes while it captures your screen and microphone, and Codex turns it into a reusable Skill you can replay or schedule. Nate B Jones also flags that Codex now has an auto-review pass, so a "draft this" instruction will not accidentally send, a direct answer to the cautionary tale of an agent that mailed an insurance appeal it was only supposed to write. The theme across both: agents are getting the boring safety and repeatability features that make them usable for real work.

4. A concrete workflow worth stealing: contract review. Dylan Davis laid out a clean four-prompt method that works in Claude or ChatGPT. Decode the document to plain fifth-grade English from your side's perspective, expose the unusual terms and the missing standard protections with exact quotes ranked by impact, force a citation check with the instruction "a made-up answer is three times worse to me than a blank one, label anything not in the document as not found," then draft the friendly redline email. For anything over fifty pages, start a fresh chat and treat every finding as unconfirmed. The citation-check prompt is the step most people skip and the one that keeps the model honest.

5. Agents as labor, and the standard to find them. Greg Isenberg argues the next wave of software sells work, not tools, a multi-trillion-dollar labor market rather than a seat-license market, and his examples (Slang AI as a restaurant host, Same Day for home-services dispatch) point at outcome-based pricing like $2K setup plus $30 per qualified appointment. The plumbing for that world took a step forward with the Agentic Resource Discovery spec (ARD v0.9), covered by Jordamøn, published by eleven companies including Microsoft, Google, GitHub, Hugging Face, and NVIDIA under the same Linux Foundation body where Anthropic donated MCP (the Model Context Protocol, the now-standard way agents plug into tools and data). It is a yellow pages for agents: publish a static `ai-catalog.json` at a well-known path and registries crawl it like search engines. The catch is telling, a census the day after launch found zero of the thirty-nine surveyed domains actually serving the file. Strong backing, no adoption yet. Watch whether publishers actually host it.

6. The counterweight: governance and sustainability. For all the "ninety percent of code is AI-generated" hype, Mark Ajzenstadt supplies the reality check with real telemetry. 72% of enterprises claim production agentic AI, but 60% have no formal governance and only one in five has a mature model. Across 22,000 developers, AI lifts throughput but raises change-failure rates: 30% more code, median PR review time up 441%, and 31% more pull requests merged with zero human review. His fix is a four-layer stack (gateway, control, tracing, impact) and his warning is that a board deck can look transformed while the org is identical. Pair that with the Reddit skeptics arguing AI is still in a pilot phase where lab revenue trails compute cost, and you have the honest bear case on how fast this actually compounds. On the industry clock, Prof G thinks an OpenAI IPO is more likely late 2026 or the first half of 2027 than 2027-and-beyond.

7. Cross-route to Investing: energy is the bottleneck now. The most investable AI signal this edition is about power, not models. Per the Limitless Podcast, US data-center demand is projected to go from roughly 80 GW in 2026 to 150 GW in 2028, power is only about 10% of a data-center budget but 100% of the bottleneck, and grid power carries a five-year lead time. New Vera Rubin racks pull over 100 kW, ten times the prior generation. The named plays: Bloom Energy ($BE) for on-site fuel cells in 90 days, Lumentum ($LITE) and Corning ($GLW) for the optical interconnects that replace melting copper (NVIDIA put roughly $2B into $LITE and $3.2B into $GLW), Marvell ($MRVL) for power delivery and custom silicon, and Nebius ($NBIS) as the neocloud with a ~$50B contract backlog. A Reddit thesis makes the same photonics-and-light argument from the retail side. The full ticker detail sits in today's Investing edition.
