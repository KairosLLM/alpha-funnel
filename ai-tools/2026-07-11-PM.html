Gerstner says Anthropic goes from $100 billion to $400 billion next year, and a single Fable prompt now costs double an Opus one

The demand side of the AI trade keeps setting records, with usage-limit resets firing across ChatGPT and Claude and Brad Gerstner pricing Anthropic's revenue ramp as the largest in history. The usable half of the week is cost discipline, one open-source skill that one-shots a cinematic website, and a thesis that the real frontier is a model etched onto silicon running at 16,000 tokens a second.

Two numbers frame the close of the week: what Anthropic's revenue is about to do, and what it costs you to run the best model for one task. The first is a headline, the second is the one you can act on. Ranked, with the buildable stuff up top.

1. The demand for AI is the actual story, and the revenue math is getting absurd. On All-In, Brad Gerstner said that if Anthropic ends this year over $100 billion in revenue, it is on a path to triple or quadruple again next year, "$100 billion to $300 billion, $100 billion to $400 billion," and called $200 billion of incremental revenue in a single year unprecedented in business history. Take the number with the skepticism any venture pitch earns, but the direction matches the plumbing.

The plumbing is the usage. Per the Hermes list, OpenAI just reset ChatGPT Work and Codex usage limits again, with the team saying it has never seen traffic climb this fast, and Claude's limits are reportedly about to be tightened too. When both leading labs are throttling paying users because they cannot serve the demand, the growth story is not the forecast, it is the rate limiter.

2. The most useful idea this week is cost discipline, because Fable 5 is expensive on purpose. The concrete tactic, from a Kris Torrington clip: Fable 5 costs about twice the rate-card price of Opus 4.5, and the real bill is worse than the token price once you add effort level, retries, and tool calls. His rule is the 80-at-half test, if Opus gives you 80 percent of the result at half the cost, use Opus, and only reach for Fable when it delivers the missing 20 percent and that 20 percent actually matters.

The full price map, from Nate Herk's Claude Code course, is worth pinning:

  • Per-token rates: Haiku runs $1 per million tokens in and $5 out, Sonnet 5 is $3 and $15, and Opus 4.8 is $25 per million out, with Fable the most expensive of all.
  • The subscription arbitrage: on the $200-a-month Max plan, filling every weekly limit works out to roughly $8,000 of inference at pay-as-you-go rates, so heavy users are buying at a deep discount.
  • Cost compounds, it does not add: a conversation that starts at 500 tokens can hit 15,000 by message 30, and one developer found 98.5 percent of the tokens in a long chat were spent re-reading history. Batch your asks into one message and edit-and-regenerate instead of chaining.
  • Prefer CLIs over connectors for the same work, and let sub-agents hold the big context so your main thread stays cheap.

3. The buildable tool of the week: one skill that one-shots a scroll-driven website. Per Chase AI, a Claude Code and Codex skill called "scrollworld," originally open-sourced by Peter Wang, builds a cinematic scroll-controlled website from a single prompt. It starts from one anchor image, generates video, extracts the frames with ffmpeg, and binds each frame to your scroll position so the page plays as you move down it.

The media runs through the Higgsfield connector, using an image model called GPT-image-2 and a video model called Seed Dance, all inside the terminal. The practical numbers, a six-scene build ran about 800 Higgsfield credits and roughly 32 minutes, and four scenes is the sweet spot. His head-to-head verdict is the useful part, Fable 5's scene transitions came out noticeably smoother than GPT-5.6's hard cuts, so the pricier model earned its keep on exactly the 20 percent that mattered.

4. The contrarian hardware thesis: the frontier might be a model burned onto a chip. On Milk Road, Emad Mostaque argued that consumer AI is constrained by memory, not speed, since roughly a billion parameters needs a gigabyte of RAM, and that mixture-of-experts designs (models that activate only a slice of their weights per query) are the workaround, pointing to GLM 5.2 at around 650 billion parameters but only 50 billion active. His sharper claim is about fixed models etched directly into silicon.

He cited a company called Talis running a service at chatjimmy.ai that etches a model onto a chip and generates at 16,000 tokens a second, against something like 50 for a cloud model, and predicts these fixed-function AI chips arrive next year near the cost of the silicon itself. He also trained a narrow medical model that beats human doctors and runs on a Raspberry Pi. The investable read is that the value keeps migrating toward memory and custom silicon, and the societal read is his blunt one, that a truck driver replaced by a robot at $1.50 an hour is a couple-hundred-billion-dollar economy erased.

5. The rest of the week's tools and arguments, quickly.

  • OpenArt Director launched a mode it calls "vibe directing," per Nate Herk: describe and refine a video by chat, and it rebuilds the whole project while keeping characters, voices, and style consistent, up to five minutes in a single generation.
  • Vitalik Buterin published a long defense of near-term superintelligence, per the Hermes list, arguing for a 2030-to-2040 arrival and for pre-agreed slowdown triggers, and taking a shot at the labs pushing an "our side should dominate" framing. It is the substantive counter to this week's open-source executive-order noise.
  • The open-source commons is closing from both ends. Several Chinese labs plan to stop open-sourcing and license instead, though MiniMax publicly says it stays open-weight, so the window to grab the best free models may be short. For the local crowd, the Hermes list still points at Qwen3.6 and GLM-class models running on 24 gigabytes of VRAM.
  • Grok 4.5 keeps winning the price-per-task comparison and ranked first in one real-world coding test at 9 out of 10 against Fable 5's 7.5, per the People list. Elon Musk said he asked Tesla and SpaceX to try it internally.

6. The human-judgment corner: Craig Mod bans his own AI tools to keep writing. Per Every, the writer Craig Mod blocks Slack and AI lookup tools during deep reading and writing because, in his words, he is too weak, since reading Heidegger with a model one keystroke away means the instinct to ask "who was that" shatters the focus. His fix is analog, keep a paper notebook, write the question down, and ask later.

The confession underneath it is the one every heavy agent user will recognize. He admits he is addicted to watching the agent work, letting it run for around twenty hours and compulsively checking in, and he has started calling agent-babysitting an after-lunch activity rather than real work. That is the honest version of the productivity story, the tool does the labor and then quietly eats the attention you freed up. Daniel Kornum's companion argument points the other way at the business, that the biggest AI winners may be thin-margin operators where cutting the coordination layer by a few points lifts profit by a quarter, so the payoff shows up in logistics and back offices long before it shows up in a chatbot.
