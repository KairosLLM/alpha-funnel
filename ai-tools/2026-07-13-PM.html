Brad Gerstner says there is no evidence anyone stops paying 5x for the best AI, and the enterprise usage data says it is already happening

Chinese open models now run a third to a half of US enterprise API traffic, with Lindy, Coinbase, and Uber all cutting their bills by defecting. The counterargument is that the smartest model pays for itself when it replaces a consultant. The same split shows up in how one builder picks a model and how a coding agent gets ranked.

The whole AI debate collapsed into one question today: does the best model keep its premium, or does good-enough-and-cheap win. Two credible voices took opposite sides with real numbers, and the answer decides a lot of valuations. Ranked.

1. The bull case for premium AI, stated cleanly. Per Brad Gerstner on All-In, the argument for paying up is that inference cost stops mattering the moment the model replaces expensive labor. Summarizing a document is 20,000 cheap tokens where a lagging model is fine, but replacing a software engineer for two hours is 2 million expensive tokens, and a model that is 95 percent good and breaks mid-task costs you the whole attempt, the tokens and the time both. Against a $200-an-hour consultant, the gap between $3 and $15 of model cost is irrelevant if the $15 output is bulletproof, and he says there is no evidence on the field today that the intelligence gap between commodity and frontier is collapsing.

2. The usage data says the gap is already collapsing. The direct rebuttal, per The Undercut: Chinese open-weight models captured 30 to 46 percent of US enterprise API traffic in about six months, up from 4.5 percent a year ago, while the American labs combined fell to 35.7 percent. DeepSeek alone is 17.6 percent of the volume and Alibaba's Qwen 13.9 percent, and Xiaomi's MiMo model became the single most-used model on OpenRouter at 4.21 trillion tokens a week against OpenAI's 7.5 percent share.

The defections are named and they are not small companies. Lindy moved all of its traffic from Claude to DeepSeek, Coinbase runs 1,200 agents on Chinese models and halved its AI spend, and Uber burned its annual AI budget in four months and started routing to whatever clears the quality bar cheapest. The price gaps are the reason, with DeepSeek's V4 Flash at $0.14 per million input tokens against GPT-5.5 at $5.00, and GLM-5.2 scoring within a point of Claude Opus on agentic benchmarks at a fifth of the cost. Both men cannot be right, and the enterprise routers are voting with DeepSeek.

3. The build-versus-buy question underneath it is priced to 2028. The same tension runs through the hardware, per Ben Bajarin. The chip industry may cross $1 trillion in revenue this year, four years early, but almost all of that is higher prices per chip rather than more units, and the whole trade is priced to a 2028 scenario that assumes the hyperscalers never slow their spending. His binary signal is the next round of hyperscaler earnings, which have to confirm capital spending is not slowing and show free cash flow turning positive. The catch is that Micron and Samsung already printed enormous memory earnings and their stocks fell anyway, the market telling you the good news was priced.

4. The practical answer to all of this is to stop picking a model and start picking a family. The most useful builder take, per Nate B Jones, is that for his hardest work he reaches for GPT-5.6 Sol even though he calls it the dumber model, because it set a new high on a long-horizon professional-work benchmark and scored 93 on his private knowledge-work test. His frame is that models are becoming families to get to know rather than scores to rank. The OpenAI line is explicit and strong at long agentic coding, while the Anthropic line has what he calls big-model smell, better at ambiguity and front-end taste, so he still uses Fable 5 as the orchestrator that farms work out to cheaper models.

That squares with a coding-agent tier list from David Ondrej, who puts the lean harnesses Amp and Droid in the top tier and, notably, demotes Claude Code out of it for one specific reason: Fable is being pulled from the subscription plans, so the model that made Claude Code special is getting more expensive to reach. Codex lands a tier below, and he calls Google's Gemini command-line tool trash that nobody serious uses. The throughline with items 1 and 2 is that harness quality and model routing now matter more than any single benchmark number.

5. The adoption gap is still the honest number. Underneath the model wars, the deployment reality has not changed, per Kris Torrington. A McKinsey survey has 62 percent of organizations experimenting with AI agents but only 23 percent scaling one into the business, and Gartner projects that more than 40 percent of agentic projects get cancelled by the end of 2027 even as up to 40 percent of enterprise apps add task-specific agents by the end of this year. The experiment-to-production gap is exactly where reliability, governance, and cost start to bite, the same wall the cheap-Chinese-model defectors are trying to climb over by spending less to fail cheaper. Whether the premium model or the commodity one wins, the thing most teams still cannot do is ship.
