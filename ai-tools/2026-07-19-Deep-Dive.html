A Chinese open model called Kimi K3 wiped roughly three trillion dollars off the world's chip stocks this week, and the best-informed investor in the room says the market read it exactly backwards

Kimi K3 topped the coding benchmarks at a Chinese-model price and set off a panic that AI spending would collapse. Then the people who actually measured it found the model is a token hog that costs more per task than the American frontier, which turns the whole selloff into a misread.

The AI story of the week was not a product launch, it was a market event caused by one. A cheap model out of China became the excuse for the worst week in chips in over a year, and the gap between what the model actually is and what the tape assumed it was is the whole lesson. Here is the arc and what it turns on next.

How one model moved three trillion dollars. The week's spine was intelligence turning into a commodity. Grok 4.5 had already undercut everyone on coding price, OpenAI's Sol shipped at roughly a fifth the cost of the frontier, and then Moonshot released Kimi K3, a 2.8 trillion parameter open-weight model that topped the front-end coding arena and matched the American frontier on several benchmarks at a Chinese-model sticker price. Per Chase AI, the early read was another DeepSeek moment, a model that was often Fable-level and consistently better than GPT-5.6 on the thing coders care about most.

The market did the math in the crudest possible way. If a Chinese open model is nearly free and nearly as good, the fear went, then the hundreds of billions being spent on advanced chips to serve expensive American models is at risk, and that capex-domino logic is what took the memory complex apart and fed the selloff the Investing memo covers. A single benchmark ranking got priced as a bill about to come due for the entire buildout.

Why the market read it backwards. The correction came from the people who measured the model instead of the leaderboard. Per Gavin Baker, Kimi K3 is 50 to 70 percent more expensive to run than GPT-5.6 because it is a token wastrel, so it costs more per task than both GPT-5.6 and Grok 4.5, and the real threat to the top labs would be a token-efficient open model, which K3 is not. His deeper point is that the whole panic is inverted, because anything that shrinks the fat margins of the model layer helps every other layer, from power to chips to the application makers, and that is exactly why Jensen Huang funds open models.

The hands-on numbers settled it. Per Chase AI's build test, Kimi K3 does win the coding arena and prices at $3 per million input tokens against $10 for Fable, but measured by cost per finished task it runs about $95 against $14 for GPT-5.6, hallucinates more than either, and on a real build burned 21.5 million tokens over an hour and a half where Fable finished in seventeen minutes.

Per the Limitless podcast, the honest headline is the one the selloff ignored, that Kimi K3 is a genuine capability milestone, closing the roughly eight-month lead the West held, and at the same time not the cheap replacement the tape treated it as. Quality and cost became separate axes this week, and the market traded a benchmark rank as if it were a price.

The quieter shifts under the noise. Three things matter beyond the headline. Per Ion Stoica, the builder behind vLLM, the gap between open and closed models is now about six months and each frontier generation costs two to five times more than the last, so the pressure that produced Kimi K3 is structural and recurring. Per Kris Torrington, the open models carry a license trap, since DeepSeek and GLM lean on permissive MIT terms while Kimi requires attribution and MiniMax has moved some releases to non-commercial, so free to download is not free to ship. And the open-weight nature that makes these models exciting also makes them unauditable, which turns the possibility of a hidden backdoor into a real enterprise question rather than a paranoid one.

What to watch next. The first thing is whether a token-efficient open model appears, because that, not Kimi K3, would be the actual Sputnik moment Baker describes, and it would do to the labs' economics what this week's panic only imagined. The second is the earnings referee, with Alphabet and the other hyperscalers reporting into a tape that just bet their spending is at risk, and the capex guidance they give is the direct answer to the fear that moved three trillion dollars.

The third is vertical integration, since the labs whose margins are threatened have every reason to move down into chips and up into applications, and the more the model itself becomes a commodity, the more the money moves to whoever owns the layers around it. The market spent this week deciding the frontier labs had been dethroned by a cheap model. The people who ran the model concluded the opposite, and next week's numbers get to say who was right.
