Fable 5 leaves the subscription tonight, and the whole ecosystem spent the last day trying to bottle what it does before the meter starts

At 11:59pm Pacific the best coding model goes credits-only, so the playbooks are all about extracting its process into cheaper models, even as builders argue you cannot keep a model's judgment at all. Meanwhile Tencent dropped a frontier model for free and Groq is three weeks from the wall.

Today is the last day Fable 5 is cheap. It leaves every Claude subscription tonight and moves to pay-per-token, so the entire creator layer spent the last 24 hours on one question: how much of what makes it good can you keep once you are paying by the token. Here is what actually matters.

1. Fable 5 goes credits-only tonight, and the economics are the whole story. Mark the clock. Per the Hermes list, Fable 5 leaves all Claude subscriptions at 11:59pm Pacific and then costs real money per token, which changes how you should drive it.

  • The biggest lever is the effort dial. Per Chase AI, Fable at low or medium effort matches or beats Opus 4.8 at a fraction of the cost, with concrete numbers of about $3.76 at low and $6 at medium against roughly $13 for Opus, and the max setting actively overthinks and produces worse output for more money.
  • The pattern to adopt is planner-plus-worker. Per Nate Herk, letting Fable or Opus orchestrate while Sonnet or Haiku do the execution lands roughly the same result at three times less cost.
  • The deadline chores worth doing today, per Chase AI: run a self-audit of your last 30 days of sessions to mint new skills, clean up your skill files, and point the model at your one hardest codebase before the subsidy ends.

2. The real argument: can you keep a model's judgment, or just its habits? This is the fight to watch. Per Nate Herk, you can install a "fable mode" skill that makes Opus 4.8 run Fable's five-gate discipline of scope, evidence, attack, verify, and report, and he even had Fable read its own leaked system prompt to extract the habits, like verifying that a file actually exists before trusting a prompt that implies it. Per Chase AI, that is "nonsense, the model is the model," and no skill file distills raw intelligence.

They are both partly right, and the honest middle is the useful part. You cannot copy the intelligence, but you can absolutely keep the process, the model-routing table, the verification gates, the effort discipline, and that process is most of what a careful operator actually felt when Fable seemed smart. Keep the checklist, not the magic.

3. The Hermes agent stack shipped again, and the security tooling is getting serious. The open-agent layer is not slowing down. Per the Hermes list, the Nous Hermes agent landed a six-feature update with a Mixture of Agents orchestrator, a `/learn` command that turns a URL into a skill, a `/journey` memory timeline, and Fable 5 built in as a callable profile.

  • The flare to watch is offensive security. Pliny released T3MP3ST, an autonomous red-team harness that runs on Claude Code, Codex, or Hermes, and it posted a 90.1% pass on the black-box XBEN benchmark and 98.7% white-box, beating the incumbent XBOW's 85%, while pinning eight of ten post-cutoff 2026 CVEs to the exact file and line.
  • The plumbing keeps consolidating too, with Nous Portal offering 300-plus models and a tool gateway under a single sign-on.

4. Tencent dropped a frontier-class model for free, and the cheap tier keeps rising. The floor under everyone keeps lifting. Per the Hermes list, Tencent released Hunyuan Hy3, a 295-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model under Apache 2.0 that it claims rivals trillion-scale flagships, with a free API on OpenRouter for two weeks and open weights on HuggingFace. Alongside it, builders are already routing DeepSeek V4 Flash, MiMo, and MiniMax through their agents, and running Qwen 3.6 locally on Nvidia's DGX Spark. The pattern from yesterday holds: the expensive frontier is for the hard 5%, and the free tier now handles the rest.

5. Under the surface, the infrastructure is straining. Two signals that the boom has a cost side. Per the Hermes list, the chip startup Groq is reportedly three weeks from collapse and floating "Groq Bonds" that swap salary for equity, with 80% of staff taking the deal, a stark reminder that not every AI-infrastructure name survives the buildout. On the research side, John Carmack is pointing at NAND-flash inference accelerators that could be 100 times cheaper per gigabyte than the high-bandwidth memory everyone is fighting over, which would reroute the whole cost curve if it works. And per the Polymarket list, Cloudflare is rolling out a monetization gateway to charge AI crawlers for sites, APIs, and even tool calls, another sign the free-scraping era is closing.
