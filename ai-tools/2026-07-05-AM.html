The Fable 5 ban taught the real lesson this weekend: one vendor in one country is one point of failure

A clean post-mortem of the export shutdown lands as the usage numbers go vertical, Nous Research turns Hermes into an operating layer, and the pros settle on Fable-plans-Codex-executes as the default. Build so a policy change is a config change.

ModelsAgentsToolingLocal/Open

The weekend's best AI writing was not about a new model. It was about what the Fable 5 outage actually proved, and the answer is an architecture warning every builder should act on. Underneath it, the demand data went vertical, the open-source camp shipped, and the practitioners quietly agreed on how to run these models. Here is the shift.

1. The real lesson of the ban is single vendor, single jurisdiction, single failure point. The cleanest post-mortem of the shutdown, a breakdown of whether the US could ban open-source AI, walks the timeline: Anthropic released Fable 5 and its lighter-guardrail twin Mythos 5 on June 9, an Amazon researcher found a narrow way to bypass Fable's safeguards on vulnerability prompts, and on June 12 the US applied export controls ordering Anthropic to restrict foreign-national access. Because nationality could not be verified in real time, both models went dark for everyone.

The key clarification is what did not happen: no open-weight model was pulled from Hugging Face, no repo was wiped. Frontier AI is now treated like advanced chips, where the lever is access, not quality. The takeaway is a rule, not a vibe: any revenue-critical or customer-facing workflow needs a fallback across vendor, jurisdiction, and deployment mode, because the failure can be silent, your dashboard green while the model underneath is quietly weaker or gated.

2. The route-around went straight to Chinese models. The same breakdown notes that when Fable went dark, builders did not wait. Some dropped to cheaper models, some went local, and a real share started testing the Chinese open models GLM 5.2 and Kimi, which are lower cost with an open door, accepting the different jurisdiction and trust profile as the price of continuity. That is the uncomfortable second-order effect of an export control aimed at denying access: it hands the open Chinese labs a live audition with exactly the builders the control was meant to keep on domestic frontier models.

3. The demand data went vertical, and that is why the labs can afford all this. Per the Crypto list, OpenRouter token volume ran from 6.4T in the first week of January to 14.8T weekly by March, up 160% in two months, and H100 rental prices, after falling for two years, reversed and climbed 40% in five months to $2.35. Enterprise AI adoption is still under 30% by the same desk's estimate, and that is the bull and the warning at once: the usage curve is near vertical and most of the enterprise has not even started. The tokens are real, and that is the ground truth under every capex headline.

4. Nous Research is turning Hermes into an operating layer. Per the Hermes list, the open-agent camp shipped all weekend. Co-founder Teknium announced an expanded plugin interface so developers with long-pending pull requests can ship stable plugins without merge dependency, Tony Simons pushed the Hermes A2A Bridge to v0.4.8 with local agent-to-agent interop, and DODOREACH wired a robodog as the first physical Hermes pet with spontaneous text-to-speech. Kevin Simback's Hermes Atlas newsletter frames the thesis plainly, that Hermes is becoming an operating layer for AI work positioned for the sovereign and open-source movement. This is the counterweight to section one: the answer to single-vendor risk is partly the open stack maturing into something you can actually run yourself.

5. The pros have settled on a division of labor, and it is Fable plans, Codex executes. Per the Hermes list, Matthew Berman is paying $200 a month each for two services and splitting the work, Fable for planning and GPT-5.5 for execution. Theo from t3.gg posted his own config, Fable plans and Codex executes, calling Codex far better at computer use, UI verification, and executing well-specified work, and noting he was throwing away about half his agent-driven pull requests before the workflow and now closes almost none. The lesson from yesterday's restraint argument lands here as a concrete setup: use the strongest model to decide what done looks like, then hand a tightly-scoped build to the executor.
