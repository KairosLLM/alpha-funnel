GPT-5.6 Sol shipped overnight and beats Fable on the coding benchmark at half the cost, so the smart-money workflow flipped from Fable-plans-Opus-builds to Fable-plans-Sol-builds

Two frontier models landed in a day, Grok 4.5 and GPT-5.6, and the cheaper worker is now also the stronger one. Fable stays the expensive foreman you escalate to, not the default, as its own users call the token bills unsustainable. Here is the signal.

Two frontier models shipped in 24 hours and the important part is not which one is smartest, it is that the cheap tier just got stronger than the mid tier. That quietly rewires how everyone builds. Here is what changed.

1. GPT-5.6 Sol is public, and the benchmark plus the price are the story. OpenAI's answer landed. Per Chase AI, GPT-5.6 "Sol" ships in a standard and an Ultra tier and scores ahead of Claude Mythos and well ahead of Fable 5 on Terminal Bench 2.1, on OpenAI's own numbers, while landing 25% pass at $0.56 against GPT-5.5's 23% at $1.24, a higher score at roughly half the cost.

  • It did not arrive alone. Per the Hermes list, Grok 4.5 shipped the same day, trained with Cursor as their Composer 3, roughly 1.5 trillion parameters, and Cursor's own Michael Truell called it Opus-class, fast, cheap, and the daily driver for many on his team.
  • The skepticism is healthy. One builder noted Grok ranks itself above Fable on its own benchmark, and Sol's real open question, per Chase AI, is writing and taste, the thing you cannot patch with a harness.

2. The org-chart trade got a new bottom layer. Yesterday's play was Fable plans and cheap Opus workers build. Today the worker lane moved to GPT. Per Chase AI, the new pattern in his open-source "grillme codeex" skill is Fable interviews and plans, then hands the build to Codex running Sol 5.6 instead of Opus, then reviews it, because the cheap worker is now also the stronger one.

  • The workflow is four stages: an interview where Fable asks 8 to 10 questions, adversarial planning where Fable and Codex argue to consensus over up to five rounds, the build handed to the GPT worker, and up to two Fable fix passes.
  • The efficiency is the point. His full "Trip Atlas" app build cost only about 130,000 Fable tokens, because Fable never touched the actual code.

3. The routing discipline got formalized, and Fable is now the escalation path, not the button. The cost backlash is real and specific. Per the Hermes list, Alex Finn burned roughly $150 of Fable credits in 30 minutes and called it economically unsustainable, with enterprises already cutting off Opus for being too expensive.

  • Per Kris Torrington, the discipline is a routing table by risk: extraction and formatting go to Sonnet or Haiku, normal coding and analysis to Opus at medium-high, hard agentic work to Opus at extra-high, and only frontier uncertainty where being wrong costs money or days escalates to Fable, which lists at twice Opus and is sliding toward usage credits.
  • His rule of thumb is the whole trade in one line: if Opus gives you 80% of the result at half the token price, use Opus, and only reach for Fable when the missing 20% actually matters.

4. One prompt can now build a whole business, if the prompt is the real work. The ceiling on a single `/goal` keeps rising. Per Nate Herk, one prompt spun up a full company, Counterbrief, a Shopify chargeback tool, in three to four hours, with Fable orchestrating and every worker running on Opus or Sonnet.

  • The heavy lifting was the prompt architecture, not the model: ten parallel research agents swept Reddit and Hacker News into 35 problems, tournaments of competing ideas scored by five judge personas, and a red team of six skeptic agents that threw 38 attacks and got zero kills.
  • The guardrails are the reusable part: no new spending, publish nothing, invent nothing and verify every fact, and never stop to ask. It cost about 500,000 Fable tokens, roughly 15% of a weekly budget, and would have been 100 times more expensive with Fable workers.

5. The human edge that survives all of this is the spec. The through-line worth keeping. Per Nate B Jones, when everyone can code, the scarce person is the one who can take a vague business need and turn it into a precise spec, and then judge whether the machine's output actually solves the customer's problem.

That is the same skill under every story today. The models get cheaper and the routing gets automated, but the person who can direct the machines with enough precision and then tell whether the result is any good is the one role none of it replaces.
