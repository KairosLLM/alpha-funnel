Fable 5 came back guardrailed to the point of revolt, and the price of intelligence is falling

The relaunched model is so locked down that power users say it is unusable for real work, China keeps undercutting the frontier on cost, and the useful question is what actually still needs a human.

ModelsAgentsToolingComputeLocal/Open

The model everyone wanted back is back, and the first thing its heaviest users did was complain that they cannot use it. That gap, between the model on the benchmark and the model you are allowed to run, is the real story this morning.

1. Fable 5 returned so locked down that power users are in revolt. The export controls came off yesterday. The guardrails did not. On the Hermes list, the reaction is loud and specific: one heavy user says Fable 5 is "not nerfed, it is SLAUGHTERED," another reports it hardblocking legitimate biology work, and the safety classifiers that route sensitive tasks to Opus are catching ordinary requests. The counter-take, from Alex Finn, is that Fable 5 is better understood as an operating-system-level tool than a chat model, so you build around it rather than talk to it.

Sonnet 5, meanwhile, is getting called "absurdly expensive" for what it does. The through-line with yesterday's fingerprint fight is the same: the frontier model you read about and the one you can actually deploy are drifting apart, and for real work the guardrails are now a bigger constraint than the intelligence.

2. The price of intelligence is falling, and China is why. This is the model-economics side of a story whose market side sits in Investing. The framing from Capital Flows that matters for builders is that a subsidized Chinese lab does not need to earn a margin on inference at all, so it can give away a model one generation back and still fund the next one. The export controls proved they cannot be contained: weights that already exist everywhere cannot be recalled, and the June suspension of the US frontier model was answered by an open-source near-peer within a day.

DeepSeek V4 Flash now sits at the top of the Hermes model rankings. If you are choosing a model to build on, the cheap-and-open tier keeps getting closer to the frontier while the frontier gets harder to actually use.

3. The honest read on Fable 5 and jobs: it kills the work with no judgment in it. Nate B Jones had the cleanest labor take, and it cuts against the panic. The only jobs a model this capable actually eliminates are the pure-execution roles with zero judgment, the ones that could honestly have been automated any time in the last decade. Everything else still needs a person, because the model does not run itself. Someone has to aim it, feed it the right context, check what it produced, and own the result when it lands on a desk. He calls that role the model manager, and it is a different job than the one you have now, not a smaller one. If your work carries real judgment, you are the one pointing the tool, not the one it replaces.

4. The release calendar is about to get loud. Two frontier drops are priced as near-certain. The market puts GPT-5.6 landing as soon as Tuesday at 77% and Grok 4.4 by month-end at 78%. That matters for anyone building right now, because a GPT-5.6 that lands into the current AI-bubble argument (the overbuild and cost-curve doubts in Investing) becomes a referendum on whether frontier gains still justify frontier spend. On the open side, Nous Research keeps shipping, with its Hermes agent now running a native mixture-of-agents setup where several models draft and critique each other. The practical move is the same one that got people through the Fable outage: keep your harness model-agnostic so next week's release is a config change, not a migration.
