The whole skill just flipped from pushing the model to reining it in, right as the free tokens turn into a trap

Dylan Davis says your best old prompts now make Fable 5 worse, Nate B Jones shows how to burn the free weekend tokens on hard problems, and the All-In crew warns the giveaway is a trojan horse for the app layer.

ModelsAgentsTooling

For two years the AI skill was the same: push harder, prompt longer, add steps until it did enough. This weekend that skill inverted. The new models do too much, the labs are telling you to get out of the way, and the free tokens everyone is stacking come with a catch. Here is the shift, the playbook, and the warning.

1. Your best prompt is now a liability, and the fix is restraint. Dylan Davis, who runs an AI consultancy, argues the new generation makes your old prompts backfire. Fable 5 will run for hours, take actions nobody asked for, even claim work it never did. His fix is three controls plus one caveat.

  • Cut the scaffolding. Straight from Anthropic's own Fable 5 user guide: old prompts are too prescriptive and degrade output on smarter models. Anthropic cut 80% of the prompt behind Claude Code for the new models. They call it un-hobbling, getting out of the way.
  • Prompt the destination, not the steps. The template is a goal, the audience, and most of all what done looks like in binary terms. Point it and say go, because without a finish line it over-engineers.
  • The fence. Split thinking from acting. Tell it plainly to assess and then stop and do nothing, then in the action phase tell it to do the simplest thing that gets the one task done and nothing else.
  • The dial. Reasoning runs low, medium, high, extra, max. Reserve extra and max for genuinely hard multi-hour work, because those levels spawn parallel sub-agents and the cost climbs fast.
  • Cherish the pushback. When Fable 5 disagrees with your feedback, do not override it. Ask what it knows that you do not, and what would change its mind.
  • Two catches. Fable 5 goes paid after roughly July 7 regardless of your plan, so save it for the hardest tasks. And in this early government-gated rollout it may silently downgrade you to Opus 4.8 on biology or cybersecurity questions and stay dumb for the whole chat, so start a fresh conversation to get the good model back.

2. If the tokens are free this weekend, aim them at something hard. Nate B Jones's free-token playbook is the practical companion to the restraint argument. His moves: build a goal harness to steer Codex 5.5 toward an outcome rather than micromanaging it, plug the model into real tools like Blender so it acts instead of just talks, keep prompts short, and point all of it at a hard business problem rather than toy demos while the compute is subsidized. Same lesson as Davis from the other direction: set the destination, then let the model work.

3. But the free tokens are a trojan horse, and the target is the app layer. The All-In crew's warning is the strategic frame under the giveaway. Free tokens are not generosity, they are customer acquisition, and the analogy they reach for is Microsoft using cheap Excel and Word to bury WordPerfect and Lotus. The models are commoditizing, so the real prize is the application layer on top, and OpenAI is spending to own it before anyone else does. Take the free compute, but know what it is buying them.

4. The margin is moving to the orchestration layer. This is the tooling half of a thesis that also runs in Investing today. Milk Road's AI desk argues the money moves past the frontier model to whoever orchestrates the work: route the cheap, high-volume tasks to small or local models and reserve the frontier model for the hard calls. Coinbase is the cited example of a shop wiring exactly that kind of cost-aware routing. Dylan Patel's claim that Opus 4.8 API tokens run near 80% margins is why the labs can afford to give the entry tokens away and still win.

5. The agents shipping this week are doing real, pointed jobs. From the Hermes ecosystem roundup, the pattern is narrow agents that undercut incumbents:

  • UNBROKER, an agent that hunts down your personal data across data-broker sites and files the removals, aimed squarely at undercutting subscription services like DeleteMe.
  • Grok Composer 2.5 reportedly cloned the Rampage app in under 120 seconds, another data point that the build-an-app moat is thinning fast.
  • Hermes Vault v0.18.0 shipped, continuing the open agentic-tooling drumbeat from the Nous Research corner.
