The Fable ban is over, and the lesson is own your harness

Washington formally lifted the export controls, Anthropic is now designing its own Samsung chip, and the operators who shrugged off the outage did it by routing to cheaper models.

ModelsAgentsToolingCompute

The best model came back. The more useful story is what the people who never depended on it were doing while it was gone. They were routing around it, and that is the skill that outlasts any one release.

1. The export controls are formally lifted, and the model is now a national-security asset. This morning's relaunch got the government stamp this afternoon. Per the People to follow list, US Commerce lifted the export controls on Fable 5 and Mythos 5, restoring global access, and Anthropic is designing its own 2nm custom chip with Samsung to cut its dependence on Trainium, TPUs, and Nvidia. That is the tell that matters. The frontier is now something governments gate and labs vertically integrate, not a pure product cycle. The noisier subplot is a fingerprint fight on the Hermes list, where one researcher found Fable 5 statistically dissimilar to Anthropic's own models but 0.97 and 0.93 similar to Kimi K2.7 and GLM 5.2, floating a possible training leak, while another called Fable "pretty much unusable for interpretability." Treat the leak claim as unproven. The signal underneath it is that the lineage of these models is getting genuinely hard to trace, which is its own kind of risk.

2. The real lesson of the outage is to own the harness and route freely. Nate B Jones made the point that the firms that shrugged off the 18-day ban were the ones that own their harness (the scaffolding and tooling around a model, as opposed to the model's raw intelligence) and just rerouted. His model-selection rules cut against the urge to chase every release.

  • Do not copy anyone, including him. Your work is not their work.
  • Ask how hard the work is, not just how much. Difficulty picks the model, volume does not.
  • Know how to tell if the output is good before you scale it, with real evals and a sniff test.
  • Do not let model choice itself become the work. Picking is overhead, not progress.
  • Do not run too many models. Sprawl is its own tax.

His split: a frontier daily driver for messy mixed work (Claude, ChatGPT, Opus 4.8) and a cheap workhorse for center-of-distribution output like memos, decks, and CRM cleanup, where GLM 5.2 is "sometimes better than Claude." The enterprise roll-call backs it up. Coinbase is raising token usage while cutting cost by routing to GLM and Kimi, and Shopify, Airbnb, Lindy, Cursor, and Microsoft are all pushing work to open-weight or Chinese models. Open-weight routing is quietly eating the routine-inference budget.

3. David Ondrej's playbook is Fable as the boss, cheap models as the hands. The most concrete "how to actually use this without going broke" came from David Ondrej, who runs Fable as the orchestrator and hands the grunt work to cheaper models.

  • Fable plans, the cheap models execute. It does architecture, competitor analysis, and PR reviews as the planner, while Kimi K2.7, GLM 5.2, MiniMax, and DeepSeek V4 do the actual coding at 7x to 25x less cost. Do not burn Fable limits fixing a front-end button.
  • Use it through the API, not the chat. He says the API is less restricted and only got silently downgraded to Opus a handful of times across hundreds of prompts.
  • Delete most of your skills. He argues Fable needs so little hand-holding that 80% to 100% of your old skill files are now dead weight.

One caution. Ondrej also promotes prompt tricks to dodge the safety classifiers and open distillation (training a cheaper model on a stronger one's outputs) that violates the terms of service, and he notes over 100 Fable-distillation datasets already sit on Hugging Face. Take the workflow, skip the parts that break the rules.

4. The number behind all the gating: someone just called Anthropic a $3 trillion company. On the All-In Podcast, a panelist argued Anthropic would trade around $3 trillion as a public company today, on revenue "well over $100 billion" this year and 85% gross margins on inference (the cost of actually running the model to answer, as opposed to training it). His frame is that the market has already absorbed the number and it is simply shifting from private to public. Take the figure with heavy salt, but it explains the behavior around it. MGX out of Abu Dhabi just closed a $49B AI fund, and Microsoft stood up a 6,000-person "Frontier" unit with $2.5B. When people believe those margins, they build like this.

5. Owning your own intelligence is turning into a real capex line. The through-line from Ondrej and the open-weight crowd is that self-hosting is becoming a genuine spend, not a hobby. He is personally putting $50,000 to $100,000 into a local GPU cluster and a 40TB-to-80TB NAS to archive open-weight models and datasets before they can be pulled, and he is telling people to buy more VRAM and Nvidia's Spark. Nous Research shipped Hermes v0.18 with a mixture-of-agents design in the same spirit. The bet is that per-person token consumption keeps going exponential and you do not want your intelligence living behind a switch someone else controls.
