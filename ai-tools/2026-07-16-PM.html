Three frontier models shipped in 48 hours and the story is not who is smartest, it is that good-enough got ten times cheaper overnight

Grok 4.5, a new Meta model, and OpenAI's GPT-5.6 all landed at once, and the cheapest now runs at a fraction of Opus pricing. OpenAI folded ChatGPT, Codex, and its browser into one app, Mira Murati's lab put out a giant open-weights model, and the practitioner advice this week is all about picking tools by your own workflow instead of the leaderboard.

The AI evening has one big idea and several practical ones. The frontier stopped being the whole game, because the middle of the market just got cheap enough to do most of the work. Ranked.

1. The model market split in two, and the cheap half is where the volume goes now. The framing that ties the week together is a divorce between intelligence and price. Per the Limitless podcast, three frontier models shipped inside 48 hours, SpaceX AI's Grok 4.5, OpenAI's GPT-5.6, and Meta's first priced model, MuseSpark 1.1, and the news is not the top of the stack but the bottom. Grok 4.5 is pitched as Opus-class at roughly a third of the price, at $2 in and $6 out per million tokens, while MuseSpark undercuts even that at $1.25 and $4.25.

The argument underneath is that cheap models now deliver 90 to 95 percent of frontier intelligence at about a tenth of the cost, and that is good enough for the roughly 80 percent of work that is routine, leaving the true frontier for the fifth of tasks where a mistake is expensive. The evidence is in enterprise behavior, with a widely cited token-spend index down 20 percent as companies hunt for cheaper models, Amazon retiring an internal usage leaderboard, and Coinbase and Walmart capping consumption. The counterweight is Anthropic, rumored profitable last quarter and reportedly heading toward a listing, the bet that expensive but sustainable beats cheap and subsidized.

2. OpenAI stopped shipping features and shipped one app. The product move that matters as much as the model is consolidation. Per Kris Torrington, OpenAI merged ChatGPT, Codex, and its browser into a single unified app, with a Work mode that acts as a general assistant across your files and connected tools and a separate Codex mode for engineering. The GPT-5.6 launch was the headline, but folding three products into one surface is the bigger tell about where this is going.

The same week brought GPT Live, OpenAI's real-time voice mode, and the honest test for it is interruption. Per Kris Torrington again, the way to judge a live voice model is to cut it off, change one instruction mid-sentence, and see whether it keeps the thread, since that is the failure point of every voice assistant that came before. A single app that listens, browses, and codes is a different kind of product than a chat box, and it raises the same trust question every ambient device does.

3. Mira Murati's lab put a giant on the open-weights table. The open side got a heavyweight entrant. Per Diet TBPN, Thinking Machines released its first model, an open-weights system called Inkling with 975 billion total parameters and about 41 billion active, positioned not as the strongest model anywhere but as a broad, balanced base meant to be fine-tuned through the lab's Tinker interface. The strategy is the Red Hat move, give away the weights and keep the fine-tuning relationship, and it is aimed squarely at the lead OpenAI and Anthropic hold.

The launch immediately caught the distillation crossfire that is defining the open-model fight. One researcher claimed Inkling was the rare open model not distilled from OpenAI or Anthropic, got community-corrected, and semi-walked it back, while Thinking Machines' own notes say it bootstrapped its post-training on synthetic data from other open models. Treat the benchmark claims as contested for now, because the interesting thing is the shape of the bet rather than the leaderboard slot.

4. The practitioner corner: pick tools by your workflow, and mind the tool that manages the tools. The concrete lesson this week is to stop chasing rankings. Per Kris Torrington, the move is to test one real workflow and measure three things, cost per usable result, time to finish, and how much correcting is left over, then switch only when a new model clearly wins on at least two, because cheap output gets expensive when you have to repair all of it. The companion warning is that running a model locally is really three separate decisions, per this breakdown: the parameter tier, the license, and the hardware you actually own, and a downloadable giant that needs a datacenter is just cloud with extra steps.

The tool built for the multi-agent reality is Herder, an open-source multiplexer described as tmux for AI agents. Per Chase AI, it solves the mess of running Claude Code and Codex side by side across six or eight loose terminals by putting every agent in one monitored view that shows which is working, idle, or blocked, and running them as a background server so closing a window does not kill the job. It is free and it names the real bottleneck of this whole evening, the way the human keeps becoming the thing that shuttles work between too many tools.
