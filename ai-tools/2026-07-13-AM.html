NVIDIA's own Kaggle champion says agent skills beat MCP tools by 100x on context, and a 4-billion-parameter model just solved half of ARC-AGI for 20 cents

Jean-Francois Puget shipped 121 agent skills and a benchmark warning that most leaderboards are contaminated, while a distilled small model keeps beating frontier systems on cost. The efficiency story is now the whole AI debate, an ex-OpenAI forecaster puts catastrophe at 70 percent, and the practical builder lesson is simpler than either.

The AI story this morning is about efficiency, and it points two ways at once. Small models keep doing frontier work for pennies, either the death of the compute trade or the reason it keeps growing. The most useful voice is a NVIDIA engineer who benchmarks for a living. Ranked, with the buildable stuff up top.

1. Skills beat MCP tools by roughly 100x on context, and that is the sleeper shift in how agents work. The standout, per Jean-Francois Puget, a NVIDIA distinguished engineer and top-three Kaggle competitor: the model-context-protocol tools that every agent loads eat your context window whether you use them or not, because their descriptions all sit in the prompt. Agent skills use progressive disclosure instead, where the agent reads only a short front-matter line and loads the full instructions on demand, and teams report that moving from MCP tools to skills cut context consumption about 100-fold.

NVIDIA shipped a repository of roughly 121 of these skills so generic coding agents like Claude Code and Codex can drive NVIDIA libraries they were never trained on, plus an automated pipeline that tests whether a skill loads when it should and stays quiet when it should not. The deeper idea is that skills are becoming a new form of learning. NVIDIA won a data-science benchmark by having its agent solve each task, distill the winning approach into a skill, then compress a hundred skills into a few general ones, all in plain markdown with no fine-tuning.

2. A 4-billion-parameter model solving half of ARC-AGI for 20 cents is the efficiency argument in one number. Puget's team keeps winning the ARC-AGI reasoning contest with tiny models. Per the same interview, a 4-billion-parameter model on Kaggle's limited hardware now solves close to 50 percent of the puzzles at about 20 cents a task, where frontier models need dollars a task to reach the same mark, built by distilling bigger models into a million synthetic puzzles and training the small one on those.

That connects straight to the market's biggest question, and a sharp Reddit thread frames both sides. DeepSeek erased $589 billion of Nvidia value in a day last year on a cheap-training claim, GLM-5.2 is a 744-billion-parameter open model its maker says trained entirely on Chinese Huawei chips, and small vision models keep matching bigger ones. The counter is Jevons, that cheaper inference has so far expanded total compute demand rather than shrinking it. Puget lands on the bullish side, arguing this is not a bubble because token costs fall every few months and adoption sticks, but he is scathing about the benchmarks everyone cites.

3. Most AI leaderboards are contaminated, and a NVIDIA engineer is calling the reckoning. The warning worth internalizing, per Puget: most language-model benchmarks do not check for overfitting, so public test sets get memorized. He watched a frontier model pass a coding benchmark by picking the right files to edit without reading their contents, because the fixes were in the public training data, and NVIDIA stopped working on that benchmark as a result. The only scores he trusts are the ones where you commit before you see the answer, the way Kaggle works, and he predicts a bloodbath for the teams now letting coding agents chase leaderboard numbers with no notion of validation.

The practical builder lesson sits next to it, per Kris Torrington. Do not abandon workflows for agents, combine them: use a deterministic workflow when the steps are known and mistakes are expensive, like payments and compliance, and an agent only when the input is messy and the decision stays bounded. The data backs the caution, with n8n growing revenue tenfold to past $40 million, Klarna quietly rehiring humans after overshooting on AI support, and Gartner projecting that more than 40 percent of agentic projects get cancelled by 2027 even as adoption climbs.

4. The lock-in is the real moat, and an ex-OpenAI forecaster is worried about much more than that. Two voices this morning argue the durable advantage is switching cost, not model quality. Per Nate B Jones, once Claude lives in your Slack as a team-level harness sitting next to everything your people do, you are effectively renting your own data back to yourself, and it becomes impossible to rip out. Puget makes the same point from the cost side, that once a team is hooked on Claude Code or Codex, going back is the hard part.

The heavier note comes from Daniel Kokotajlo, the ex-OpenAI researcher who walked away from about $2 million rather than sign a non-disparagement clause. On the Diary of a CEO he puts a 70 percent chance on catastrophe, which he walks back on tape from strict extinction to something like an AI takeover, and pegs superintelligence around 2029. His new AI 2040 plan argues for slowing that arrival to 2040 through research transparency and verification, and the transparency piece is the market-relevant part, because publishing model architectures would commoditize exactly the labs whose valuations depend on secrecy. He also notes Anthropic's revenue went from roughly $1 billion a year ago to about $60 billion now, the number that makes everyone act like the timelines are real.

5. Model housekeeping, quickly. Per the Hermes list: OpenAI says GPT-5.6 Sol crossed 6 million active users heading for 7 million, walked back an overcharging change that had cut its context window from 372,000 tokens to 272,000, and temporarily removed the 5-hour usage cap for paid tiers. Anthropic extended Fable 5 on all paid plans and raised Claude Code weekly limits by 50 percent through July 19, which reads as a direct answer to the deprecation worry from the weekend. And an independent build test of Grok 4.5 produced a working project from a cold start in three minutes while noting the model still has not solved taste, the one thing the benchmarks never measure.
