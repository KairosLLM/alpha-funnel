Fable 5 is back for real, and the day's other news is that the labs suddenly make money

The model everyone missed returned with new guardrails and genuinely new tricks, Anthropic reportedly turned its first profit while a startup chip threatens Nvidia on inference, and the cheap-clone war got a price cut aimed straight at China.

This morning the story was that Fable 5 came back so locked down its power users revolted. This afternoon fills in the other half. The model can actually do things again, the economics under the whole industry quietly turned positive, and the frontier labs are now pricing to kill their Chinese clones. The unit-economics doubt from this morning got a real answer.

1. Fable 5 is genuinely back, and the capabilities are the part worth your attention. The guardrail revolt from this morning is real, but the Limitless podcast fills in what the model does when you get past them. It now has what they call visual intelligence, an actual sense of aesthetic taste, so the websites and artifacts it builds stop looking like AI slop. One demo built a fully playable Hogwarts game (physics, sound, spatial awareness) from a single prompt in one shot. And Claude Science, a research-tuned version with a tool harness, is doing 100-plus-page literature reviews for the Allen Institute in hours instead of what used to take up to two years.

The new safeguards are the price of the return. Fable now monitors every prompt for attack patterns and routes anything it flags to a heavier, slower model, and that is why ordinary requests keep getting caught. The practical facts to plan around, from The AI Advantage: it burns roughly 70% more tokens than Opus 4.8 (not the 2x people assumed), it is free only through July 7 before usage-based pricing kicks in, and it carries its own 50% weekly usage cap. If your work has real judgment in it, this is the sharpest tool on the shelf right now. Just budget for it.

2. The unit-economics story flipped today, and that answers this morning's bear case. The 5:30am edition ran OpenAI's cash burn as evidence the whole thing leaks money. The afternoon numbers cut the other way.

  • Anthropic reportedly turned profitable in Q2 2026, the first frontier lab to do it, on inference margins said to run near 80%. If that holds, the "nobody makes money on inference" thesis is already dated.
  • OpenAI claims it halved its own inference cost, dropping the GPU count needed to serve free-tier users from hundreds of thousands to a couple hundred, per the same podcast. Take the exact figures with salt, but the direction matches Anthropic's.
  • Etched is the wild card in the hardware stack. The inference-chip startup has raised $800M and booked $1B in orders for a chip that has not shipped, promising 75% less power for the same inference output versus a GPU. The backers are the story: Peter Thiel, Jane Street, and TSMC, which took equity. A chip foundry taking a stake in a would-be Nvidia alternative is the kind of signal that does not show up in a benchmark. It ships by year end.

Put together, the picture is not an industry bleeding out. It is one where the cost of serving a model is falling fast enough that the margins are finally showing up.

3. Sonnet 5 got priced to kill the cheap-clone trade, and the distillation war went public. The strategic move today was pricing. Sonnet 5 launched cheaper than Sonnet 4.6 (through August 31) while doing roughly Opus 4.8's job, an open bid to keep builders inside the Claude ecosystem instead of drifting to a subsidized Chinese model like GLM 5.2. This is the same cost-curve pressure from this morning, answered with a price cut instead of a shrug.

The uglier subplot: that "spyware-like" code people found in Claude Code was reportedly an experiment to detect distillation attacks (rivals training their cheap models on the outputs of an expensive one). Alibaba's Qwen got caught doing exactly that, and Dario Amodei confirmed it publicly. DeepSeek V4 Flash still sits at the top of the open-model rankings, so the cheap tier keeps closing the gap. The frontier's answer is to make its own mid-tier too cheap to bother cloning.

4. If you are actually building with this stuff, here is the day's practical playbook. The most useful content today was not the model news, it was people showing how to run these tools without going broke or hitting a wall.

  • Cut Fable's cost by dialing effort, not switching models. Per Chase AI, the same task ran $22 at max effort and $3.76 at low, an 80%-plus cut, and Fable-on-low still beat Opus-on-max. Token-saver skills like ponytail (about 22% cheaper on Fable) and caveman help, and `/deep-research` can fan out over a hundred sub-agents when you actually need the depth.
  • Every agent demo dies at the last step, so design for it. Nate B Jones laid out a reusable agent skeleton of nine primitives (context pack, ingest, chunk, normalize, store, retrieve, cite, export, and a human gate at the end) and pointed it at real drudgery like insurance appeals and tax prep. His point ties to the cost story: once your data is clean, a cheap or open model does the job, so the moat is the plumbing, not the model.
  • Build a second brain that any model can read. Nate Herk wired Andrej Karpathy's LLM-wiki idea into Claude Code and Obsidian: drop in sources, the model splits them into cross-linked markdown pages with routing rules, and any agent can reason over the result. Fable understood a "keep it simple for a beginner" design instruction that Opus 4.8 could not get after a full day. Because it is just markdown with routing, it is model-agnostic.
  • UNBROKER, an open-source privacy tool for your weekend. Released as a Hermes skill by the Nous Research crowd, it finds where data brokers expose your personal info and files the legal removals for you, all locally. It is a free replacement for the $330-a-year DeleteMe tier, and a clean example of an agent pointed at a boring, valuable job.

5. One caveat before you trust next week's benchmark war. GPT-5.6 is priced to land as soon as Tuesday, and the wiki-building demo surfaced a sharp catch: OpenAI benchmarked GPT-5.6 Sol against a Mythos preview model, not against Fable 5, and used different test harnesses. So when the launch numbers hit, the head-to-head claims will not line up cleanly. Read the release, but do not let the marketing pick your model. The move that got everyone through the Fable outage still holds: keep your harness model-agnostic so next week's drop is a config change.
