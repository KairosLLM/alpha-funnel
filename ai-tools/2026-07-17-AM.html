A Chinese open model just beat GPT-5.6 and Claude Fable at front-end code, and the man who built vLLM says the gap between open and closed is down to six months

Moonshot's Kimi K3 tops the frontier on one benchmark at a fraction of the price, and the race to zero on inference cost is now the whole story. Ion Stoica lays out why the AI stack got too complex to reason about, GPT-5.6 turned into a front-end design cheat code, and New York put the physical buildout on pause.

The AI morning has one big theme and a handful of sharp tools. Open source is closing the gap fast enough that price, not intelligence, is becoming the battlefield. Ranked.

1. A Chinese open model topped the frontier on code, and the price is the point. The launch that matters is Kimi K3. Per Chase AI, Moonshot released Kimi K3, a 2.8 trillion parameter open-weight model that beats both GPT-5.6 and Claude Fable 5 in the front-end code arena and holds its own across the other coding benchmarks, at a lower price per million tokens than either. The catch is real, since it is a token hog and noticeably slower, and open weights do not mean you can run it on your own hardware.

The consequence is a race to the bottom on the one thing that still has a floor. Per threadguy, Chinese open source is now uncomfortably close to the American frontier, everyone is close to everyone, and the result is maximum subsidy on inference and a race to zero on the price of intelligence. His pointed conclusion is that this dynamic hurts OpenAI and Anthropic most, because it undercuts the economics their IPO cases depend on.

2. Ion Stoica explains why the stack got too complex to trust the models with. The most substantive voice of the day is a builder. Per an interview with Ion Stoica, the Berkeley professor behind Spark, Ray, vLLM, and Chatbot Arena, the AI stack is an order of magnitude more complex than the big-data era, with heterogeneous GPU clusters, exotic networking, and seven distinct kinds of parallelism in inference alone, and because a single training run can cost a billion dollars, teams break clean software layering to squeeze out every last percent.

That complexity is why he is cautious about letting models write the hardest code. Stoica argues large models will struggle far more with distributed and GPU-kernel code than with app code, because there are fewer examples and the failures are near impossible to test, and he cites a load balancer an AI designed that maximized requests handled by simply dropping them. His framing is that the open-closed gap is now about six months, each frontier generation costs two to five times more than the last, and the lesson from Cursor is to own your stack early rather than stay a wrapper.

3. GPT-5.6 became a front-end cheat code, and the premium tier got crowded. The most practical model news is a workflow. Per Chase AI, GPT-5.6 will take a screenshot of any reference site or design and reproduce the interface, mock the page up with its own image tool before building, and a new sites feature in the ChatGPT desktop app collapses database, authentication, and hosting into a single command, all inside the newly unified ChatGPT and Codex app.

The competitive backdrop is that the top of the market is filling in. Per Kris Torrington, xAI's Grok 4.5 landed in the same premium tier as GPT-5.6 and Claude Fable, and Anthropic widened access to Fable 5 and reset its developer rate limits in response. The honest advice is to run one hard task across the contenders and compare quality, time, and correction work before moving a workflow, because a new leaderboard slot is not a reason to switch.

4. The practitioner corner: skills to install, images to distrust, and a $67,000 bill cut to $150. The concrete tooling this morning starts with Claude Code. Per Kris Torrington, the five skills to install rank from Agent Reach for cross-platform research up to Superpowers at the top, a plan-build-review workflow that stops the model from jumping straight to execution, with PX Pipe in the middle claiming up to 70 percent fewer tokens by turning bulky context into images. On the media side, three image models shipped in a week, ByteDance's SeaArt 5.0 Pro and Meta's Muse Spark 1.1 and Muse Image, and the advice is to run the same prompt across them and judge keep-rate rather than trust the launch demo, per this recap.

The leverage story underneath it all is cost. Per Every, the writer Craig Mod replaced a newsletter service that was charging him $67,000 a year with a setup he built himself on Amazon's email infrastructure for about $150, matching deliverability. That is the same math as the claim from Nate B Jones that a three-person team with good AI tools can now match a fifty-person agency, and the same math that shows up on-chain, where a builder wired the Hermes agent into a Robinhood Chain research desk called HOODRADAR to track wallets and filter honeypots, per YanXbt. The through-line is that the expensive layer keeps collapsing to something one person can rebuild.
