Meta ripped through $650 on BofA's half-price buildout math, the crowd threw $200 billion at the Hynix book, and crypto's bulls and bears just agreed on a second-half plan

BofA says Meta is adding AI capacity at $22 billion a gigawatt, half the going estimate, and the stock recaptured its 200-day on the news. Bitcoin is sitting on the $64,000 line with dated bottom calls stacking beneath it, and the Treasury Secretary gave crypto its policy speech of the year. Here is the session.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Two stories owned the tape today, Meta's melt-up and the SK Hynix debut, and they are the same bet on AI infrastructure at two different prices. The quieter action was in crypto, where the bulls and the bears spent the day converging on the same trade. Here is the close, then six things.

The close. Risk-on around two anchors. Per the Market list, Meta ran more than 6 percent through $650 into the $660s, recapturing its 200-day moving average (the long-term trend line traders treat as the bull-bear divide) with a gap left to fill at $668.50, while the SK Hynix ADR began when-issued trading around midday under SKHYV after this morning's $149 pricing.

The index-level gate has not moved: Mr_Derivatives argues that if Nvidia, still near bear-market territory, and Microsoft, in one, turn up, the S&P can test 7,900 to 8,000, since the two are roughly 12 percent of the index. Bitcoin held just above $64,000, the level tonight's whole crypto argument turns on. One flag into tomorrow: a Prof G Markets guest argued that oil is priced for the best case in the Middle East, so yesterday's Iran relief is carrying more weight than it should.

1. Meta went vertical because BofA did the capex math and got half the number everyone feared. Per the Market list, BofA's note pegs Meta's new AI capacity at roughly $22 billion per gigawatt, 6.5 gigawatts added on $145 billion of capex, against the prior $45 billion per gigawatt assumption. The kicker is the resale case: if Meta sells half that capacity at $15 billion of revenue per gigawatt, that is up to $150 billion of incremental revenue, and anything under $30 billion per gigawatt of cost beats Amazon's and Google's cloud economics of $10 to 16 billion per gigawatt. BofA's own caveat: the in-house Iris chip only starts manufacturing in September, so the savings barely touch 2026 costs.

The tape treated the note as a regime change. Cathie Wood bought about $21 million of the stock, Jim Cramer called the model change worth 100 points, and amitisinvesting frames it as a 33 percent grower at 18 times forward earnings with $700 to $750 in play into the July 29 report, where the sell side already models $200 billion-plus of 2027 capex. Meta also broke ground on another giant data center yesterday, per the Teradyne episode. The dissent came from inside the same feed: WisemanCap's reaction was "everyone is long META now? Give me a break." Hold that thought for the close of this note.

2. The Hynix book hit $200 billion, and the valuation skeptics showed up the same afternoon. Per the Market list, Bloomberg's detail on the debut is heavier than this morning's: roughly $200 billion of demand across 500-plus accounts, with the top 25 taking about two thirds of the deal, and JPMorgan lit its headquarters with the South Korean flag. Daniel Newman calls it a multi-year memory run with supply constraints past 2030, and the rack math backs him, HBM (the stacked memory AI accelerators feed on) climbing from low hundreds of thousands of dollars per AI rack toward $1 million-plus with Nvidia's Rubin Ultra generation. Cramer expects a wave of analyst initiations Monday when SKHY converts to regular-way trading.

The counterweight landed the same day. Rick Orford reads Micron at 9.2 times earnings as the market pricing a cycle peak rather than offering a bargain, memory being a business where supply eventually catches up and margins compress, and he makes the same normalization argument about Nvidia's below-peer multiple. Cheap multiples on cyclical AI hardware are a warning label as often as an invitation. For the debut itself, Mr_Derivatives offers last month's SpaceX analog: SPCX popped 17 percent intraday on day one and closed up 7, so the shape of the first print is usually the froth, and the real vote comes later.

3. Bitcoin is parked on $64,000, and for once both sides put dates on the calendar. Milk Road's John Gillen maps it cleanly: hold $64,000 on the retest and the move runs to $70,000 to $71,000, lose it and the sweep goes to $58,000 with the channel bottom near $57,000. He leans toward the lower path, and he ties the fork to Washington: the CLARITY Act (the bill that decides whether the SEC or the CFTC regulates crypto) sits at 40 percent odds on Polymarket, down 25 points, with the Senate back July 13 and roughly three working weeks before the August recess as the last realistic 2026 window. Heisenberg independently flags $60,000 holding and $70,000-ish on a breakout, two sources landing on the same upside number.

The bottom-callers got specific too. Lark Davis says the monthly stochastic RSI (a slow momentum oscillator) is bouncing from the exact zone that marked the 2015, 2018, and 2022 bear bottoms, and separately that Bitcoin is its most oversold against gold since roughly 2011, a condition that preceded a multi-year rally of about 660 percent. Benjamin Cowen runs the bear case with the same destination: 2026 is tracking the 2018 midterm-year script nearly beat for beat, realized price near $53,000 (the average cost basis of every coin, a level midterm years historically dip below) argues one more leg down, and his bottom window is late September to early October. Note what just happened there. The loudest bull and the loudest bear both end at "accumulate the second half."

On the Saylor overhang, Ansom and Matt Hougan make the same read: Strategy sold $225 million into the low, the market absorbed it, so the seller everyone feared is priced. And Tom Lee's frame for the whole complex is that crypto today is where memory stocks were before the parabola, the ignored downstream AI trade, though he holds nearly 5 percent of ETH's supply through BitMine, so weigh the source with the claim.

4. The Treasury Secretary told markets who writes the rules of money, and a stablecoin bank got its charter the same week. The item that came in hot this afternoon: Jordi Visser says the required reading is Scott Bessent's speech and Mohamed El-Erian's New York Times op-ed calling it remarkably important. Bessent's line, verbatim: digital assets, stablecoins, tokenization, and new payment systems will help shape the future of money, and the United States should not consign itself to the sidelines while that future is built elsewhere. Matt Hougan distills it as principle three, America will write the rules of the next economy. Visser's practical conclusion is that with agentic commerce coming you can no longer afford to have no view on crypto.

The follow-through was concrete. Per the Market list, Circle received final OCC approval (the national bank regulator) for Circle National Trust, a federally regulated trust bank positioned to manage USDC reserves. And Lark flags Fed chair Kevin Warsh adding Marc Andreessen to a Fed task force, his blunt read being that the liquidity people are now the crypto people.

5. The Ethereum setup is stacking, and the trigger is a daily close above $1,810. Lark's trade is precise: long from $1,750 with a stop at $1,700, a daily close above $1,810 validates a double-bottom breakout, and the target is $2,100 near the 200-day. Tom Lee bought 20,000 more ETH into it. The institutional case behind the chart came from John Gillan, six years a BlackRock VP on the Aladdin platform: BitMine and SharpLink together hold about 7 percent of ETH's supply throwing off $400 to 500 million a year in staking rewards, the new EthLabs org is backed by both, and Standard Chartered's Geoff Kendrick told him ETH reaches $40,000 by 2030. Gillan's own nearer call is a resolution above $5,000.

Keep the two honest counterweights in frame. David Hoffman, one of Ethereum's loudest longtime voices, sold his ETH and debated Gillan on it. And Gillan concedes the Clarity Act has drifted toward protecting the banks, with BNY's $50 trillion custody franchise and Jamie Dimon actively fighting public chains. The bull case here is real and it is also crowded with people paid to believe it.

6. The rotation chatter got loud: out of AI software, into physical AI and the beaten-up middle. Trader Mayne's one-liner captures the mood, AI software has run its course and physical AI owns the next five to ten years. The specifics were everywhere today. Per the Market list, the photonics complex is being bottom-called with names attached: Lumentum sold out roughly two years, likely into 2029, Applied Optoelectronics holding a $471 million-per-month revenue projection for late 2027, AXT controlling about 40 percent of indium phosphide substrate supply (the base material for optical chips), and Lee_Trades counting a 40 percent swing off the panic lows. The same list ran the humanoid-robot pairings map, Mitsubishi with Highlanders by 2027 joining Figure with BMW, Apptronik with Mercedes, and Optimus with Tesla.

The neocloud crowd is pressing. Investor wliang holds 86,000 shares of $WYFI at a $34.50 average and is targeting 100,000, likes $NBIS, $IREN, $WULF, and $CIFR, and calls the TeraWulf-Anthropic deal validation of the whole thesis. Lark is adding Marathon at its 200-day and notes Galaxy owns the biggest single US data center at 1.66 gigawatts while still being priced like a crypto company. Orford's caution belongs next to all of it: IREN is still becoming an AI infrastructure company, capital-intensive and unproven, and that description fits half the basket.

  • $JTO (Jito). Per 0xResearch, its JTX self-custodial trading front end launches Monday July 14, moving Jito from backend plumbing to the interface layer where Solana interfaces already beat Binance's best pricing by 7 to 8 basis points. A dated catalyst on a beaten-down sector.

Psychology. Read today's two crowds side by side. In AI hardware, the money threw $200 billion at a $26.5 billion book, JPMorgan hung a flag on its building, and even the bulls were exasperated, WisemanCap's "everyone is long META now" being the sound of a trade getting heavy. In crypto, Cowen counts crypto YouTube at about 400,000 views a day against 3 to 4 million in 2021, apathy deep enough that the marginal seller may simply be done. Euphoria in one room, boredom in the other, and the interesting fact is that the boredom trade is the one where Lark the bull and Cowen the bear now agree on the plan, accumulate through the second half with defined levels.

So the discipline check tonight is about which impulse you feel. If it is the urge to chase Meta up 6 percent or buy the Hynix open, notice that the entry only feels safe because everyone else already took it, and the SpaceX debut's fade from plus 17 to plus 7 shows how day-one froth resolves. If it is the urge to look away from crypto because it has been dead money, notice that both camps just handed you levels, $64,000 and $1,810, with stops and dates attached. Trade the plan that exists at the price nobody is excited about, and size the crowded one like the crowd can leave.
