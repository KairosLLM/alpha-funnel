Micron was worth more than Berkshire Hathaway on Wednesday and had shed nearly half a trillion dollars by Friday, and the crash needed no thesis to break, just a rate hike in Seoul and a cheap model out of China

The memory trade round-tripped an entire euphoria in a single week, and the round-trip is the lesson. The demand behind Micron is still measured in gigawatts and sold out through 2027, but the leverage stacked on top of it is what showed you what it does on a bad headline.

This was the week the biggest trade in the market got a real test, and it was not a test of the story. It was a test of how much leverage the crowd had piled onto a story that was mostly right. Here is the arc, the bull and bear cases, and what to do with it.

The arc of the week. On Tuesday the memory blowoff was still going, with Micron briefly worth more than Berkshire Hathaway and the crowd calling for $2,000, while SanDisk printed the highest weekly relative-strength reading ever recorded on any stock. By Wednesday it had round-tripped, with Micron breaking below $900 in a single session and the money that left the chips going straight into Apple.

Then the cause arrived, and it was not the AI thesis cracking. Per Jose Najarro, the Bank of Korea raised its base rate 25 basis points to 2.75 percent, its first hike in three and a half years, which lifted the borrowing cost on the Korean-financed fabs that SK Hynix and Samsung are building, sent the KOSPI down 7 percent, and dragged the whole memory complex with it.

The key detail is that the hike does not touch the demand curve for high-bandwidth memory or the fabs Micron builds with US money, so the sharpest fall was in the names with the local rate exposure, not the ones with the best long-run position. By Friday the momentum unwind had turned historic, with Micron having lost $459 billion of market value in fourteen trading days, per the Polymarket list.

The final shove came from an unrelated direction. A cheap Chinese model, Kimi K3, topped the coding benchmarks and set off a fear that AI spending would slow, and the memory names took the hit even though, as the AI memo this week lays out, the model is not actually the cost bomb the tape treated it as. Two exogenous shocks, a Seoul rate decision and a Beijing model release, took apart a trade that had nothing to do with either.

The bull case, stated cleanly. The demand is real and it is contracted. Per Morgan Stanley by way of Shay Boloor, Micron's gross margins could reach 90 percent in 2027, supported by sixteen supply agreements with floor pricing and high-bandwidth memory that is sold out through 2027, with the HBM4 for Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform already shipping since March at roughly twice the ramp pace of the prior generation. The scale behind it is not a slogan, with data-center capacity set to add more than 100 gigawatts by 2030 against over $5 trillion of total spending.

The confirming tell is the calmest company in the chain. Per Najarro, ASML raised its four-year guidance and said its lithography capacity grows 30 percent next year and another 30 percent after, with 2027 already sold out, the bottleneck that stretches the cycle rather than ending it. When the pick-and-shovel supplier at the very top of the chain is booked three years forward while the crowd panics over a quarter-point hike in Korea, the panic is the opportunity and not the signal.

The bear case, taken seriously. The round-trip itself is the warning. A stock that is worth more than Berkshire on Tuesday and below $900 on Wednesday is not being valued, it is being traded on leverage, and the leverage is the problem. The selloff was a momentum-factor unwind from the 100th percentile of positioning, the worst such stretch in 27 years, which means the buyers at the top were mechanical and forced, and mechanical buyers become mechanical sellers the instant volatility turns.

The sharper worry is who is short and what they know. Michael Burry turned bearish on Micron in early July and the stock fell 18 percent, per Stocks with Josh, and the professionals are already shorting the next Chinese memory IPO before it even lists. Per threadguy, CXMT, described as China's SK Hynix, goes public July 27 and is already trading as a pre-IPO perpetual at an implied $470 billion against an $80 billion offering, and the two largest positions are both professional shorts. When the smart money is short the shiny new memory name before its first trade, the supply side of this cycle is not the sure thing the bulls make it sound.

What to do with it. The level is the referee, not your conviction. Per Stocks with Josh, Micron needs to reclaim and hold $861 to confirm the bottom and $805 is the line that breaks it, so let the price prove the thesis rather than catching the exact low on a Monday. If you want the memory cycle, the cleaner expressions are the ones the leverage cannot round-trip, ASML booked solid through 2027, or SK Hynix at 7.6 times forward earnings against Micron's 11.7 if you want the direct exposure a third cheaper.

The discipline is to own the bottleneck and avoid the leverage. The 2x memory funds and the CXMT pre-IPO perps are the instruments that just showed you what they do on a bad headline, and the semiconductor earnings landing this week are the referee for the whole argument. Keep the theme, which stays intact, and refuse the leverage, because the two are not the same thing.

Psychology. Read the week as one emotional round-trip. On Tuesday the crowd owned Micron above Berkshire and was calling for $2,000, and that is euphoria wearing the costume of conviction, and by Wednesday the same crowd was liquidated below $900 on a Korean rate hike it could not have named the day before. Nothing about the AI story changed in those 48 hours. What changed is that the leverage got a reason to unwind, and once it started, the 2x ETFs and the pre-IPO perps did the rest, carrying out the very people who felt most certain at the top.

The tell of the week is who stayed calm and who got carried out. ASML, the company that actually controls the supply, raised its guidance and said 2027 is sold out, while the leveraged holders got liquidated on 25 basis points and the smart money was already short the next Chinese memory IPO before it lists. The people closest to the real economics were the least rattled, and the people furthest out on borrowed exposure were the ones getting stopped out at the lows.

So the discipline check into the new week is to ask yourself which side of that you were on. If the round-trip in Micron made you feel like the AI trade is broken, you were positioned in the leverage, not the theme, because the theme did not move. Separate the two before Monday. Let Micron prove it can hold $861 and let the chip earnings print before you decide the panic was the top or the gift, and own the sold-out bottleneck rather than the 2x expression that round-trips on a headline out of Seoul. The story was right all week. The only question the market actually asked was how much you had borrowed to believe it.
