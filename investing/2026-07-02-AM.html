Micron got flushed, and for once every desk is buying the same dip

The memory pullback is being bought hard, Bitcoin is bleeding under $60K, and Strategy just traded below its own stack for the first time ever.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Yesterday was the flush. This morning is the argument about what it meant, and the loudest voices have already answered it: they bought.

Into the open. Cash markets are open today and then closed tomorrow, Friday July 3, for Independence Day (observed, since the 4th is a Saturday), so today is a pre-holiday session with thin afternoon liquidity. Two real catalysts land in that shortened window. The June jobs report was pulled forward to today ahead of the holiday, so the number hits into a market that is already jumpy about whether the Fed hikes at all this year. And Tesla ($TSLA) reports Q2 deliveries, where the estimate gap is enormous: the Street sits near 406k against Troy Teslike at 466k. The level that matters is $425. Watch whether $MU reclaims its 21-day and whether the $SOX steadies after yesterday's 6%-plus gash, watch $BTC and the $60K line, and note oil stayed soft with WTI near $68. Size for a holiday tape, not a trend day.

1. The memory dip is the most crowded buy on the board. This is the one to lead with, and not because I am sure it is right. It is because I have rarely seen this many independent people land on the exact same trade at the exact same time, which is a tell either way. After the semis got taken to the woodshed yesterday, the Stocks list lit up: Marcos Milla calling $MU "under $1,000 equals smash buy" while warning leverage could still shake out 30% in three days, Milk Road Macro adding with a "$MU to $4,000" target, Melvin booking a 144% gain and staying long. $MU closed below its 21-day for the first time since April, which the chartists are framing as a momentum reset, not a top. The fundamental spine under all of it is the strongest part. In the inbox, Markos laid out the HBM math that actually matters: efficiency gains are running around 5% a year and decelerating, you would need a 25% jump on top of the 30% already priced to flip 2027-28 into surplus, and CXL and flash tiers are additive not substitutive because the hot decode set stays pinned to HBM by latency. His line is the keeper: no GPU generation has ever shipped with less memory per system, 80GB to 288GB and climbing, so this is Jevons paradox in silicon. CEO Sanjay Mehrotra is selling the same durability story with receipts, 16 take-or-pay agreements running 2026 to 2030, 14 of them carrying a $100B cumulative revenue minimum against $22B in customer cash deposits. Even the Reddit desk reads the pullback as healthy consolidation after names ran two to three times, not a cycle peak.

Here is the tell that makes me take it seriously rather than fade it. Kobeissi flagged that retail is now just 6% of Mag 7 volume, a four-year low and down 79% from the April 2025 peak, with the money rotating out of the megacaps and straight into semiconductors. That is not a froth signal, that is a rotation. My read: the dip is buyable, but the crowding is real, so scale in and keep the 21-day reclaim as your confirmation rather than chasing the first green candle.

2. Marvell is the "next bottleneck" everyone is pre-positioning into. Milk Road's follow-on to the memory trade is $MRVL, and the thesis is clean: the bottleneck arc runs compute to memory to connectivity, and Marvell owns the connectivity leg. The CXL memory-fabric market goes from $1.3B to $7-10B by 2030, Marvell bought XConn for $540M to lead it, and management guides CXL revenue to $1B in FY27 and $2B in FY28. Custom silicon is the bigger number: 18 programs, 12 of them at top-four hyperscalers, an FY27 guide of $11.5B up 40%, and a path to $10B-plus by FY29. The risk is not the story, it is the price and the concentration, Amazon is 30-40% of revenue and $AVGO is coming for the same sockets. This is a position-builder's name, not a chase.

3. Bitcoin is risk capital squared, and the AI vacuum is the reason. $BTC is grinding lower, closed below its monthly 50 and weekly 200, and keeps refusing to hold $60K. The datapoint that stops you cold is on the Crypto list: Strategy ($MSTR) just traded below the value of its own Bitcoin for the first time ever. Michael Saylor holds more than 800,000 BTC and the stock is down about 80%, and he is out talking his book with a coherent story, that the "AI black hole" pulled $10-20B out of crypto and $500B into the AI IPO complex, that BTC dominance is back near 70% from 41% in 2021, and that Bitcoin is now undervalued. The bears do not disagree on the mechanism, they just are not waiting around: Novogratz says crypto "lost its sponsorship," and David Hoffman sold his $ETH as Ethereum's smart-contract dominance fell from over 90% to 60%. Levels: $60K is the line, a final flush to $50-55K is on the table, and you do not get a confirmed bottom until a higher high above $67K and a reclaim of the 200-day. Stocks with Josh is buying that fear with a weekly death cross as his signal, a $56K target zone and a deeper add near $44K. The one alt with a real bid is $SOL, where Ignas (the SIMD-547 base-fee burn turning it deflationary), andyyy, and Nebraskangooner independently called a bottom. If Bitcoin pukes again Solana goes with it, so it is a lead-dog trade, not a hedge.

4. The macro regime quietly changed, and almost nobody is pricing it. The sharpest macro take of the morning came from Eric Wallerstein: the Warsh Fed kills "kangaroo macro," the print-to-print obsession where every data release is a trade. Warsh pledged 2% inflation eight times in one press conference and bond yields fell, and the new game is cycles and trends over reacting to a single jobs number, especially with post-Covid data reliability degraded. That reframes today's jobs report as noise inside a trend, not a verdict. The risk-off tells are still worth respecting: Kalshi prices a 50% chance of a Fed rate hike this year, and Bank of America's peak-indicator model has 7 of 10 triggered. Watch funding and liquidity conditions as the accelerant, not the headline number.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $IREN, a two-sided setup, name both sides. The governance red flag first: the markets desk flagged the Roberts co-CEOs granted 18.2M RSUs combined, roughly $400M and 5.09% dilution, with the performance hurdles removed, against a share count that went from 100M to 357M and a $6B ATM. That is the kind of thing that caps a multiple. The other side, from Chris: the 4-hour RSI is the most oversold in a year, and the prior two times it got here the stock rallied roughly 90% and then 130%, with price now backtesting broken downtrend resistance as support. A trade, not a marriage.
  • $PLTR, the anti-frontier-lab bet. Palantir was up around 7.5% expanding its Nvidia partnership for US-government AI systems. Alex Karp's pitch, quoted at length on the Financial Markets list, is that enterprises "pay for tokens that create no value" and are training their own replacements by running confidential data through frontier models, so the winners are the ones who let you own your compute, weights, and alpha. Whatever you think of Karp, that narrative is a real tailwind for the on-prem, data-sovereignty names.
  • $META, treat the "compute pivot" with suspicion. The bullish frame is Meta selling excess AI capacity like a neocloud. The skeptical read, which I lean toward, is that Google throttled Meta's Gemini compute, Meta is burning around $261M a month and just locked $40B across $NBIS, $CRWV, and Cursor, so "excess to sell" and "compute-constrained" cannot both be true. Reconcile the CoreWeave-scale contracts before you trust the story.
