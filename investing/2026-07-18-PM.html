The chip selloff made it feel like a crash, but outside of tech nearly every index is sitting near a record high, and the smart money spent the week buying the dip into a huge earnings stretch

The Nasdaq is off about 7 percent from its peak while the equal-weight index barely moved, which reframes the week as a tech unwind rather than a market break. Alphabet, Tesla, and ServiceNow all report in the days ahead, robotics stocks are the new speculation, and a consortium digital dollar is quietly rewiring how US debt gets financed.

MacroStocksCrypto

The tape that scared everyone on Friday looks different from a Saturday distance. The damage is real but narrow, and the coming week is when the fundamentals get a vote. Here is the week ahead, then five stories.

Week ahead. This is the week the earnings do the talking. Per the Market list, the Nasdaq sits about 7.2 percent off its high while the equal-weight S&P is down barely 1.4 percent and the broad index is only about 2 percent from a record, so the selloff was a tech-momentum unwind, not a market collapse. Alphabet, Tesla, Intel, and ServiceNow all report over the coming days, per the Stocks list, with Alphabet the clear swing factor. Only one of the 23 largest US companies carries an overbought reading, and it is Apple, which tells you where the crowd already ran. Korea's market reopens Monday after missing the whole move, and the memory names spend the week trying to prove Friday was a bottom.

1. The people who manage money bought the dip, and Alphabet is the name they bought. The weekend's clearest signal is accumulation, not panic. Per a Milk Road paid update, their analysts added Alphabet near $372 during the selloff on the case that it is the cheapest of the Magnificent Seven at about 22 times forward earnings while owning the entire AI stack, from the Gemini model to its own chips to distribution.

The fundamentals under that bet are strong on the numbers they cite. Per the Stocks list, Alphabet's cloud revenue grew 63 percent to $20 billion with its backlog doubling to $462 billion, its custom chips now sell to Anthropic and Meta, and Apple is reportedly paying roughly a billion dollars a year to use Gemini in the new Siri. This is a promotional source talking its own book, so treat the entry price as marketing, but Alphabet reporting into a beaten-down tape is the single most important print of the week.

2. Robotics became the new speculation, and one name is the cleanest way to play it. The rotation out of memory is looking for its next story, and this weekend it was humanoids. Per Milk Road, the pitch is that Nvidia and Micron are the safe picks-and-shovels, with a humanoid robot carrying roughly ten times the memory of a self-driving car, while the pure play is Agility Robotics, going public through the Churchill Capital shell to trade as AGLT.

The specifics are what set Agility apart from the vaporware. Its Digit robot has logged more than 65,000 real-world hours across nine customers including Toyota, holds over $300 million in multi-year contracts, and runs a roughly 75 percent US-based supply chain, which the host contrasts with Tesla's still-undeployed Optimus. The watchlist behind it is XPeng, Hyundai as the owner of Boston Dynamics, and the parts makers Schaeffler and Harmonic Drive. This is a heavily promoted pick from a subscription funnel, so the enthusiasm is the product, but a SPAC-listed humanoid with real contracts is a genuine thing to track rather than a meme.

3. The memory bull case got sharper even as the stocks bled. The stock everyone is fighting over is still Micron. Per the People list, Morgan Stanley argues Micron's gross margins could reach 90 percent in 2027, backed by 16 supply agreements with floor pricing and high-bandwidth memory that is sold out through 2027, with the HBM4 for Nvidia's Vera Rubin already shipping since March.

The comparison worth holding is against the Korean leader. Per the Stocks list roundup, SK Hynix owns about 56 percent of the high-bandwidth memory market to Micron's 23 percent and locked a Nvidia contract covering 70 percent of the memory going into Vera Rubin, yet it trades at 7.6 times forward earnings against Micron's 11.7, a third cheaper. Micron's counterweight is US policy, with CHIPS Act grants funding a plan to build 40 percent of American DRAM at home. The whole data-center buildout adds more than 100 gigawatts of capacity and over $5 trillion of spending by 2030, so the demand is not the question, the entry price is.

4. A consortium digital dollar and a crypto law are quietly rewiring the plumbing. The most consequential crypto story is not a token, it is legislation and a stablecoin. Per an a16z op-ed by former Congressman Patrick McHenry, the CLARITY Act moving through Congress is the most important technology law since the 1996 Telecommunications Act, the first non-crisis financial framework since the late 1990s, and a16z has a direct interest in it passing.

The market mechanism underneath it is the part to watch. Per Lark Davis, a consortium of BlackRock, Google, Visa, and many others is launching a digital dollar whose deposits, under the GENIUS Act, must sit in US Treasuries, so every coin issued becomes automatic demand for government debt against a $40 trillion balance and more than a trillion in annual interest. His specific roster and the coin's name are unverified and the framing is promotional, so treat the details loosely, but the direction is real, that stablecoins are becoming a debt-financing tool dressed as a payment upgrade.

5. Bitcoin is range-bound and the Trump memecoin is the weekend's cautionary tale. The crypto majors are stuck. Per the Crypto list, Mike Novogratz sees Bitcoin trapped between $60,000 and $80,000 for the rest of the year absent a real catalyst, long-term holders now sit on a record 79 percent of supply, and the four-year-cycle crowd still points at an October bottom.

The lesson of the week came from a token with a name attached. Per Lark Davis, Trump's memecoin has fallen more than 90 percent from its peak, leaving retail buyers with an estimated $4 billion in losses while Trump reportedly booked over a billion, the same asset producing opposite outcomes depending on who got in when. Those figures are the creator's estimates rather than audited, so hold them at arm's length, but the shape of it is the oldest one in the market, insiders distributing to a crowd that arrived late.

Single names to track.

  • $GOOGL (Alphabet). Bought on the dip near $372 as the cheapest Magnificent Seven name at about 22 times forward earnings, reporting this week with cloud up 63 percent and a $462 billion backlog, per Milk Road and the Stocks list.
  • $MU (Micron). Gross margins seen reaching 90 percent by 2027 on 16 floor-priced supply deals with memory sold out through 2027, up 197 percent on the year, per the People list.
  • AGLT (Agility Robotics). Going public via the Churchill Capital shell, with its Digit robot logging 65,000 hours across nine customers and over $300 million in contracts, per Milk Road.
  • $GLXY (Galaxy Digital). Fell 9 percent on Friday but landed a $500 million credit line and a Russell 1000 spot, the dual crypto and data-center play, per the Milk Road update.
  • BTC (Bitcoin). Range-bound between $60,000 and $80,000 with long-term holders at a record 79 percent of supply and the cycle low modeled for October, per the Crypto list.

Psychology. The weekend is a study in how a narrow move feels like a broad one. The Nasdaq is off 7 percent and it felt like the end of the world, while the equal-weight index barely flinched and Apple is the only mega-cap that is actually overbought, which means the pain was concentrated in exactly the crowded momentum names that had run the furthest. Per the Market list, a lot of the selling was mechanical, vol-targeting funds cutting exposure by formula as volatility spiked, not investors deciding the story was broken.

The tell of a top is not a selloff, it is the froth that survives one. This weekend brought a wave of new leveraged single-theme AI funds filed for launch, a robotics SPAC being pitched as the next Micron, and a presidential memecoin that cost retail an estimated $4 billion, and all of that speculation is still very much alive. When the leveraged product filings and the meme launches keep coming through a 7 percent drawdown, the excess has not been cleared, it has just been repriced.

So the discipline into earnings week is to separate the accumulation from the animal spirits. Buying Alphabet at 22 times forward earnings into a beaten tape is a different act than chasing a humanoid SPAC or a memecoin, even though both are called buying the dip. Let Alphabet and the memory names print real numbers before you decide Friday was the bottom, own the businesses whose demand is measured in gigawatts and supply agreements, and remember that the mechanical selling that felt like conviction can reverse just as mechanically the moment the volatility fades.
