The memory trade is cracking in Seoul at the exact hour SK Hynix starts trading here, and oil, credit, and a short-seller are all leaning the same way

SK Hynix fell 10 percent overnight and Korea tripped its circuit breaker while Iran and Washington argue over whether Hormuz is open, oil sits up but contained, and Goldman warns the hyperscaler bond market is choking. Micron has a clean line to defend, semiconductor earnings start Tuesday, and Michael Burry is short the whole thing.

MacroStocksCrypto

The weekend did not resolve the war and this morning did not resolve the memory trade. Both arrive at the open as live risks, and for once the bear has company. Here is the pre-open picture, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. Oil is the first thing paying for the weekend. US crude is up 4 to 5 percent after Iran again declared the Strait of Hormuz closed and the US again declared it open, coordinating roughly 20 tankers through overnight, per the Market and Kalshi lists. The level to watch is a gap at $83.20 on crude futures, and Brent is still under $80, so this is a spike, not yet a crisis. Gold lost $4,100 as the dollar jumped, a risk-off-into-dollar move rather than a classic panic, per the Financial Markets list.

Equity futures lean slightly red, and several traders are already calling it a war rug-pull that closes green. The bigger tell is in Asia. SK Hynix fell 10 percent in Seoul, the KOSPI dropped 3 percent, and the exchange activated a sidecar to halt program selling, per the Financial Markets list. The calendar is loaded: SK Hynix starts regular-way $SKHY trading here today, the $SKHX 2x long and $SKHZ short ETFs launch Tuesday at 4am, and June CPI (the inflation print that settles the July 29 Fed debate) lands Tuesday morning, per the People list.

1. The memory supercycle is showing its first real cracks, and two of them are brand new. For a week the memory trade shrugged off everything. This morning it is not shrugging. Beyond the 10 percent SK Hynix drop and the Korean circuit breaker, two fresh datapoints cut against the "infinite demand" story, per the Stocks list. Murata is cutting prices on its MLCC capacitors, and Nvidia dropped the 800-volt direct-current requirement from its Rubin Ultra rack, a near-term headwind for the Vertiv and silicon-carbide names that rallied on exactly that spec.

Neither breaks the multi-year thesis, but both puncture the idea that nothing can go wrong. Micron now has a line to defend rather than a launchpad. Per Stocks with Josh, $MU failed to close above 996 over the weekend, so the upside target at 1089 is off the table until it reclaims 996, the stop is 950, and losing that opens 900 and then 861. The whole complex trades off whether Korea's selling washes into the US session or exhausts itself into the $SKHY debut.

2. The bear finally has a real thesis, and it is about debt, not just valuation. The fresh crack this morning is in credit. Per the Financial Markets list, the 2-year Treasury yield pushed above 4.23 percent, its highest since February 2025, and Goldman's trading desk is circulating a note about carnage in the hyperscaler bond market, warning it is very difficult to fund another $360 billion over the next year when the hyperscalers need roughly $1 trillion annually. Banks are sitting on their largest-ever net short in corporate bonds. And Michael Burry's AI shorts surfaced in the filings, roughly $456 million against $PLTR, $187 million against $NVDA, and about $100 million against the $SOXX semiconductor ETF.

The efficiency side of the bear case got louder too. A sharp Reddit thread lays out the pattern that keeps denting the Nvidia capex story: DeepSeek erased $589 billion of Nvidia market cap in a day last year on a cheap-training claim, GLM-5.2 is a 744-billion-parameter open model the maker says trained entirely on Chinese Huawei Ascend chips, and a robotics vision model matched a Meta model at a twentieth of the size. The honest counter is Jevons, that cheaper compute has so far expanded demand rather than shrinking it, so this is a genuine two-sided fight rather than a settled short. Either way, the multiples now have a coherent thing to be afraid of.

3. Semiconductor earnings start Tuesday, and they will referee the whole argument. The week's real judge is the tape from the chip names themselves. Per Jose Najarro, $AEHR (Aehr Test Systems) reports Tuesday after the close, $ASML Wednesday before the open, and $TSM (Taiwan Semiconductor) Thursday after the close. Aehr is the fresh story, having raised its full-year bookings guide above $92 million on a record $41 million production order from a lead hyperscale AI customer, with new silicon-photonics follow-ons and memory flagged as an emerging customer.

The other two set the tone for the whole trade. Najarro is explicitly not a buyer of $ASML at a forward price-to-earnings near 46 against a decade median around 32, though he stays long-term bullish, and he calls $TSM the swing factor, watching its capital-spending guide and whether it lifts 2026 revenue growth from above 30 percent toward 35 to 40. Strong TSM guidance pulls up $AMD, $AVGO, $MRVL, and $NVDA. A cautious one confirms the bears.

4. Opendoor is the cleanest new single-name idea, and a law nobody read handed it a gift. Per a detailed Reddit breakdown, the new housing law that markets treated as a blunt ban on institutional buyers actually blocks large investors from accumulating 350-plus single-family homes while expressly exempting homes that are newly built, renovated, or converted for sale and not held as rentals. That exemption is a statutory lane cut almost precisely around the $OPEN (Opendoor) business model, which turns inventory rather than building a rental empire, and it kills the cleanest regulatory bear case against the stock.

The operating turn underneath it is real, whatever the ugly headline numbers say. Homes purchased rose 45 percent quarter over quarter, Opendoor signed more than 5,000 acquisition contracts (double the prior quarter and the most since 2022), inventory sitting longer than 120 days fell from 33 percent to 10 percent, and fixed operating costs dropped from $39 million to $33 million. Management guided to roughly 25 percent sequential revenue growth and about breakeven adjusted EBITDA, targeting positive adjusted net income on a forward basis by the end of 2026.

5. Robinhood Chain flipped Hyperliquid over the weekend, and the stock has decoupled from crypto. The crypto-equity crossover keeps compounding. Per the Crypto list, Robinhood Chain overtook Hyperliquid in 24-hour decentralized-exchange volume, $433 million against $296 million, on more than 17 million transactions and about $250 million in deposits, and $HOOD is up 80 percent off its May lows and no longer tracking Bitcoin. A Substack breakdown adds the mechanics, that the chain runs on Arbitrum's technology with Ether as its gas token and is aimed squarely at tokenized real-world assets, with Robinhood's Vlad framing a listing on it as the new Binance listing. Watch whether the on-chain volume is real adoption or launch-week momentum, the same test the daily tape keeps setting.

Psychology. This is the first morning in a while where the tape is handing the reader more than one honest warning at once, and the test is whether the crowd can hear any of them. The memory complex is melting down in Seoul with a circuit breaker tripped, yet the loudest voices on the Stocks list are still posting $2,200 Micron and $3,250 SanDisk targets, and the reflex to call the oil spike a war rug-pull and buy the dip is already forming before the cash open. That reflex has paid every time this month, and that is exactly what makes it dangerous the one morning the cracks are real.

The countersignal is who is on the other side. Michael Burry is short Palantir, Nvidia, and the semis into this, Goldman is warning its own clients about the hyperscaler bond market, and Nvidia just quietly pulled a spec that a whole basket of stocks had rallied on. When the crowd is raising price targets while the credit market chokes and the early money builds shorts, the odds that this dip is different go up, not down.

So the discipline check into the open is to respect the possibility that today is not a rug-pull to buy but a first crack to size for. If you are tempted to add memory names on the SK Hynix debut or chase the oil trade, remember Micron has a line at 996 and a stop at 950 for a reason, and CPI lands tomorrow. Let $MU prove it can hold 950 and let oil prove the $83.20 gap before you decide the crowd was right to keep buying.
