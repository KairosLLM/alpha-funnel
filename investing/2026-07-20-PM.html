The broad tape was weak and Alphabet is carving a bear flag into Wednesday, but the neocloud names exploded higher on a wall of new contracts, and oil round-tripped from over $90 to under $80 in a single session

IREN jumped 16 percent on $2.8 billion of fresh AI-cloud deals while the sector printed one signing after another. Oil crashed below $80 on a reported Iran ceasefire proposal, gold is stuck in a two-sided fight, and the whole market is holding its breath for one earnings print in 48 hours.

MacroStocksCrypto

A Monday of crosscurrents. The index went nowhere, the compute-infrastructure names went vertical, and the war that pushed oil over $90 this morning pushed it back under $80 by the afternoon. Here is the session recap, then five stories.

Session recap. The broad tape was heavy but the AI-infrastructure trade was on fire underneath it. Per the Market list, the neoclouds bounced hard on a run of new deals while Alphabet carved a weekly bear flag into Wednesday's earnings and Oracle sat in its largest drawdown in more than 25 years. The war whipsawed the whole session, with oil crashing below $80 on a reported Iranian ceasefire proposal after trading over $90 in the morning, gasoline back over $4 at the pump, and SpaceX printing a new all-time low, per the Polymarket list. Volatility sat calm near 17.65, Jim Cramer declared tech simply too hard, and everything is now queued behind the Alphabet print.

1. The neocloud names exploded on a wall of new contracts. The trade of the day was compute infrastructure. Per the Market list, IREN jumped as much as 16 percent after signing $2.8 billion of new multi-year AI-cloud contracts and raising its year-end run-rate target above $4 billion, with 85 percent of that already under contract, customers prepaying roughly 45 percent of the GPU cost, and $7.6 billion of cash worth about 60 percent of its market value.

It was not one name, it was the whole sector. Per the same tape, Hut 8 landed a fifteen-year $9.8 billion deal, CleanSpark signed its first high-performance-computing deal at $6.6 billion, Nebius booked $1 billion with Reflection AI on top of 684 percent revenue growth, and TeraWulf inked a roughly $19 billion agreement with Anthropic, while BlackRock led $12 billion of financing for Meta's Texas data centers. The read-through, per the Stocks list, is that the capex-collapse fear that drove last week's selloff keeps running into signed contracts, and the customers are paying up front.

2. Oil round-tripped in a day and gold is fighting itself. The macro whipsaw was in commodities. Per the Polymarket list, oil crashed below $80 after Iran reportedly proposed a ten-day ceasefire to revive an interim deal, a violent reversal from the morning's move above $90, even as CENTCOM completed its ninth straight night of strikes and the Houthis declared a naval blockade on Saudi Arabia. A war being fought and de-escalated in the same day is producing a fuel price that cannot decide what it is.

Gold is running its own split-screen. Per VirtualBacon, gold has broken its 40-week average at 4,500 and entered a technical bear market with downside eventually below 3,000, pressured by the same commodity margin-call selling. Yet per Lark Davis, China's central bank bought 480,000 ounces in June, its biggest purchase since 2023 and its twentieth straight month of buying, stepping into an 11.6 percent price drop. The chartists see a breakdown and the largest strategic buyer sees a discount, and only one of them is trading for the next quarter.

3. Alphabet on Wednesday is still the referee, and it just showed its hand on chips. The print the whole week hinges on is 48 hours out. Per the Market list, Alphabet is developing a new in-house chip called Frozen V2 that reportedly runs its Gemini models up to ten times more efficiently than its latest processors, a bullish signal for its own compute even as the stock carves a bear flag into the report.

The number hanging over it is the bill. Per the Polymarket list, Goldman Sachs warned that the $5.3 trillion of AI spending planned by Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Alphabet through 2030 could start to pressure the bond market, the macro version of the same question the neocloud deals keep answering with a yes. Alphabet's capital-spending guide on Wednesday is where the market finds out whether that $5.3 trillion is a promise or a warning.

4. The memory bull got a huge buyback number, and the bottleneck may be power. The single-name memory story got sharper. Per the Polymarket list, UBS expects Micron to generate more than $400 billion of free cash flow through 2028 and to repurchase over 40 percent of its shares once its buyback restriction expires in December 2026, though the desks still flag SK Hynix as the cleaner way to own the same cycle, per the People list.

The constraint underneath the chips is electricity. Per this breakdown, Navitas makes the gallium-nitride and silicon-carbide power chips that feed Nvidia's move to 800-volt, one-megawatt racks, with about $450 million of design wins as data-center power demand heads past 1,000 terawatt-hours by the end of the decade. It is unprofitable and a design win is not an order, but the theme is real, that the AI trade is running into a physical wall of power and heat, and the same New Mexico permit fight that just hit Bloom Energy is the proof.

5. Crypto is stuck in a box while the contrarians circle Ethereum. The majors are going nowhere. Per the Crypto list, Mike Novogratz says Bitcoin is stuck between $65,000 and $85,000 and needs both the CLARITY Act to pass and the Fed to start cutting to break higher, with one of those two legs currently broken, while stablecoin supply fell another $1.24 billion and public companies added just 101.5 Bitcoin last week.

The bolder call is on the most hated major. Per Lark Davis, Ethereum is down about 65 percent with its weekly momentum reading at the third-lowest level ever recorded, a setup he compares to the June 2022 low rather than the final bottom, and the Ether-to-Bitcoin ratio just printed a bullish crossover that last preceded a 132 percent stretch of outperformance. The catalyst he points to is Robinhood Chain, where bridged Ether jumped seventyfold in a week, so the thesis is that the rotation into Ethereum simply has not happened yet. Those are one promoter's indicators, so weigh them as a setup and not a certainty, but universal hatred of an asset is at least the right precondition.

Single names to track.

  • $IREN. Jumped 16 percent on $2.8 billion of new AI-cloud contracts, run-rate target above $4 billion with 85 percent under contract and customers prepaying 45 percent of GPU cost, per the Market list.
  • $GOOGL (Alphabet). Reports Wednesday as the referee for the AI-capex debate, carving a bear flag but developing a Frozen V2 chip said to run Gemini ten times more efficiently, per the Market list.
  • $MU (Micron). UBS sees more than $400 billion of free cash flow through 2028 and a buyback of over 40 percent of shares once the restriction lifts, per the Polymarket list.
  • $NVTS (Navitas). The power-semi bottleneck play feeding Nvidia's 800-volt racks, roughly $450 million of design wins but still unprofitable, per this breakdown.
  • ETH (Ethereum). Down 65 percent with the third-lowest weekly momentum reading ever and a bullish ratio crossover, the contrarian setup, per Lark Davis.

Psychology. Watch how fast the crowd flips. This morning the story was oil over $90 breaking the disinflation trade, and by the afternoon oil was under $80 on a ceasefire headline and the neoclouds were up double digits, the same market swinging between panic and greed inside a single session. When the tape moves that violently on headlines, the temptation is to trade every swing, and the cost of doing that is usually your patience and your capital both.

The tell of the moment is the irony nobody is naming out loud. Two weeks ago the market sold every AI stock because a cheap Chinese model supposedly proved the buildout was overbuilt, and this weekend that same model, Kimi K3, had to pause its subscriptions because it ran out of compute, per the Market list. The bear case and the bull case were the same fact read in opposite directions, and the neocloud deals signed today are the market quietly admitting which reading was right. Michael Burry's line that 95 percent of investors have no idea what they own fits a tape where IREN and its peers just proved the demand while their stocks were still being sold as broken.

So the discipline into Alphabet on Wednesday is to let the print settle the argument instead of front-running it with leverage. The neocloud contracts and the Micron cash-flow math say the demand is real and paid for, but Jim Cramer calling tech too hard and oil whipping $10 in a day say the price is going to keep lurching regardless. Own the names whose customers are prepaying, respect that a single earnings report can override every chart on your screen, and keep enough cash that the next violent swing is your entry rather than your undoing.
