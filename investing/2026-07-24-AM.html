Intel just posted its strongest growth in fifteen years and jumped double digits after hours, the one green light in a market that erased a trillion dollars and watched Korea trip a circuit breaker on memory

The chip everyone left for dead beat on every line while the dip-buyers stepped into Tesla and the bears called it phase two of a bear market. Oil is holding above $100, the 30-year yield has now sat above 5 percent for 27 days, and the tariff wall went back up at midnight.

A Friday that opens on a split screen. Intel delivered the best print of the week into the worst tape of the week, and the crowd is already fighting over whether yesterday was the bottom or the beginning. Here is the pre-open preview, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. The bounce has one clear leader and a lot of doubt behind it. Per the People list, Intel reported second-quarter revenue of $16.13 billion against $14.42 billion expected, up 25 percent for its best growth since 2022, with adjusted earnings of $0.42 against estimates near $0.21, data-center revenue up 59 percent, and the stock up 12 to 13 percent after hours, though a $12.5 billion non-cash charge on its CHIPS Act escrow shares produced a headline GAAP loss that the operating numbers sit underneath.

The rest of the setup is heavier. Per the Market list, Korea's Kospi fell 6 percent intraday and tripped a circuit breaker after Morgan Stanley's Shawn Kim warned that memory demand has evaporated, with NAND inventory near 13 weeks, even as futures at home pointed to a mixed-to-slightly-green open after yesterday's rout wiped roughly $800 billion off the Magnificent Seven. Per the Stocks list, crude is holding above $100, the 10-year yield crossed 4.70 percent, and jobless claims came in at 187,000, a labor market too strong to rescue the tape. A blowout from the market's turnaround story against a memory scare and $100 oil is the exact tension the open has to resolve.

1. Intel is the one that delivered, and the government is sitting on a $38 billion gain. The turnaround got real numbers behind it. Per the People list, Intel's foundry revenue rose 31 percent to $5.8 billion and its AI and data-center segment rose 59 percent to $6.3 billion, gross margin expanded to 41.8 percent from 38.8, and the third-quarter guide of $15.8 to $16.8 billion cleared the $15.1 billion the Street expected. Strip out the one-time CHIPS charge and the company earned $2.2 billion, and the US government's stake bought at $20.47 last year is now worth about $45.8 billion, a roughly $38 billion paper gain.

The debate is whether it is cheap or just early. Per the Market list, Gene Munster notes Intel trades at 61 times 2027 earnings against Nvidia at 17 and calls it the third inning, while Jim Cramer flatly said Intel is the one. Per Trader Mayne, the print beat earnings by about 90 percent and revenue by 11 percent, and CEO Lip-Bu Tan says 14A customers are showing up for a 2027 ship. The caution is that a CFO musing about needing to tap capital markets on the call is not what a fragile tape wants to hear, but the operating turn is the realest bull story in the group.

2. The memory scare is the thing that could break the AI trade, and Korea felt it first. The bear case found a fresh target. Per the Stocks list, Morgan Stanley's Shawn Kim published a bearish memory note arguing demand has evaporated, with NAND inventory around 13 weeks, price hikes slowing to 5 percent a quarter into the fourth quarter, and high-bandwidth memory growth capped near 40 percent, and the Kospi fell 6 percent to around 6,500 on it because Samsung and SK Hynix dominate that index.

The other side is not backing down. Per the Market list, one desk is openly calling a bottom in AI semis across Intel, Micron, and the leveraged chip fund, with named support at Micron around $850 and Taiwan Semi near $388, and Morgan Stanley's own equity desk is telling clients to buy memory even as its analyst cuts demand. That contradiction inside one bank is the whole market in miniature, a supply story that has been the cleanest bull trade of the year running straight into the first real demand warning, and the memory names are where the next move gets decided.

3. The dip-buyers are already calling the bottom, and the bears say it is far too early. The rush to buy the wreck started immediately. Per the Stocks list, Cathie Wood bought 160,151 shares of Tesla worth about $52 million into the 14 percent drop, one desk pegged the Tesla bottom at $286 to $292, and the memory-bottom callers piled in, all on day two of a selloff.

The bearish read has history on its side. Per Trader Mayne, the market is in phase two of a three-phase bear, where the indices chop sideways while individual leaders like Arm, SanDisk, and Bloom Energy take 30 to 50 percent corrections before the mega-caps finally snap, and he sees Tesla trading to $200 before it sees $400 again. Per the Stocks list, one voice drew the dot-com parallel that Cisco took 12 to 13 months to bottom in 2000 and the market is only one month in, while Chamath Palihapitiya warned the wheels are coming off AI spending. Buying the first dip in a rout that just started is how the crowd gets caught, and the tape has not earned the all-clear yet.

4. The SaaS names left for dead are quietly the AI winners, starting with ServiceNow. The most contrarian bull thesis of the morning is in software. Per Milk Road, the SaaS apocalypse narrative has it backwards, because cheaper intelligence rewards the incumbents already wired into the enterprise rather than the startups trying to replace them, and ServiceNow is the flagship, an AI control tower whose Now Assist product has already generated $1 billion in revenue with contracted obligations still growing 20 percent.

The proof is that it is replacing labor, not just selling seats. Per that show, ServiceNow runs 20 internal AI agents doing work people used to do and its CEO said on the call that AI is already arbitraging away headcount, yet the stock fell on the day inside the broad tape, which the analyst calls nonsense and used to add 16 percent to his position. His second pick is AppLovin at about $400 with 85 percent margins and a mobile-ad exchange spanning 140,000 games, a data flywheel he would add to near $380. The point is that the boring back-end names with real switching costs may be the safest way to own AI.

5. The cost of money is the vise underneath all of it, and housing is frozen. The macro that decides everything is the bond market. Per Arbitrage Andy, the 30-year Treasury yield has now traded above 5 percent for 27 days this year, the most since 2007, the 10-year pushed above 4.70 percent for the first time since January 2025, and the national debt sits near $40 trillion so every refinancing at these rates balloons the interest bill. Per the Crypto list, Jim Bianco notes the 10-year real yield now runs above 3 percent against a 0.23 percent average from 2009 to 2022, a completely different regime for every asset priced off it.

Housing is where the higher-for-longer rate shows up as a frozen market. Per Arbitrage Andy, home prices have risen 36 straight months to a record median near $440,000 even as the 30-year mortgage sits around 6.55 to 6.77 percent, with 46 percent of sellers now offering concessions at an all-time high and foreclosure filings up 21 percent on the year. The problem is not collapsing prices, it is collapsing volume, owners locked into 2 to 4 percent mortgages who will not sell into buyers who cannot afford a doubled payment. That same rate regime is why gold overtook Treasuries as the largest reserve asset and why Bitcoin near $64,500 keeps stalling, per Trader Mayne, who is waiting to buy below $60,000 rather than chasing here.

Single names to track.

  • $MU (Micron). Sits at the center of the memory scare that tripped Korea's circuit breaker, with named support near $850 and a desk calling a bottom in AI semis, per the Market list.
  • $NOW (ServiceNow). Beat and kept contracted obligations growing 20 percent with Now Assist past $1 billion in revenue, yet fell on the day, per Milk Road.
  • $TSLA (Tesla). Drew a $52 million buy from Cathie Wood into its 14 percent drop, with one desk pegging a bottom near $286 and another seeing $200 first, per the Stocks list.
  • BTC (Bitcoin). Trades near $64,500 and keeps stalling, with Trader Mayne waiting to buy below $60,000 rather than chase, per Trader Mayne.

Psychology. Watch how fast the crowd moved to call the bottom. One day after a trillion dollars vanished and Tesla fell 14 percent, Cathie Wood was buying the dip, desks were pegging exact bottoms in Tesla and memory, and Intel's blowout gave everyone the excuse to say the worst is over. That reflex, the need to be early to the turn, is the most human thing on a screen full of red, and it is exactly what a phase-two bear feeds on.

The honest read is that both sides have a real case and the tape has not settled it. Intel genuinely delivered and the government is up $38 billion on it, but Morgan Stanley's memory downgrade just tripped a circuit breaker in Korea, and Trader Mayne's framework says the leaders can still lose 30 to 50 percent before the mega-caps break. When a bank tells its clients to buy memory on the same morning its own analyst says demand has evaporated, the market is not confused, it is genuinely undecided, and undecided is not the same as safe.

So let the weekend and next week's Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon capex guides do the talking before you plant a flag. Own the turn that has numbers behind it like Intel rather than the ones that only have hope, respect that oil above $100 and a 30-year yield above 5 percent are constraints no dip-buyer can wish away, and keep enough cash that if this really is only month one of a Cisco-style grind, you are buying the third dip and not averaging down into the first.
