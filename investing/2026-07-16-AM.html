The memory crash finally got a real cause, and it was not the AI thesis breaking, it was Korea raising rates for the first time in three years

Bank of Korea hiked 25 basis points, the KOSPI fell 7 percent, and Micron slipped under $900 as SK Hynix dropped another 11 percent. The Iran war restarted overnight and oil still would not break out, ASML told everyone 2027 is already sold out, and a Chinese memory giant is trading before it even lists.

MacroStocksCrypto

The story everyone feared was the AI trade cracking, and the actual cause turned out to be a central bank in Seoul. That distinction is the whole edge this morning. Here is the pre-open picture, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. The memory selloff has a name now. Per Jose Najarro, the Bank of Korea raised its base rate 25 basis points to 2.75 percent, its first hike since January 2023, and the KOSPI fell 7 percent with SK Hynix down 11 percent after hours and Samsung off 8, since those two names are more than half the index. Micron slipped under $900 to $896 and closed below its 50-day average for the first time in three months. Today brings Netflix and Taiwan Semiconductor earnings and US retail sales, the Iran war is back on with oil still stuck near $84, and the July 29 Fed meeting remains priced for no hike.

1. The memory round-trip was a rate story, not a thesis story, and that changes what you do with it. The distinction matters because it is tradeable. Per Najarro, the Korea hike raises borrowing costs on Korean-financed fabs, and that is why SK Hynix and Samsung led the drop, but it does not touch the demand curve for high-bandwidth memory or the fabs Micron builds with US money. So the sharpest fall is in the names with the local rate exposure, not the ones with the best long-run position.

The bull rebuttal came from the calmest company in the chain. ASML raised its four-year guidance and said its lithography capacity grows 30 percent next year and another 30 percent the year after, with 2027 already sold out and 2028 heading there, per the same breakdown. The argument is that ASML is the true bottleneck, and a bottleneck that is sold out three years forward stretches the cycle rather than ending it. When the pick-and-shovel supplier is booked solid while the crowd panics over a rate hike, the panic is the opportunity.

2. A Chinese memory giant is already trading, and the pros are shorting it before it lists. The freshest catalyst under the memory tape, per threadguy: CXMT, described as China's SK Hynix, goes public July 27, and it is already tradable as a pre-IPO perpetual future on Hyperliquid and Binance. It is trading at roughly ten times its IPO price, an implied valuation near $470 billion against an $80 billion offering, near Tencent territory.

The tell is the positioning. The two largest positions are professional shorts, and an anonymous whale pulled $75 million off Coinbase to accumulate it, so the smart money is split and loud. The open question threadguy raises is whether the CXMT hype is itself feeding the dump in the listed memory names as traders rotate into the shiny new thing.

3. The Iran war restarted and oil still refused to break out, the most important non-event of the week. Per the Market list, the US ran a second wave of strikes on Chabahar and Bandar Abbas, Kuwait intercepted four cruise missiles and 21 drones, and Trump swung from warning Iran to behave to saying they want to settle, all in a day. And oil sat at $84. A market that shrugs off active strikes on the Strait of Hormuz has decided this war is a series of spikes to fade, and that decision is what keeps the disinflation trade alive.

The bigger frame under it, per Capital Flows, is that the re-industrialization of America is the reason rates are not lower. The argument from New Vista Capital is that the US is rebuilding hardware, defense, and energy at the same time it retools software with AI, and the named plays are the vertically integrated defense-tech names, Anduril, Palantir, and SpaceX, plus the automation stories like using physics-based AI to lift General Dynamics artillery-shell output. The macro point is that a country rebuilding its industrial base while China out-produces it in shipbuilding by 200 times does not get cheap money.

4. The bank boom is the SpaceX-IPO machine, and PayPal is the deal it wants next. Per Prof G Markets, Jamie Dimon said it is getting close to as good as it gets for the banks, on a perfect storm of a resilient economy, high real rates that fatten net interest margins, and a resurgence of dealmaking, with all five big banks underwriting the SpaceX IPO and the OpenAI and Anthropic offerings flagged as the next tailwinds.

The live deal is PayPal, where Stripe and the private-equity firm Advent have put up a $53 billion offer with $50 billion of bank financing behind it, per the Market list. Michael Burry, a holder, says he will not sell at this price, and the stock still ran 15 percent, so the market thinks a higher bid is coming.

5. Crypto is quietly rotating, and the tell is XRP. Under the Bitcoin weakness, the flows are diverging. Per Lark Davis, Bitcoin ETFs bled hundreds of millions while the seven US spot XRP ETFs pulled in positive inflows, roughly $60 million in June and $1.49 billion cumulative since launch, with more than 970 million XRP now locked in them. The analytics firm Santiment reads it as capital rotating into XRP and Solana while Bitcoin and Ether ETFs bleed, the kind of divergence that shows up when smart money is positioning at a potential bottom rather than chasing the leader.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • ASML. Raised four-year guidance with 2027 sold out and capacity growing 30 percent two years running, the disciplined bottleneck that stretches the cycle, per Najarro.
  • $MU (Micron). Under $900 and below its 50-day average on the Korea rate hike rather than any demand miss, per Najarro.
  • CXMT. China's memory giant, public July 27, already trading as a pre-IPO perpetual at an implied $470 billion with pros short, per threadguy.
  • $PYPL (PayPal). Up 15 percent on the $53 billion Stripe and Advent bid, with Michael Burry holding out for more, per the Market list.
  • $ASTS (AST SpaceMobile). Priced $1 billion of convertible notes due 2034 into a correction, the stock having round-tripped from $133 in June to $57, per the Market list.
  • XRP. The rotation tell, with $1.49 billion of cumulative ETF inflows while Bitcoin ETFs bleed, per Lark Davis.

Psychology. Two days ago Micron was worth more than Berkshire and the crowd was calling for $2,000, and this morning it is under $900 because a central bank in Seoul raised rates a quarter point. That is the whole lesson of the week compressed into one name. The move up was leverage dressed as conviction, and the move down did not need the AI story to break, it just needed the borrowing cost on a Korean fab to tick higher, and the leverage did the rest.

The tell is who is calm and who is not. ASML, the company that actually controls the supply, raised its guidance and said 2027 is sold out, while the leveraged holders got liquidated on a 25-basis-point hike and the smart money is already short the next Chinese memory IPO before it lists. The people closest to the real economics are the least rattled, and the people furthest out on leverage are the ones getting carried out.

So the discipline check into the Netflix and Taiwan Semiconductor prints today is to separate the trade from the theme again. The theme is intact, ASML is booked through 2027 and the demand is real, but the leveraged expressions of it, the 2x memory ETFs and the pre-IPO perps, are the ones that just showed you what they do on a bad headline. If you want the memory cycle, own the bottleneck that is sold out, not the leverage that round-trips on a rate hike. Let Micron prove it can hold $900 and let the chip earnings print before you decide the panic was the top or the gift.
