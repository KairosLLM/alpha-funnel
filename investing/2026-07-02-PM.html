The crowded memory buy got run over, and $MU closed under $1,000

Every desk was buying the dip this morning. The tape spent the afternoon proving them early, semis puked to open Q3, and a soft jobs print pulled the Fed hike off the table.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

This morning every desk was buying the memory dip. The afternoon made them look early. The crowding I flagged at the open is exactly why it stung, and it is still the trade to watch, just not at the price the morning wanted.

Where the session landed. Semiconductors led everything lower to start the quarter. The $SOX (the Philadelphia semiconductor index) fell about 4% on top of yesterday's 6% gash, down roughly 10% in the first day and a half of a quarter that just closed its best three months ever. $MU broke $1,000 and stayed under it, a second straight close below the 21-day moving average (the short-term trend line) it lost yesterday, with chart desks now marking 952, 934, 921, and 909 as the shelves below.

The rest of the tape held up better than the chip complex. The Nasdaq and S&P finished red on the chip drag, the Dow was roughly flat, and $AAPL ripped about 4% to the low $300s as money parked somewhere it trusted. The $VIX slipped to a two-week low near 15.87. Nobody was paying up for protection. Crude stayed soft on Iran-talks headlines, the 10-year yield ticked up after Warsh repeated that inflation is still too high, and $BTC clawed back above $62K into the close. Cash markets are closed tomorrow, Friday July 3, for Independence Day and reopen Monday.

The June jobs report, pulled forward ahead of the holiday, is the pin under all of it. Payrolls came in at +57,000 against expectations near 115,000, a clean miss, while the unemployment rate actually fell to 4.2% from 4.3% for the wrong reason, a shrinking labor force rather than more hiring. The market read it as soft but not breaking and did the obvious thing, it pulled the rate-hike risk off the table. Odds of a hike at the July 29 meeting collapsed from around 32% a week ago to under 18%, and a hold now prices above 82%. The regime here is hike-versus-hold under a hawkish Warsh Fed, not the cut-versus-hold everyone traded last year.

1. The memory thesis is durable, but the morning's "smash buy" was a day early. The fundamental case actually got stronger today. On the Stocks list, CEO Sanjay Mehrotra said Micron is meeting only 50% to two-thirds of demand, that the shortage runs well beyond 2026 with no new supply until 2028, and that the take-or-pay book (contracts where the customer pays whether or not it takes the chips) is now 16 agreements, with $GM freshly added and 14 of them carrying a $100B minimum revenue floor. He is putting $200B into a US buildout.

None of that broke. The price did. $MU lost the $1,000 line the morning crowd called a floor, kept falling through the level, and dragged $SNDK, $TSM, $AMD, $MRVL, and the rest of the complex with it. My read is unchanged from the open except on timing. Build it slowly, because the tape just told you the buyers were early. Let it base and use a reclaim of $1,000 as your signal instead of catching the knife.

2. Nvidia's new backstop is the reason the neocloud dip got bought. The fresh structural story today, and it was not in this morning's edition, is a revenue-share model where $NVDA rents the unused capacity of the neoclouds (the pure-play AI compute landlords like CoreWeave) and takes a cut of the cloud revenue. The street's line, and it fits, is that Nvidia now sells the shovels, guarantees the loan, and clips the gold on the way out. That flipped the morning's fear into the afternoon's buy: $CRWV, $NBIS, $IREN, and $APLD sold off on $META dumping "excess" compute, then got bought hard by desks treating the selloff as an overreaction.

The bear case is still live, and it is not small. Ed Zitron argued on Prof G that roughly 80% of AI compute is used by just OpenAI and Anthropic, that over 100GW is under construction against maybe 6GW of real demand, and that OpenAI just delayed its IPO after failing to get a $1T mark. But the hard datapoint cut the other way today: AWS raised its reserved GPU rates about 20% on July 1, pushing an H100 from $4.33 to $5.19 an hour, a hyperscaler repricing compute up into the Blackwell ramp.

3. $TSLA beat big and still got sold. Q2 deliveries came in at 480,126, the best second quarter ever and the fourth-best quarter in company history, crushing the roughly 406,000 consensus and even topping Troy Teslike's aggressive 466,000, with 13.5 GWh of storage on top. The stock fell anyway, sliding 2% to 6% intraday, losing its 200-day moving average and pressing toward $400. That is a textbook buy-the-rumor unwind after the number everyone pre-positioned into finally printed. Gene Munster's framing is the one I lean toward: the EV winter that started in early 2024 is ending, and $RIVN raising full-year guidance to 65,000 to 70,000 units backs that up. Own the read, respect the $400 level.

4. Bitcoin bounced to $62K, but the flows never turned. The bottom-callers got loud on the Crypto list, with Bitwise's Matt Hougan "nearing the bottom" and Cantor calling the bear "in its final stretch." Lark Davis stacked the cleaner bull case: Bitcoin has never printed two red years in a row, and the weekly RSI just made a bullish divergence (price at a lower low while momentum makes a higher low, the 2022-bottom setup) pointing at the mid-$70Ks. Against that, the flows are ugly and getting uglier. James Seyffart laid out the damage on Milk Road.

  • Spot Bitcoin ETFs have bled more than $11B off the peak and the pace is accelerating, including a $700M single-day outflow on June 25.
  • Ethereum's ETFs are down to $10.5B, about a third of their flows gone.
  • Three alt products are still taking in cash while everything else bleeds: the $SOL and XRP ETFs (over $1.6B each) and the new $HYPE funds ($192M). That is where the relative strength lives.

On $MSTR, Trader Mayne put the hole at roughly negative $14B and framed Saylor's only real play as survive the bear, then unwind above his cost basis. My read: this is a tradeable bounce inside a bear. Until the ETF bleed stops I treat $56K as the accumulation zone and $44K as the deeper add, and I keep the size honest.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $ZIM, a clean merger arb. The WSB desk laid out the Hapag-Lloyd cash deal at $35.00 a share against a roughly $25 price, about 40% upside, with a second bid at $37.50 already surfacing. The spread is wide because Israel holds a "golden share" approval (a state veto right over the deal), but the precedent there is to negotiate rather than block. Downside if the deal breaks is around $19 to $20, so the risk is defined going in.
  • Energy is the AI bottleneck, and the power names are how you play it. The on-site generation thesis names the tier with revenue today: $BE (fuel cells at data centers now), $CEG (restarting Three Mile Island on an 835 MW deal with Microsoft), $TLN (a 1,920 MW Amazon contract through 2042), and $VST. The SMR pure-plays (small modular reactors, factory-built nuclear) like $OKLO and $SMR are the satellite bet, and $OKLO's 1.2 GW Ohio deal for Meta's data centers is the one to anchor on. On the battery leg, CATL (3750.HK) grew grid storage 120% last quarter and targets storage at half of sales by 2030 on $40B of net cash.
  • $ASML, the chokepoint nobody can route around. Rick Orford walked the case: 100% share of commercial EUV lithography (the machines that pattern every 2nm and 3nm chip), a genuine toll road under the whole industry, up about 130% on the year. The catch is the price, roughly 39x earnings with analyst targets implying only 3% to 33% upside, so I wait for the pullback rather than pay 39x today.
  • $INTC got a real upgrade. HSBC doubled its price target to $200 from $100. In a session where the rest of semis got sold, a doubling from a major desk on the most-doubted name in the group earns a slot on the watchlist.
  • The second-order AI winners, $UBER and $NU. Milk Road made the picks-and-shovels-on-the-demand-side case: platforms the market fears AI will disrupt actually get stronger as AI cuts their cost to serve. Uber is the top pick at 6.1% of the book (202M monthly users, $5.6B operating income, autonomous rides live in eight cities heading for 15-plus), Nubank second at 4.3% ($13.30 monthly revenue per customer against under $1 cost to serve). Waymo self-aggregating demand is the risk to Uber.
  • $RBRK, the cyber laggard with a reason. Chip Stock Investor flagged Rubrik near $70, down about 20%, the one security name that has not recovered like $CRWD did, because AI spend is compressing its margins even as subscription revenue grows near 30%. A reverse DCF (pricing the stock backwards to see what growth it implies) now shows a bar roughly half what it was, which the analyst calls doable but no longer a steal. I want a quarter of stable margins before touching it.
