Michael Burry is short Micron, and the whole AI-capex trade wobbled overnight

The memory bull wall now has a famous bear leaning on it, three separate voices called the AI buildout a bubble, and Bitcoin's bounce is being sold by whales while the chartists call a bottom.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Markets are closed today for the holiday, which gives you a full session to think instead of react. Use it, because two things shifted overnight. A brand-name bear stepped in front of the memory trade, and the AI-capex bull case took its first coordinated punches.

Closed today, and what to watch Monday. US cash equity and options markets are shut today, Friday July 3, for Independence Day (observed, since the Fourth is Saturday), and reopen Monday July 6. So there is no session to react to. What is worth pre-loading for the reopen: whether the semis that got flushed Thursday ($MU, $SNDK, $MRVL) find a bid or keep sliding, whether Bitcoin holds the $62K bounce or rolls over toward the mid-$50Ks, and the calendar into next week. $SPCX joins the Nasdaq-100 on July 7, which forces index funds to buy it, and the market expects GPT-5.6 as soon as Tuesday, which would land straight into the AI-bubble argument below.

1. Michael Burry is short Micron, and that reframes the whole memory fight. The biggest new thing overnight is that Michael Burry opened a bearish $MU position, flagged across the Stocks list and four others. He is leaning directly on the most crowded bull wall on the board. The bull case is genuinely strong: Q3 revenue was $41.46B, up 346% year over year and a beat, gross margin ran 84.9%, the Q4 guide is $50B in revenue and $31 EPS, and the stock trades near 6x forward earnings against a CEO target of $4,000.

The bear case is now a person rather than a chart, and it comes with a real risk attached, that $AAPL is exploring Chinese memory suppliers and SK Hynix is pulling price caps off its long-term contracts. My read: I still think the durable shortage story wins over a multi-year horizon, but Burry short into a $1,000 breakdown is exactly the kind of thing that keeps you from sizing up early. Watch how $MU trades on the Monday reopen and let the $4,000 target wait.

2. The flush was a momentum unwind, not a market break. This is the framing that matters for how you play the reopen. Thursday's damage was concentrated and brutal in one place: $AEHR fell 18.3%, $AAOI 17.1%, $SNDK 14.8%, $TER 13.8%, $MRVL 11.2%, and $NBIS ran 30% from its peak. Meanwhile the $SPX finished down just 0.13% and the Dow actually printed a record high, with the $VIX (the market's fear gauge) closing at its lowest in a month and recession odds collapsing from 32% toward 12%. That is high-beta AI names getting sold while the broad market shrugs. Call it rotation rather than a risk-off break. The tell to respect is that it happened into a three-day weekend, when traders cut the risk they do not want to hold blind.

3. The AI-capex bull case took three separate punches overnight. Yesterday the doubt was one bear (Ed Zitron). Overnight it became a chorus, and that convergence is the actual signal.

  • Meta stopped pretending. On Prof G, the read on Meta launching a business to sell its "excess" AI compute is blunt, that the second or third largest compute buyer on earth is walking away from building its own AI products. Zuckerberg's own May line is the receipt: he would not sell "because we think we have a use for the compute, but obviously if we get to a point where we feel that we have overbuilt, then that is an option." In a town hall he added that agent development has not accelerated the way he expected and the stock "would be higher" without the AI spend.
  • China is the mechanism that could crack it. Capital Flows made the sharpest macro case: roughly half the S&P 500 is now an AI trade funded on borrowed money, and the risk is not who wins, it is that the price of intelligence falls faster than the most crowded trade in history can adjust. China runs the Amazon playbook, models one generation behind priced 10x to 100x cheaper and state-subsidized so they never need the API margins US labs depend on. When the US suspended its top model on June 12, a Chinese lab open-sourced a near-frontier rival the next day. The template is DeepSeek in January 2025, which erased $589B from $NVDA in a single day.
  • The unit economics are leaking. Zitron, quoted at length via threadguy, pegs OpenAI's 2025 cash burn at $20.9B with costs that scale linearly with revenue, and notes the IPO reportedly slipped to 2027 after it could not clear a $1T valuation. Nvidia's revenue-share model, confirmed by Bloomberg, hands token credits to AI startups for a cut of future sales. That is vendor financing dressed as demand. And the physical backlash is showing up, with Blackstone abandoning a Virginia data center and 20 Florida counties weighing data-center moratoriums.

The counter is real and I hold it partly: OpenAI delaying an IPO from a position of private funding is optionality, not panic, and none of these bears has been right on timing. But when Meta, a China cost-curve, and OpenAI's own burn rate all point the same way in one night, you respect it by trimming the most levered AI-beta names, not by flipping short.

4. Bitcoin's bottom-callers are buying what the whales are selling. The chart signals are genuinely bullish. On the Crypto list, long-term-holder SOPR (a metric that reads above 1 when holders sell at a profit and below when they sell at a loss) printed 0.615, meaning long-term holders are capitulating at a 38.5% average loss, a sub-0.65 reading seen only at the March 2020, October 2022, and July 2023 bottoms. $IBIT put in its first-ever weekly tweezer bottom and one entity absorbed 270,000 BTC at $59K.

Against that, the whales are handing it to them: an Irish dealer wallet sent 500 BTC to Coinbase Prime with 4,500 ($276M) left to go, a Tim Draper-linked wallet moved 1,000 BTC, and Novogratz keeps saying Bitcoin "lost its sponsorship." Kalshi still prices only 15% odds of $100K this year. Benjamin Cowen is the patient bear worth hearing: total crypto market cap sits near $2.125T, below its 2021 level, and he thinks the whole class stays under its fair-value trend line all year, with a summer counter-rally, a lower low later in 2026, and the real bull into 2027 and 2028. My read: accumulate the dips you were already going to buy, but the flows say the bottom is a process, not this week.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • The on-site power thesis has a small-cap leg now. Yesterday's names ($BE, $CEG, $TLN, $VST) were the blue-chip version. The small-cap version, driven by 3-to-5-year lead times on high-voltage transformers, adds $CGEH (microturbines with an 800-volt "Microgrids 4 AI" product aimed at server racks) and $FCEL (megawatt fuel cells, a 4-gigawatt-plus pipeline), with $POWL, $MOD, and $NVT flagged to research. Size these as high-risk satellites, because fuel-cell and microturbine small caps have a long history of burning cash and diluting holders.
  • $AVAV, the drone super-cycle name. From the People list: AeroVironment printed roughly $642M in fiscal Q4 revenue, a backlog up 65% to $1.2B, and a $500M Defense Department award, with $KTOS getting a JPMorgan upgrade alongside it. Defense-tech is the non-AI momentum pocket that kept working while semis flushed.
  • $TSLA, a defined swing setup into next week. Per Stocks with Josh, Tesla flushed 11% to 12% on its record 480,000-plus Q2 delivery beat, a classic sell-the-good-news move, rejecting $425 resistance and finding support near $381.68 with a $369 pivot below that. His stat: 85% odds of trading above the intraday low within 30 days, 95% within 60. Cathie Wood bought about $40M of it into the drop. A trade with clear invalidation, not a conviction hold.
  • $MRNA, the biotech that quietly went vertical. Fresh two-year high, up about 160% year to date, now the eighth-best performer in the S&P 500. When a beaten-down name reclaims a multi-year base while the hot trade unwinds, it earns a look.
