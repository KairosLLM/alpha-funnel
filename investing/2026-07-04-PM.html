The memory trade got a bear, a structural bull, and a $500 Trump-basket receipt on the same holiday tape

Burry is shorting Micron on nothing but a chart while Shkreli and Aschenbrenner argue the supply is structurally locked, the Fed conversation quietly flipped from when-they-cut to might-they-hike, and Bitcoin is being bought and doubted at the exact same $62,500.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Nothing traded today and nothing trades tomorrow, so this is the second holiday session to decide what you believe before Monday can price it for you. Three arguments hardened over the afternoon. The memory trade split into a clean bull-bear fight with names on both sides, the rate debate flipped from cuts to a live hike, and Bitcoin sat at one price with the accumulators and the doubters both pointing at it.

The week ahead, and the one thing that changed since this morning. US markets were closed Friday July 3 for Independence Day and stayed closed for the weekend, reopening Monday July 6. Five things to pre-load, and one of them is new since the AM. First and biggest: the Fed conversation is no longer only about cuts. Per the Market list, Polymarket now prices the odds of a rate hike at roughly a coin flip, up from near zero earlier this year, with Chris Waller already calling for one and new chair Kevin Warsh refusing to rule out July. Second, the FOMC minutes land Wednesday at 2pm ET and every line now gets read for hike risk, not just cut timing.

Three more to pre-load. The SK Hynix US listing is still on deck around July 10, a fresh pure-play memory bet in front of American money. $SPCX joins the Nasdaq-100 on July 7. And a record pile of money-market cash still sits on the sideline waiting for one of these to break.

1. The memory trade is now a real bull-bear fight, and the bull case got structural. This morning the receipts were Samsung's DRAM invoice. This afternoon the argument matured into two named sides. On the bear side, per the Market list, Michael Burry's Substack short on Micron is pure technical analysis, Micron overextended from its 200-day average targeting roughly a 30% drawdown, with nothing fundamental or HBM-related under it. That matters, because a short with no thesis about supply is a bet on gravity, not on the business.

The bull side answered with structure. In the Reader inbox, Martin Shkreli argues DRAM is not a commodity at all, because a commodity is something anyone can make with a little capex, and DRAM needs cleanroom fabs full of $ASML and Lam Research machines nobody can stand up on demand. His read is that rational new entrants match the incumbent price rather than start a self-harming war, so memory prices behave like a disciplined oligopoly and the market's assumed back-half decline for $MU may be too bearish. Leopold Aschenbrenner is voting the same way with real weights, per Milk Road Macro: his book is 35% $NBIS, 14.9% $SNDK, and 12.7% $BE, with $MU, $CRWV, $TSM, and $INTC behind them, roughly two-thirds in three names, a pure bet that compute, power, memory, and storage are the only bottlenecks that matter.

2. The 2026 scoreboard says the memory bulls have been right all year, and that is exactly when you check the crowding. The tables that circulated today are a gut-check. Per the People-to-follow list, Shay Boloor's top fifteen performers of 2026 are led by $SNDK up 635%, then $MU up 242%, $INTC up 226%, $WDC up 213%, $MRVL up 189%, and $ARM up 188%. Memory and semis are not participating in this market, they are the market. Stretch it to the decade on the Market list and the same names show up: $NVDA up 16,602%, $AMD up 10,113%, $MU up 7,704%, Lam Research up 4,135%.

The fresh catalyst under the leaderboard is real. Stocks with Josh put a new $1,500 target on Micron off a measured move, activating once price reclaims $1,250, with a hard bounce expected first off the $895 fibonacci level. And the supply side keeps invoicing: per the Stocks list, Infineon raised power-chip prices 10% to 25% for the second time in six months, with silicon content per data-center rack climbing from about $15,000 at 125kW toward $100,000 at a megawatt by 2029. My read: the fundamental bull case is the stronger one, but Burry is shorting the crowd, not the chips, and a tape this one-sided can hand him his 30% on positioning alone before the fundamentals win. Own the trade, respect the unwind.

3. The Fed just became the summer's biggest event, because Warsh made a hike thinkable again. The regime change under Kevin Warsh is the macro story the memory tape is riding on top of. Per the Stocks list, Warsh is killing forward guidance, would not confirm or deny a July hike, and wants what he calls a good family fight at a Fed that John Gillen describes as the most divided in decades, with Powell still seated and Waller already pushing to hike. The last time the Fed hiked was July 2023, and the market fell 10% after. A coin-flip on a hike that the tape has spent a year assuming was impossible is the definition of an unpriced risk.

4. Tom Lee mapped the rest of the year, and the scary part has a date on it. Two independent versions of Tom Lee's roadmap landed today and they agree. Via Lark Davis, Lee sees three phases: a bull run into late summer, an abrupt September-to-October correction that feels like a bear market, then one of the strongest recoveries in decades to close the year. The Market list version from Heisenberg puts numbers on it, the S&P at 7,500 to 7,800 in the next couple months, a 10% to 15% pullback to 6,600 to 7,000, then 8,000 by year end.

The named trigger for the autumn drop is specific and worth marking: the Anthropic and OpenAI IPOs are expected to hit at once and dump fresh supply equal to 5% to 6% of the entire S&P market cap. That is a supply shock with a date attached.

5. Bitcoin is being bought and doubted at the same $62,500, and the alts are where the conviction actually is. Every list quoted Bitcoin near $62,500 today, which the Kalshi list frames brutally: it was $108,100 on last July 4 and $58,660 the July 4 before, so the asset roughly halved year over year. The bulls are leaning in. On Lark Davis, the tell is a record 270,000-coin whale accumulation over 90 days against the worst sentiment in years, a price-bear-market sitting on top of a fundamentals-bull-market. Milk Road makes the patience case that bottoms are a bathtub, not a V, so you buy conviction and let time do the work rather than chasing the exact low. The Crypto list counters with Rekt Capital's caution that this still looks 2022-esque, with any relief rally turning the 50-month average into resistance.

Where the crypto conviction is loudest is not Bitcoin, it is the revenue coins. Per the Crypto list, Hyperliquid's Assistance Fund hit a $3.16B record having already burned $1.18B of $HYPE, Solana did $78M of protocol revenue last month against Ethereum's $39M at a fifth of the value locked, and Ansem is betting hard on a memecoin revival led by the $ANSEM runner. The through-line: if you believe the cycle, the cleaner risk-reward is in the protocols that actually earn, not the majors.

Single names worth a place on the watchlist.

  • $ETN, the pick-and-shovel for the power bottleneck. Rick Orford's case on Eaton is that the real AI constraint is not chips or plants but the transformers and switchgear that connect them, and Eaton owns that bottleneck with 42% electrical order growth and a backlog up 48%. The catch is a 40x multiple that leaves no room for a miss.
  • $INTC, the turnaround with a working fab. Per the Market list and Polymarket, Intel reportedly fixed its 18A yield variability and is ramping toward 30,000 combined wafers a month, framed as the only leading-edge US foundry. It is already up 226% on the year, so the easy money on the recovery is behind you.
  • $PLTR versus $MSFT, the same AI bet at two prices. Rick Orford's head-to-head frames Palantir at roughly 92x forward against Microsoft near 21x. His verdict, which I share, is that Microsoft is the safer way to own the same theme and Palantir is the higher-beta option only if AIP becomes the standard operating layer.
  • $NBIS, the neocloud that keeps signing. The Crypto list and Polymarket both flag Nebius adding an 18MW Spain lease on top of its $27B Meta deal and $2B Nvidia investment, with Q1 revenue up 684%. High-beta and not for the faint of heart, but the contracts are real.
  • $CCXI, the first robotics play from a team that called the memory run. Per the Milk Road PRO recap, Melvin bought Churchill Capital XI at $16.87 as a robotics SPAC while the same desk sold $MU at a 145% gain, a small tell that the smart money is starting to rotate the winnings forward.

The bigger rotation to settle: infrastructure, orchestration, or the apps. The single best debate on the tape is where the AI money goes next, and today it split three ways with real money behind each. Aschenbrenner is all-in on the physical infrastructure. Milk Road's AI desk argues the margin moves past the frontier models to the orchestration layer and the hyperscalers who own compute, citing Dylan Patel's claim that Opus 4.8 API tokens run at 80% margins and noting the Mag 7 trade at low-teens multiples with revenue landing in 2028 to 2030. And the Milk Road PRO desk is rotating out of infra into the applications on a profit-per-user thesis, liking $UBER at about $28 of operating profit per user, $NU at $35, and $COIN at $158. You do not have to pick one today, but you should know which of the three you are actually long.
