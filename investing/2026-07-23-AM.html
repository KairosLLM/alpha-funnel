Google delivered the best cloud number the market has ever seen and the stock fell anyway, because it raised its spending again to $200 billion and posted its first negative free cash flow in a decade

Cloud grew 82 percent with a $514 billion backlog, and Alphabet still dropped after hours because the capex guide went up and the cash went out. Tesla beat on revenue and missed badly on margins, the 30-year yield sits above 5 percent, and the bond market is bracing while stocks sit 1 percent below their high.

MacroStocksCrypto

A Thursday that arrives with the verdict already in and the verdict is unsettling. The single best set of AI numbers a company has printed was not enough to lift the stock, and that gap is the whole story. Here is the pre-open preview, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. The earnings landed after yesterday's close and the read is a market that has stopped rewarding the spend. Per the Market list, Alphabet grew Google Cloud 82 percent to $24.8 billion against a Street looking for the mid-60s, carried a $514 billion cloud backlog, and still fell 3 to 4 percent after hours because it raised its full-year capital-spending guide to $195 to $205 billion and its free cash flow turned negative by $5.8 billion. Tesla told the same split story, beating on revenue at $28.2 billion but missing badly on profit with adjusted earnings of $0.33 against roughly $0.50 expected, and it slid about 3 percent.

The macro underneath is heavy and getting heavier. Per the Financial Markets list, the 2-year Treasury yield hit 4.31 percent, the highest since February 2025, the 30-year holds above 5 percent, and the Nasdaq 100 has now closed below its 50-day average for six straight sessions. Per the Polymarket list, the S&P sits 1 percent below its all-time high while the fear-and-greed gauge reads Fear, and oil stayed bid over $95 on the Strait of Hormuz. The setup into the open is a tape that got exactly the demand it wanted and did not like the bill.

1. Google's cloud was a monster, and the market still balked at the spending. The number itself was spectacular. Per Jose Najarro, Google Cloud grew 82 percent to $24.8 billion, accelerating from 63 percent last quarter, with a $514 billion backlog the company expects to convert more than half of into revenue within 24 months, and the Gemini app now has 950 million monthly users processing 22 billion tokens a minute. The reported earnings looked enormous at over $9 a share, but per the Financial Markets list roughly $98 billion of that was unrealized gains on Alphabet's stakes in SpaceX and Anthropic, so the operating story is the cloud line and the spending, not the headline profit.

The spending is what spooked the tape. Per Najarro, Alphabet raised its capex guide by about $15 billion on each end to $195 to $205 billion, the second hike this year, and attributed it to accelerating capacity rather than to memory prices, which reads as buying more chips faster. That is why free cash flow went negative for the first time in years, and it is the exact moment a growth story becomes a spending story. The one investor voting the other way is the loudest one, with Warren Buffett's Berkshire increasing its Alphabet position 202 percent to $31 billion per the Financial Markets list.

2. The tell inside the print is that the neoclouds went up while Google went down. The most actionable read-through is a rotation. Per Najarro, Google said it would lean on third-party capacity in the third quarter to meet demand it cannot build fast enough, and the names that rent out that capacity rallied on the news, with CoreWeave up about 2.9 percent, Nebius up 3.9 percent, and IREN up 2 percent even as the hyperscaler itself fell.

The mechanism is the important part. When the largest builder of its own data centers admits it has to rent, the shortage of compute is real enough that the landlords get paid first. Per the Market list, Baird kept Nebius at Outperform with a $250 target, and the candidates for Google's overflow include SpaceX, Oracle, and CoreWeave. The trade this print rewards is not the mega-cap doing the spending, it is the second-tier names that sell the capacity the spending cannot keep up with.

3. Tesla beat on trucks and missed on money, and the robotaxi is the only story that matters now. The quarter was a tale of two lines. Per the Kalshi list, Tesla delivered $28.2 billion in revenue against $27.6 billion expected but earned only $0.33 a share versus $0.55, with gross margin at 16.8 percent against a 19.4 percent estimate, and the stock fell about 3 percent after hours. Deliveries rose 25 percent to 480,000, so the volume is there, but the profit on each car keeps shrinking.

The bull case has fully migrated to autonomy. Per the Polymarket list, the robotaxi fleet has now run more than 380,000 miles across seven metros with zero notable incidents, full self-driving subscriptions rose 56 percent to 1.48 million, and the CFO guided 2026 capital spending above $25 billion and rising for another two to three years. Per the People list, the order backlog is the largest since 2023 and Optimus production starts in Fremont later this year. The read is that the market is done paying for the car company and is waiting to see if the robot company is real.

4. The bubble question is no longer theoretical, because the negative cash flow just showed up at the best name. The bear case got a data point from the bull. Per Prof G Markets, the state of Wisconsin is demanding $7 billion in collateral from Oracle before it will let construction on a data center begin, because S&P cut Oracle to BBB-minus one rung above junk after it borrowed $43 billion against $67 billion of revenue while burning about $24 billion of cash. His line is that bubbles are built with debt, not equity, and Oracle is the most obvious name in the danger zone but not the only one.

What makes today different is that the same signature appeared at Alphabet. Per Najarro, the company with the strongest cloud business on earth just posted negative free cash flow to fund the buildout, and per the Financial Markets list SpaceX's stock is down about 48 percent and trading below its IPO price. The four largest hyperscalers are on track for more than $725 billion of combined capex this year, and the market just told you it will no longer clap for that number on its own. The demand is real and the financing is the risk, and last night was the first time both showed up in the same release.

5. Gold is down a quarter from its high and crypto stalled, and both are waiting on the same yields. The haven trade took its pain. Per ClearValue Tax, gold has fallen about 26 percent from its early-2026 peak near $5,500 to around $4,000 as the 30-year yield climbed past 5 percent and the dollar firmed, but the structural buyers have not blinked, with central banks buying roughly 1,000 tons a year for four years and China adding 15 tons in June alone against $39.6 trillion of federal debt. The case is to dollar-cost-average the correction rather than call the bottom.

Crypto is stuck at the same wall. Per VirtualBacon, Bitcoin has stalled dead at $66,000, the resistance that has capped every bounce of this correction, with confirmation only above 82,000 and a bear-case retest into the low $50,000s, while oil at $100 is the line that locks in another Fed hike. His named catalysts are Aave moving to Avalanche with tokenized index collateral and Chainlink as the oracle for a new stablecoin, but the honest frame is that gold, Bitcoin, and the whole risk complex are all hostage to a bond market that keeps pricing tighter money.

  • Gold. Down about 26 percent from its $5,500 peak to near $4,000 while central banks buy roughly 1,000 tons a year, a dip to average into, per ClearValue Tax.

Psychology. Sit with what actually happened last night. Alphabet printed an 82 percent cloud number with a half-trillion-dollar backlog, the kind of result the whole AI trade was praying for, and the stock went down. That is not a company problem, it is a positioning problem, and the message is that the market has quietly switched from rewarding capital spending to fearing it, all while the fear-and-greed gauge reads Fear with the index a single percent off its record.

The discipline is to notice which way the money moved when the news was good. It did not chase the mega-cap that delivered, it rotated into the neoclouds that rent the shortage and into Buffett quietly tripling a stake while everyone else sold the print. Meanwhile the bond market keeps pricing the 2-year at cycle highs and the bears keep pointing at Oracle's junk-adjacent debt and the negative cash flow now bleeding through even the best names. When good news cannot lift a stock, the tape is telling you the price already holds the good news and then some.

So carry two facts into the open at once. The demand is real, the cloud backlog is real, and the robotaxi miles are real, and that is why the wreckage keeps finding buyers, but the financing is real too, and a market that fears a raised capex guide is a market running low on the thing that lifts prices from here. Own the shortage through the names that get paid first, let the spending stories prove they convert to cash before you add, and keep enough dry powder that if the bond market is right and the debt is the thing that cracks, you are the one buying it.
