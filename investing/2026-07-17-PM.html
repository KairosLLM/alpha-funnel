The worst week for chips in over a year ended with a violent reversal, and with Apple retaking the crown from Nvidia by doing the one thing that now looks smart, refusing to spend

The Philadelphia semiconductor index fell 12.5 percent on the week and Micron dropped below a trillion dollars before the tape ripped back green into the close. A cheap Chinese model became the excuse for the selloff, Wall Street rotated hard into tokenization, and the crypto crowd is loudly split on whether $57,000 was the bottom.

The morning looked like the top was in. By the close it looked like a shakeout. Both readings drew on the same week, and the gap between them is the whole story tonight. Here is the session recap, then five stories, then the single names.

Session recap. Friday was a rout that reversed. Per the Market list, the Philadelphia semiconductor index fell 12.5 percent on the week for its worst stretch in more than a year, and Micron closed below a trillion dollars for the first time in six weeks after shedding more than $400 billion since late June, more than Qualcomm and Marvell combined, per the Barron's recap.

Then the tape turned. The market opened deep red with the semis down three and a half percent, and by early afternoon the Dow had erased its losses and gone green while the memory names bounced double digits off the lows, per the Financial Markets list. Apple ended the day as the most valuable company in the world again at $4.871 trillion, edging Nvidia's $4.837 trillion by falling half a percent on a day Nvidia fell nearly four, per the People list. Netflix sank 11 percent on its print and SpaceX fell below its IPO price as a Starship test aborted, now rescheduled for Monday.

1. The thing that cracked the market was a cheap Chinese model, and the panic outran the math. The trigger had a name. Per threadguy, Moonshot's new Kimi K3 hit number one on the front-end coding arena and carried a headline price of roughly 50 cents per million tokens against something like $56 for the top American model, and that number is what set off the capex-domino fear, that if US firms lean on cheap Chinese models the spending on advanced chips slows.

That fear is what took the memory complex apart, and it is also what reversed. Per the Market list, hedge funds spent the afternoon calling the bottom and buying the dip, and the DRAM fund that tracks Micron, SK Hynix, and Samsung bounced 12 percent off its lows after taking in $10 billion of new cash during the drawdown. The panic priced a demand collapse that the actual product, as the AI channel lays out tonight, does not yet justify. When the whole tape moves on one leaked price and then takes half of it back by the close, the move was positioning, not proof.

2. Apple won the day by being the company that refuses to play the spending game. The leadership flip is the tell of the whole rotation. Per the People list, Apple retook the title of most valuable company not by rising but by falling the least, and HSBC upgraded it to a buy with a $366 target, the market rewarding the one megacap that is not pouring hundreds of billions into AI infrastructure.

The logic threadguy named is that if intelligence is becoming a cheap commodity, the winner is whoever owns the device it plugs into, per that stream. That is the exact inverse of the trade that led all year, when the capex spenders were the leaders and Apple was the laggard for underinvesting. On a day the market got scared that the spending might not pay off, it ran to the one name that never made the bet.

3. While Wall Street sold AI, the plumbing of a fully digital market quietly went live. The rotation had somewhere to go, and it was tokenization. Per Blockworks, the Depository Trust and Clearing Corporation, which holds more than $114 trillion in securities, ran its first live production trades of tokenized stocks and Treasuries on Wednesday, with JPMorgan, Vanguard, BlackRock, and the NYSE among more than thirty firms tokenizing real assets like the Invesco QQQ, Microsoft shares, and Circle stock.

This is the institutional version of the crypto trade, and it is why the rotation names are the picks and shovels of on-chain finance. The full service does not launch until October and rests on a temporary regulatory letter, so it is a rollout and not a finished system, but the direction is set. Per Arbitrage Andy, the money leaving crowded AI semis is rotating toward Chainlink, Ondo, and Aave, the tokenization rails, and even the retail version of the trade, per a ValueInvesting post, is a list of non-AI compounders like Mercado Libre, Uber, and Booking rather than another chip.

4. Galaxy Digital is the cleanest way to see the AI selloff and the crypto rotation collide. One stock sits on both sides of the trade. Per Mike Novogratz on Prof G Markets, his Galaxy Digital is building a Texas data center campus called Helios with 1.6 gigawatts approved and another gigawatt in the queue, which he frames as roughly $75 billion of contracted capex and potentially over $100 billion fully built, alongside a crypto-trading business he guided to $90 to $100 million of quarterly profit.

The catalyst is close. Per a Milk Road breakdown, Galaxy reports earnings at the end of the month, its 800 megawatts already contracted to CoreWeave imply about a billion dollars of revenue at 90 percent margins, and the analyst pegs fair value near $57 against a stock around $24. That analyst holds it as a fifth of his portfolio and both sources are the same promotional house, so weigh the target accordingly, but the structure is real, a crypto balance sheet stapled to a data-center landlord at the exact moment both trades are in play.

5. The crypto crowd is loudly split on whether the bottom is already in. Two credible framings point in opposite directions. Per the Benjamin Cowen cycle memo, Bitcoin near $65,000 is most likely not at its low yet, with his day-count model pointing at an October bottom, August and September negative in all three prior midterm years, and the bull case confirmed only on two weekly closes above the 50-week average near $86,500.

The other side says the bottom already printed. Per Lark Davis, whales bought a claimed 270,000 Bitcoin near $59,000 in what he calls the largest on-chain accumulation spike ever, long-term-holder metrics sit at 2018 and 2022 bottom levels, and the June wave of record ETF outflows is already flipping back to inflows. That clip is heavy with paid-group promotion, so treat the specific figures as his, but the disagreement itself is the signal. Novogratz splits the difference, calling a $60,000 to $80,000 range for the rest of the year and noting the speculative crowd has left crypto for sports betting, zero-day options, and Korean chip stocks, per that interview.

Single names to track.

  • $AAPL (Apple). Retook the most-valuable-company title at $4.871 trillion by falling half a percent while Nvidia fell four, upgraded to buy at HSBC with a $366 target, per the People list.
  • $MU (Micron). Closed below a trillion dollars for the first time in six weeks, down more than $400 billion since late June, then bounced with the group into the close, per the Barron's recap.
  • GLXY (Galaxy Digital). The dual crypto-and-data-center play, Helios at 1.6 gigawatts approved with 800 megawatts contracted to CoreWeave, earnings at month-end, per Milk Road.
  • $ARM (Arm Holdings). Record royalty revenue near $2.6 billion, up 21 percent, with version-nine chips carrying double the royalty rate of version eight across 350 billion cumulative devices, per this breakdown.
  • $NOW (ServiceNow). Subscription revenue near $3.7 billion, up 22 percent, with its Now Assist agents embedded across the workflow layer, per this breakdown.
  • Solstice. The Honeywell materials spinoff is buying Element Solutions for $10 in cash plus half a share, a roughly $24 billion combination funded with a $4.5 billion bridge that the analysts call a reason for caution, per this review.

Psychology. The market handed you whiplash and called it a verdict. The Philadelphia chip index fell 12.5 percent on the week and Micron lost a trillion-dollar handle, and then hedge funds bought the afternoon and the Dow closed green, which means the same session that felt like the top to a seller at the open felt like a gift to a buyer at two o'clock. The lesson is that a move built on one leaked model price and a wall of forced momentum selling is a move that can reverse in an afternoon, and it did.

The tell of the day is where the crowd ran when it got scared. It ran to Apple, the company that spent the least, and it ran to tokenization, the trade with a catalyst rather than a crash, while it abandoned the memory names it worshipped a month ago. Per Novogratz, the speculative money has already left for sports betting and zero-day options, and equity leverage is at a record high above the 2007 and 1999 peaks, the condition that makes both the crash and the snapback so violent.

So the discipline into next week is to refuse the single-day story in either direction. The Cowen memo says the Bitcoin low is probably still two months out while Lark Davis says whales already bought it at $59,000, and both cannot be right, so let the level prove itself rather than picking a side on conviction. The chips are not worth 12 percent less because of one Chinese model, and they were not worth the June highs either. Own the businesses whose demand is real, let the leverage finish washing out, and remember that the tape that scared you at the open paid the buyer who waited for the close.
