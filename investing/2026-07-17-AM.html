Taiwan Semiconductor told everyone the demand runs to 2030 and the chips sold off anyway, because this is a momentum unwind and momentum does not read earnings

TSMC raised its spending and guided growth above 30 percent with visibility into 2030, yet Micron has shed $459 billion in fourteen days and the tech-momentum factor just had its worst stretch in 27 years. Netflix capitulated after the close, SpaceX broke its IPO price as a Starship test scrubbed, and Apple quietly printed a record high.

MacroStocksCrypto

Yesterday the lesson was that beating every number does not save a stock in a momentum unwind. This morning Taiwan Semiconductor made the case even harder to ignore, and the tape still is not listening. Here is the pre-open picture, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. This is a Friday opex, and the setup is capitulation meeting good fundamentals. Per the Stocks list, TSMC raised its 2026 capital budget to a range of $52 to $56 billion and guided revenue growth above 30 percent, and chips fell anyway, with the tech-momentum factor down 35.9 percent over 17 days, its worst in the index's 27-year history, per the Financial Markets list.

Netflix reported after Thursday's close and washed out near $68, Apple printed a new all-time high as the best Magnificent Seven name of the year, and SpaceX closed below its $135 IPO price at $131.11 while a Starship test scrubbed on the pad. Korea's market was closed, Japan fell about 4 percent overnight, oil is firmer on the sixth straight night of US strikes on Iran, and June inflation came in cool.

1. TSMC gave the AI bulls their cleanest talking point in weeks, and the market walked right past it. The reframe is that the most disciplined company in the chain just extended the runway. Per Jose Najarro's read of the call, TSMC lifted its full-year growth guide toward the 40 percent range, raised capex about $10 billion, and said demand visibility now runs clearly into 2028, 2029, and 2030, while deliberately declining to overbuild the way it did in past cycles. The bull case is that rationing supply makes the cycle last longer rather than blowing off. Treat the exact figures as a viewer's recap of the call rather than the filing.

The problem is that none of that is what moved prices. The same session saw TSMC down 2 percent, Nvidia down 2 percent, Broadcom and Intel off more than 5, and Micron down 6, per the Stocks list. Micron alone has now shed $459 billion in market value in fourteen trading days despite an 80 percent operating margin and its entire 2026 high-bandwidth memory output already committed to Nvidia, per the Polymarket list. When the best fundamental news of the month lands on a stock down six percent, you are not in a fundamentals tape.

2. Netflix capitulated into its own print, and that is the setup traders are circling. The most-watched washout of the week has a clean structure. Per the Stocks list, Netflix reported after Thursday's close having already collapsed to around $68 on a multi-month slide, and the read from Mel Mattison is a textbook capitulation, a washed-out name reporting on a Thursday so the next day's options expiration forces volume through a flush.

The fundamental footnote is its own small controversy, with Netflix disclosing that roughly 300 of its shows and films used generative AI in production this year, per the Polymarket list. The trade and the theme are separate here. A capitulation flush is a setup for a bounce, and a studio quietly automating 300 productions is a longer story about margins that the panic is not pricing.

3. The real fear under the tape is that the AI demand is partly financing itself. The bearish thesis getting louder is circularity. Per a widely shared ValueInvesting post, Nvidia has invested in IREN, which buys Nvidia chips, taken a stake in CoreWeave, a major Nvidia buyer, and bought into Intel, so the worry is that vendor capital is doing a lap and coming back booked as demand, against roughly a quarter-trillion dollars of AI bonds issued this year. Those figures are the poster's rather than sourced, but the pattern is the one every bull has to answer.

The cleaner version of the question is Amazon. Per another writeup, Amazon is spending about $200 billion in capex this year against roughly $148 billion of operating cash flow, a $52 billion gap, betting AWS growth of 28 percent and its own Trainium chips at a $20 billion run-rate justify it. The counterweight is the oldest argument in the book, and a retail deep-dive attributes it to Warren Buffett around a claimed $31 billion Alphabet stake, that this is a race where a company that stops to catch its breath gets run over. That stake and its numbers are unconfirmed, so hold them loosely, but the logic is why the capex does not stop.

4. SpaceX broke its IPO price and its rocket scrubbed on the same morning. The most-owned new stock keeps finding new lows. Per the Financial Markets list, SpaceX closed below its $135 IPO price for the first time at $131.11, and as many as 911.5 million locked shares, about 1.4 times the entire IPO float, become eligible to sell after the first earnings report, and that is the supply overhang the bears keep pointing at. Michael Burry put it bluntly, that nothing in the S-1 suggests the company is worth a trillion dollars, let alone two.

The operational headline did not help. Per the People list, the Starship Flight 13 test scrubbed moments after ignition when several Raptor engines failed to start, pushing the retry out by days. The Prof G view frames the whole stock as an infrastructure play plus a non-top-tier AI model, with Starlink the real asset but facing the same brutal economics as terrestrial telecom once rivals add capacity, per that discussion. A falling stock before the lockup even opens is the tell that supply, not story, is in charge.

5. Crypto is quietly building a base while the equity crowd capitulates. The two most credible cycle reads agree on the level. Per Benjamin Cowen, Bitcoin near $64,000 is about 100 days from where the last two cycles bottomed on his day-count model, pointing at a fourth-quarter low, and per David Duong, the former Coinbase research head, the confluence of support sits at $56,000 to $58,000, already touched, with the bottom either in or very close.

The structural story is Robinhood Chain, which launched last week and went straight into the top five decentralized exchanges by volume, with about 28 million customers and $300 million of stablecoins on it, per Duong. Real assets are trading there too, with tokenized versions of SpaceX, gold, and oil changing hands on-chain, per this rundown. The caution is that Bitcoin ETFs just saw record monthly outflows near $4.5 billion, so the base is being built on capitulation rather than demand, per the Crypto list. Duong's framing is the useful one, that this looks closer to 1996 than 1999.

Psychology. The market spent the week teaching one idea from every angle, and today it wrote it in bold. Taiwan Semiconductor told you demand is booked into 2030, and Micron still bled $459 billion in fourteen days, which means the selling has nothing to do with whether the AI buildout is real and everything to do with a momentum factor unwinding from the 100th percentile of positioning. When a stock cannot rally on the best possible news, the crowd is not weighing the company, it is being forced out of a trade.

The tell of a capitulation is that the fundamentals and the price point in opposite directions at once. Netflix is washed out to $68 the same week it disclosed automating 300 productions, SpaceX is breaking its IPO price before a single insider is even allowed to sell, and Bitcoin is printing record ETF outflows right where two independent cycle models say the low forms. Each of those is a story of forced supply, not broken value, and forced supply is the thing that exhausts itself.

So the discipline into a Friday opex is to stop trying to catch the exact bottom and start separating the ones being sold from the ones being abandoned. The circularity fear is real and worth respecting, but Buffett's answer, if the writeup has it right, is that the spenders have no choice, so the demand does not vanish because the stocks fell. Let Netflix print, let the Micron selling find a seller of last resort, and remember that Goldman's own history says selloffs this violent average a gain the very next week. Own the bottleneck and the base, and let the momentum crowd finish being carried out.
