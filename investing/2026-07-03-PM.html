The memory bulls spent all day answering Burry, and Asia already voted with a green tape

Micron is now the third-largest name in the Nasdaq-100 with record numbers behind it, the AI-capex bear case from this morning got its counterpunch, and Bitcoin's bottom-callers got louder even as the ETF money kept walking out the door.

StocksMacroBitcoinCryptoBiotech

This morning the story was Michael Burry leaning on the most crowded trade on the board. By this afternoon the other side had shown up with receipts, and the overseas tape had already picked a side. Markets are closed here for the holiday, so none of this trades until Monday, which gives you the weekend to decide whether the bounce is real or a trap.

The week ahead, and what actually matters at Monday's open. US cash and options markets were shut today, Friday July 3, for Independence Day, and reopen Monday July 6. Four things to pre-load. First, whether the memory names ($MU, $SNDK, $MRVL) follow Asia's Friday bounce or fade it. Second, the SK Hynix US listing: the Korean memory giant plans roughly a $29B Nasdaq ADR (a US-listed proxy for a foreign stock) around July 10 under ticker $SKHY, which puts a fresh pure-play memory bet in front of American money.

Third, $SPCX joins the Nasdaq-100 on July 7, which forces index funds to buy it. Fourth, the calendar is loud into the week: GPT-5.6 is priced to land as soon as Tuesday, the crypto Clarity Act has a coin-flip shot at a July vote, and Bitcoin has to prove the bottom-callers right or wrong at the $62K to $63K line.

1. Micron is now the third-biggest name in the Nasdaq-100, and the bulls spent all day making Burry look early. The counter to this morning's short case is not a vibe. It is a stack of numbers. Micron posted record fiscal Q3 revenue of $41.5B with adjusted EPS of $25.11 against a $20.49 estimate, and CEO Sanjay Mehrotra told Bloomberg the company can only meet "about 50% to about two-thirds" of key-customer demand right now, with no real new supply until 2028. Wolfe Research pegs Micron's DRAM gross margins above 90%.

The structural piece is that this cycle runs on two legs at once. AI servers pull memory twice, once as HBM (high-bandwidth memory, the expensive stacked chips bolted onto the GPU) and again as ordinary high-capacity DRAM feeding the rest of the box. That is why Wolfe calls this "more structural than the usual memory spike." $MU is now the third-largest weight in the Nasdaq-100 at roughly 4.9%, ahead of Microsoft and Amazon, so index money has to own it whether it loves the story or not.

And Asia already moved. On the inbox tape, SK Hynix closed up 11%, Samsung up 8%, and the Kospi up 6% on Friday. My read: I said this morning I still think the durable-shortage story wins, and today's numbers plus the overseas bounce are the bull case answering the bell. Burry short into a $1,000 print can still be right on a wobble, so let Monday's follow-through tell you whether to add, and respect that H1 already ran this group vertical ($SNDK finished the half up 764%, $MU up 301%, $WDC up 278%).

2. The AI-capex bear case from this morning got its counterpunch by the afternoon. At 5:30am the story was three voices calling the buildout a bubble. By the close the other side had answered on every front.

  • Meta's own numbers say the panic is old. The Meta capex breakdown is the sober version. FY2025 capex hit $69.7B, about 35% of revenue, and free cash flow fell 15% to $46.1B. But Meta did this exact thing in 2022, when a capex surge dropped free cash flow to $19B and the stock under $90 before it ran to $790. A discounted-cash-flow model (valuing the stock on future cash discounted back to today) says Meta is worth $659 if the buildout is temporary and $160 if 35% capex is permanent. That gap is the whole debate in one number.
  • The compute-glut story got refuted by the people building it. On the Market list, Meta AI chief Alexandr Wang said a model in training, codenamed Watermelon, is already matching GPT-5.5 on an order of magnitude more compute than its predecessor. SemiAnalysis went further and said Meta's data-center spend will accelerate and 2027 capex will be "shockingly high." Bill Ackman called the stock "very cheap." That is the opposite of a company that thinks it overbuilt.
  • Nvidia quietly changed how it gets paid. Nvidia rolled out a revenue-share and credit-support model for the neoclouds (the rent-a-GPU firms like CoreWeave). Instead of a one-time chip sale, Nvidia now takes a cut of the cloud revenue on that capacity. That turns a boom-bust hardware sale into a recurring stream, and it is a direct tailwind for $CRWV, $NBIS, and $IREN, all of which got flushed 12% to 17% yesterday on the Meta scare. $IREN alone is sitting on a $9.7B Microsoft contract and a $3.4B Nvidia one.

The honest close is that both mornings can be true. The bears are right that the trade is levered and crowded. The bulls are right that the buyers keep buying. When a name like $IREN gets sold 17% on a narrative that its biggest customers just contradicted, that is where the dip-buyers live.

3. Tom Lee raised his target to 8,000 and told you to expect a scary autumn first. Fundstrat's Tom Lee lifted his year-end S&P 500 target from 7,700 to 8,000, built on 2027 earnings of $400 times a 20x multiple. His real point is that the market is cheaper now than in January despite being up 9%, because earnings estimates rose faster than price (the forward multiple actually fell from 19.4 to 18.4). The S&P sits near 7,300, so 8,000 is another 10%.

The catch is he expects a "very severe correction" in the fall before that, and he named the triggers. New Fed chair Kevin Warsh is tearing up forward guidance (the Fed's practice of telegraphing its next move), which leaves the market without its usual script. The SpaceX share unlock this fall dumps a wall of new supply onto a stock with only a $90B float. Iran and the Strait of Hormuz keep an oil-shock tail alive. And margin debt (money investors borrow against their portfolios to buy more) is up 55% year over year, the fifth-largest jump in about seventy years, which historically front-runs a drawdown. Take the bull target, but keep some dry powder for the air pocket he is describing.

4. Bitcoin's bottom-callers got louder, and the ETF money kept ignoring them. The chart crowd piled in today. An on-chain "dorito" indicator that last flashed at the FTX bottom lit up again on the inbox tape, the monthly RSI (a momentum gauge, now its most oversold reading ever) is stretched, and Lark Davis notes 270,000 BTC were accumulated near $59K, the single largest accumulation event on record. Standard Chartered is still carrying a $500K long-term BTC target and $40K on ETH.

Against all that, the flows say wait. Bitcoin ETFs have bled for weeks, with BlackRock's fund alone posting ten straight days of outflows totaling $2.24B, and over $11B has left the ETF complex since the peak. The cleanest floor framework came from Charles Schwab, which prices Bitcoin off miner economics: the cheapest, best-run miners produce a coin near $60K, and that production cost has marked the bottom in past bear markets.

Benjamin Cowen is the patient-bear counterweight worth holding in mind, with total crypto market cap near $2.125T and a call for a lower low later this year before the real move into 2027. My read is unchanged from this morning. Accumulate the dips you already wanted, respect the $60K miner floor as your line, and let the ETF flows turn green before you believe the bounce.

The thesis is a rotation from AI infrastructure to AI application, and healthcare is the first place that spend actually earns its keep. It trades at 377x sales, a pure belief stock, but the government validation is real. Analysts carry a strong-buy with targets implying it could double. Satellite-sized position only.
