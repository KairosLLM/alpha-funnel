The market punished the best earnings it got, sending Tesla down 14 percent in its worst reaction in years and Alphabet down 7 percent in its biggest drop since 2008, and erasing $900 billion on a day the news was arguably fine

Google grew cloud 82 percent and still took seven analyst price-target cuts, because the spend keeps rising and the cash keeps leaving. Oil broke back above $100 on Houthi tanker strikes, jobless claims hit their lowest since 1969, and the fear gauge fell to 9, lower than it read during the FTX collapse.

A Thursday that turned a decent set of prints into a rout. The tape sold the winners hardest, the fear gauge cratered, and the bears found their proof in the debt underneath the whole build. Here is the session recap, then five stories.

Session recap. The reaction to last night's earnings was violent. Per the Financial Markets list, Tesla fell about 14 percent in its second-largest earnings reaction ever and Alphabet dropped 7.6 percent in its worst earnings response since July 2008, even though Google grew cloud 82 percent, because the same list notes that Alphabet's record $112 billion in net income was 87 percent unrealized markups on its SpaceX and Anthropic stakes, leaving only about $33 billion from the actual business. Per the Stocks list, the stock still drew seven analyst price-target cuts on the day, from firms including Piper, UBS, and Raymond James, on the first negative free cash flow in more than 20 years and a raised capex guide.

The macro turned against risk at the same time. Per the Kalshi list, the S&P erased more than $900 billion in value, the Nasdaq 100 fell more than 2 percent, weekly jobless claims came in at 187,000, the lowest since 1969, and gold overtook US Treasuries as the world's largest reserve asset. Per the Polymarket list, Brent crude surged back above $100 for the first time since May as the fear over the Strait of Hormuz deepened. A strong labor market that keeps a Fed hike live, oil breaking $100, and the market selling its best names is the exact cocktail that ends a bounce.

1. The tell of the day is that the strongest print took the hardest hit. The verdict was not about the numbers, it was about the price. Per the Stocks list, Alphabet fell more than 5 percent even after cloud grew 81.8 percent with operating income tripling to about $9 billion and a $514 billion backlog, and it caught an EU antitrust fine of $1.02 billion on top of the print. Tesla was worse, per the same list, down about 13.5 percent to near $323 in its worst single day in over a year, with Samsung briefly worth more than the whole company after a quarter that missed on margins at $0.33 a share.

The pattern is what matters. Per the Market list, the memory and semiconductor names actually held up while the broad Nasdaq fell 2 percent, with Micron carrying price targets from $1,550 to $1,750 and the whole memory complex bid, so this was not a sector-wide flush. It was a rotation out of the crowded mega-caps that everyone owns and into the shortage names, the same divergence that has run for weeks, and a market that sells an 82 percent cloud number is telling you the good news was already in the price.

2. The bear case got its hard number, and it is $1.65 trillion of hidden debt. The most substantive bear thesis of the day is about the balance sheet. Per Prof G Markets, a Nikkei investigation found that Alphabet, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle now carry roughly $1.65 trillion in off-balance-sheet debt against $1.35 trillion reported, with Meta's hidden debt about three times its visible debt and Oracle's up roughly thirtyfold in four years. Ed Zitron walks the mechanism, that the data centers are financed through special-purpose vehicles where outside investors like Pimco and Blue Owl own 80 percent so the debt never hits the parent's books, and he calls them the AI bubble's version of the CDOs with an Enron parallel.

The scary part is the math on whether it ever pays back. Per that show, Zitron estimates real AI compute demand at about $120 billion a year, while building out the roughly 130 gigawatts of planned capacity would require $1.68 trillion in annual compute revenue to service, against a global software industry under $800 billion. Per the Stocks morning list, Michael Burry is making the same point, that the off-balance-sheet obligations of the five giants grew eightfold in four years to $1.65 trillion with Oracle hiding $273 billion tied to Stargate and OpenAI. Last night Alphabet's cash actually went negative, and that is the first time the thesis showed up in the numbers of the best name.

3. Oil above $100 is the macro story now, and it is why the Fed is stuck. The commodity is back in the driver's seat. Per the Polymarket list, Brent surged above $100 from $71 at the start of the month as the Houthis struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea and Trump signaled he is close to a large strike on Iran, and per the Stocks list prediction markets now show a three-way split on Fed policy this year between two hikes, one hike, and none.

That is the vise the market is caught in. Per the Financial Markets list, jobless claims at a 1969 low mean the labor market is too strong to force a cut, so an oil shock that reignites inflation leaves the Fed leaning toward hiking into a slowing tape. Gold overtaking Treasuries as the largest reserve asset, per the Kalshi list, is the quiet vote of no confidence underneath it all, the world reaching for the asset that has no counterparty while the bond market prices tighter money and the war keeps bidding crude.

4. Where the value is now is the beaten-down names, and the list starts with Netflix. The flip side of a rout is that things get cheap. Per Chip Stock Investor, Netflix trades below $70 at under 20 times forward earnings, the cheapest of the entire bull market, on 325 million subscribers and a 33 percent operating margin, and the case is a reprice from growth stock to value stock rather than a broken business. Per Invest with Henry, the same logic runs through a basket of ad-driven names, with Meta at $640 growing revenue 33 percent, Palantir up 85 percent with US commercial up 133 percent, and Amazon's AWS reaccelerating to 28 percent growth.

The infrastructure side has its own value pitch. Per Rick Orford, Nebius is building specialized AI factories that earn off the same GPUs for years at high utilization rather than selling them once, with named agreements from Meta and Microsoft and backing from Nvidia, and per the Crypto list it just installed the first Vera Rubin rack in Finland while trading near $220 against a $300 target. The through-line is that a day like today hands you the names you wanted at prices you did not expect, and Netflix at a value multiple is the cleanest of them.

5. Crypto sentiment just hit levels last seen at the bottom, and the signals agree. The fear in crypto is now extreme. Per Lark Davis, Bitcoin sits at $65,500 against its 200-week average near $68,500 with the fear-and-greed index at 9, lower than the 12 it printed during the FTX collapse, and he argues this bear market is nothing like 2022 because there is no Terra, no 3AC, no FTX, just a drawdown with rates set to fall under a Warsh Fed. Ethereum inflows are the tell he watches, with $72 million into ETH funds equal to a $400 million Bitcoin day on a market-cap basis.

The high-timeframe work says the low is close in both price and time. Per the Bottom Signals report, Bitcoin's realized price sits at $53,000 and only 12 percent of its history has traded below that cost basis, its relative strength versus the Nasdaq is the most oversold on record, and the cycle clock points to a low by late November 2026 with a new high before February 2028. The catalyst that could turn it is legislative, and per Blockworks the CLARITY Act now has a Senate floor vote committed before the recess around August 7, though the deepest prediction markets still price passage below 50 percent.

Psychology. Read today for what it actually was. Alphabet delivered the strongest cloud number in the market and fell 7 percent, Tesla had its worst earnings day in years, and the tape erased $900 billion on news that, taken plainly, was not a disaster. When the crowd sells the best print this hard, the problem is not the companies, it is that everyone already owned them and everyone had already priced the good news, so there was nobody left to buy it.

The mood underneath has tipped into something close to capitulation. The fear-and-greed gauge fell to 9, lower than it read the week FTX imploded, Michael Burry is short and quoting the fiber bubble, and Ed Zitron is walking through $1.65 trillion of hidden debt while the bond market prices a Fed that may hike into the slowdown. That is a crowd that has stopped believing, and the signals from the Bottom Signals work say Bitcoin is the most oversold it has ever been against the Nasdaq, the kind of reading that historically sits near a low and not a top.

So hold the tension honestly. Extreme fear is usually closer to a bottom than a top, and the memory names holding while the mega-caps bled tells you the rotation is orderly rather than a panic, but oil above $100 and a Fed leaning toward a hike are real risks that a sentiment reading cannot wish away. Buy the beaten value where the cash flow is real, like Netflix at a value multiple, keep the size small into an oil shock you do not control, and remember that the day the crowd gives up on the best names is the day you want to be adding, not the day you want to be the last one selling.
