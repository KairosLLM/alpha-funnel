Nvidia is sitting at a decade-low valuation while every overnight list screams that compute demand has never been higher

The memory trade grew a twin this weekend as Jensen Huang mapped a path to a trillion in revenue, five hyperscalers committed $700 billion to build in 2026, and Bitcoin defended $60,000 for the fourth time since February. Here is the week ahead.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

The overnight lists all pointed at the same gap. AI compute demand is running at numbers nobody has seen, and the stock most levered to it is trading like the story is over. That disconnect, not any single headline, is the thing to carry into Monday. Below it, the memory trade added new names, Bitcoin sat at one price with three different year-end targets stapled to it, and the money quietly rotated.

The week ahead. US markets were closed Friday and reopen Monday July 6. Four things to pre-load. First, the FOMC minutes land Wednesday July 8 at 2pm ET, and every line gets read for hike risk now that a July move is live. Second, the SK Hynix US listing is still on deck around July 10, a fresh pure-play memory bet in front of American money. Third, the levels: per Stocks with Josh, SPY needs a weekly close above 755 to confirm the next leg up and holds its trend only above 740, with a close below 740 opening an air pocket down toward 679. Fourth, the macro is genuinely two-sided now, because the same Kevin Warsh who put a July hike back on the table is also arguing that AI is deflationary, and Wednesday's minutes are where that fight shows up.

1. Nvidia at a decade-low valuation is the week's real tell. Per the Market list, $NVDA is sitting at fresh multi-year-low valuation against $MU, $INTC, and $AMD, with Shay Boloor flagging it as a decade low. Set that against what Jensen Huang is actually saying: per the People-to-follow list, a 1GW AI factory that costs about $55B today is heading toward $100B, roughly half of it flowing to Nvidia, against McKinsey's 156GW of AI demand by 2030 and a path to $1T in annual revenue.

The demand side is not a forecast anymore. Per the Crypto list, the five hyperscalers are spending $700B on AI infrastructure in 2026, six times their 2022 pace, and Wells Fargo models $2.47T of capex across 2026 to 2028. When the buyer of a company's chips is committing that much and the stock trades at a decade-low multiple, the market is telling you it does not believe the demand lasts. That is the bet to have a view on.

2. The memory trade grew a second and third name. Micron is no longer the whole story. Per the Stocks list, Gavin Baker is calling DRAM and HBM the single most important bottleneck in all of AI, with only three makers on Earth, $MU, SK Hynix, and Samsung, and Micron has now signed supply agreements covering about half its revenue at floor pricing already above last cycle's peak gross margins. The 2x Micron ETF ($DRAM, on Hyperliquid) is up 8 to 9% and above $65. The fresh wrinkle is where the extra upside sits: Marcos Milla argues $SNDK and SK Hynix have more room left than $MU, with Samsung the laggard, and that is why Leopold Aschenbrenner's book carries a 14.9% $SNDK weight next to his 35% $NBIS.

3. Bitcoin is defending $60,000 while three houses argue the target. Per the Kalshi list, Bitcoin reclaimed $63,000, with Kalshi Crypto pricing just a 15% chance it sees $100,000 this year. The bottom is the actual debate. Per the People-to-follow list, Michael Terpin notes BTC breached its 200-week average and touched 59K a week ago, then lays out the split: Standard Chartered sees the bottom at 59K and 100K by year end, while Galaxy sees the bottom in October at 40K to 46K.

Zach Pandl puts support near $58K and hangs the cyclical bottom on the Fed pausing and the CLARITY Act passing. VanEck's Matthew Sigel, per the Crypto list, wants clients at full positions by October. And per the Financial Markets list, Peter Brandt is contemplating selling some Bitcoin into gold, calling for gold to gain substantially on BTC. My read stays the same as Friday: this is a price bear market on a fundamentals bull market, and the accumulation zone is below $60,000, not chasing $69,000.

4. The rotation under the tape is the part nobody is tweeting. Per the Financial Markets list, tech stocks are on track for the largest annual inflow in history while energy just saw a $3.2B outflow, the largest in two years, and semiconductors are flashing as very expensive. The St. Louis Fed adds the structural number: information-processing and data-center construction now make up about a third of all US business investment, the highest share since 1947, and the producer price index including semiconductors is up 19% from January to May. Per the Kalshi list, Chinese chip exports to Hong Kong hit a record $15B in May, up over 200% since early 2025. The money is voting for compute and against everything else, and that is exactly the kind of one-way positioning that gets violent when it turns.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $NBIS, the neocloud dip everyone is calling a gift. Per the Crypto list, Wayne Liang is buying the $NBIS, $IREN, $CRWV, and $CIFR selloff, arguing the $META excess-compute narrative behind it is simply false with enterprise AI adoption still under 30%. High-beta, but the contracts are real.
  • $SNDK, the memory name with the most room left. Per the Stocks list, Sandisk carries more upside than Micron on Marcos Milla's read and is Aschenbrenner's second-largest position at 14.9%. It sits up 1,781% over three years on the S&P 10x leaderboard, second only to Applovin.
  • $UBER, the super-app that actually earns. Per the Stocks list, Uber runs about $28 of operating profit per user at roughly 28x, with over $10B committed across about 30 autonomous partners and robotaxis targeting 15-plus cities by end of 2026. The profit-per-user frame is the cleaner way to own the platforms than the multiple alone.
  • $TEM and $LLY, the AI-biotech thesis to keep on a back burner. Per Lark Davis, Dario Amodei's biotech-renaissance comments point him at Tempus AI applying models to cancer data and Eli Lilly's pipeline. It is a thesis with no levels attached yet, so it is a name to watch, not a trade.
