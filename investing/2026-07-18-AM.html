A private company just teamed up with Jack Dorsey to make a run at PayPal, and the real target is not PayPal, it is Visa and Mastercard

Stripe, Block, and the buyout firm Advent put roughly $53 billion on the table for PayPal, and the argument is that combining 600 million accounts with stablecoin rails could finally build a third payments network. Meanwhile the memory selloff looked for a floor, and the loudest investors are warning that the AI spending itself is the risk.

MacroStocksCrypto

A quiet Saturday with one very loud deal and a market still digesting the worst week for chips in over a year. Here is the week ahead, then four stories.

Week ahead. The setup into Monday is a market trying to decide whether Friday's reversal was a bottom or a bounce. The memory complex spent Friday testing its lows and clawing back, big-tech earnings begin to land over the coming week with Alphabet first in line, and Korea's market reopens Monday after its holiday having missed the whole selloff. The through-line from the weekend's smartest voices is that the danger is no longer whether AI demand is real, it is whether the spending to meet it has become a furnace that burns cash faster than it returns it, a theme that runs straight through the PayPal deal and the chip tape alike.

1. Stripe, Block, and Advent are bidding for PayPal, and the prize is the payments duopoly. The weekend's biggest deal is an ambush on an incumbent. Per the All-In Podcast, Stripe, Jack Dorsey's Block, and the private-equity firm Advent are bidding about $53 billion, near $60 a share, for PayPal, with Block contributing $17 billion of equity, and the market expects the final price to land closer to $70.

The bull case, as Chamath Palihapitiya laid it out, is that this is the only combination that could actually challenge Visa and Mastercard. Stitch together PayPal's roughly 400 million accounts plus Venmo and Braintree, Block's Cash App, and Stripe's stablecoin rails from its $1 billion Bridge acquisition, and you get the scale to drive payment costs toward zero across a real third network. The skeptic in the room was David Sacks, who called PayPal a legacy product growing only about 7 percent a year, the same checkout it invented 27 years ago, so the deal only works if an AI-native operator can rebuild a business that has drifted for two decades.

2. The pattern under the deal is AI-native operators buying stale digital businesses. The framing worth keeping is bigger than one target. Per that discussion, David Friedberg called the PayPal bid the second dot on a line after Ryan Cohen's run at eBay, a wave of AI-first operators buying mature, founder-less, digital-native businesses that never captured their own AI potential.

The template he pointed to is the Italian roll-up firm Bending Spoons, which bought AOL, Vimeo, WeTransfer, and Evernote and runs them with young AI-first executives to cut waste and print cash. If that pattern holds, the takeaway for a portfolio is that the stale, cheap, cash-generating names the market wrote off as dead money become acquisition targets, and the operator matters more than the asset. Chamath even teased that the one person who should look hardest at a rival PayPal bid is Elon Musk, whose fingerprints are on the original.

3. The memory selloff hunted for a bottom, and the smart money is split on Micron. The single name at the center of the chip wreck is Micron. Per Stocks with Josh, Micron dipped to its only real support near $800 to $805 on Friday, bounced as high as $905, then faded to close at $848, so the level to reclaim and hold is $861 and the line that breaks the setup is $805.

The reason it matters is that two respected names are on opposite sides. Michael Burry turned bearish on Micron about two weeks ago and the stock fell 18 percent, while Bank of America upgraded the memory group on the argument that AI hardware spending is not slowing. That disagreement is the whole memory trade in one stock, and the tell to watch is whether the buyers who paid up for call options at the ask on Friday are early or trapped. This is the same money-burning-furnace question from a different angle, whether the capex that fills Micron's order book keeps flowing or finally pauses.

4. Ethereum got a roadmap and crypto got a liquidity warning, in the same weekend. The one genuinely new crypto item is a protocol plan. Per Lark Davis, Vitalik Buterin unveiled a roadmap called Lean Ethereum built on three pillars, quantum resistance, privacy, and long-term simplification, with the quantum piece framed as a problem to solve now rather than later. There are no numbers attached and the clip is promotional, so it is a direction rather than a catalyst.

The sober counterpoint is that there is very little money actually moving. Per threadguy, on-chain liquidity has thinned to the point where he estimates only $10 million to $50 million is really rotating, and that thinness is why the memecoin drama produces headlines without producing gains. A roadmap for the next 50 years is worth little in a week when the tape has no fresh buyers, and the honest read is that Ethereum's upgrade is a story for later, not for Monday.

Single names to track.

  • $PYPL (PayPal). The target of a roughly $53 billion bid from Stripe, Block, and Advent near $60 a share, with the market expecting closer to $70, per the All-In Podcast.
  • $MU (Micron). Held its $800 support and bounced to $905 before fading to $848, with Michael Burry short and Bank of America upgrading the group, per Stocks with Josh.
  • $NFLX (Netflix). Bought on the dip near its post-earnings low and bounced, though the structural bear case is that it cannot match YouTube's open creator model, per Stocks with Josh and threadguy.

Psychology. The weekend gave you two versions of the same fear. The PayPal deal is a bet that a 27-year-old business can be revived, and the memory selloff is a bet that a red-hot one can be broken, and both come down to whether you trust the spending behind the story. Per the All-In crew, the warning is that enterprise token spending is climbing so fast that a public-company finance chief is going to miss a quarter on it, and that is the first time the AI trade has been framed as a cost problem rather than a demand one.

The tell of the moment is disagreement among people who are usually right. Michael Burry is short Micron while Bank of America upgrades it, one side of the room calls PayPal obsolete while the other calls it a Visa killer, and Vitalik is planning for 2076 while there is barely $50 million moving on-chain today. When the sharpest investors split this cleanly, the honest move is to admit the outcome is not knowable from here.

So the discipline into a fresh week is to let the levels referee the argument rather than your conviction. Let Micron prove it can hold $861 before you call the memory bottom, let the PayPal price settle before you decide whether $53 billion was a floor or a ceiling, and treat the capex-burn warning as the real variable to track, because the day the spending pauses is the day both the deal math and the chip math change at once. Patience is cheap this weekend, and it is the only edge on offer.
