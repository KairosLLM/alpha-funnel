The whole market is holding its breath for tonight, because Alphabet and Tesla both report after the close and Alphabet's capital-spending line is the first real read on whether the trillion-dollar AI build is still working

Hedge funds have quietly sold tech in six of the last eight weeks while retail levers up and the beaten-down memory names rip, and the Kimi K3 shock from Friday has the market rethinking who actually wins the AI trade. IREN signed $2.8 billion of new cloud contracts, oil is back near $89, and gold has bled 30 percent from its high.

A Wednesday that hinges on one number after the bell. The tape has already sold the leaders and bought the wreckage, and now it waits for Alphabet to say whether the spending is paying off. Here is the pre-open preview, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. Everything about today points at the closing bell. Alphabet reports after the close tonight as the first major hyperscaler out, and the line the market actually cares about is the capital-spending guide, the read on whether the AI trade is still justified. Tesla reports the same evening as the other side of the barbell, a robotaxi story running maybe ten cars a city against a stock priced for far more.

Coming in, the tape is still digesting Friday's Kimi K3 shock that took the Nasdaq down about 1.5 percent, and the memory names that got crushed for three weeks have kept ripping, with Micron near $945 after Bank of America added it to its best ideas. Under that bounce the positioning is thinning fast. Per Trader Mayne, Goldman has hedge funds as net sellers in six of the last eight weeks through mid-July, the biggest eight-week outflow from tech in more than a decade, with fund tech exposure down to a 2026 low near 15.5 percent even as retail adds leverage.

The rest of the board is heavy. Gold sits near $4,000 and down about 30 percent from its high, oil is back near $89 with US gasoline over $4, and the odds of a Fed rate hike this year are still around 62 percent. The setup into tonight is a market that has pre-positioned for the print and now needs the capex number to tell it which side was right.

1. The AI cloud contract boom is real and it is landing in the second-tier names first. The cleanest growth story of the morning is a neocloud. Per Jose Najarro, IREN signed roughly $2.8 billion of new customer contracts and lifted its 2026 AI cloud run-rate revenue floor from $3.7 billion to more than $4 billion, with 85 percent of that already under contract and named customers including Microsoft, Nvidia, Perplexity, Figure AI, and Together AI. The company is moving from bare-metal rental into higher-margin managed cloud, funding it partly with customer prepayments that cover about 45 percent of the associated GPU capex, and targeting 480 megawatts of AI cloud by the end of this year and 1.2 gigawatts by the end of next.

The chip side of the same trade has a catalyst on the calendar. Per that video, Microsoft will deploy AMD's rack-scale Helios systems for frontier-model inference on Azure in the second half of this year, joining OpenAI and Meta, and AMD holds its own AI event Thursday. Najarro flags that the Nebius pop on Nvidia's disclosed 9 percent stake is stale news tied to a March warrant deal rather than a fresh bid, so the honest read is that the contracted revenue at IREN and the Azure win at AMD are the parts of this move with actual signatures behind them.

2. Kimi K3 broke the price of intelligence, and the trade it points to is compute, not the labs. The most important repricing of the week is not a stock, it is a cost curve. Per Prof G Markets, Charlie O'Neill of Baseten calls Moonshot's Kimi K3 a second DeepSeek moment, a roughly 3-trillion-parameter open model that beats some US flagships on benchmarks while costing about a third of Anthropic's top model, and Chinese open-weight models now hold the top five spots by weekly token usage on OpenRouter. His framing is that this is open source versus closed source rather than China versus America, and that lab margins rumored north of 80 percent compress toward 40 as the surplus flows to consumers and to whoever serves the inference.

That is why the Limitless read lands on the same place. Per Limitless, Kimi K3 runs about $0.50 to $1 per million input tokens against roughly $50 for a US frontier model, and Moonshot just paused new signups because it ran out of GPUs, the clearest sign that the constraint is compute rather than cleverness. Both takes reach the trade the same way, that infrastructure is the moat and the capital keeps flowing to the bottom of the stack, the same logic pushing inference toward simpler packaging where Intel's EMIB approach finally has a lane, per Chip Stock Investor.

3. The next bottleneck to trade is light, and the names are AAOI and Marvell. The most specific new thesis of the morning is optical. Per Milk Road, the big four hyperscalers are guiding to more than $700 billion of combined capex this year and Morgan Stanley now models $1.44 trillion by 2028, with networking running 15 to 18 percent of cluster cost and Goldman projecting co-packaged optics revenue climbing from about $1 billion this year toward $71 billion by 2028 as clusters move from copper to fiber.

The two named plays sit at opposite ends of the risk scale. Per that breakdown, Applied Optoelectronics grows its own lasers in-house against an industry laser shortage and has booked $124 million of cumulative 800G orders from one hyperscaler, but its top two customers are 82 percent of revenue, which makes it the high-beta way to own the theme. Marvell is the steadier version, a near-monopoly in 1.6-terabit optical DSPs that just bought the photonic-fabric startup Celestial AI and took a roughly $2 billion Nvidia investment, trading down about 45 percent from its June high with the analyst pegging a floor near $150. These are a promoter's picks, so size them as ideas rather than gospel, but the capex math underneath the theme is the same number Alphabet reports tonight.

4. Positioning is the fragile part, and the global unwind is already showing up in Korea and China. The risk under the bounce is that the people who have to sell are still selling. Per Trader Mayne, semis are now about 14 percent of US market cap, roughly double the dot-com peak, while hedge funds cut tech to a 2026-low exposure and retail adds leverage into the gap, the kind of split that snaps hard when it snaps.

The forced-selling is visible abroad. Per that stream, the 60-day correlation between Korea's Kospi and the Nasdaq has jumped to 0.46, the highest in years, roughly one in thirty working-age Koreans has been liquidated as margin balances hit a record 38 trillion won, and Chinese margin debt just posted its largest daily drop in about a decade with memory-chip names at the center of the liquidation. Per Capital Flows, the same fragility shows in a US market where the most-shorted stocks have squeezed for months on expanded liquidity, the sort of positioning that unwinds violently once the flow reverses. The lesson is that the AI trade has never been more global, so a margin call in Seoul now prints in Santa Clara.

5. Crypto is where the leverage already flushed, and the cash flow stories are getting sharper. The corner of the market that already took its pain is the miners. Per On-Chain Mind, Bitcoin near $66,000 sits below an estimated $76,000 cost of production, so the average miner is running a roughly 13 percent loss, hash rate is down about 18 percent in the worst drawdown since last November, and the Puell Multiple and back-to-back Hash Ribbon capitulation signals are printing in the zone that has marked past bottoms.

Two builders are turning that bear tape into revenue. Per Milk Road, Sid Powell's Maple Finance has pushed loans outstanding near an all-time high around $2 billion in the downturn and just wired its SYRUP token to rules-based buybacks that scale from 10 to 30 percent of revenue, while powering the new Robinhood Chain earn product for a platform with 30 million accounts. Per 0xSammy, the further-out bet is that AI agents become crypto's largest investor class and will only allocate into tokens with machine-readable ownership rights, and that is why projects like MetaDAO and Umia are racing to make what a token actually owns legible before the agents show up with capital.

Single names to track.

  • $IREN (Iren). Signed about $2.8 billion of new cloud contracts and raised its 2026 run-rate floor above $4 billion with 85 percent contracted, per Jose Najarro.
  • $AMD (Advanced Micro Devices). Microsoft will run its Helios rack-scale systems for inference on Azure in the second half, with AMD's own AI event Thursday, per Jose Najarro.
  • $AAOI (Applied Optoelectronics). Booked $124 million of cumulative 800G orders from one hyperscaler and grows its own lasers into a shortage, though two customers are 82 percent of revenue, per Milk Road.
  • $MRVL (Marvell). Near-monopoly in 1.6-terabit optical DSPs, down about 45 percent from its June high with a floor pegged near $150, per Milk Road.
  • Gold. Near $4,000 and down about 30 percent, with the bull-market support band near $3,800 and Benjamin Cowen looking for a low in the July-to-October window, per Benjamin Cowen.

Psychology. Notice what the tape has already done before the news arrives. Hedge funds have sold tech in six of the last eight weeks and cut exposure to a yearly low, retail has levered into the space they left, and the memory names that fell hardest have ripped back the fastest. That is a market that has pre-traded its own catalyst, and the danger in a setup like that is that the print becomes an excuse for whatever the positioning already wanted to do.

The Kimi K3 shock is the deeper unsettling thing, because it says the price of intelligence is falling faster than almost anyone modeled, and a falling price for the output is not automatically good for the companies that spent the most to build it. That is the question buried inside Alphabet's capex line tonight, whether the trillion dollars of planned spending compounds into a moat or into stranded capacity as the cost of a token collapses. The market that bought the memory dip and sold the mega-caps has already placed a quiet bet on that answer.

So hold both truths into the close. The contracted revenue at IREN and the physical bottleneck in optics and memory are real, and that is why the wreckage found buyers, but the forced liquidation running through Seoul and Shanghai is the reminder that leverage decides the tape when positioning is this stretched. Let the capex guide print before you decide the trade is fixed, keep the size small enough that a gap either way does not force your hand, and remember that the night the whole market agrees on what matters is exactly the night it tends to be wrong about which way it breaks.
