Bitcoin is sitting on the line it has not lost since 2023, and the two sides have never disagreed this loudly

Whales bought 270,000 coins into the drop while Citi cut its target and Strategy sold for the first time ever, the memory hike went from forecast to invoice, and the new Fed chair is quietly arguing that AI makes rate cuts easier.

It is a holiday weekend and nothing trades until Monday, so this is a morning to decide what you believe before the tape can bully you. The loudest argument on the board is Bitcoin at its 200-week average, where the accumulation data and the outflow data point in opposite directions at the same price. Under it sits a regulation story that finally has a real framework, and next to it a memory trade that just turned a price forecast into an actual invoice.

The week ahead, and what to pre-load before Monday's open. US markets were closed Friday July 3 for Independence Day and reopen Monday July 6. Five things to load. First, the FOMC minutes land Wednesday at 2pm ET, and after new chair Kevin Warsh started tearing up the Fed's script, every line gets read twice. Second, the SK Hynix US listing is still on deck for around July 10, a fresh pure-play memory bet in front of American money. Third, $SPCX joins the Nasdaq-100 on July 7 and GPT-5.6 is priced to arrive as soon as Tuesday.

Fourth, the crypto Clarity Act still has a coin-flip shot at a July vote. Fifth, a record $8.3T sits in money-market funds. That is either the fuel for the next leg up or a crowd that likes getting paid to wait.

1. Bitcoin lost its 200-week average, and the accumulation data and the outflow data are screaming past each other. Bitcoin is trading right at the 200-week moving average (the four-year trend line that has marked every major cycle low), the first time it has closed below it since 2023. Lark Davis makes the bull case with on-chain receipts. Whales accumulated roughly 270,000 BTC, about $16B, near the $60K zone, against only $5B of ETF net outflows, and the weekly RSI (a momentum gauge) is oversold for just the fourth time since 2012, with three of those prior four marking bottoms.

His key argument is that the one exception, 2022, only broke because Terra, Celsius, and FTX detonated a leverage chain that no longer exists in the same form. The reclaim line is clean: a weekly close back above roughly $63K puts the trend back on side.

The other side showed up with its own receipts on Swan's World Cup of Money. Citi cut its Bitcoin target to $82K and zeroed out its 12-month ETF inflow forecast, and for the first time in its history Strategy (formerly MicroStrategy) reportedly sold Bitcoin, which pokes a hole in the Saylor-never-sells thesis the whole treasury trade was built on. Kalshi bettors now put 55% odds on Bitcoin tagging $50K before $100K. My read: the accumulation is real and the miner-cost floor near $60K still holds, so I am accumulating the dips I already wanted and treating a weekly close under $60K as the signal to stop. The Strategy sale is the single fact that would flip me, so I want it confirmed, not rumored.

2. The ETH short is the most crowded trade in crypto, and that cuts both ways. Underneath the Bitcoin fight is a barbell the whales have all piled onto: long Bitcoin, short Ether. Per the Crypto list, a single 54,200 ETH short worth about $89.2M sits on Hyperliquid with a liquidation price of $1,674, and roughly $95.6M of ETH shorts were already liquidated in 24 hours. When one side of a trade gets this lopsided, the fuel for a violent squeeze is the crowd itself.

The tell to respect is that 33% of all ETH supply is now staked and growing by 50,000 coins a day, which quietly pulls float off the market even while the derivatives crowd leans bearish. I am not chasing the short here.

3. Crypto regulation finally has a real framework, and it is more radical than the market has priced. The positioning story is the Clarity Act, the crypto market-structure bill, and Kevin O'Leary frames its signing as the trigger for the largest capital inflow in Bitcoin's history, with trillions waiting on legal clarity. The substance under that hope is worth reading directly. In the SEC's own "Project Crypto" outline, Chairman Paul Atkins lays out a four-bucket token taxonomy where digital commodities, digital collectibles, and digital tools are explicitly not securities, and only tokenized versions of real stocks and bonds stay securities.

The radical part is a doctrinal shift: an investment contract can end. A token sold under one does not stay a security forever, and once the network decentralizes and the promises are fulfilled, later trades are no longer securities transactions. Atkins wants rules letting those tokens trade on CFTC or state-regulated venues, a "Regulation Crypto" exemption package, and single-license "super-apps," and he states plainly that most tokens trading today are not securities. My read: this is the fundamental case sitting under O'Leary's flood. If the Clarity Act codifies even part of this taxonomy, the compliance box that has kept the largest pools of institutional money on the sidelines finally gets checked.

4. The memory hike went from forecast to invoice, and the trade got fresh receipts. Yesterday this was Micron's record print against Burry's short. This morning it is the supplier sending the bill. Samsung has begun issuing verbal notices to raise DRAM prices (dynamic RAM, the standard memory in every server and phone) by around 20% quarter over quarter for Q3, per the Market list. A price hike you forecast is a thesis, and a price hike your supplier invoices is a fact.

The numbers underneath keep getting louder. Bernstein sees Micron's DRAM gross margins above 90% in 2026 and above 92% in the back half, and Milk Road's Stocks list carries a $4,000 target with Sanjay Mehrotra saying Micron can meet only half to two-thirds of demand and no new industry supply arrives before 2028. SK Hynix just posted a single quarter's profit of $26.15B, which tops its entire 2023 revenue, on 60% HBM (high-bandwidth memory, the stacked chips bolted to AI accelerators) share and a 72% operating margin, and it lists on the Nasdaq next week. Jensen Huang called Marvell the "next trillion-dollar company," with $MRVL guiding FY2027 revenue to $11.5B, up 40%.

The fragility to keep in view is the crowding. South Korea's leveraged-ETF assets hit a record near $45B, up 800% year to date and about 2.9% of free float, so the same names everyone loves are also where the levered money is stacked. When that unwinds it does not ask politely.

5. The new Fed chair's real argument is that AI makes cutting easier. Kevin Warsh is not just tearing up forward guidance (the Fed's habit of telegraphing its next move). Per the Stocks list, his framing is an AI-deflation thesis: frontier inference cost fell from roughly $30 per million tokens in early 2023 to pennies, and AI has added only about 0.3 point to core PCE (the Fed's preferred inflation gauge). He wants a "good family fight" at the FOMC about four weeks out, will not confirm a July hike, and wants a smaller balance sheet, leaning on the Greenspan 1990s analogue where productivity ran hot and inflation stayed tame.

The honest counter is that software prices are going the other way, with Microsoft 365 up 30%, Adobe up 50%, and Intuit up 45% on the year. My read: this is the rates story for the back half, and a chair who removes the script guarantees a wider reaction to every print.
