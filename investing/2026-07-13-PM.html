Korea tripped its circuit breaker and SanDisk printed the highest RSI ever recorded on any stock, and the memory blowoff still would not admit it was a blowoff

The session opened with a trillion dollars gone from Asia and $300 billion off the US at the bell, then Apple hit a record and rescued the tape. Trump put a 20 percent toll on the Strait of Hormuz, SpaceX broke below its deal price with a governance story underneath, and CPI lands tomorrow.

MacroStocksCrypto

The cracks the morning warned about arrived, and then the tape half-healed anyway. That gap between what broke and what closed is the whole story tonight. Here is the session recap, then five stories.

The close. It opened ugly. Roughly $1 trillion came off Asian markets overnight and Korea's KOSPI fell 8.95 percent into a circuit breaker, its worst since Lehman, with SK Hynix down more than 15 percent, and the US wiped over $300 billion at the open, per the Kalshi list. Then the megacaps carried it back. Apple printed a new all-time high above $4.7 trillion, the S&P clung to 750 where a $1.7 billion put wall sat as support, the Dow actually closed green, and the Nasdaq finished down only about 1.2 percent, per the Stocks and Market lists. Semis took the pain, with the $SOXX off 5 percent and gold slipping toward $4,000.

Two things set up tomorrow. June CPI (the inflation print that settles the July 29 Fed debate) lands in the morning alongside the launch of two leveraged SK Hynix ETFs, a 2x long and a 1x short, per the People list. And oil is now a policy story, not just a war one, after Trump declared the US the guardian of the Strait of Hormuz and put a 20 percent fee on all cargo moving through it.

1. The memory blowoff cracked, and the clearest signal is a record nobody should want. SK Hynix fell more than 15 percent in Seoul, its biggest drop ever, the day after ringing the Nasdaq bell on its new US listing, and the whole complex followed, with $SNDK down about 10 percent and Micron off 5 and a half. The tell is on the Lark Davis read: SanDisk's monthly RSI (a momentum gauge where 70 is considered overbought) hit 99.3, the highest reading ever printed on any stock in history. That is not strength, that is a parabola running out of buyers.

The setup into tomorrow is almost a dare. The two new leverage products launch at 4am, a 2x long and a 1x short on a name that just fell 15 percent, and Leopold Aschenbrenner is reportedly buying $7 to $10 billion of the SK Hynix ADR at a 10 percent premium into the drop, per the same stream. Somebody is going to use those ETFs to pick a bottom in a stock still in free fall, and the exchange in Seoul had to halt trading to slow the selling. Respect the level, not the leverage.

2. SpaceX broke below its deal price, and there is a governance story hiding under the buy ratings. The cleanest new idea tonight is a short-seller's story about SpaceX. Per Patrick Boyle on Prof G Markets, $SPCX joined the Nasdaq 100 on fast-track rules and 18 of 19 analysts slapped a buy on it the same week, yet the stock fell below its $135 IPO allocation price to a post-listing low near $140, down 34 percent from its peak, leaving every retail buyer underwater. It trades around 101 times sales while burning roughly $5 billion a quarter and growing only about 15 percent.

The part worth your attention is why the ratings are so uniform. The price targets run from Morgan Stanley's $300 to Raymond James at $800, which implies a $10 trillion company at 542 times sales on a projection of $4.9 trillion in AI revenue by 2035. Boyle ties the unanimity to money, since SpaceX has roughly $235 billion of spending to fund by 2030 and no bank writing a sell rating wants to lose the fees. His flag is that the SEC quietly terminated the 2003 research-analyst settlement about seven months ago, the rule that separated analysts from bankers after the dot-com scandals, and he expects something ugly to surface in these numbers. Whatever you think of the stock, that is the real story.

3. The whole AI trade is priced to 2028, and the next hyperscaler earnings are the referee. The framing that ties the memory crash to the bull case comes from Ben Bajarin, who has covered semiconductors since 2000. His argument is that the industry may cross $1 trillion in annual revenue this year, four years early, and that the growth is almost all price per chip rather than more units, because the foundries run at 70 to 75 percent utilization by design and cannot conjure new wafer capacity. The catch is that everything is priced to a 2028 scenario, so the binary signal is whether the hyperscalers' next earnings say they are not slowing capital spending and show free cash flow inflecting positive.

The demand-destruction counter showed up in the tape today. Micron and Samsung printed what Bajarin calls some of the most absurd earnings ever and their stocks fell anyway, and global smartphone shipments dropped 11 percent to the worst second quarter in 13 years, blamed directly on the memory shortage pricing consumers out, per the People list. Higher prices per chip are wonderful until they start killing the units.

4. Crypto shrugged off the war again, but Bitcoin quietly lost a line. Bitcoin barely moved on the strikes and the oil spike, holding around $62,000, but it lost the $64,000 level that had been support, per the Crypto list. The Milk Road desk splits on what that means: John sees a bathtub bottom, a long boring grind toward $57,000, while Martin turned more bullish on the logic that if a war only knocks $1,000 off, the weak hands are already gone. The structural support is real, with whales buying 270,000 Bitcoin worth about $16 billion, three times what the ETFs sold, a Fidelity model floor near $56,500, and spot ETF inflows turning positive at $197 million to end an eight-week outflow streak.

The louder crypto story is a rotation. Money is leaving the Solana names and pouring into the Robinhood Chain and Ethereum ecosystem, per Blockworks, with Jito, Jupiter, and Metaplex all down 15 to 18 percent while Uniswap, Arbitrum, and their peers ripped. Uniswap is the cleanest beneficiary, now doing $5.2 million a day in fees with its fee switch finally turned on, which makes the token a revenue-sharing asset for the first time.

5. Two fresh single names, one a pharma giant and one a part you have never thought about. The pick with the most conviction behind it is Eli Lilly as the AI reinvestment play, framed as the next Google. The case, per Milk Road: $LLY is a $1 trillion company guiding revenue from $65 billion to $83 billion in a year, throwing off about $10 billion in free cash flow from Mounjaro and Zepbound, and plowing it into its own data center with more than 1,000 Nvidia chips, an AI drug-discovery platform, and a partnership with Google's Isomorphic Labs, with $75 billion committed to deals since 2023.

The stranger idea is bearings. Per a Morgan Stanley note, bearings are the one robotics part that is architecture-agnostic, since every motor needs them no matter how the robot is designed, and the firm sees the robot-bearings market growing roughly 300 times through 2050, with OpenAI listing precision bearings as one of six critical components in its hardware manufacturing request. A humanoid needs more than 70 of them, the top six makers control over half the market, and the US-listed pure play in that oligopoly is Timken ($TKR).

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $SPCX (SpaceX). Below its $135 deal price at a post-listing low near $140, down 34 percent from the peak, on 101 times sales with a governance question over its unanimous buy ratings, per Prof G Markets.
  • $MRVL (Marvell). Per Rick Orford, record Q1 revenue near $2.4 billion with Q2 guided to $2.7 billion and a $55 billion custom-silicon market by 2028, though it trades near 97 times forward earnings. It fell 5.5 percent with the semis today.
  • $TSLA (Tesla). Jefferies raised its target to $400 from $375 on a Q2 delivery beat, and the stock broke to 392 support into the July 22 print, per the People list.
  • $LLY (Eli Lilly). The pharma-as-AI-reinvestment name, guiding to $83 billion in revenue and building its own compute, per Milk Road.
  • $TKR (Timken). The US-listed way into the Morgan Stanley robot-bearings thesis of 300-times growth through 2050, per the MS note.
  • $AMZN / $SHOP. Per Rick Orford, the AI-commerce pair, with AWS at $38 billion of quarterly revenue and $14 billion of operating income, and Shopify growing 34 percent on more than $100 billion of merchandise volume at a richer 82 times forward earnings.
  • Seasonality. Per ClearValue Tax, 2026 is historically the weakest year of the cycle (median 5 percent, positive only 58 percent of the time) while 2027 is the strongest (median 22.5 percent, up 91.7 percent of the time), an argument for positioning now for next year.

Psychology. The single most revealing number today was not a loss, it was a record. SanDisk's monthly RSI hit 99.3, the highest reading ever recorded on any stock, on the exact day SK Hynix fell 15 percent and rang the Nasdaq bell, and cash on the sidelines sits near a record-low 0.42 percent of market cap. That is what a top-of-cycle crowd looks like from the inside, all-in on a parabola, treating a circuit-breaker crash in Korea as a dip to buy rather than a warning to heed.

The tell is in what the crowd did with the megacaps today. It did not sell the news, it hid in Apple at a $4.7 trillion record and told itself the semis crash was contained, the same reflex that has paid all month. Meanwhile the people who study this for a living, Boyle on SpaceX's rigged ratings and Bajarin on a trade priced to 2028, are pointing at exactly the assumptions the crowd has stopped questioning.

So the discipline check into CPI is about the leverage. The 2x SK Hynix ETF launching tomorrow exists to let you bet bigger on a name that just fell 15 percent, and a 99.3 RSI is the market telling you the easy buyers are spent. If you are tempted to catch the memory knife or fade the oil spike on the open, size it like the SanDisk chart is a warning and not an invitation, keep $57,000 on Bitcoin and 750 on the S&P in front of you, and let the CPI print come before you decide the crowd was right to stay all-in.
