IBM just had the worst day in its hundred-year history by warning that its customers are moving money out of software and into AI hardware, and that is the bull case for the exact chips that got crushed this week

IBM pre-announced a big revenue miss and lost a quarter of its value, and the reason is not that AI spending is slowing, it is that it is shifting. Alphabet, Intel, and AMD all report or present this week into a tape where the smart money is buying the neocloud dip and calling the selloff a speed crash, not a top.

MacroStocksCrypto

A quiet Sunday with a loud read-through. The single most useful data point of the weekend is a legacy tech giant blowing up, because of what it says about where the money is going. Here is the week ahead, then five stories.

Week ahead. This is the week the AI-capex debate gets refereed by earnings. Per Jose Najarro, Alphabet reports Wednesday as the first hyperscaler read on capex and cloud backlog, Intel reports Thursday after the close, and AMD holds an AI event Thursday morning, all landing while the memory and neocloud names sit deep in a selloff, with CoreWeave down 15 percent and Nebius down 16 over five days. The technical backdrop is fragile, since the S&P fund opened and closed below 745 on Friday after needing to hold 755 for a new high, a breakdown by the chart, per Stocks with Josh. Korea reopens Monday after a leverage blowup and SpaceX flies its rescheduled Starship the same day.

1. IBM's crash is the clearest tell of the week, and it points at hardware. The number that reframes everything is a warning. Per Joseph Hogue, IBM pre-announced quarterly revenue of $17.2 billion, well below the Street, and the stock fell more than 25 percent for the largest single-day drop in its hundred-year history, wiping out roughly $70 billion, because customers told it they are shifting spend away from traditional software and toward AI infrastructure like chips, memory, and servers.

The read is that the money is moving, not disappearing, and that is bullish for the hardware that just sold off. Hogue's basket splits into the near-term hardware bounce, naming Broadcom, Micron, Marvell, and Arm, and the longer-term software winners that impose AI cost discipline, naming Cloudflare, ServiceNow, Datadog, and Palantir. It is a promotional channel with an options-course upsell, so weigh the picks accordingly, but the underlying signal is real and it comes straight from an enterprise buyer's own guidance.

2. The neocloud dip is the trade the informed money is actually buying. The most contrarian move of the weekend is buying the compute landlords into the fear. Per a Nebius breakdown, Nebius sold off as much as 17 percent in a session on reports Meta will rent out its own excess compute, yet Meta is Nebius's largest customer with a five-year deal worth up to $27 billion, and every new data center it builds is sold out before completion against a US compute demand Goldman sees more than doubling to 66 gigawatts next year.

The structural case is that the cost of building has exploded. Per a neocloud panel, the price of a one-gigawatt buildout jumped from roughly $50 billion to $100 billion in three months, which means only the largest neoclouds and hyperscalers can afford new capacity, and Nvidia is now backstopping the financing by guaranteeing minimum revenue so lenders will fund the clusters. That makes Nvidia both the landlord and the bank, a powerful moat and also the concentration risk the bears will point at when the custom-silicon projects from OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google mature.

3. The best macro read on the selloff calls it a speed crash rather than a top. The framing worth internalizing came from a serious voice. Per Jordi Visser, the Morgan Stanley tech-momentum factor fell about 40 percent in 18 days, the worst on record back to 1998, but he reads it as a momentum unwind driven by AI agents and volatility-control selling rather than a business-cycle break, the same forced mechanics that gave gold its biggest three-day move in 35 years and knocked Bitcoin down 40 percent.

The reason he stays bullish is the earnings underneath it. Of the first 43 S&P names to report, sales beat by 4 percent and earnings by 16, with JPMorgan surprising by 34 percent and both big banks at all-time highs while credit spreads sit near record tights, and that is not what a real top looks like. Visser bought back Micron on the thesis that AI equals memory, and rotated some proceeds into Bitcoin, Ether, and silver, and the distinction he draws from 2007 is the one that matters, that spreads are not widening and the economy is not rolling over, so this is rotation rather than systemic risk.

4. Crypto is building a bottom in the low fifties while the institutional wave forms behind it. Three independent reads converge on the same floor. Per an altcoin breakdown, the downside target for Bitcoin is roughly $53,000 with a bull flip near $82,600, and per the Milk Road weekend note, the on-chain confluence of realized price and long-term-holder cost sits at $50,000 to $53,000, the level where buying has historically paid.

The bigger story is who arrives next. Per Matt Hougan of Bitwise, the baton of Bitcoin's largest buyer has passed from retail to GBTC to Strategy and is about to reach institutional capital, the final boss, with the tell being Vanguard, an $11 trillion manager that refused crypto for years and just posted a job for a head of digital assets. The parallel bet is Ethereum as the Wall Street rail, where former BlackRock executives argue its real competitor is banks' legacy plumbing, not Solana, per that discussion. These are all promoters talking their book, so treat the timing as hope rather than fact, but the direction of institutional adoption is the one thing the bears cannot easily argue away.

Single names to track.

  • $MU (Micron). Needs to reclaim and hold $861 after closing at $848, the level the technicians and Jordi Visser are both watching as the memory bottom, per Stocks with Josh.
  • $NBIS (Nebius). Sold off 17 percent on the Meta-rents-compute fear despite Meta being its largest customer on a deal worth up to $27 billion, with capacity sold out before completion, per this breakdown.
  • $NVDA (Nvidia). The sovereign-AI angle, running its open Nemotron models inside Palantir for air-gapped government agencies, a moat that diversifies demand beyond the hyperscalers, per this read.
  • $SOUN (SoundHound). The model-independent voice-AI toll booth, revenue growing from $46 million in 2023 to $169 million in 2025 with Casey's now on 2,600 locations, though still unprofitable, per this breakdown.
  • $RDW (Redwire). The in-space manufacturing play at $335 million of 2025 revenue, with its roll-out solar arrays on the ISS and microgravity fiber, dependent on government timing, per this breakdown.

Psychology. Read the weekend against Seoul. The clearest emotional lesson is not on Wall Street, it is in Korea, where retail borrowed to chase the same semiconductor mania and roughly one in every thirty working-age Koreans got liquidated on the pullback, tripping the market's eighth circuit breaker of the year. That is what leverage into a crowded top actually does, and it is the same trade in a more degenerate form than the one the US crowd is running.

The tell of where we are is that the buy-the-dip reflex is already loud. Najarro is rotating from low-beta into high-beta neoclouds, the promoters are calling Bitcoin's bottom to the dollar, and the margin data flagged this weekend shows leverage at a level reached only twice before, ahead of the tech bubble and ahead of 2007. Buying weakness can be right and being early can still get you liquidated, and the Korean tape is the reminder that the second half of that sentence is the one that hurts.

So the discipline into a heavy earnings week is to size for the speed crash Visser describes rather than the recovery you hope for. IBM just told you the money is moving into hardware, and that is a reason to own the bottleneck rather than a reason to lever into it. Let Alphabet and Intel print, let Micron prove it can hold $861, and keep enough dry powder that the next forced-selling wave is your entry instead of your margin call. The crowd is buying the dip with borrowed money again, and Korea just showed you how that ends when the timing is wrong.
