Iran shut the strait overnight and the US answered with a blockade, so the cheap oil that gave the Fed cover just went away and the memory trade came roaring back

Micron is worth more than Berkshire again, SK Hynix ran 27 percent and killed the Korea discount, and Aehr guided miles past the Street. Bitcoin crossed $65,000 as the on-chain crowd stacked its bottom signals, and PayPal is suddenly in play at a $53 billion bid.

MacroStocksBitcoin

The market spent yesterday selling a cold inflation print, and overnight the reason that print was cold started to reverse. Iran reshut the Strait of Hormuz, the US put warships back around it, and oil is bid again. Here is the pre-open picture, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. The overnight regime change is geopolitical. Per the Market list, Iran's Revolutionary Guard declared the Strait of Hormuz shut until US attacks stop, and Washington reinstated the naval blockade with more than 20 warships after another round of strikes on southern Iran. Oil is up, gold is red below $4,050 as the dollar firms, and Asian stocks still rose on the soft US inflation data, per the Financial Markets list. The calendar adds producer prices (the PPI, the wholesale-inflation read) this morning and Netflix and Taiwan Semiconductor earnings Thursday, and the July 29 Fed meeting is now priced at only a 7 to 17 percent chance of a hike, per the People list.

1. The disinflation that let the Fed stand pat is sitting on a barrel of oil, and Iran just kicked it. Yesterday's inflation print was cold in large part because energy fell 5.7 percent, and energy fell because oil had come off. That is the piece now unwinding. The strait is shut, the blockade is back, and the oil bid returned overnight, per the Financial Markets list. The trade some are already in is the obvious one, threadguy is long Brent from around $82 after Trump's 20 percent Hormuz toll sent crude up 8 to 10 percent in a session, the first move that size since late April.

The read-through is that the Fed's room to relax just narrowed. A cold June number bought the market relief, but if crude climbs back toward $90 on a real blockade, the July inflation print reverses and the hawkish new chair gets his excuse. Kevin Warsh is already leaning that way, telling Congress that inflation is a choice, so the disinflation trade and the oil trade are now the same bet running in opposite directions.

2. The memory trade did not just survive the leverage scare, it took out its price targets. Two days after Korea's leverage-squared crash, the memory names roared back. Micron is back above $1,000 and worth more than $1.1 trillion, which once again makes it larger than Berkshire Hathaway, per the People list. SK Hynix ran about 27 percent, tagged the $195 target before settling near $181, and in doing so erased the discount its US listing had opened, per the Market list. The 2x long ETF that launched into the chaos held, with high-bandwidth memory sold out through 2027.

The read underneath the bounce is that the shakeout was forced selling, not a thesis break. Jordi Visser called it capitulation, pointing to record margin debt liquidated on the highest-volume back-to-back sessions in a month with the lows printing on the heaviest 30-minute volume right before the intraday reversal, per the Hermes list. The theme is widening past the majors too. Aehr Test Systems ($AEHR) beat and guided fiscal-2027 bookings to $130 to $150 million against a Street number near $85 million, and the stock ran 26 to 36 percent, per the Market list.

3. The Bitcoin bottom case got hard sub-levels, and they all point at the same number. The most detailed on-chain read of the week, per Milk Road with Matt Crosby: the levels that mark accumulation bottoms are converging near $50,000, with realized price around $53,000, the long-term-holder cost basis near $50,000, and a deeper floor metric near $48,000. Bitcoin is trading at its production cost, its 200-week moving average sits around $63,000, and at the $57,000 to $58,000 cycle low it was in the cheapest 5 percent of its entire history. Crosby's timing overlay of the 2022 capitulation points at July 12, so his read is that the time-based bottom may already be in, and Bitcoin crossed $65,000 overnight, per the People list.

The honest counterweight is that the same crowd cannot agree on when. Benjamin Cowen's seasonal read has the cold CPI fueling a rally into late July or early August, the same July-relief pattern as 2018, before a possible final low later in the year. So the levels say close and the calendar says maybe not yet, and that is exactly why the discipline here is dollar-cost-averaging into the zone rather than calling the exact bottom.

4. PayPal is in play, and the bid is real money. The biggest new single name, per the Market and People lists: Stripe and the private-equity firm Advent have bid $60.50 a share for $PYPL (PayPal), a roughly $53 billion offer at about a 28 percent premium, with $50 billion of financing behind it. A fintech incumbent that spent two years as a value trap getting a cash bid from the private-market leader in payments is the kind of event that reprices the whole sector, and it lands the same week Oracle's collapse (down 62 percent from its September high, with a 2x short ETF launching today) shows how fast the market punishes the AI names that miss.

5. The AI-payoff skeptics keep stacking receipts under the buildout. Under the memory euphoria, the return-on-investment question got louder. An MIT study cited on the Hermes list found 95 percent of enterprise generative-AI pilots show no profit-and-loss return, IBM just fell 25 percent because its clients are diverting budget away from it, and New York became the first state to freeze data-center construction. Set against that, Kalshi launched forward-price curves for renting Nvidia B200, H200, and A100 compute, pricing hyperscaler compute spend above $700 billion this year, per the Kalshi list. The spend is real and enormous, the proven payoff is not there yet, and that gap is the single biggest thing hanging over every name in this briefing.

Psychology. The memory crowd got exactly the vindication it was praying for, and it got it fast. Two days after Korea tripped a circuit breaker and the leverage-squared trade looked broken, Micron is back over Berkshire, SK Hynix ran 27 percent, and someone on the tape is already slapping a $2,500 target on SanDisk. That is not a market that learned anything from Monday, that is a market that treated a 15 percent crash as a dip and came back more convinced.

The Bitcoin side is the calmer mirror image, where Milk Road, Crosby, and Cowen are all quietly saying the same thing, that the levels are cheap and the move is to accumulate rather than to call the low. The contrast is the tell. In memory the crowd is euphoric and reaching for leverage and price targets, and in Bitcoin the smart voices are patient and talking about production cost and dollar-cost-averaging, and only one of those postures survives an oil shock.

So the discipline check this morning is to notice which of your positions is running on conviction and which is running on leverage. Iran just reshut the strait, oil is bid, and the Fed chair is looking for a reason to stay hawkish, the exact backdrop that punishes a crowded, levered trade like the 2x memory ETFs and rewards a patient one like averaging into Bitcoin near its production cost. Let PPI print and let oil show its hand before you decide the memory names get to be worth more than Berkshire without a fight.
