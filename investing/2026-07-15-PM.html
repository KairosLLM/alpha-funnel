Micron was worth more than Berkshire at the open and broke below $900 by the close, and the money leaving the chips went into Apple instead

Producer prices came in cold too, so the Fed hike is basically off the table, but the session was a rotation, not a rally. Semis crashed while the Nasdaq held on megacap strength, the 2x SK Hynix ETF fell 15 percent on day two, and oil quietly refused to break out even with the strait shut.

MacroStocksCrypto

The morning looked like a memory coronation and the afternoon looked like a hangover, and the gap between them is the whole session. Underneath it, the market bought a second cold inflation print and rotated hard out of what ran. Here is the recap, then five stories.

The close. Producer prices (the wholesale-inflation read, the PPI) came in at 5.5 percent against a 6.2 expected, cold on the heels of Tuesday's cold consumer number, and the July 29 Fed meeting is now priced 95 percent for no change, per the Stocks and Polymarket lists. But this was not a broad rally. Semiconductors were in freefall while the Nasdaq held green, the worst semis-down-on-an-up-Nasdaq day since November 2024, with money rotating into megacap software as Apple printed a new all-time high near the $5 trillion club, per the Financial Markets list. PayPal ran 16 to 20 percent on its takeover bid, and Bitcoin held $65,000.

1. The memory trade round-tripped in a single day, and the round-trip is the message. Tuesday's melt-up got its coronation at Wednesday's open, with Micron back over $1,000 and worth more than Berkshire Hathaway. By the close it had broken below $900, per the People list, and the 2x long SK Hynix ETF that launched into the frenzy fell about 15 percent on its second day. The SK Hynix ADR itself had tagged a record $193.90 on Tuesday, up 25 percent, so the whole complex went vertical and then gave a chunk of it back inside 24 hours.

The rotation underneath is the important part. Semis crashed while Apple, Microsoft, and Google caught a bid, money leaving the crowded, levered memory trade for the megacaps that had lagged. The bull case did not change, the Milk Road desk still says buy the dip on Micron and SanDisk, citing Dylan Patel's call that memory demand grows 20 to 40 percent a year through 2028 and Morgan Stanley lifting hyperscaler capital spending to $1.2 trillion in 2028. But a trade that can be worth more than Berkshire at 9:30 and cracked by 4:00 is telling you it is running on positioning, not on news.

2. Oil refused to break out even with the strait shut, and that quietly saves the disinflation. The single most important thing the tape did today was almost nothing. Despite Iran reshutting the Strait of Hormuz and a US naval blockade, crude did not spike to a new high. Per the Financial Markets list, the average price of WTI crude is $80 in July against $82 in June and $98.50 in May, so even after a 15 percent bounce off the low, July is on pace for a lower average than June. The blockade is priced, and a Trump headline that Iran wants a deal capped the rest.

That matters because the whole disinflation story rested on cheap energy, and this morning that looked fragile. The counter-read from Mark Zandi is that underlying inflation is still stuck near 3 to 3.5 percent and the June drop was mostly oil, so a real crude spike would reverse it. The market's answer today was that the spike is not coming, and that is why it bought the cold PPI and let the hawkish new Fed chair fade into the background.

3. The bottleneck moved past GPUs, and the rotation has names. The cleanest framing of where the AI money goes next, per the Milk Road note: the GPU scarcity trade is over, and the new binding constraints are memory, optics, packaging, and power. The picks are specific, Micron for memory (high-bandwidth memory sold out through mid-2027, operating margins of 80 percent that beat Nvidia's), Corning for optics on a new Nvidia partnership to build US optical-connectivity manufacturing, and Infineon for power, raising prices a second time this year as its AI data-center revenue goes from 1.5 to 2.5 billion euros.

The cautionary note is that the highest-beta version of this trade broke today. The neoclouds cracked hard, with Nebius down 36 percent even after signing a $1 billion compute contract and CoreWeave off 40 percent, per the Milk Road memory panel. The theme is right and the leveraged expressions of it are the ones getting punished, the same lesson the SK Hynix ETF taught this morning.

4. PayPal is being fought over, and the plumbing under stocks is going on-chain. The takeover is live and the holders want more, per the Stocks list: PayPal jumped past $55 on the $60.50-a-share Stripe and Advent bid, prediction markets put the deal at 74 percent, and shareholders are calling the offer too low. The bigger structural story landed the same day. The Depository Trust and Clearing Corporation, which settles nearly all US stock trades, launched a pilot with JPMorgan, BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, and Vanguard to tokenize stocks and Treasurys, per the same list, and BlackRock crossed $15 trillion in assets for the first time. The tokenization trade stopped being a crypto pitch and became a project run by the people who own the market.

5. Two fresh names and one cautionary post-mortem. The genuinely new single name is Aehr Test Systems, per Jose Najarro, which jumped 30 percent after hours to $93.90 and guided fiscal-2027 revenue growth of 160 to 200 percent as its chip-testing business pivots into AI accelerators and memory. The second is CleanSpark, per Stocks with Josh, which jumped more than 10 percent on a $6.6 billion, 20-year data-center lease at its Georgia campus, reframing a Bitcoin miner as an AI-infrastructure play, with a stop just below $12 and a target at $15.

The post-mortem is Intel, and it is a warning about the whole sector. Per Pat Gelsinger on All-In, Intel got demolished because bean-counters ran it over technologists, handing $100 billion back to shareholders in buybacks while skipping a fab for a decade, passing on the iPhone chip, and killing its own GPU a week after he first left. His sharper warning is geopolitical, that Taiwan has under three weeks of energy reserves and a blockade would brown out the fabs that need 90 days to restart, an impact he calls greater than the Great Depression with no shot fired.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $AEHR (Aehr Test Systems). Up 30 percent after hours to $93.90 on a fiscal-2027 guide of 160 to 200 percent revenue growth, pivoting into AI-accelerator and memory testing, per Najarro.
  • $CLSK (CleanSpark). Up double digits on a $6.6 billion, 20-year data-center lease, a stop below $12 and a $15 target, per Josh.
  • $PYPL (PayPal). Past $55 on the $60.50 Stripe and Advent bid, deal odds 74 percent, holders calling it too low, per the Stocks list.
  • $ASTS (AST SpaceMobile). Per Rick Orford, the carrier-moat satellite play validated by AT&T and Verizon across nearly 60 mobile networks, rated hold but with analyst targets implying roughly 70 percent upside.
  • $INTC / TEM / VST (Intel, Tempus AI, Vistra). The non-obvious Trump-and-Pelosi overlap buys, per Joseph Hogue, smaller and more policy-driven than the mega-cap names both also hold.
  • Bitcoin. Cathie Wood called the bottom with a $750,000 to $1.25 million five-year target, per Lark Davis, and the level to reclaim is the 200-day average near $74,000, per his positioning read.

Psychology. The memory crowd got its entire emotional cycle compressed into one trading day. Micron was worth more than Berkshire at the open and broke below $900 by the close, someone on the tape was still calling for $2,000 Micron while the 2x SK Hynix ETF fell 15 percent, and the neoclouds that everyone piled into cracked 40 percent. That is not a market digesting news, that is a market unwinding leverage in real time and pretending it was a plan.

The tell is where the money went. It did not chase the crack lower and it did not double down, it rotated into Apple at a record and the megacap software that had done nothing, the move smart money makes when it thinks the easy part of a trade is over. Bitcoin is the calm version of the same instinct, with Cathie Wood and the on-chain crowd talking about accumulation and an October bottom rather than reaching for a leveraged bet on a name that already tripled.

So the discipline check into Netflix earnings tomorrow and the megacap prints later this month is to notice which of your positions needs the story to keep getting louder to keep working. The 2x memory ETFs and the neoclouds are that kind of position, they require the melt-up to continue, and they round-tripped in a day when it paused. The names worth holding through the next oil headline or hot data point are the ones the smart money rotated into today, not the ones it rotated out of. Let Micron prove it can hold $900 before you decide the coronation was real.
