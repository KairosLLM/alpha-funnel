The memory trade is the only trade that mattered this week, and it survived a short-seller, a revenue miss, and a war without breaking

Micron gapped up on a Burry short Monday, cracked 26 percent by Tuesday on a Samsung miss, then ran straight back over $1,000 on a $250 billion capex commitment and the biggest foreign listing in US history. Here is the full bull and bear case on Micron, SK Hynix, and the HBM supercycle, the names that ride it, and how crowded the boat has already gotten.

StocksMacro

This was a one-story week. Iran and the Strait of Hormuz owned the headlines, but on the tape the entire market was a proxy for one question: is the AI memory shortage real and durable, or is it 2018 in a nicer costume. Every session this week answered it, and the answer kept coming back the same, so this memo goes all the way on it.

How the week actually played. Monday $MU (Micron) gapped up about 7 percent overnight after breaking ground on a high-bandwidth-memory plant in Hiroshima with Japanese government money, and in the same session Michael Burry disclosed a short and called the whole AI-semiconductor complex a bubble about to pop, per the Stocks list and this Burry breakdown. The stock faded the gap all day, a first warning.

Tuesday it cracked. Samsung posted a second-quarter operating profit up roughly nineteen times year over year and still missed on revenue, and the stock fell almost 8 percent and dragged the whole memory complex with it, per this account of the Samsung reversal. By the close it was the worst day of the year for the group, $SNDK down 14 percent, $MU through $900, and month-to-date $WDC was off 33 percent, $SNDK 30 percent, and $MU 26 percent, per the rotation note. A best-in-class quarter that gets sold is the market telling you the good news was already owned.

Then it healed, and fast. Wednesday the US bombed Iran and oil jumped 5 percent, and the crowd answered a group down 16 percent in two weeks by shoving a record $5.4 billion into the $SOXX semiconductor ETF in a single day, four times its old record. Thursday Micron committed more than $250 billion to US memory through 2035, up from $200 billion a year earlier, and the stock ran 6.5 percent back over $1,000 with shorts including Burry reportedly scrambling to cover, per the Micron capex breakdown. Friday SK Hynix priced the biggest first-time US share sale by a foreign company ever, and by Saturday its shares had run about 13 percent on day one on $18 billion of volume, per the week-levels note.

The bull case, in full. Start with the structure, because it is the whole argument. Three companies make the world's DRAM (the working memory in every server and phone), Samsung at 39.3 percent, SK Hynix at 28.9, and Micron at 22.8, so roughly 90 percent of supply sits with three names through 2027, per the melt-up note. Only those three make HBM (the stacked high-bandwidth memory that every AI accelerator needs), and SK Hynix says its HBM is sold out into 2027.

The valuation is the part that makes bulls loud. Gavin Baker's framing, carried in the same note, is that memory is the cheapest thing in the AI stack at 3 to 5 times earnings while power, cooling, and optical names carry multiples that imply a very different future, so one side is mispriced. Micron trades around 9 times earnings with contracts covering about half its revenue at floor prices above the last cycle's peak margins. Its Cloud Memory unit grew revenue from $3.4 billion to $13.8 billion in a year while operating margin went from 46 to 78 percent, per the capex breakdown.

Then the demand story, which the bulls stretch out for decades. Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra argues a humanoid robot carries ten times the memory of a self-driving car and that the real multi-decade demand cycle has not even started, per Lark Davis. The SK Hynix chair went further this week, saying doubling 2025 capacity within five years still will not meet demand and that memory is no longer a cyclical business. The year-to-date scoreboard is the bull case with no commentary, $SNDK up 707 percent, $MU up 243, and $WDC up 238, per the People list, and a mid-year audit of 30 finance YouTubers found the winners clustered on Micron, AMD, and Marvell while the losers sat in software names like Adobe and Salesforce.

The bear case, in full, because it is better than the bulls admit. The strongest bear document of the week was not Burry, it was a teardown of the HBM4 timeline. Stacking fourteen to sixteen memory layers drives manufacturing temperatures toward 260 degrees, warping the silicon and pushing yields below the roughly 65 percent break-even, and HBM3 took a year to pass Nvidia's qualification while twelve-layer HBM3E took nineteen months. The read-through is that real HBM4 volume likely lands in 2027 and 2028, not late this year, only Samsung has cleared baseline qualification, and one respected note even expects Micron to get zero initial HBM4 allocation from Nvidia. Micron itself says it has no line of sight to supply catching demand before 2028.

The rest of the bear case is timing and history. The 2018 memory glut is the reason nobody should treat 78 percent margins as permanent, and Micron's own new fabs are years from output, first Idaho wafers in mid-2027 and a second fab in late 2028. China is the wildcard, with CXMT testing next-generation bonded DRAM and closing the gap faster than expected, and the one thing that would truly crack the three-supplier scarcity story is Chinese DRAM sold cheap into the US. Burry's short sits on top of all of it, betting on roughly a 30 percent drop, and the honest version of his case is that 9 times earnings is a warning that earnings already peaked as often as it is a bargain, per Rick Orford's normalization take.

The tell that decides it. The cleanest signal all week was the Samsung reaction. A company can print a record quarter, up nineteen times on profit, and still get sold 8 percent, and that only happens when the good news is fully in the price. That cuts both ways. It means the easy money in this trade has been made, and it means the next leg needs genuinely new information, an HBM4 qualification, a pricing print, a capacity number, not just another headline about how big the cycle will be.

What the reader might actually do. If you already own the theme, this week was a gift and a warning at once. The gift is that the trade survived a short-seller, a revenue miss, and a war in the same five sessions, the way durable trends do. The warning is that you are now late enough that position sizing matters more than direction. The Kyber optics delay points the same way it did Monday, at $AMD, $MRVL, and $AVGO gaining as hyperscalers accelerate custom silicon, and at $AAOI keeping its pluggable transceivers bid for two more years.

On the single names, the cleanest expressions are still the obvious ones. $MU is the balance-sheet play, $SNDK is the highest-beta expression of the same trade at 707 percent year to date, and $SKHY, which converts to regular-way trading Monday, is the purest HBM margin story at a roughly 48 percent operating margin against Micron's 26. For levels, Josh's read on Micron is a bounce off $86 that ran to $99.60 and then failed to close above it, so he flips cautious into Monday with an air pocket toward $90 and $86.10, and a reclaim of $99.60 re-arms the move toward $108.90 and $125.

What to watch next week. Monday SK Hynix converts from the when-issued line to regular-way $SKHY. Tuesday the $SKHX 2x long and $SKHZ short single-stock ETFs launch, and June CPI (the inflation print that settles the July 29 Fed debate) hits the same morning. Netflix reports Thursday as the first big-tech print of the season. The one variable that would change the whole call is an HBM4 qualification result, because the bull case is priced for the shortage to run to 2028 and the bear case says the yields are not there yet, and the first real qualification number tells you which one is right.

Psychology. This week was a clinic in how a crowd behaves when a parabola cracks and then heals. Monday the timeline was posting $1,800 targets on Micron and $3,000 on SanDisk and saying Micron under $1,000 is a smash buy no matter what Burry does. That is exactly the sound euphoria makes right before a revenue miss takes 26 percent out of the group. The euphoria did not break the trade. A Samsung miss did, and the difference matters, because it means the crowd was not wrong about the story, only careless about the price.

Then came the more dangerous move, the capitulation-buy. After the worst day of the year the crowd put a record $5.4 billion into $SOXX in a single session with fund-manager cash at a twenty-year low. That is not fear, it is total conviction that every dip is a gift, and that belief is cheapest to hold right after the best trade in the market cracks. It is also the one that gets punished when the second leg down does not bounce.

The named portrait of the mood is on the Crypto list. A trader who lost $4.4 million on the Hynix run turned around and re-longed $30.17 million of the same exposure at 2x leverage, with another account opening a $20 million 3x Nvidia long into the weekend. That is a scorned position trying to get even, and it is happening while options sentiment sits at December 2020 highs and the Shiller PE (price against ten years of averaged earnings) kisses its dot-com record.

So the discipline check going into the new week is about your own hands, not the tape. The story is real and you can hold it with conviction, but ask whether you are adding because the thesis advanced or because the chart already ran, because those are different trades wearing the same ticker. If you are sitting on a loss from Tuesday's crack, size any re-entry like that loss taught you something rather than like the market owes you the money back. The levels are on the page, $99.60 on Micron, $195 to $200 on the Hynix line, the CPI print Tuesday. Let the trade come to you instead of chasing it into the ETF-inflow record.
