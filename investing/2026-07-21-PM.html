The memory names that got crushed for three weeks ripped 10 to 20 percent today while Amazon, Microsoft, and Alphabet all closed red, and that split is the whole market telling you what it fears

Micron jumped to $945 and the DRAM fund is up 20 percent in days while the mega-caps that lead the index faded. A draft US Treasury report just compared the AI boom to the dot-com bubble, Mark Cuban named who actually gets wiped out, and Alphabet and Tesla both report tomorrow.

MacroStocksCrypto

A Tuesday that rewarded the wreckage and punished the leaders. The beaten-down chips melted up while the trillion-dollar names slipped, and that is not how a healthy rally usually looks. Here is the session recap, then five stories.

Session recap. The bounce was violent and narrow. Per the Market list, Micron hit $945 intraday and kissed its 50-day average after Bank of America added it to its best ideas, the DRAM fund tracking Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron is up 20 percent in days to around $58, and Nebius jumped more than 10 percent after Nvidia disclosed a roughly 9 percent passive stake. The tell was underneath, with the Nasdaq 100 up 2 percent while Amazon, Microsoft, and Alphabet all closed red, per the Financial Markets list. Oil hit a five-week high with Brent back over $91, the Fed hike odds for the year jumped to 62 percent, and Alphabet and Tesla both report after tomorrow's close.

1. The memory melt-up is real, and the supply chain says the dip was overdone. The trade of the day was buying back the exact names that led the selloff. Per the Stocks list, SanDisk, Western Digital, and Seagate printed monster bounces alongside Micron, with Morgan Stanley calling the memory pullback a compelling entry and forecasting DRAM prices up 25 percent into the third quarter while UBS sees the market undersupplied until at least 2028.

The structural case is that the bottlenecks are physical rather than financial. Per Ticker Symbol YOU, TSMC's advanced packaging is sold out into 2027 with some customers waiting more than a year, ASML will build only about 65 of its most advanced machines this year, and a reported delay to Nvidia's next rack could push its top chip to 2028, and the preferred way to own the shortage is the toolmakers, ASML, Lam Research, KLA, and the power name Vertiv. Those are a promoter's picks, so weigh the specifics, but the point that the constraint is capacity rather than demand is the reason the dip found buyers this fast.

2. The bubble question just went mainstream, and the answer is that the pros get wiped out, not you. The bear case got a government stamp. Per Lark Davis, a draft internal US Treasury report obtained by NOTUS warns that the AI boom is so interconnected across stocks, banks, chipmakers, cloud providers, data centers, and private credit that a sharp downturn could hit all of them at once, making an AI bust potentially far broader than the dot-com crash that stayed mostly contained.

Mark Cuban drew the sharper distinction. Per the All-In Podcast, this is not the dot-com bubble because there is no wave of no-revenue companies IPO-ing to retail, so the damage lands on the venture funds and private equity that went all in at $40 to $60 million valuations before products even launched, and on the mega-caps borrowing billions against cash flow to fund capex while priced to perfection. His analogy is the one to keep, that just as the fiber buildout left dark fiber selling for pennies, a breakthrough in AI power efficiency could turn a lot of data centers into pickleball courts.

3. Alphabet and Tesla report tomorrow, and Google fired first. The two prints the week hinges on are 24 hours out. Per the People list, Alphabet reports after tomorrow's close as the first major hyperscaler and the first real test of whether the AI trade is still justified, with the market watching cloud growth, AI monetization, and above all the capital-spending guide, and Google pre-empted the report today by launching its Gemini 3.6 Flash models and confirming that Gemini 4 is already in pre-training.

Tesla is the other side of the barbell. Per the same list, Tesla reports the same evening having just expanded its robotaxi service to Tampa and Orlando, though skeptics note the expansion dropped one day before earnings, each city runs only about ten vehicles, and Tesla has missed its short-term robotaxi guidance three reports in a row. The bull case that a strong Alphabet cloud and capex guide reignites the whole trade, per Milk Road, is real, and so is the risk that any equivocation on AI spending deepens the correction.

4. The AI power crunch has a date now, and it is 2028. The most substantive new thesis of the day is about natural gas. Per Matt Smith on Invest Like the Best, after 18 months of well-level modeling he projects US gas storage drawing down below all prior history by 2028 as LNG exports climb from 15 to 35 billion cubic feet a day and AI compute adds another 5 to 15, against a production ceiling that cannot keep up, which takes a complacent $3.50 gas price toward $8 to $10 and pushes energy from about 10 percent of a hyperscaler's cost to 20 or even 40 percent by 2029.

His named plays are the ones with the gas and the power. Per that interview, the winners are Expand Energy, which controls roughly 70 percent of the remaining core Haynesville at about four times cash flow, plus Range Resources, Cameco for its Westinghouse nuclear stake, and BWXT, while the losers are Caterpillar and Bloom Energy building gas generation into the shortage, and the American consumer who pays the power bill. The same physical reality is why Kevin O'Leary is fighting to build the largest data center in US history in Utah against local opposition, per Anthony Pompliano, because whoever locks in power now wins the next phase.

5. Crypto went up while stocks softened, and the CLARITY Act is finally moving. The divergence flipped in crypto's favor. Per the Crypto list, Bitcoin broke $66,000 and rose about 4 percent as capital rotated out of crowded AI positions into crypto treasury names, Bitcoin ETFs took in $229 million on the day, and the prediction markets now price better than 70 percent odds that Ether holds above $2,000 into month-end, with Tom Lee targeting $2,200 by August.

The catalyst underneath it is legislative. Per Lark Davis, the White House agreed to an ethics package that could unstick the CLARITY Act, with a floor vote possible before the August recess and passage odds recovering to around 42 percent, and per Blockworks, Senate leader John Thune targeted this week for action. That same Blockworks note makes the case that PumpFun's token, up 35 percent, is throwing off a 12.7 percent buyback yield after rallying through its own unlock, the kind of durable cash flow that could re-rate it if the memecoin volume holds.

  • $NBIS (Nebius). Rose more than 10 percent after Nvidia disclosed a roughly 9 percent passive stake, per the Market list.
  • $GOOGL (Alphabet). Reports after tomorrow's close as the first real test of the AI trade, having launched its Gemini 3.6 Flash models today, per the People list.

  • BTC (Bitcoin). Broke $66,000 on rotation out of AI, with the 200-week average near $68,500 the line that flips the structure, per the Crypto list.

Psychology. Read the divergence honestly. The names that fell the hardest ripped 10 to 20 percent today, and the mega-caps that everyone owns quietly gave ground, the market rotating rather than rallying, buying the capitulation in memory while trimming the leaders that carry the index. That is the kind of day that feels like an all-clear and is actually a rebalancing, and the tell is that the biggest bounce came in the most beaten-down names rather than the strongest ones.

The mood underneath it is a bubble debate breaking into the open. The US Treasury is drafting warnings about AI contagion, Mark Cuban is telling Anthropic and OpenAI employees to hedge their stock, and Jamie Dimon said flat out he would not buy stocks right now, all while the Fed hike odds jumped to 62 percent and the beaten chips melted up anyway. When the smart money is quoting the dot-com comparison and the crowd is buying the dip with both hands, the two are describing the same tape and betting opposite outcomes.

So the discipline into Alphabet and Tesla tomorrow is to respect both truths. The memory shortage is physical and the demand is real, and that is why the dip was bought, but the leverage and the off-balance-sheet spending are exactly what a Treasury report and a billionaire investor are pointing at. Let the capex guides print before you decide the AI trade is fixed, own the shortage through the toolmakers and the power names that the math actually supports, and keep enough cash that if Cuban is right and some of these data centers become pickleball courts, you are the buyer and not the bag.
