The memory trade got its invoices this weekend, and on the very same tape the companies that rent out the compute had their worst two days since COVID

UBS put hard numbers on the DRAM price hikes and Micron locked five-year take-or-pay contracts through 2030, while the neoclouds fell 20 to 25% in two sessions on one Meta headline. The AI trade is splitting into the part you get paid to own and the part that just got dangerous.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Two things happened at once and they point in opposite directions. The memory supercycle stopped being a forecast and became a set of signed contracts and quoted price hikes. At the same moment, the equities built on renting AI compute went through the fastest drawdown in years on a single Meta headline. Owning the AI trade now means knowing which half you are in. Here is the week ahead and the split underneath it.

The week ahead. Markets reopen Monday July 6 after the long weekend. Three dates matter. The FOMC minutes land Wednesday July 8 and get read for whether a July hike is real. The SK Hynix Nasdaq listing arrives Friday July 10 under $SKHY, a second pure memory play beside $MU with a 91% DRAM gross margin last quarter. And earnings season opens the following week, with $SOFI and $HOOD both reporting July 29. Per Stocks with Josh, SPY still runs on 755 as the breakout and 740 as the breakdown, with a note worth holding: July averages up 2.5%, but every fourth year July is weak, and 2026 sits on that cycle.

1. The memory trade got its invoices, and that changes the argument. This morning the memory story was a thesis about scarcity. Tonight it is paper. Per the Market list, UBS now models DDR contract prices up 32% in Q3 and 18% in Q4, above its own prior 17% and 12% estimates, with NAND up 30% quarter over quarter and Samsung pushing a 20% DRAM hike. Micron is leading a fresh 1.5 trillion yen, roughly $9.3B, Hiroshima buildout.

Per the Milk Road Micron breakdown, the structure under it is the tell: 16 strategic customer agreements, five-year take-or-pay running through 2030, customers have already posted $22B in cash deposits against a roughly $100B backlog, and about half of company revenue is heading under contract. On a PEG screen, per the Market list, $MU and $SNDK both price near 0.1x growth while $TSLA sits at 4.5x. The one caution worth respecting: per the People-to-follow list, LuxAlgo is already flagging memory stocks as trading in a bear market even as the fundamentals scream, and that gap between the invoice and the chart is the whole trade.

2. The neoclouds just had their worst two days since COVID, and the reason matters more than the move. Per the Crypto list, the neocloud complex fell 20 to 25% across two straight sessions, described as the worst two-day momentum selloff since COVID, triggered by Meta signaling it will rent out excess data-center capacity. Per Joseph Hogue, that pulled $NBIS, $CRWV, $IREN, and $ORCL down 20 to 35% in two weeks, and he lays out the deeper worry: these companies borrow heavily, their hardware is obsolete in about two years, and if Meta and SpaceX both dump spare capacity into the market, a default wave is not impossible.

The bull answer is that the demand is real and this is a gift, and per the Crypto list Meta is still spending $125B to $145B on infrastructure in 2026 and signaling it remains compute-constrained. My read: this is the high-beta, balance-sheet-sensitive end of the AI trade, and it is exactly the part that breaks first when the story wobbles. Own it small or own it on the flush, never with size into strength.

3. TSMC is quietly repricing the entire AI supply chain. The under-covered lever this weekend is not a chip, it is a price list. Per Rick Orford, $TSM is raising 3nm wafers as much as 15% and pricing its new 2nm node near $30,000 a wafer, the highest ever, into a sold-out order book. Because the fabs and staff are already paid for, repriced nodes running at about 74% of a $100B-plus revenue base flow almost straight to earnings, pointing at a 10 to 15% profit uplift on top of volume. The risk to name: an ITC investigation from two patent firms is seeking an exclusion order on chips built at 7nm and smaller, with $TSM, $AAPL, $QCOM, and $AVGO named, a preliminary ruling due within about a month and a final decision in October. Low odds, but a binary date on the calendar.

4. Bitcoin's bottom debate turned more bearish, and the ETF story is the reason. The dueling targets sharpened into a real spread. Per Milk Road, the three houses now split cleanly: Galaxy says no bottom yet with a $40K target, NYDIG says maybe with a roughly $50K read, and Standard Chartered says the bottom is already in at $59K.

Under it sits a structural drain. Per the Milk Road letter, James Seyffart of Bloomberg Intelligence calls Bitcoin ETP diamond hands a myth, with the products now more than $11B below their October peak and about $700M leaving in a single day on June 25, capital getting pulled toward AI and space. Per the Financial Markets list, Rekt Capital notes BTC has deviated below its 200-week average and expects a July relief bounce before a deeper flush toward the final bear-market bottom. The one bright spot is a decoupling: per Milk Road, the Bitwise crypto-equities index is up about 30% this year while Bitcoin is down about 30%, which says the equities, names like $COIN and $CRCL, are becoming the cleaner way to hold the theme than the coin.

5. The macro fight is now explicit: is AI inflationary or not? This morning the Fed picture was two-sided. Tonight both sides showed up with data. Per the Financial Markets list, Morgan Stanley argues the case that AI is disinflationary should be re-examined and possibly rejected, because faster productivity lifts the neutral rate and undercuts the reason to cut. Against that, per Jordi Visser, the market has priced out cuts and priced in roughly three hikes while the Cleveland Fed nowcast points to a slightly negative June CPI print and July near -0.2%, dragging the annual rate lower, which sets up a bullish unwind for gold, silver, and Bitcoin if the hikes never come, and Lark Davis relays Cathie Wood making the same call, inflation dropping toward 0 to 1% while the economy booms.

Underneath the argument, the rotation is already moving: per Joseph Hogue, more than $2 trillion has come off the Magnificent Seven in six weeks with $NVDA down 14% and $MSFT flagged as the highest-risk of the group, while cybersecurity ($PANW up 23% in a week) and defensives ($XLV, $XLP, $XLRE) lead.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $HIMS, the name that says GLP-1 beats AI. Per Prof G Markets, Hims and Hers CEO Andrew Dudum guides to about $3B in revenue this year, now partners directly with Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly selling their products at $150, and argues GLP-1s will do more for society over five years than AI. A July meeting on moving peptides to a category-1 list is the near catalyst. The stock is wildly volatile, so it is a watchlist name with a wide range, not a set-and-forget.
  • $SKHY, the memory IPO that lands Friday. Per the People-to-follow list, SK Hynix lists on the Nasdaq this Friday with a 91% DRAM gross margin last quarter, the second direct memory vehicle beside Micron. If you believe the invoices in section one, this is the freshest way to express it.
  • $CCXI, the first pure humanoid-robot ticker. Per the Stocks list, Churchill Capital XI is merging Agility Robotics at a $2.5B pre-money valuation with a $200M PIPE led by Foxconn, and Agility's Digit already logs Amazon and Toyota hours with $300M-plus in binding orders. Speculative, but it is the cleanest listed way to own the robotics demand layer Micron keeps citing.
  • $TSM, the one to buy on the ruling, not the record. Per Rick Orford, the stock hit a record near $476 then slid about 7%, and the pricing story is a multi-year tailwind. The move is to keep dry powder for a panic around the October ITC decision rather than chase the high.
