Micron was the whole week, and by Sunday the trade had a famous bear, a structural bull, and a number under $1,000 to explain

The AI memory trade ran through all eight editions this week: record fundamentals and a supplier now invoicing 20% price hikes on one side, Michael Burry's chart short and a crowded-buy flush on the other. Here is the full case, and what to actually do with it.

StocksMacroBitcoin

Every trading day this week had memory at the center of it, and no other story came close. Micron opened the week getting flushed under $1,000, spent the middle of it absorbing a Michael Burry short, and closed it with a supplier literally invoicing a 20% price hike and a decade-scoreboard showing memory as the best-performing corner of the entire market. This is the one position the whole book keeps circling, so it earns the long form. The bull case and the bear case are both real, they are arguing about different things, and knowing which is which is the difference between owning this well and getting run over in it.

Why Micron is the week's subject and not just another semi in the AI basket. The tell is that the same name led the tape from two opposite directions. On Monday the memory air pocket showed up and $MU gave back 6.7% in a session. By Wednesday Micron had closed under $1,000 and every desk that bought the dip looked early. Then on Thursday Michael Burry opened a short and the entire complex wobbled, and by Friday the bulls had answered with a record print and Asia had voted with a green tape. A stock does not do that unless it is the fulcrum of the market's biggest live debate. It is.

The bull case, in full. Strip out the noise and the long side rests on four legs, and they are stronger than the price action this week suggested.

  • The supply is structurally short, and management keeps saying so on the record. CEO Sanjay Mehrotra told Bloomberg that Micron can meet only about 50% to two-thirds of key-customer demand right now, that the shortage runs well beyond 2026 with no meaningful new supply until 2028, and that the take-or-pay book, the contracts where the buyer pays whether or not it takes the chips, is up to 16 agreements with $GM freshly added and 14 of them carrying a $100B minimum-revenue floor. He is committing $200B to a US buildout on the back of it.
  • The margins are the kind you see once a cycle. The most recent quarter ran an 84.9% gross margin, and Wolfe Research pegs Micron's DRAM gross margins above 90%. The reason it is durable is physical: high-bandwidth memory eats four times the wafer capacity of standard DDR5, so adding wafers does not add bits, and the clean-room shortage caps supply well into 2027.
  • The valuation still prices a cyclical, not a monopoly. Fiscal Q3 revenue was $41.5B, up 346% year over year, with adjusted EPS of $25.11 against a $20.49 estimate, and the Q4 guide is $50B in revenue and $31 EPS. Against a CEO target of $4,000 the stock has traded near 6x forward earnings. That is a value multiple stapled to a hypergrowth print.
  • The forecast became an invoice. The single most important development of the week was that the thesis stopped being a thesis. Samsung began issuing verbal notices to raise DRAM prices roughly 20% quarter over quarter for Q3. A price hike you model is a hope. A price hike your supplier sends in writing is a fact, and it flows straight to Micron's line.

The smart-money vote backs the fundamentals. Martin Shkreli made the cleanest structural argument, that DRAM is not a commodity because it needs cleanroom fabs full of $ASML and Lam Research machines nobody can stand up on demand, so rational entrants match the incumbent price instead of starting a war, and memory behaves like a disciplined oligopoly. Leopold Aschenbrenner is voting with real weights, 14.9% of his book in $SNDK and more in the memory-and-power complex. And the 2026 scoreboard says they have been right all year: $SNDK up 635%, $MU up 242%, $WDC up 213%, $MRVL up 189%. Memory is not participating in this market, it is leading it.

The bear case, in full, and it is not what most people think. The mistake is treating the Burry short as a fundamental call, when it is nothing of the kind. Per the Market list, Burry's Substack short is pure technical analysis, Micron overextended from its 200-day average targeting roughly a 30% drawdown, with nothing about HBM or supply under it. That is a bet on gravity and positioning rather than on the business.

And on that narrow point he might be right, because the real bear case this week was crowding. This was, in the words of Tuesday's edition, the most crowded buy on the board, the rare moment when every independent desk piled onto the same trade at the same time, and crowds like that either front-run a move or mark a local top. The stock closing under $1,000 mid-week, then the concentrated flush that took $SNDK down 14.8% and $MRVL down 11.2% in a session, is what an overowned trade does when it exhales.

The two legitimate fundamental worries are about timing rather than thesis. The first is the 2028 air pocket, the point where the new supply Mehrotra is building finally lands and the shortage eases, a real risk to the out-years even if it does not bite in 2026. The second is macro. The Fed turned hawkish enough this week that a July hike went from unthinkable to a coin flip under Kevin Warsh, and Tom Lee mapped a September-to-October correction driven by the Anthropic and OpenAI IPO supply shock. A high-beta, heavily-owned name like Micron is exactly what gets sold first in that kind of drawdown, regardless of how good the invoice looks.

The verdict, and what to do with it. My conviction is that the bull case is the stronger one and the bear case is mostly about the entry, not the asset. When the supplier is invoicing the price hike, the CEO is turning away a third of his demand, and the stock trades at 6x forward, you are being paid to own the structural short in memory. But Burry is shorting the crowd, and a tape this one-sided can hand him his 30% on positioning alone before the fundamentals win. So the play is to own the core position through the noise and treat any Fed-driven or IPO-supply-driven flush as the place to add rather than the reason to exit.

  • Levels that matter. Stocks with Josh put a measured-move target of $1,500 on Micron, activating on a reclaim of $1,250, with a hard bounce expected first off the $895 fibonacci level. That $895-to-$1,250 zone is the accumulation range if the autumn correction Tom Lee flagged actually arrives.
  • The watchlist around it. The bottleneck arc runs compute to memory to connectivity, so the same trade lives in $MRVL on the connectivity leg, $SNDK and $WDC on the storage leg, and the neoclouds that rent the compute, $NBIS above all, which kept signing capacity all week. $INTC is the wild card, up 226% on the year as the only leading-edge US foundry with its 18A yield reportedly fixed.
  • The risk control. Size it like the high-beta position it is and never like a core holding you can forget about. The thing that breaks this trade is not a bad Micron quarter, it is a Warsh hike or the IPO air pocket dragging every crowded AI name down together. Keep dry powder for that, because that is when you want to be buying the invoice, not selling the shakeout.
