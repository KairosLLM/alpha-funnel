Taiwan Semiconductor beat every number and raised its spending, and the stock fell anyway, because the thing scaring this tape is no longer demand, it is the bill for meeting it

TSMC posted a double beat and lifted capex again, pledging another $100 billion for Arizona, and Wall Street sold it. The momentum unwind that started in memory is now the widest in decades, Netflix reports after the close, and the day's real argument moved from whether the AI trade is real to who actually gets paid inside it.

The morning was a rate story out of Seoul. The afternoon is a spending story out of everywhere, and the two are the same lesson from different angles. Here is the session recap, then five stories, then the single names.

Session recap. The tape spent the day punishing good news. Per the Market list, Taiwan Semiconductor beat on revenue at $40.2 billion, beat on earnings at $4.31 a share, guided next quarter up to $45.2 billion, and still fell around 4 percent because it raised capital spending again and committed another $100 billion to Arizona. The momentum unwind that began in memory got historic underneath it, with Goldman's high-beta momentum basket down 24 percent on the month, its worst since April 2009, and Morgan Stanley's tech-momentum measure posting its worst 17-day stretch in 27 years, per the Financial Markets list.

Producer prices came in cooler at 5.5 percent against 6.2 percent expected, UnitedHealth blew out and ran 8.5 percent, and Netflix reports after the close with the options market pricing an 8.5 percent move. The one clean win in chips was SK Hynix, whose new Nasdaq listing raised $26.5 billion and closed at $168 after pricing at $149, per the People list.

1. TSMC proved the demand and got sold on the spending, and that is the whole market in one print. The result was not the problem. Per Diet TBPN, Taiwan Semiconductor beat and lifted its capex guidance, and the Nasdaq slid about a percent on the spending plan rather than the earnings, the same reflex that hit the stock itself. When the best-run company in the supply chain tells you it has to spend more to keep up, the market now hears risk instead of confidence.

That reflex is showing up in the megacaps too. The reason Oracle, Meta, and Microsoft have been the ones getting hit, per a widely shared ValueInvesting writeup, is that the market is actively pricing their leverage and their capex rather than ignoring it, so the crowd has not fully detached from fundamentals. Oracle is the sharpest version, funding a roughly $56 billion buildout with tens of billions in new debt while it lacks the cash flows of the other hyperscalers. The uncomfortable question underneath the whole tape is who is paying for all the tokens, because a lot of the demand traces back to unprofitable startups with no moat.

2. The rotation everyone is arguing about is really two different questions, and they have two different answers. The bullish read is patience. Per AskLivermore, the average tech-out-of-favor rotation since 2000 has lasted 201 days and this one is only 42 days in, which puts the historical turn near mid-October, and in all nine prior episodes the money eventually came back to tech on a Fed pivot or an earnings beat. That is a reason to hold, and it is also a reason not to expect the bottom this week.

The sharper take is that the rotation is not out of tech at all. Per Chip Stock Investor, the move nobody named is the hyperscaler capex flood itself, roughly $482 billion trailing that heads toward $800 billion by year-end and likely past a trillion in 2027, rotating out of big-tech free cash flow and into memory, optical, power, and construction. The real danger day is whenever that spending stops, because margins would snap back on a much larger revenue base.

For the wreckage in the meantime, Shay Boloor laid out the damage from the highs, with Oklo down 76 percent, CoreWeave down 60 percent, AST SpaceMobile down 58 percent, Rocket Lab down 55 percent, and even Arm off 42 percent. The cold inflation prints that cut July hike odds hard, from 42 percent to 17 percent, are the setup for the pivot the patient case is waiting on, though Kevin Warsh spent two days testifying that he has no tolerance for sticky inflation, per VirtualBacon and Blockworks.

3. SpaceX broke its own IPO price, and the people who model it for a living think the floor is far lower. The most-owned new stock in the market cracked. Per Prof G Markets, SpaceX fell below its $135 IPO price for the first time and touched an all-time low of $132.75, now down 40 percent from its peak and more than a trillion dollars off the top, one week after joining the Nasdaq 100. Morningstar's Nicholas Owens puts fair value at $62 a share on a probability-weighted basis, with the medium case alone near $71 and the $130-to-$160 bull case carrying only about a 7 percent weight.

The mechanical problem is supply. The float is only about 4 percent of the company, so every move is amplified, and roughly 7 percent more becomes unlockable by insiders about two days after the August 8 earnings report, against index-inclusion demand Owens estimates at just $8 billion. The sell side has not caught up, with 27 of 31 analysts still rating it a buy and an average target of $242, one desk carrying $800, and Cathie Wood buying about $17 million of it into the drop, per the People list. Starlink is the real asset, but a 4 percent float meeting an insider unlock is a market-structure trap before it is a fundamental one.

4. The single-name tape is quietly picking AMD over Nvidia and hardware over hype. The most detailed bull case of the day is a reappraisal of the chip order. A long ValueInvesting breakdown argues the race has moved from raw chip speed to system-level memory and total cost, and lays out a model where an AMD Instinct node carries 768 gigabytes of memory against 384 on Nvidia's dual-die part, at an average price near $25,000 versus $45,000 and up. Those figures are one anonymous author's engineering math rather than vendor-confirmed numbers, and the case leans on a disputed report that Nvidia slipped a rack design by a year, which Nvidia denies, so treat it as a thesis and not a spec sheet.

What is not modeled is the price action, with AMD up 274 percent over the past year as investors reprice it as AI infrastructure rather than a CPU maker, per Stocks with Josh.

The same preference for the physical layer runs through the smaller names. Applied Digital just brought another 75 megawatts online to reach 175 megawatts operational at a fully leased campus targeting 400, with CoreWeave as the anchor tenant, per this breakdown. And the software-side bet with a real number attached is Robinhood, where the Milk Road thesis is that its roughly 30 million customers each adding trading and spending agents multiplies the transactions it clips, against a McKinsey estimate of a trillion dollars of agent-driven consumer revenue by 2030.

5. Crypto is rotating inside itself again, and the tells are Ethereum and Hyperliquid, not Bitcoin. Bitcoin is the laggard and the leaders are elsewhere. Per Lark Davis, Larry Fink called leverage washed out and said he is bullish over the next 12 months, but Bitcoin keeps getting rejected at its 50-day average and the 200-day near the old $83,000 level remains the line that separates a bounce from a bull market, with the last rejection there dumping it to $57,000 in weeks. Underneath, three fresh wallets pulled 30,000 Ether off Coinbase Prime, the accumulation signal that tends to lead.

The loudest Ethereum bet is Tom Lee. Per this rundown, his BitMine now holds 5.75 million Ether, close to 5 percent of supply, earning roughly a million dollars a day in staking yield, and he added another 20,000 Ether last week while sitting on a paper loss near $9 billion. And the token generating its own demand regardless of Bitcoin is Hyperliquid, which rose 5.5 percent on a day Bitcoin managed 2.1 percent while routing about 99 percent of its trading fees into buybacks, per Milk Road. The near-term caution stays real, with the technicians eyeing a push toward $69,000 that fails and a flush to the mid-$50,000s before the next leg.

Single names to track.

  • $TSM (Taiwan Semiconductor). Beat on revenue and earnings, guided up to $45.2 billion, and fell about 4 percent on a raised capex plan and another $100 billion for Arizona, per Diet TBPN.
  • $SPCX (SpaceX). Broke its $135 IPO price to an all-time low of $132.75 against a Morningstar fair value of $62 and a 4 percent float facing an August insider unlock, per Prof G Markets.
  • $AMD. Up 274 percent on the year as the market reprices it as AI infrastructure, with the bull case now built on memory capacity and cost per node, per Stocks with Josh and the AMD breakdown.
  • $APLD (Applied Digital). Added 75 megawatts to reach 175 operational at a fully leased campus targeting 400, anchored by CoreWeave, per this breakdown.
  • $HOOD (Robinhood). The agentic-commerce bet, roughly 30 million customers each able to run multiple trading and spending agents, up about 50 percent off the year's lows, per Milk Road.
  • $BMNR (BitMine). Holds 5.75 million Ether near 5 percent of supply at about a million a day in staking yield, adding into a $9 billion paper loss, per this rundown.
  • HYPE (Hyperliquid). Outran Bitcoin more than two to one on the day while routing 99 percent of fees into buybacks, per Milk Road.

Psychology. The market handed you two clean experiments today. Taiwan Semiconductor did everything right, beat every line, and got sold anyway, while the drawdown list from Shay Boloor shows Oklo down 76 percent and CoreWeave down 60 percent from names that were untouchable two months ago. The lesson is that in a momentum unwind, being right on the business is not the same as being right on the stock, and the widest momentum reversal in 27 years does not care which company reported.

The trap in a tape like this is treating a rotation as a verdict. Per AskLivermore, the money has come back to tech every single time across nine rotations since 2000, and this one is barely six weeks into an average of nearly seven months, so the crowd calling the top today is likely as early as the crowd that called Micron to $2,000 was late. The people who model SpaceX for a living put fair value at $62 while 27 of 31 analysts still say buy, and both of those cannot be right.

So the discipline into the Netflix print tonight is to separate the timeline from the thesis. If you believe the buildout is real, the capex math says the demand is not the risk, the day the spending stops is, and that day is not on tonight's tape. Let the momentum finish puking, let the insider unlocks clear, and buy the physical layer that is sold out rather than the leveraged expression that round-trips on a headline. Patience is a position here, and it is the only one the history actually rewards.
