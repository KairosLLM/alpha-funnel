The AI trade is going into Alphabet and Tesla this week carrying $662 billion of data-center leases that are not on anyone's balance sheet yet, and that off-balance-sheet pile is the bear case nobody is pricing

A careful accounting read says the five hyperscalers have signed lease commitments worth more than their entire adjusted debt, none of it recognized until the buildings switch on. Micron reclaimed its line, Hasbro reports this morning, and Tesla and Alphabet both print tomorrow into a market that looks priced for perfection.

A Tuesday of quiet accumulation before a loud two days. The tape recovered its footing, but the most important thing written this weekend was not a price target, it was a footnote. Here is the pre-open picture, then five stories.

Pre-open preview. The setup is calm on the surface and stretched underneath. The S&P sits just 2.18 percent off its record, per Capital Flows, while the memory names keep bouncing and Micron reclaimed the level the chartists were watching. The earnings calendar is the whole story, with Hasbro reporting before the open this morning, then Alphabet and Tesla both after tomorrow's close and Intel on Thursday. Oil is softer after Iran floated a ceasefire, and the market is treating this week's prints as the referee for whether the AI selloff was a scare or a top.

1. The $662 billion nobody has to report yet is the real risk under the AI trade. The most important analysis of the weekend is an accounting one. Per a detailed ValueInvesting writeup, the five largest hyperscalers had $662 billion of signed data-center leases sitting off their balance sheets at the end of 2025, more than two-thirds of their $969 billion of total future lease commitments and roughly 113 percent of their adjusted debt, none of it recognized because the accounting rule defers a lease until the building commences.

The obligation is real, it is just disclosed in three places at once. Per the same writeup, Moody's flags potentially hundreds of billions more in unquantified residual-value guarantees, and more than $120 billion of spending has been moved into off-balance-sheet vehicles funded by Pimco, Apollo, and Blue Owl, including Meta's roughly $30 billion Hyperion structure and $69 billion of Oracle sale-leasebacks.

Michael Burry's work projects Oracle overstating its 2028 earnings by about 27 percent and Meta by about 21 percent. The author's conclusion is the disciplined one, that he will not pay above roughly 20 times corrected earnings for any of these names until the leases commence and the guarantees get a number, which lands the group at 14 to 16 times reported. That is the bear case going into Alphabet, and it is invisible on every screen.

2. Micron reclaimed its line and the memory bounce has a target now. The technical story stayed constructive. Per Stocks with Josh, Micron fell all the way from $1,255, dropped to $804 in the morning, then recaptured the $861 level that the same trader had flagged on the way down, and if it holds $861 into a close the next target sits near $1,089.

The read across the group is that the pullback may be exhausting. The broader risk-off caution is still there, with the same trader warning that midterm-election years tend to see weakness from August into October before a rally, per that breakdown. These are chart calls from a promotional channel, so treat the exact levels as one technician's map, but the message that the memory names found support while the accounting question hangs over them is the tension the whole week has to resolve.

3. Tesla reports tomorrow and Hasbro reports this morning, and the options market is braced. The single-name catalysts bracket the week. Per Stocks with Josh, Tesla reports after tomorrow's close with the options market pricing a 7.1 percent move against a 4.3 percent recent average, its key support levels stacked at 350, 322, and 300, and one desk flagging heavy buying of the $150 puts expiring this Friday.

The sleeper same-day print is Hasbro. Per a Wall Street Bets writeup, Hasbro reports before the open this morning with the whole growth story riding on Wizards of the Coast and Magic the Gathering, where the Marvel Superheroes set is outselling expectations, a Nintendo signing for 2027 is teased as bigger than Final Fantasy, and direct-selling on Amazon captures margin that used to leak to distributors. The toy business only has to stop shrinking for the market to look past it, so a Magic beat this morning is the quiet setup nobody outside the hobby is watching.

4. The valuation reckoning is not just the hyperscalers, it is Apple too. The theme underneath the week is that the whole large-cap complex is priced for a world that may not arrive. Per a reverse-DCF writeup, Apple at around $334 implies free-cash-flow growth of 14.1 percent a year for a decade, taking cash flow from $129 billion to nearly $484 billion, while its actual three-year cash-flow growth has been negative 3.9 percent and its earnings growth has come mostly from buybacks rather than the business.

The scenarios put the stock above even the bull case. The same analysis lands Apple at roughly $120 in a bear case, $143 in a base, and $162 if everything goes right, which means the price sits above the optimistic outcome at 26 times trailing earnings. And while the crowd chases the leaders, the one name with the cleanest moat gets ignored, with a ValueInvesting post noting that Taiwan Semiconductor trades neglected on its Taiwan risk even as Intel's 18A process reportedly reaches 85 percent yield and becomes the geopolitically safe alternative.

5. The crypto crowd is compressing into October and eyeing a forced seller. The trading collectives are converging on the same clock. Per the Price optimist newsletter, the summer thesis is playing out with strong assets going sideways and weak ones drifting down, the S&P's 7,100 to 7,300 range flagged as value, and Bitcoin expected to compress into October before a fourth-quarter rally, with a loss of $57,000 the level that breaks the case. The specific risk they name is a forced seller, a digital-asset treasury company under credit stress with Michael Saylor behind it, where a reversing premium could bring fast selling toward the $40,000s.

The AI angle is the one that ties back to the stock market. Per threadguy, Kimi is raising money at roughly $30 billion while shipping a model that beats Claude Fable at a third of the cost, against OpenAI and Anthropic both valued near a trillion, which he frames as the emperor-has-no-clothes moment for the model makers, where the capex names stay bid but the model providers are squeezed. The bull counter is the one Avichal Garg lays out, per Milk Road, that real-world assets moving on-chain against $5 trillion of stablecoins hunting yield is the ETH-ICO moment for the whole category, the multi-trillion case that only pays off in 2027 and beyond.

Psychology. Hold two facts side by side this morning. The neocloud names ripped higher yesterday on signed contracts, and the most careful analyst of the week just showed that $662 billion of the leases underpinning those contracts is not on anyone's balance sheet, with Michael Burry's math implying Oracle and Meta may be overstating their future earnings by a fifth or more. The crowd is buying the demand and ignoring the disclosure, and both of those cannot keep being true forever.

The tell of a market priced for perfection is that even the safe names fail the arithmetic. Apple trades above its own best-case valuation, the hyperscalers only look reasonable if you ignore the leases they have not booked, and the trading desks are quietly naming a forced seller with Saylor's name attached as the thing that cracks crypto toward the $40,000s. When the bull case for the index requires lower rates and higher growth than the economy is actually delivering, single-stock analysis becomes a macro bet whether you meant it to or not.

So the discipline into Alphabet and Tesla tomorrow is to separate the demand from the accounting. The AI buildout is real and the memory names found their footing, but a trade whose leverage lives in footnotes and off-balance-sheet vehicles is one where the risk shows up all at once when the leases commence. Let the prints land, hold enough cash that a soft capex guide or a Saylor-driven crypto flush is your entry, and remember that the $662 billion does not disappear because it is hard to see. It just waits for the building to switch on.
