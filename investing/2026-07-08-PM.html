The chips broke, a war reignited, and the crowd answered by shoving a record five billion dollars into semiconductor ETFs to buy the dip

Nvidia lost its 50-day and Bitcoin fell to 62,000 as oil pushed past 80 on the Iran strikes, yet fund-manager cash sits at a 20-year low and every analyst in the room is doubling down. Under the crypto panic, whales just made the biggest accumulation in history. Here is the recap.

StocksMacroBitcoinCrypto

Today the market had every reason to be afraid and refused. A war reignited, oil spiked, semis broke their trend, and the response was not to sell but to pile in harder. That reflex, buying every dip with no cash left over, is the whole story tonight. Under it, in the asset everyone has given up on, the smart money did the opposite. Here is the close, then the six things that matter.

The close. Risk-off, and it was the war, not the Fed. Per the Market list, the Nasdaq closed down about 1% with the $VIX up hard and millions of $QQQ puts on the tape, driven by the overnight strike on 80-plus Iranian targets and Trump declaring the ceasefire over. Per the Financial Markets list, Brent pushed past $80 and WTI is up roughly $7 in two days as Russia banned diesel exports, while the 10-year yield tore 20 basis points higher this month to 4.6%, a quiet bond rout under the oil headline.

The Fed minutes came out this afternoon and the tape barely noticed. The rate path is the real puzzle: per the People-to-follow list, a soft June jobs print of 57,000 had cut July hike odds toward 18%, but the oil shock is now pulling them back the other way.

1. The dip-buyers showed up in size, and that is the debate. The most important number today was not a price, it was a flow. Per the Crypto list, the $SOXX semiconductor ETF took in $5.4B in a single day, four times its old record, with $7.1B flooding into long semi ETFs to buy a group that had fallen 16% in two weeks.

The chorus is loud to match. Per Milk Road, all five of its analysts are buying the dip, with Melvin doubling down on $NBIS and $CRWV and adding $MRVL, and per the Stocks list, fund-manager cash sits at its lowest in more than 20 years. Everyone is already long the bounce, and that is exactly the condition Joseph Hogue warns about: per Hogue, the Dalbar data shows the average investor earns 7% against the S&P's 10.6% precisely by chasing and panicking, a gap worth $1.3M over 30 years.

2. Nvidia is cheap now, and it is still the fulcrum of everything. The one name that decides the tape got cheaper and weaker at once. Per the Stocks list, $NVDA lost its 50-day average alongside the whole chip complex, but it trades below 20 times earnings, the cheapest since 2019 and a steep discount to Broadcom's 53 times, and it caught a bid after China cleared Alibaba, ByteDance, and DeepSeek to buy roughly 200,000 H200 chips.

The bull catalyst is concrete. Per Rick Orford, Anthropic is now running Claude on Nvidia's GB300 Blackwell through Microsoft Azure, a full hardware-cloud-model agent stack, and 49 analysts rate it a strong buy. The tension is the whole market in one chart: the best fundamental story in tech, sitting on a broken 50-day, into a war.

3. Memory bifurcated, and Micron is the only one still standing. The best trade of the year is splitting in two. Per the Stocks list, $MU was the only name in the complex to hold its 50-day, needing 971 to confirm a reversal, while $SNDK, $WDC, $STX, and $INTC all lost theirs.

The Samsung tell is the one to sit with. Per Prof G Markets, Samsung posted a blowout with revenue more than doubling and profit up 1,800%, and the stock fell 7% and dragged the market, because investors decided it had already gone too far. Michael Burry is short Micron betting on a 30% drop, and memory was the single best group in the market in the first half, up 276% against 49% for the rest of semis. When the best fundamentals produce a selloff, the price already knew.

4. Bitcoin capitulated into the biggest whale buy in its history. The crypto panic hid the real signal. Per Lark Davis, Bitcoin fell to $61,600 on the Iran escalation and is trying to hold wedge support, and per the Crypto list sentiment is flatly dire, with per 0xResearch 25 of 28 crypto sectors closing red on the day.

But look at who was on the other side. Per Lark Davis, whales accumulated 270,000 Bitcoin in late June, the largest single accumulation spike ever, larger than the COVID or FTX bottoms, buying near $59,000 while forced sellers redeemed a record $4.5B out of the ETFs, the worst month ever per Lark Davis. The counterweight to respect, per Benjamin Cowen, is that this is a countertrend window of strength in a midterm year, a rally into late July before a probable lower low into a Q4 bottom. Forced sellers meeting patient whales is how bottoms are built, but the calendar says not yet.

5. The rotation already has names, and they are AI dressed as something else. The money leaving crowded chips is finding cheaper AI exposure. Per Milk Road, Galaxy Digital $GLXY near $25 owns a data-center business, Helios, worth roughly $38 a share on its own through a 526-megawatt CoreWeave deal worth over $1B a year, which means the market is pricing its five other businesses below zero.

Per a second Milk Road note, Nebius $NBIS carries a $300 year-end target on a $49B backlog from Meta and Microsoft, growing revenue 684% while owning its full stack instead of renting and marking up. Per Milk Road, the same rotation has lifted $LLY, $HOOD, and software since June 25 as memory and the neoclouds bled.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $NBIS (Nebius). Per Milk Road, a $300 year-end target on Q1 revenue up 684% to $399M, a $49B backlog, and a guided $7 to $9B annualized run rate, the neocloud that owns its own stack. The risk is the same capex-versus-cash question hanging over the whole group.
  • $PLTR (Palantir). Per Rick Orford, near $130 with an average target of $194 and a BofA high of $255 on 72% revenue growth, but priced at 142 times earnings, so the August 3 earnings are the whole trade. Above 40% growth and a raise is the bull, a drift to 30% is the bear.
  • $GLXY (Galaxy Digital). Per Milk Road, a sum-of-parts where the Helios data-center business alone covers the price and a loan refinancing from 9% toward 7% would roughly double free cash flow to equity. The catalyst is the first reported Helios rent next earnings.
  • $COIN (Coinbase). Per Milk Road, the agentic-payments play that owns stablecoins, the Base chain, and an agent marketplace, framed as the pick-and-shovel if machines start transacting.
  • $TER (Teradyne). Per Milk Road, a chip-equipment name down about 30% that one analyst is buying into the semis washout, a cyclical way to play a memory bottom without the single-name risk.

Psychology. Watch what the crowd actually did today. The chips broke their trend, a war reignited, oil ran past $80, and the answer was to shove a record $5.4B into semiconductor ETFs, per the Crypto list, with fund-manager cash at a 20-year low, per the Stocks list. That is not fear, it is total conviction that every dip is a gift, and it is the most expensive belief there is right after the best trade in the market cracks. When everyone is already fully long the bounce and holding no cash, there is nobody left to buy it higher.

Now look at Bitcoin for the clean mirror. Per Lark Davis, forced sellers just dumped a record amount out of the ETFs straight into the single largest whale accumulation in history, 270,000 coins bought near $59,000 while the crowd called it dead. One tape is euphoric dip-buying into a broken chart with no dry powder left, the other is patient whales quietly eating a panic. Per Joseph Hogue, the question that separates the two is the only one that matters tonight: if you did not already own it, would you buy it here, and do you have anything left to buy it with. The edge is never where the crowd has already spent all its cash.
