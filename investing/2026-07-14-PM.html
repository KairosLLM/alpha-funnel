June inflation printed its biggest monthly drop since 2020 and the market sold it, while IBM had its worst day since 1987 and the money it lost went straight into memory

Core CPI came in at 2.6 percent and July hike odds collapsed from 42 to 15 percent, yet stocks gave back the pop because the new Fed chair is hawkish and oil is a live wire. IBM fell 25 percent as clients diverted spending into the memory chips that ripped, and Korea just showed what leverage squared does on the way down.

MacroStocksCrypto

Today handed the market a genuinely great inflation number and the market shrugged, because the story underneath the print is a violent rotation, not a soft landing. Here is the session recap, then five stories.

The close. June CPI came in ice cold, headline 3.5 percent against 3.8 expected and core (which strips out food and energy) at 2.6, with the monthly reading down 0.4 percent, the biggest drop since May 2020, per the CPI breakdown. Odds of a July rate hike cratered from 41.7 percent to 16.6, yet the tape sold the news, spiking the S&P about 60 handles and giving back 40, per the Financial Markets list. The Nasdaq held a 1.3 percent gain and semis ran 4.5 percent while software fell, small caps are now on track for their best year since 2003, and the VIX (the market's fear gauge) sits at a calm 17.

Two things capped the rally. New Fed chair Kevin Warsh testified hawkish, saying inflation is a choice and holding rates at 3.5 to 3.75 percent, so the debate is now one hike or none with zero cuts priced this year. And oil whipsawed, spiking toward $86 when Trump reinstated the Hormuz blockade and then reversing when he walked the 20 percent toll back within a day.

1. IBM's worst day since 1987 is the clearest read-through to the memory bull all year. IBM fell about 25 percent and erased $50 to $65 billion of market cap on a soft quarter, its largest drop in decades, per the Market list. The reason matters more than the number. CEO Arvind Krishna said clients diverted their spending into servers, storage, and memory to get ahead of price hikes, and did not anticipate the magnitude, so the miss is not weak demand, it is demand leaving software for hardware.

The tape proved the point in real time. Software names sold off in sympathy, with Microsoft, Adobe, Palantir, Salesforce, and Figma all lower, while the memory complex ripped, SK Hynix up as much as 15 to 20 percent to a new high, Micron up 5 to 6 with its calls jumping 40 percent, and SanDisk up 7, per the Market AM list. Memory gross margins now top Nvidia's, Micron at 80 percent, SK Hynix at 72, and SanDisk at 69 against Nvidia's 66. When the money leaving the biggest software name in the world shows up the same day in memory margins, the rotation is not a theory anymore.

2. Cold CPI, hawkish chair, and one live oil wire, so the Fed cannot fully relax. The print was the kind of number that should have run stocks all day, energy fell 5.7 percent and pulled headline inflation down hard. The reason it did not stick is the same Fed that spent the spring flirting with hikes. Warsh is not the dovish chair the market wanted, and with September hike odds still above 50 percent and no cut priced before year-end, a soft CPI buys relief rather than a pivot.

The wildcard is the thing that made this print good in the first place. Energy fell because oil had come off, and oil is one Trump headline away from erasing that, as today showed when Brent spiked toward $86 on the Hormuz blockade and then gave it all back on the walk-back, per the Financial Markets list. The disinflation that let the Fed stand pat is sitting on a barrel of crude in the Strait of Hormuz, and that is not a stable foundation.

3. The memory trade got its own leverage circus, and Korea is the warning label. SK Hynix's two leveraged US ETFs launched today into the melt-up, and the skew tells you where the crowd is, the 2x long taking 722,000 shares against just 102,000 for the short, a seven-to-one tilt, with the long product up 9 percent, per the People list. That is the American version of what already broke in Seoul.

The cautionary tale is on the Prof G episode: Korea has launched 16 single-stock leveraged ETFs since late May, tripling to over $9 billion, and those products plus Samsung and SK Hynix now make up more than 70 percent of Korean trading volume. The guest called it leverage squared, margin loans buying 2x products, and it is exactly why SK Hynix could fall 15 percent Monday and rise 15 percent Tuesday. The BofA fund-manager survey names the risk directly, 82 percent call long semiconductors the most crowded trade on the board, per the People AM list. A crowded trade with a fresh leverage layer bolted on is not safer for the new toy.

4. Neoclouds are the next leg of the buildout, and the balance sheet is the whole call. The freshest capital-spending number, per Milk Road, is Morgan Stanley lifting hyperscaler capex to $1.23 trillion in 2027 and $1.4 trillion in 2028, now counting SpaceX as a hyperscaler, with neocloud revenue projected to compound 58 percent a year to roughly $400 billion by 2031. The two names to know sit on opposite ends of the risk spectrum. $CRWV (CoreWeave) has a $99.4 billion order backlog against a $50 billion market cap, but carries $24.9 billion of debt and burns cash. $NBIS (Nebius) grew first-quarter AI-cloud revenue 841 percent to $389 million with $9.3 billion of cash and almost no debt, plus a ClickHouse stake worth about 30 percent of its market value.

The distinction is the trade. In a business where most of the more than 100 emerging neoclouds will fail, per the same note, the one with the cash survives the moments the levered one cannot, and Nvidia is now backstopping GPU financing for these players directly to keep the revenue floor under them. Milk Road's targets are $NBIS at $300 to $325 and $CRWV at $150, but the durable version of the trade is the balance sheet, not the backlog.

5. Bitcoin's bottom looks close, but everyone can see the same October. The on-chain read is constructive without being a green light, per the On-Chain Mind panel: Bitcoin's median holder cost basis sits near $63,000, its production cost near $60,000, and the share of supply in profit has fallen to 45 percent, which has marked the 2020 and 2022 bottoms. The structural floor calls cluster at $50,000 to $60,000, with a bottom only confirmed above $74,000, and both the long-term-holder cost crossover and the four-year cycle point at October. Bitcoin bounced to $64,000 on the CPI print today with Ether leading for once, up 5 percent past $1,800 as more than $100 million of shorts got liquidated.

The catch is that the whole market sees the same date. Per Milk Road, VanEck wants a full Bitcoin allocation by October and Jordi Visser says Bitcoin and Ether become the crowded trade within a year, and that is precisely why one trader argues the move front-runs the consensus and comes sooner. When everyone is waiting to buy the same low, the low rarely waits for them.

Single names worth your watchlist.

  • $SKHY (SK Hynix). Per Edelbridge, the HBM memory near-monopoly at 56.4 percent global share, trading at a forward price-to-earnings of just 7 with 72 percent operating margins and a locked allocation in Nvidia's next-generation Vera Rubin chips. The weak spot is geopolitics, helium and the Strait of Hormuz.
  • $NBIS / $CRWV (Nebius, CoreWeave). The neocloud pair, per Milk Road, Nebius the cash-rich survivor and CoreWeave the levered backlog bet.
  • $VST (Vistra). Per Rick Orford, 44,000 megawatts of generation and a 20-year, 2,600-megawatt nuclear deal with Meta, at 13 times earnings, as data-center power demand is set to more than double by 2030.
  • $QCOM (Qualcomm). Per Chip Stock Investor, a quiet data-center pivot targeting $15 billion of AI data-center revenue by 2029 from zero, via the AlphaWave and Modular acquisitions, at roughly $190.
  • $NFLX (Netflix). Per Stocks with Josh, the earnings-dip trade around Thursday's print near $76, with a $1.8 million call buy at the $78 strike.
  • $IONQ (IonQ). Per Rick Orford, the quantum infrastructure bet acquiring US chipmaker SkyWater for vertical integration, speculative with no proven moat and no hard numbers yet.

Psychology. The two records that bracket this session tell the whole story. Yesterday SanDisk printed the highest monthly RSI ever recorded on any stock, and today IBM had its worst day since 1987, and they are the same trade seen from both ends, money fleeing everything that is not memory and piling into everything that is. The BofA survey caught the crowd in the act, with 82 percent calling long semiconductors the most crowded position on the board, right as SK Hynix's 2x ETF opened to a seven-to-one long skew.

That is what a late-stage rotation feels like from the inside, total conviction that this one trade cannot lose, expressed through more and more leverage. Korea already ran the experiment, where single-stock 2x products became 70 percent of the volume and the index swung 15 percent in a day in both directions. The American memory names are now getting the same machinery, and the market sold a great CPI print rather than chasing, the tape quietly telling you the easy buyers are getting scarce.

So the discipline check is about which trade you are being handed. Buying the 2x SK Hynix ETF at a new high, the day after the underlying printed a record RSI, is the leverage-squared trade Seoul just showed you the downside of. The harder and better positioning is where the survivors are, in the cash-rich neocloud like Nebius over the levered one, in the memory near-monopoly at seven times earnings rather than the 2x wrapper on top of it. Let Warsh and the oil wire settle before you decide the crowd was right to need this much leverage to stay long.
