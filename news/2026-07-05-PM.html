Khamenei's own son skipped his funeral, and China put a number on the robots taking the jobs: 700,000, with 120 schools to retrain the survivors

A missing heir hinted at a succession fight in Tehran, JD.com named the workforce it will automate away, and Europe spent the weekend discovering its money and its rules do not reach as far as it thought. Here is what moved after the AM edition.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The morning edition left Iran in a mourning pause and the robots as a projection. By evening both had a sharper edge: a succession signal from a funeral, and a company naming the exact number of jobs it will erase. Underneath, Europe had a quietly bad weekend on two fronts at once. Here is the update.

1. The missing mourner in Tehran is the story. Per the Polymarket list, Mojtaba Khamenei, long treated as a possible successor to his father, was reportedly absent from Ali Khamenei's funeral while the rest of Iran's top officials attended. In a system where presence at the funeral is a claim on power, an absence like that reads as friction inside the succession rather than around it. It advances this morning's thread, the US-granted mourning pause and the July 11 talks in Pakistan, by pointing at the question those talks will actually turn on: who Washington will be negotiating with a year from now. In a related move, per the same list, France is sending its carrier the Charles de Gaulle home after roughly two months stationed near the Strait of Hormuz, a quiet de-escalation signal from Europe.

2. China put a hard number on automation, and a retraining plan under it. Per a Nikkei-sourced report, JD.com CEO Liu Qiangdong says robots will replace about 700,000 of the company's delivery workers, and JD has signed with roughly 120 schools to retrain those workers into robot repair and maintenance roles. Beijing has reportedly started tracking AI's hit to employment as a national priority. The comparison that frames it is Amazon avoiding around 600,000 future hires through automation, and the read-through names for who follows next are DoorDash, Uber, and MercadoLibre. This is the concrete escalation of the morning's jobs story: not a warning about displacement, but a headcount, a timeline, and a plan for what happens to the people.

3. Europe found the limits of its money and its rules in the same weekend. Two stories rhymed. On rules, per the Milk Road letter, the MiCA transition window shut on July 1, and roughly 80% of about 1,200 EU crypto firms lack a compliant license and must now wind down, migrate, or leave, even as Brussels fast-tracks a digital euro. On money, per the Polymarket list, France drew 15.1 billion euros from the EU's SAFE defense fund against the 16.2 billion it requested after Franco-British missile projects were ruled ineligible under a 65% EU-origin rule, and Germany's cabinet is set to approve a budget with more than 203 billion euros in fresh borrowing while 20,000 protesters gathered at the AfD's annual conference. A continent trying to build sovereignty in defense and finance kept bumping into its own rulebook.

  • United States, 2028. Per the Kalshi list, Gavin Newsom leads the Democratic nomination market at 21%, down from a 37% high in November, and John James sits at 84% to win Michigan's GOP gubernatorial primary.
