The US bombed more than 80 targets in Iran overnight after Tehran hit ships in the Strait of Hormuz, and oil is the first thing paying for it

A broken ceasefire and three attacked vessels drew a major American strike, sending crude up 5% and halting gas shipments through the Gulf. At home, the jobs picture keeps quietly rotting and the political map swung hard toward Democrats overnight. Here is what moved.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The overnight news was war. The US answered an attack on Gulf shipping with a large strike inside Iran, and the immediate cost is showing up in the oil price. Underneath it, the American labor market is sending a quieter warning and the 2028 political picture reshuffled overnight. Here is the update.

1. The US struck Iran after it attacked ships in Hormuz. The ceasefire is over. Per the Market list, the US hit more than 80 targets inside Iran overnight after Iran attacked three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz, and crude jumped about 5% on the news. Per the Polymarket list, the Treasury revoked Iran's oil-export license, gas shipments through Hormuz have halted, and Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth is set to visit Israel and Netanyahu today. This is the sharpest escalation of the conflict yet, and because roughly a fifth of the world's oil moves through that strait, the market is treating it as a supply shock first and a diplomatic story second.

2. The American jobs picture is quietly deteriorating. The slow story matters as much as the loud one. Per Prof G Markets, the labor force participation rate fell, and the drop is concentrated in prime-age workers between 25 and 54, the cohort that cannot be explained away by boomer retirement. The share of the unemployed who have been out of work for six months or longer has now risen for eighteen straight months with no sign of slowing. The picture is not a collapse and not a recovery, it is a market where there are simply fewer opportunities, the kind of grinding weakness that does not make a headline until it suddenly does.

3. The political map swung toward Democrats overnight. The prediction markets moved hard. Per the Kalshi list, the odds that Maine Democrat Graham Platner drops out crashed from a 96% high yesterday to a 38% low, a wild reversal that keeps him in a race many had written off. At the same time the market for Democrats sweeping the presidency, House, and Senate hit an all-time high of 51%, the odds of a House flip reached 82%, and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez climbed to 18% for the 2028 nomination, within striking distance of Gavin Newsom. A single overnight turned a story of Democratic disarray into one of Democratic momentum.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • China. Per the Polymarket list, China will produce more than 100,000 humanoid robots this year and its central bank is extending its longest gold-buying streak since 2015, two moves that both read as preparing for a more self-reliant future.
  • France. Per the Kalshi list, Marine Le Pen has entered the 2027 race and is the favorite at 38% despite her legal troubles.
  • Argentina. Per the same list, Javier Milei sits at 57% for re-election, a vote of confidence in his shock therapy.
  • United Kingdom. Per the same list, Reform UK is a 96% favorite to win the Clacton by-election triggered by Nigel Farage's resignation.
  • South Korea. Per the Financial Markets list, roughly 183 trillion won, about $123B, was wiped from the Korean market in the semis selloff, even as SK Hynix's $28B US listing came in oversubscribed.
