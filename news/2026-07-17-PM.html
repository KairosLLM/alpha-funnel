The Iran war hit a sixth night with the US now trying to sever Bandar Abbas by knocking out its bridges, and Apple opened a legal front against the people it lost to OpenAI

US strikes moved from Iran's ports to the bridges feeding them while Iran claimed hits on American aircraft and the US denied a deadly base attack. Apple sent legal preservation notices to about forty former employees now at OpenAI, and a fully digital Wall Street ran its first live trades.

WorldTechMarkets

A war grinding into its infrastructure phase, a corporate lawsuit over who built the future, and a quiet milestone in how markets settle. Ranked.

1. The Iran campaign is now trying to strangle a port by taking out its bridges. The war entered its logistics phase. Per the Polymarket list, the sixth consecutive night of US strikes hit bridges in Hormozgan province to cut off the port of Bandar Abbas, destroyed a naval tower at Chabahar again, and struck a railway station and the airport at Iranshahr, moving from hitting ships to hitting the roads and rails that supply them.

The escalation ran both ways and the claims got murkier. Iran's Revolutionary Guard claimed strikes on US jets and refuelers at the Azraq base in Jordan and on naval units, while US Central Command flatly denied an Iranian claim of a deadly attack on the al-Tanf garrison, per the Financial Markets list. A Kuwaiti desalination station was hit and a tanker was struck near Khasab in Oman, the US is sending dozens more refueling planes to Israel, and Chevron is already scouting Iraqi pipeline routes to bypass the Strait of Hormuz. When the fight moves to bridges and desalination plants, the target is no longer the military, it is the country's ability to function.

2. Apple went legal against the talent it lost to OpenAI. The fight over who built the AI device turned into a paper trail. Per the Polymarket list, Apple sent legal preservation notices to about forty former employees now working at OpenAI, roughly a tenth of its former Apple hires, telling them to retain evidence and calling what it has so far the tip of the iceberg, while OpenAI says it sees no merit in the claim.

This is the same dispute that produced the leaked OpenAI speaker earlier in the week, now formalized into a legal threat. Preservation notices are the step before a lawsuit, a signal that Apple is building a case that its former engineers carried hardware secrets across the street. The bigger picture is a company that fell behind in AI reaching for the courts to protect what it could not out-ship, and a talent war between the two most valuable consumer-tech franchises spilling into discovery.

3. Wall Street quietly ran its first fully digital trades. The least dramatic story may be the most durable. Per Blockworks, the Depository Trust and Clearing Corporation, the plumbing that holds more than $114 trillion of American securities, executed its first live production trades of tokenized stocks and Treasuries on Wednesday, with JPMorgan, Vanguard, BlackRock, and the New York Stock Exchange among more than thirty firms involved.

These were real securities settled on a blockchain rather than synthetic copies, tokenizing names like Microsoft, Circle, and the Invesco QQQ. The full service does not launch until October and rests on a temporary regulatory exemption, so this was a controlled first step rather than a finished system. Still, when the institution that settles nearly the entire US market runs live trades on-chain, the tokenization story stops being a crypto pitch and becomes the direction of the market itself.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • A major earthquake struck off Mexico. A magnitude 7.3 quake hit off the coast of Chiapas and triggered a tsunami warning, per the Market list. The Pacific coast spent the evening watching the water.
  • The American consumer perked up. University of Michigan sentiment jumped to 54.4 against expectations near 51, and the one-year inflation outlook eased to 4.2 percent, per the People list. A small bright spot under a heavy tape.
  • Britain is changing prime ministers. Andy Burnham is set to become the UK's prime minister on Monday, per the Polymarket list. A leadership handover lands at the start of the week.
  • A drone-boat startup drew first blood and a giant shipyard. Saronic's autonomous Corsair boats were used in their first combat strike, on an Iranian submarine at Bandar Abbas, and the company announced Port Alpha, a shipyard of more than $3 billion in Brownsville, Texas, the site California had courted and lost, per Not Boring.
