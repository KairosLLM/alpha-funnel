Iran says the Strait of Hormuz is closed, Trump says it is open, and the US ran a third round of strikes while both sides claimed the water

The dueling declarations landed seventeen minutes apart, an Iranian attack left a container-ship crewman missing, and a Jordanian air base took damage as the war widened past Iran's borders. At home, Senator Lindsey Graham died suddenly, and Congress quietly banned a Federal Reserve digital dollar.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

Two stories dominate the evening, a war that widened over the weekend and a Senate seat that emptied without warning. Ranked.

1. Iran and the United States are now openly contradicting each other over whether the world's most important oil chokepoint is open. The cleanest snapshot is on the Kalshi list: at 2:52pm a headline that Iran says the Strait of Hormuz is closed until further notice, at 3:09pm a headline that the US says it is open. Trump made the American position explicit on Meet the Press, saying the strait is open to commercial shipping with US forces positioned for freedom of navigation, per the Market list. Both claims are now live at once, and the ships in the water have to pick which one to believe.

The fighting escalated to make the point. The US ran a third round of strikes Saturday evening, bringing the week's total to roughly 140 targets, and an IRGC attack hit the Cyprus-flagged container ship GFS Galaxy, leaving a civilian crewman missing, per the Crypto and Financial Markets lists. The war also stopped staying inside Iran, with Prince Hassan Air Base in Jordan taking damage from Iranian strikes and explosions reported over Kuwait, while Qatar condemned attacks on its territory and told its vessels to suspend sailing.

There is a de-escalation track running underneath it. Per the Market AM list, Oman has proposed managing the strait through two separately controlled routes, and Oman and Iran agreed to keep talking under the framework of their existing memorandum. Against that, Polymarket puts a 53 percent chance the US blockades Iran by the end of next month, per the Polymarket AM list. A war that Washington declared finished in June is now in what one analyst called its twentieth week, and the two governments cannot even agree on whether the sea lane is open.

2. Lindsey Graham died suddenly, and a safe Republican Senate seat is now open. Per the Polymarket list, Senator Lindsey Graham of South Carolina died Saturday after a brief and sudden illness, with first responders performing CPR for cardiac arrest, and Trump delivered a eulogy. The names already circling the seat are Representative Nancy Mace and 78-year-old Representative Joe Wilson. It is a reliably Republican seat, so the national balance is not in question, but a four-term senator and one of the chamber's louder foreign-policy voices is gone in the middle of an active war he would have been at the center of.

3. Congress banned a Federal Reserve digital dollar, and almost nobody noticed. Per the People list, the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act is now law, and folded inside it is a ban on the Federal Reserve issuing a central-bank digital currency (a government-run digital dollar) through the end of the decade, while privately issued stablecoins like USDC and USDT are explicitly exempt. This is the quiet win the crypto lobby spent two years arguing for, on the claim that a Fed digital dollar would be a surveillance tool, and it passed inside a housing bill rather than as its own fight.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Microsoft cut 4,800 jobs, per the Kalshi AM list, and the backlash is notable because it lands the same week the company is spending record sums on AI. The layoffs-plus-capex combination is becoming the defining corporate posture of the year.
  • Fed Chair Kevin Warsh recommitted hard to the 2 percent inflation target, saying he will not tolerate inflation above it, per the Financial Markets AM list. For a market hoping the new chair means easier policy, that is the opposite signal.
  • Zelenskyy is replacing Prime Minister Yulia Svyrydenko in a reshuffle, with the outgoing premier possibly headed to Washington as ambassador, per the Polymarket list.
  • Argentina's peso hit a new all-time low against the dollar, per the Kalshi AM list, a reminder that the currency crises did not stop just because the headlines moved to the Gulf.
  • The 2028 race is already a market. On Kalshi, JD Vance and Marco Rubio sit tied near 18 percent for the Republican nomination while Gavin Newsom slipped to 12 percent on the Democratic side, per the Kalshi AM list.
