Trump declared the US the guardian of the Strait of Hormuz and put a 20 percent toll on everything that sails through it

The war stopped being about whether the strait is open and became about who taxes it, with the first US combat use of sea drones off Bandar Abbas. Asia lost a trillion dollars overnight, Korea tripped a circuit breaker, and Ukraine widened its own war by hitting a dozen ships in the Sea of Azov.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

The Iran story escalated from a standoff into a policy, and the financial aftershock hit Asia hardest. Ranked.

1. Washington is no longer just calling Iran's bluff, it is taxing the strait. Per the Polymarket and Market lists, Trump declared the United States the guardian of the Strait of Hormuz, reinstated a blockade of Iranian oil, and imposed a 20 percent fee on all cargo transiting the waterway, saying the process begins immediately. That is a different posture than last week. The argument was whether the strait was open, and the answer is now that it is open on American terms and American tolls.

The force behind it escalated too. CENTCOM used sea drones in combat for the first time, three Corsair unmanned vessels striking a submarine and ship-maintenance facility at Bandar Abbas, on top of dozens of strikes on air defense and coastal radar, per the Polymarket list. Brent briefly touched $80, commercial crossings through Hormuz fell 52 percent week over week, and Dubai is now planning a bypass port to route around the chokepoint entirely. Iran's foreign minister says the June memorandum is in a crisis phase and may be abandoned. A strait that a superpower is escorting tankers through while taxing the cargo is technically open and functionally a war zone.

2. The financial shockwave was biggest where the war is not. The clearest measure of how markets took the escalation was in Asia, not the Gulf. Per the Kalshi list, roughly $1 trillion was wiped from Asian markets overnight, with South Korea's KOSPI falling almost 9 percent into a circuit breaker, its worst session since the 2008 crash. The trigger was the memory-chip complex, where SK Hynix fell more than 15 percent a day after listing in New York.

The knock-on is already showing up in the real economy. Global smartphone shipments fell 11 percent to the worst second quarter in 13 years, blamed directly on the memory-chip shortage pricing devices out of reach, per the People list. The AI-driven memory boom that has minted fortunes on Wall Street is now expensive enough to be denting the phone market that memory was supposed to serve.

3. Ukraine widened its own war while the world watched the Gulf. Overnight, per the Polymarket list, Ukraine struck seven tankers, five cargo ships, two tugs, and a ferry in the Sea of Azov and hit a Russian radar and an S-400 air-defense system near Kerch. On the diplomatic side, the UK joined a roughly 90-billion-euro loan package for Kyiv. The pattern from earlier this month holds, with Ukraine treating enemy shipping and energy infrastructure as the front line, and it is landing while Washington's attention is fixed on Iran.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • California and 11 states sued to block the Paramount-Warner Bros. merger, and Paramount is moving its headquarters and redirecting $30 billion out of California, per the People and Polymarket lists. A media megamerger and a corporate exit from the state landed in the same news cycle.
  • Volkswagen is cutting 50,000 jobs, per the Kalshi list. The legacy-automaker squeeze is not a forecast anymore, it is a payroll number.
  • South Carolina is scrambling for Lindsey Graham's seat. His sister Darline Graham has been recommended as interim senator, and the Republican primary is already forming behind her, per the Polymarket list.
  • The Tylenol autism lawsuits are back, with more than 500 revived, per the Polymarket list. A litigation thread markets thought was closed is open again.
  • France shut three nuclear reactors because of the heat, per the Polymarket list. Climate is now a supply-side constraint on the grid that AI is racing to power.
