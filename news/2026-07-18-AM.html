Apple went to court against OpenAI over a texted brag about copied files, and New York became the first state to slam the door on AI data centers

Apple's lawsuit quotes a former engineer messaging a colleague that he could access network storage, and it claims OpenAI poached more than 400 of its people. The AI industry is floating a plan to police itself, New York banned the data centers the industry depends on, and a new study reversed the age of human skin in a dish.

TechPoliticsScience

A courtroom fight, a regulatory experiment, a state saying no, and a genuine scientific jolt. Ranked.

1. Apple sued OpenAI, and the ugliest detail is a text message. The lawsuit got specific. Per the All-In Podcast, Apple filed a 41-page complaint on July 10 alleging OpenAI took trade secrets to build its consumer hardware device, centered on Tang Tan, Apple's former iPhone-design lead who is now OpenAI's chief hardware officer, and allegedly told Apple candidates interviewing at OpenAI to bring actual parts for show and tell.

The line that will follow the case is a text. A departing Apple engineer allegedly messaged a colleague still inside the company that he had found he could access the network storage, adding that it was so funny, and OpenAI has hired more than 400 Apple employees over the past couple of years. Chamath Palihapitiya noted that Apple has not been litigious in 25 years, so the fact that it moved at all suggests something genuinely upset it. The rule David Sacks drew from it is the oldest one in Silicon Valley, that when you leave you can carry what is in your head and nothing else.

2. The AI industry is proposing to police itself, and the fight is over who writes the rules. The most consequential idea of the weekend is a regulator built by the regulated. Per the same discussion, DeepMind's Demis Hassabis proposed a self-regulatory body modeled on FINRA, industry-funded but federally overseen, where frontier labs submit models 30 days before release for testing on catastrophic risks like cyber and biological threats, voluntary at first and mandatory later.

The endorsements ran across the industry, from Elon Musk to Sam Altman to Sundar Pichai, but the argument was about capture. Sacks said he could support it only under strict conditions, that it cover frontier models alone, focus on catastrophic risk rather than speech, and replace a new government agency rather than add to one, warning that a permission-based regime would slow AI from months to years and hand the race to China. His sharper charge was aimed at Anthropic, which he accused of pushing a state-by-state patchwork of tough rules as a form of regulatory capture, a claim Anthropic would dispute.

3. New York banned the data centers the whole industry runs on. The buildout hit its first statewide wall. Per the All-In crew, Governor Kathy Hochul signed the nation's first statewide moratorium on hyperscale data centers, citing power draw, land use, water, and utility bills, a decision the besties spent twenty minutes rebutting point by point.

Their counterarguments were that modern centers can bring their own power behind the meter, that the water fear is overstated because the newest designs recirculate in closed loops, and that the land concern is odd in a state that is mostly undeveloped. The provocative claim from Sacks was that some of the opposition traces to organized influence campaigns, including one that a recent OpenAI post tied to Chinese-linked operations targeting US AI debates, with a congressional look expected. Underneath the politics is a real constraint that Chamath named, that the country faces an energy deficit of several California-sized economies by 2050, and roughly 40 percent of announced data-center projects are already being shelved.

4. And a study reversed the age of human skin. The weekend's science was the optimistic note. Per the show's science segment, Google's longevity company Calico and Revel Pharma published work targeting the glue between cells, where a sugar-protein gunk called CML builds up with age and nothing in the body breaks it down, using the protein-folding tool AlphaFold plus directed evolution to design an enzyme that does not exist in nature.

The result was striking. On donated skin from patients over 70, the enzyme cleared 55 percent of that CML buildup, effectively resetting the skin's biological age to that of a 31-year-old, with in-vitro tests clearing as much as 97 percent on some proteins. The open question is delivery, whether it arrives as a cream, a shot, or something that makes the protein inside the body, and Friedberg framed the first market as cosmetic skin care worth a trillion dollars. It is early and unproven in living people, but it is the rare headline this week that points up.
