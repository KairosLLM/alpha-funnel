Trump handed Iran a week off for Khamenei's funeral, and the robots came for 700,000 jobs in a single announcement

US-Iran talks resume July 11 in Pakistan and Netanyahu heads to the White House, Ukrainian drones reached 850 kilometers inside Russia, and JD.com said it will replace 700,000 delivery workers with machines. The AI jobs story stopped being a projection.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Independence Day weekend gave the feed a split screen: a careful pause in one war and fresh strikes deep inside another, a president clearing 702 rules while his rival warned about democracy, and the clearest sign yet that automation is now a headcount decision, not a thought experiment. Here is what actually moved.

1. The US gave Iran a week off, and the calendar now has real dates on it. Per the Polymarket list, Trump revealed the US granted Iran a pause for the funeral of the late Ayatollah Khamenei, with no shooting until the funeral events conclude, and formal US-Iran talks resume July 11 in Pakistan covering sanctions, frozen Iranian assets, and the nuclear file. Alongside it, Netanyahu is reportedly headed to the White House as early as next week. This is the diplomacy that yesterday's Strait of Hormuz brinkmanship was building toward, and the fact that both sides agreed to a mourning pause is itself the signal that neither wants the shooting war right now.

2. Ukraine reached 850 kilometers into Russia while the Kremlin said nothing has changed. Per the Polymarket list, Ukrainian drones struck Russian oil sites more than 850 kilometers inside Russia on Saturday and destroyed a MiG-29 at the Belbek airbase in Crimea. The Kremlin's Dmitry Peskov reaffirmed the war continues and said Zelensky is welcome to come to Moscow, while Dmitry Medvedev framed the Strait of Hormuz and Bab el-Mandeb as Iran's non-nuclear weapons. The reach of those strikes is the story: the war's front line is now wherever a cheap drone can fly, and Russian oil infrastructure hundreds of miles from Ukraine is inside it.

3. The AI jobs story went from projection to headcount. Per the Market list, JD.com CEO Liu Qiangdong warned that robots will replace 700,000 of the company's delivery workers, and the company has signed with about 120 schools to retrain those workers into robot-repair roles, with Beijing now tracking the AI jobs hit as a national priority. It echoes the earlier Amazon figure of 600,000 future hires avoided, and per the Financial Markets list, BMW is already deploying humanoid robots at its South Carolina factory. This is the concrete version of the labor-market crack the weekend memo traced: not a statistic about displacement, but a company naming the number of jobs and the schools it will send those people to instead.

4. Trump moved to cut 702 federal regulations. Per the Kalshi list, the administration announced plans to eliminate 702 federal regulations. On the same July 4 tape, Bill Clinton used a holiday statement to warn that America faces serious threats to its democracy, and Zelensky said he had a very good call with Trump. The deregulation push is the substantive policy move under the holiday noise, and it is the one to track for what actually gets struck.

5. A heat wave is stress-testing the grid, and the power price is the tell. Per the People-to-follow list, extreme heat is pushing the central and eastern US toward 105°F, and Thursday's wholesale power spot prices jumped hard: New England up 243%, New York City up 101%, the Midwest up 55%, and the Mid-Atlantic up 45.6%, with PJM placed under a federal alert to cut usage. The AI-and-cooling load and the summer heat are hitting the same grid at the same time, and the wholesale price is where that collision shows up first. The investable side of this, the power and grid names, sits in Investing.

Around the world, quickly.

  • Venezuela. Per the Polymarket list, the death toll from the June 24 earthquakes rose to 2,954 dead and 16,592 injured, per Parliament President Jorge Rodríguez.
  • Germany. AfD leader Alice Weidel claimed most migrants will return home once they stop receiving money from the German state, sharpening the same benefits-versus-work line that ran through the weekend's immigration debate.
  • Australia. New South Wales confirmed its first human H5N1 bird-flu case, a marker worth tracking rather than alarm.
  • Indonesia. Per the Financial Markets list, the country jailed entrepreneur Nadiem Makarim, the Gojek founder and former education minister, for roughly a decade.
  • Paul Pelosi. Per the Polymarket list, he reportedly faces a hit-and-run charge after allegedly striking a parked car and driving away.
  • America 250, the messy version. Severe storms cleared the National Mall before Trump's speech, which slid to 11pm, and the Brooklyn Bridge briefly caught fire during New York's fireworks. The anniversary went off, just not on script.
