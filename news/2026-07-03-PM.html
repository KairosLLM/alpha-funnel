The Strait of Hormuz is back as the world's most dangerous chokepoint

Iran is threatening ships again as Brent slips under $72, Washington quietly warned Poland that Russia may stage a provocation, and over the holiday a lab in Minnesota built the first synthetic cell from scratch.

WorldPoliticsMarketsHealth

Two stories run under the holiday quiet, and both deserve more than the thin headlines gave them. The oil chokepoint that defined this year's war risk is live again, and the labor market is softening in a way the cheerful top-line numbers hide. Then, almost unnoticed, science had one of its biggest weeks in years.

1. Iran is threatening ships in the Strait of Hormuz again, and the oil market is starting to price it. The Financial Markets list has Iran warning of a "forceful response" against ships using unapproved routes through the Strait, the channel that carries a fifth of the world's oil. Brent crude (the international oil benchmark) slipped under $72 a barrel. The measured read comes from analyst Anas Alhajji: Iran cannot actually close the Strait, but it can make it expensive and slow, and shipping data already shows crossings down about 10% day over day. A new Polymarket contract now takes bets on whether traffic returns to normal by the end of August.

The escalation is not only in the Gulf. Washington reportedly warned Poland that Russia may stage a limited provocation against NATO in the coming months, a missile strike on infrastructure or a small cross-border incursion, calibrated to pressure allies over Ukraine without triggering a full war. Layer that over Ayatollah Khamenei's funeral drawing millions into Tehran today, and the geopolitical tail that markets spent the spring fearing is back on the table.

2. The labor market is cracking quietly, even as the recession bet keeps losing. Here is the disconnect worth holding onto. The household survey shows the US has lost 1.7 million jobs so far this year, dropping employment to its lowest level since December 2024, per both the Polymarket and Financial Markets feeds. Yet the odds of a US recession this year still sit near record lows.

Both things can be true at once, and that is the point. Lyn Alden's framing captures it: this is a two-speed economy, where older Americans on fixed income and defense contractors do fine while a lot of working people quietly do not. When the jobs data and the recession odds point in opposite directions, the jobs data is the one you can count.

3. Over the holiday, science had a genuinely historic week. This is the part that got buried under the market noise, and it should not have. From Packy McCormick's roundup, three real firsts landed at once.

  • A synthetic cell, built from non-living chemicals. A University of Minnesota team led by Kate Adamala assembled the first synthetic cell from scratch, one that can feed, grow, and reproduce. It is the biology equivalent of the Wright Flyer, crude and enormous at the same time.
  • Human eggs, grown from stem cells. Conception Bio, out of Berkeley, produced the first early human eggs starting from blood cells reprogrammed into an ovary. The implications for fertility are obvious and large.
  • A machine that reads words off your brain. Meta's non-invasive decoder hit 61% word accuracy, up from 8% for previous methods, translating brain activity into text without surgery.

And the nuclear industry hit a cluster of reactor "criticality" milestones (the point a reactor sustains its own chain reaction) around July 4, with Aalo Atomics, Radiant, and Deployable Energy all reaching or loading fuel. The AI-power crunch is pulling nuclear forward faster than almost anyone forecast a year ago.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Europe is turning on Palantir. Per the Financial Markets list, France, Germany, and Spain have instructed state-linked companies to avoid Palantir, and the UK's NHS is reviewing a £330M contract. The pushback is part politics and part a real fight over who controls government data.
  • The US-China AI wall got taller. Alibaba banned its own employees from using Claude Code, and Anthropic moved to close the loopholes that let Chinese firms reach its models, per Kalshi. The decoupling is now happening tool by tool, not just chip by chip.
  • Republicans firmed up their Senate edge. The GOP is now around 60% to hold the Senate on the prediction markets, a recent high. Slow drift in a party-control market usually means more than a single poll.
  • Trump says he "didn't know" he made $1.4B from crypto. Also on Kalshi, a disclosure of the president's crypto gains that will not be the last word on it.
