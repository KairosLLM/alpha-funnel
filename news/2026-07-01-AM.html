Colorado just fired a 15-term Democrat, and the primary purge is only getting started

Diana DeGette became the fifth House Democrat to lose a primary as Colorado handed insurgents a clean sweep, the Court reshaped who a president can fire, and Trump disclosed more than $1.4 billion in crypto income

PoliticsWorldMarketsHealthCulture

The story this morning is a primary bloodbath, and it is not close. Colorado handed the establishment a clean loss up and down the ballot, the Supreme Court quietly rewrote the rules on presidential firing power, and Trump's financial disclosure put a jaw-dropping number on his crypto wealth. Here is what actually happened.

Colorado torched its incumbents

Diana DeGette, a 15-term Democrat, was unseated by Melat Kiros, who closed at 99 percent on Kalshi (Kalshi list). That makes her the fifth House Democrat to lose a primary this cycle, and the market says there is a 69 percent chance at least seven House Democrats go down before it is over. This is a pattern now, not an upset.

The rest of Colorado went insurgent too. On the Republican side, Kirkmeyer sits at 95 percent for governor after Victor Marx collapsed from 90 percent to 7 percent. On the Democratic side, John Hickenlooper is at 99 percent for Senate and Phil Weiser at 99 percent for governor. When the favorites move this hard this fast, somebody knew something the polls did not.

The 2026 governor map is a dead heat

Zoom out to the 36 governor races and the whole board splits 25 to 25 (Election Time). The names worth knowing: Vivek Ramaswamy leads Ohio by 1.3 over Amy Acton, Byron Donalds leads Florida by 3.4 over David Jolly, and Kansas flips red with Ty Masterson favored at 71 percent. The blue-wall governors are cruising, Josh Shapiro up 22.5 in Pennsylvania, JB Pritzker up 20 in Illinois and already eyeing 2028, and Gavin Newsom's California seat going to Xavier Becerra up 24. The genuine toss-ups are Arizona (Katie Hobbs plus 4.3 over Andy Biggs), Wisconsin (Mandela Barnes plus 4 over Tom Tiffany), and Michigan (Jocelyn Benson plus 3.6 over John James). One honest caveat from the source: the polls have been overestimating Democrats in Iowa and Georgia, so read those leans with a discount.

The Court reshaped presidential firing power

The Supreme Court overruled Trump's attempt to fire Fed Governor Lisa Cook, protecting the central bank, but in the same breath expanded the president's firing power at other agencies (Prof G Markets). So the Fed stays walled off while the rest of the executive branch gets more exposed to the Oval Office. The Court is also pegged at 95 percent to strike down Trump's birthright-citizenship ban (Polymarket list). Two very different signals from the same bench.

Markets crossover: Trump's crypto disclosure is staggering

Trump's 2025 financial disclosure ran more than 800 pages and reported over $1.4 billion in crypto income, $515 million from World Liberty Financial token sales and $635 million in meme-coin royalties, with his single largest holding being 114.75 million shares of $DJT (Financial Markets list). He also disclosed $86.5 million in media legal settlements (Polymarket list). A sitting president earning nine figures in meme-coin royalties is a genuinely new thing in American politics. The market angle on $DJT and stablecoins sits in Investing.

Around the world

Iran is selling oil at 20 percent above its prior price and asserting sovereignty over the Strait of Hormuz, which helped push crude higher (Market list). Netanyahu vowed no withdrawal from Lebanon (Polymarket list). The Venezuela earthquake death toll climbed to 1,943. And the World Bank said it will phase out lending to China by 2031, a slow but real decoupling signal.

Health and culture: the height obsession goes mainstream

The cultural thread of the morning is heightmaxxing. Roughly 1 million South Korean teenagers are now on human growth hormone for height, with about 60 percent of pediatric HGH going toward growth rather than medical treatment in a market worth around $350 million (threadguy). The driver is the brutal correlation that 58 percent of Fortune 500 CEOs are six feet or taller versus 15 percent of American men. The counterintuitive science, for anyone doing the math on their kids: making a kid taller with HGH does not meaningfully shorten their lifespan, and height on net is slightly protective, improving lung function and lipid profiles even as it carries a mild cancer-risk premium from having more cells (Cremieux).

Elsewhere in culture: the FDA is now allowing Zyn to market itself as "less harmful" (Polymarket list), AI music got a little dystopian as one creator generated a passable "threadguy Drake" song in 20 seconds and then found 238 real songs sitting in the training set, and LeBron James is now 75 percent to join the Warriors and will not play for the Lakers (Kalshi list).
