Trump declared the Iran ceasefire dead, threatened to seize an oil island, and the war he ended in June is back on

The US followed its overnight strike by hitting Bandar Abbas, Iran fired at Gulf bases, and the President floated taking Kharg Island as oil pushed past 80. The whole episode is accelerating a global arms race, and at home Gavin Newsom's 2028 case is falling apart. Here is what moved after the AM edition.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The morning's strike was not the end of it. By evening the US had widened the campaign, Iran had answered, and the President was talking about seizing Iranian territory. The war Washington declared over in June is fully back, and its shockwaves are running through oil, defense budgets, and the 2028 race. Here is the update.

1. The US widened the Iran war and threatened to take an oil island. The escalation kept building all day. Per the Polymarket list, after the overnight strike on more than 80 targets, the US hit Bandar Abbas, and Trump declared the ceasefire and memorandum over, calling Iran's leaders scum, saying "we attacked Kharg Island last night" and that the US may take it over, and promising to hit them hard again tonight. Iran fired back, with Kuwait intercepting two ballistic missiles and thirteen drones and sirens sounding in Bahrain. Kharg Island handles a large share of Iran's oil exports, so a threat to seize it is a threat to the oil market itself, and that is why Brent pushed past $80.

2. The war is pouring fuel on a global arms race. The spending follows the fear. Per Prof G Markets, the NATO summit in Turkey produced more than $50B in new defense-industry deals plus $40B for counter-drone systems, and Trump's 2027 budget carries $1.5 trillion for defense, the largest ever. Defense-technology venture funding has already passed $19B in six months, beating all of last year. The detail that lands hardest is where the software companies now sit: Palantir's Maven targeting system, paired with Claude, was used in the Iran war, which puts the same names that sell you consumer AI squarely inside the kill chain.

3. Gavin Newsom's 2028 case is falling apart. The Democratic front-runner is fading fast. Per the All-In Podcast, Nate Silver lays out why: Newsom has dropped from roughly 25% to 15% in primary polls and from 33% to 22% on the betting markets, he was running on name recognition that is now meeting younger rivals like Georgia's Jon Ossoff, the party is trending toward fresher faces including Zohran Mamdani and AOC, and Newsom has tied himself to continuity with Biden and Harris, the exact strategy that already lost. It is a defensive posture for a man who was supposed to be the presumptive choice.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • South Korea. Per the Kalshi list, the Kospi has fallen into a bear market with roughly $968B wiped out in three weeks and a 5.6% crash today that triggered a trading halt, as its two chip giants, Samsung and SK Hynix, unwound.
  • United States, housing. Per Anthony Pompliano, a Dallas Fed paper found that a 1% rise in unauthorized workers lifted home prices about 2.2%, and the H-1B crackdown is now running that in reverse, with home prices in the fast-growing Texas suburbs of Collin County down almost 9% year over year.
  • Spain. Per the Polymarket list, Trump ordered a cutoff of all trade with Spain, calling it a wasted cause and a terrible NATO partner that does not pay.
  • Maine. Per the Kalshi list, the Senate race whipsawed again, with the party odds snapping back to Democrats at 61% and Republican Susan Collins leading the individual field at 43% as Graham Platner's exit odds cooled through the day.
