The AI race stopped being a technology story overnight and became a trade war, with Washington weighing sanctions on Chinese models and Xi Jinping taking a Shanghai stage for the first time in seven years to call for cooperation

A cheap Chinese open model rattled the US labs, and the response is moving into tariffs, export controls, and an executive order rather than better products. Oil is climbing again as America bombs Iran for a ninth straight night and China quietly stops buying crude, and a viral claim blaming data centers for immigration turns out to be false.

WorldPoliticsTech

A day when the contest over artificial intelligence hardened into statecraft, the oil war kept grinding, and a popular grievance fell apart under the data. Ranked.

1. The AI fight moved from the lab to the statehouse, on both sides of the Pacific. The story stopped being about who has the best model and became about who can regulate the other. Per Diet TBPN, OpenAI and Anthropic executives are warning that cheap Chinese open models are a security threat that demands new rules, Treasury Secretary Bessent says the administration can sanction overseas models that steal US intellectual property, and the White House is weighing an executive order aimed at open models. David Sacks called the move the weaponization of regulatory uncertainty by two firms preparing to go public who want to eliminate the competition.

Beijing answered on the same stage and in the same register. Per Limitless, Xi Jinping appeared in person at the World AI Conference in Shanghai for the first time since 2018, calling for AI to be a symphony of international cooperation rather than a solo performance and warning against overstretching national security, even as Trump simultaneously accused Beijing of the largest compromise of election data in history. The quieter tell is that China may be turning protectionist too, with its commerce ministry reportedly talking to Alibaba and ByteDance about limiting the export of training data and restricting downloadable model weights. Both governments spent the day discovering that the models are now strategic assets, and neither wants the other's flowing freely across its border.

2. Oil is climbing again, and the reason is a war that will not end and a buyer that walked away. The energy squeeze is back and the mechanics are unusual. Per Prof G Markets, Matt Smith of Kpler says crude is near $89 with US gasoline back over $4 a gallon and diesel above $5, driven by the US bombing Iran for nine consecutive nights and Houthi forces declaring a naval blockade on Saudi Arabia that threatens the reroute of millions of barrels a day.

What is keeping the market from breaking higher is China. Per that interview, Smith says China has simply stopped buying, cutting its oil imports by more than 5 million barrels a day, which has offset the Middle East supply loss and pushed the pain into refined products like the diesel that moves the economy. His forecast is that if the same standoff persists another four or five months, crude pushing above $100 becomes realistic, and he notes the paper market is thin enough that a single Trump social-media post can drop the price 10 to 15 percent. The war sets the ceiling and the world's largest importer sets the floor, and right now they are canceling each other out.

3. The claim that data centers are driving Indian immigration is false, and the data is blunt about it. A viral political grievance met the census and lost. Per Cremieux, the argument that data centers attract an overwhelmingly foreign, mostly Indian tech workforce does not survive contact with the numbers, because a 250,000-square-foot facility employs only about 50 people, roughly half of them contractors, and almost none on H-1B visas.

The secondary version of the claim fails the same test. Per that analysis, event studies show that opening a data center, or even a cluster of them, does not spawn a local tech ecosystem, and Indian tech professionals cluster in established tech metros where the jobs actually are rather than around server farms. The author ties the myth to a broader anti-H-1B current running through parts of the current administration and warns against dressing up an ethnic-replacement narrative as an infrastructure complaint. The useful correction is that the jobs follow demand and talent, and the data center is a warehouse of machines, not a magnet for people.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • A judge approved Anthropic's $1.5 billion book settlement. Per Diet TBPN, Judge William Alsup signed off on Anthropic's copyright settlement at about $3,000 per work across roughly 500,000 books, after ruling the company illegally downloaded and stored millions of them. The bill for training on the world's writing now has a per-title price.
  • The cyber cost curve is bending. Per Diet TBPN, Google launched cheaper security-tuned Gemini models to push down a market where firms had been paying millions to have a closed model read every line of their code for a single vulnerability. Cheaper defensive AI is arriving at the same moment cheaper offensive AI is.
  • A model-routing product drew praise as the practical fix. Per Diet TBPN, Ramp launched a router that sends each task to the cheapest capable model to cut wasteful token spend, a sign that the near-term money is in spending less on intelligence rather than buying the most expensive kind.
