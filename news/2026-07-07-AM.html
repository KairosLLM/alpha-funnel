Iran put missiles back on the shipping lanes in the Strait of Hormuz, and Maine's Senate race fell apart overnight

A fresh strike at Gulf shipping revived the oil-and-war premium, a Democratic front-runner collapsed as his own party pulled its endorsements, and six million kids are now signed up for the government's new investment accounts. Here is what moved overnight.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Quiet morning at home, loud one abroad and in the markets. Iran reminded everyone the Gulf is still a tinderbox, a marquee Senate race imploded in a single day, and the government's plan to hand every child a brokerage account crossed its first big milestone. Here is the update.

1. Iran fired missiles at ships in the Strait of Hormuz again. The Gulf risk is back on the board. Per the Financial Markets list, Iran reportedly fired at least two missiles at ships in the Strait of Hormuz overnight, the chokepoint through which a large share of the world's oil and jet fuel moves. Even a limited strike puts a premium back into crude and shipping, and it lands at a moment when gold is negative on the year and traders had grown comfortable that the summer would stay calm. The market reaction will tell you whether this reads as a one-off or the start of another escalation cycle.

2. Maine's Senate race came apart in a single day. Watch how fast a front-runner can fall. Per the Kalshi list, the odds that Democrat Graham Platner drops out of the Maine Senate race ramped through the day from 16% to 85% before settling near 76%, after Elizabeth Warren, Ro Khanna, and Ruben Gallego all pulled their endorsements over allegations Platner calls categorically false. The scramble reset the whole contest, with Troy Jackson now the favorite for the Democratic nomination at 62% and incumbent Republican Susan Collins and Jackson running close in the head-to-head. A race that looked settled at breakfast was a different race by dinner.

3. Six million kids are signed up for Trump Accounts, and how you use one matters. The government's child-investing program cleared a real milestone. Per the Polymarket list, Robinhood says six million children have now signed up for Trump Accounts, and Gwynne Shotwell is donating SpaceX shares into more than two million of them. The accounts drop a one-time $1,000 into an S&P 500 fund per eligible child.

The mechanics reward planning. Per BowTied Bull, a Trump Account takes up to $5,000 a year and behaves like an IRA the child controls at 18, and paired with a 529 that can roll about $35,000 into a Roth and a taxable custodial account, a disciplined $19,000 a year across the three can compound past $600,000 by adulthood. The same piece flags the obvious risk of handing an 18-year-old a seven-figure balance with no strings, and makes the case for trusts with age and milestone triggers instead.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • South Korea. Per the Kalshi list, the Kospi fell 5.7% and authorities halted program selling after Samsung's revenue miss, a reminder of how much of that index rides on two chipmakers.
  • Texas. Per the Polymarket list, Toyota is putting $3.6B into a Texas plant and moving some Tacoma production out of Mexico, roughly 2,000 jobs, the latest concrete reshoring receipt.
  • United States. Per the same list, Trump touted Walmart cutting prices, including about 15% off ground beef, as evidence the inflation fight is turning.
  • China. Per the Stocks list, Alibaba is barring its staff from using Anthropic's Claude Code by July 10 amid a dispute over China-detection code, another brick in the US-China tech wall.
  • Washington. Per the Kalshi list, a Treasury internal report is now flagging AI-bubble risk, a notable tell when the official machinery starts naming the thing out loud.
