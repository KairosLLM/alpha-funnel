The government is trying to buy into AI, and towns are trying to keep it out

Trump walked back the proposed OpenAI stake on camera even as bettors still price it as likely, and the physical AI buildout is now running into local moratoriums.

PoliticsMarkets

The AI story turned into a government story overnight, and it cut both ways. Washington is circling equity in the labs while local governments start slamming the door on the data centers. Both are worth more attention than the holiday-thin headlines suggest.

1. Trump walked back the OpenAI stake on camera, but the market still bets it happens. Yesterday the framing was concrete, a proposed 5% government stake in OpenAI worth about $42.5B. On CNBC overnight, per the People to follow list, Trump would not confirm it and pointed instead to the 10% stake the government already holds in Intel. Yet Polymarket still prices a US government equity stake in OpenAI at 57%, and Sam Altman is on record calling public ownership the best way to share AI's upside, in an arrangement that reportedly would have pulled in other labs too.

The specifics are soft and the president is hedging in public. The direction is not soft at all. The idea of the state taking direct equity in frontier AI has moved from unthinkable to a coin-flip bet in a matter of days.

2. The AI buildout is hitting the one wall money cannot move, local government. The counterweight to all that federal enthusiasm is showing up in county commission meetings. Blackstone abandoned a Virginia data center, and roughly 20 Florida counties are weighing data-center moratoriums, per Polymarket. This is the part of the AI story that does not live on a chart. The buildout needs land, water, and enormous amounts of power, and the people who live next to it are starting to vote on it. When the physical footprint of the boom becomes a ballot-box issue, that is a real constraint on the timeline, not a talking point.

3. Around the world, quickly.

  • Republicans firmed up their Senate edge. On Kalshi, the GOP is now 61% to hold the Senate, up from the high 50s a day earlier. Slow, quiet drift in the party-control market is usually more real than any single poll.
  • The recession trade got unwound. Odds of a US recession this year fell to about 8%, an all-time low on Kalshi, even after a soft jobs print. The market is pricing a slowdown that keeps not arriving.
  • The world's assets hit a new high. Global market capitalization set a record near $166T. Whatever the AI-bubble argument does to individual names, the aggregate pile of wealth has never been larger. That is its own kind of risk when sentiment turns.
