Friedberg drew an immigration line by asking one question, and the AI job scare put numbers behind why it lands

The makers-versus-takers test, 1.8 million Americans stuck in long-term unemployment as half the country fears AI takes the next job, and a Reuters tally that says the Trump family's crypto ventures are now worth $2.3 billion even as the tokens crater.

PoliticsWorldCultureMarkets

Independence Day gave the feed a theme it did not plan: who gets to be an American, and what work is still worth doing once the machines want the same jobs. The immigration argument got a clean test, the labor data got grim, and the president's family got a $2.3 billion crypto scorecard. Below that, a quieter world kept moving.

1. Friedberg reduced the whole immigration fight to one question, and it is a good one. On the All-In podcast, David Friedberg made his immigration test not about culture or country of origin but about a single motive: are you coming to work or coming for benefits. His makers-versus-takers frame would deny entry to anyone whose primary aim is social support and grant it to anyone whose aim is to produce, earn, and advance their family. His deeper claim is the one people resist, that immigration is not a zero-sum fight over a fixed pile of jobs, because anyone who creates more value than they consume grows the economy and creates more jobs for everyone. Agree or not, it is the rare framing that gives both sides a rule instead of a slogan.

2. The AI job scare stopped being a vibe and started being a number. Friedberg's faith in work meets a labor market that is quietly hollowing. Per the Polymarket list, the count of Americans unemployed for at least 27 weeks has averaged above 1.8 million this year, half of Americans now say they fear AI could cost them or a family member a job, and in a tell about where the displacement runs, AI hiring in India's IT sector is rising even as overall recruitment there declines. The machines are not taking every job, they are taking the entry rungs and the routine middle, exactly the ladder Friedberg's makers climb. One more sign of the new normal from the same list: voters are reportedly turning to AI chatbots for help before casting their ballots, so the tool reshaping the labor market is now in the voting booth too.

3. The Trump family's crypto empire is now a $2.3 billion number, and the tokens are down anyway. Per Trader Mayne relaying Reuters, the Trump family's crypto ventures are collectively valued near $2.3 billion, even though the flagship assets have cratered: the TRUMP memecoin has generated roughly $635 million in fees while falling about 96% from its peak, and World Liberty Financial's WLFI token is down around 70% despite paper values in the hundreds of millions. The story is the gap. The ventures print fees and paper wealth for the family while retail holders eat the drawdown, the conflict-of-interest engine in one chart. It pairs with the fresh Trump Accounts wrinkle on the Polymarket list, where the Treasury will now reportedly accept donations of publicly traded stock to fund the new children's accounts.

4. Galloway put a price on the creator economy, and the money is leaving the middle. From Cannes Lions, Scott Galloway's read on the creator economy is that brand spending on creators is up about 24% year over year, with roughly half of it now flowing to nano and micro creators rather than the megastars. His blunt law of the attention economy is that the most takes the most, a winner-take-more dynamic that mirrors the same top-heavy pull reshaping labor and markets everywhere else in today's feed.

Around the world, quickly.

  • Iran. Per the Polymarket list, Ayatollah Ali Khamenei's funeral procession will travel through five cities across Iran and Iraq, a staged succession moment for a state in transition.
  • Iran and China, at the strait. The same list reports Iran declaring that China will get special considerations in any new Strait of Hormuz fee arrangement, a sign the emerging chokepoint tolls will be political, not neutral.
  • Taiwan. China's Coast Guard deployed a new patrol task group east of Taiwan, per the Polymarket list, pushing its presence past the island's far side.
  • Moldova. The prime minister resigned, triggering the collapse of the government, another wobble on Europe's eastern edge.
  • Germany. Chancellor Friedrich Merz said Germany has no reason to shy away from its military spending push, while Berlin is reportedly weighing an end to its one-year crypto tax exemption under the 2027 budget.
  • Drones as an industry. German startup Quantum Systems raised $1.2 billion at an $8 billion valuation, per the Polymarket list, as Russia's largest jobs site advertises for drone operators to defend Moscow. The war economy is now a hiring market.
  • Canada. Ottawa unveiled plans for an oil pipeline to Asia in a bid to become an energy superpower and cut its dependence on the US.
  • Japan. Amid record immigration, Japan is hiking residence permit fees, including a reported 1,900% jump for permanent residency applications, the exact taker-screening instinct Friedberg was arguing about, applied with a price tag.
