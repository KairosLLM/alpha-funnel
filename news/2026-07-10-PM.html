Bessent declared America will write the rules of money, four nuclear startups made Trump's July 4 reactor deadline, and a lone voice says the Iran relief is mispriced

The Treasury Secretary's speech on digital money drew a rare El-Erian endorsement while Circle collected a federal bank charter. The advanced-nuclear race quietly hit its milestone, the Kremlin floated talks while reportedly preparing to escalate, and China stuck its first reusable rocket landing. Here is the evening read.

PoliticsMarketsWorld

This morning the story was the Iran panic unwinding. Tonight the bigger story is structural, Washington telling you in plain language where money is headed, while the nuclear buildout the AI trade depends on hit a real milestone. Here is what matters, ranked.

1. Bessent's money speech is the policy document of the week, and the plumbing moved with it. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent laid out five principles, and per Jordi Visser the one that counts is the third, America will write the rules of the next economy. His own words: digital assets, stablecoins, tokenization, and new payment systems will help shape the future of money, and the United States should not consign itself to the sidelines while that future is built elsewhere. Mohamed El-Erian called it a remarkably important speech in a New York Times op-ed, and when the establishment's favorite economist blesses a crypto speech, the Overton window has moved.

The plumbing moved with the words. Per the Market list, Circle won final approval from the OCC (the national bank regulator) for a federally regulated trust bank to sit under USDC. The words and the charters are now pointing the same direction.

The test comes fast. The Senate returns July 13 with about three working weeks before the August recess, and per Milk Road that is the last realistic 2026 window for the CLARITY Act, the bill deciding whether the SEC or the CFTC regulates crypto, with Polymarket odds at 40 percent and falling. A government that says it will write the rules still has to pass one.

2. The oil market is treating the Iran pause as a done deal, and that is the vulnerable assumption. A Prof G Markets guest made the uncomfortable case against this morning's relief: an enormous amount of Middle East geopolitical risk is simply not priced into oil, markets are planning for the best case, and nothing about an unstable region with no functioning diplomacy justifies that. Twenty-four hours ago the tape was pricing a wider war, today it prices a handshake, and both cannot be right. The honest position is that a pause in strikes is a data point, and crude at best-case pricing has asymmetric room to reprice if Iran's phone call turns out to be a stall.

3. Four nuclear startups hit the July 4 deadline, and the customer is the data center. Per Packy McCormick, Aalo Atomics' reactor went critical at 12:20am on July 4, making it the fourth advanced-nuclear startup to hit the executive-order deadline, after Antares Nuclear, Valar Atomics, and Deployable Energy, with Radiant, Oklo, X-Energy, Deep Fission, and Last Energy expected next. The Abundance Institute's Chris Koopman puts the speed in one line, 13 months from executive orders to four critical reactors. The field has shifted from science project to manufacturing race, and the buyers driving it are data centers rather than utilities.

The supporting cast is forming around that same customer. Josh Zoffer argued in the FT that hyperscaler demand is now doing more than subsidies or tariffs to rebuild American industrial supply chains, and American Turbine came out of stealth under John McElhone to sell small, mass-manufacturable gas turbines aimed at data-center speed-to-power. Energy for compute is becoming its own industrial economy, and it is being built on deadlines measured in months.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Russia. Per the Market list, the Kremlin answered a Reuters report that Putin is likely to escalate the Ukraine war by saying Russia remains open to talks. Read the two together: the openness is the message, the escalation is the plan.
  • China's space program. Per the same list, China landed a reusable rocket for the first time, the capability that made SpaceX cheap. The gap is still years, but it just stopped being categorical.
  • Congressional trading. Rep. Richard McCormick, who sits on House Armed Services, filed a purchase of defense contractor L3Harris, per the Market list. The committee-to-portfolio pipeline remains undefeated.
  • Musk, at maximum Musk. Per the Market list, after unveiling an orbital AI-compute program, he posted that SpaceX will be worth more than the rest of Earth if it accomplishes its goals. File under ambition as pricing strategy.
