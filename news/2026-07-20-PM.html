The Iran war ran its ninth straight night of US strikes and the Houthis declared a blockade on Saudi Arabia, and then Iran proposed a ten-day ceasefire and oil fell off a cliff, all in one day

The Gulf spent the day escalating and de-escalating at the same time, with a tanker on fire near Hormuz and a peace offer that crashed crude below $80. Britain swapped prime ministers, and China stood up the first major AI-governance body headquartered outside the West.

A day of the war getting worse and better at once, a quiet change of government in London, and a Chinese bid to write the world's AI rules. Ranked.

1. The Iran war escalated and de-escalated in the same day. The Gulf gave two opposite signals within hours. Per the Polymarket list, US Central Command completed its ninth consecutive night of strikes on Iran near the Strait of Hormuz, the Houthis declared a naval blockade on Saudi Arabia in a stated siege-for-siege, a tanker caught fire near Hormuz, and air-raid sirens sounded across Bahrain and Kuwait as their forces intercepted drones.

Then the war blinked. Per the same list, Iran reportedly proposed a ten-day ceasefire to revive an interim deal, and oil crashed below $80 on the headline after trading over $90 in the morning, even as gasoline at the American pump pushed back over $4. The prediction markets now put the odds of the US lifting its Iran blockade by the end of next month at 51 percent, per the Financial Markets list. A conflict that can bomb a country by night and float a peace plan by afternoon is one where the tail risk never actually clears, it just reprices by the hour.

2. Britain changed prime ministers. The government in London turned over. Per the Polymarket list, Keir Starmer resigned with the words my work is done, and Andy Burnham was formally appointed prime minister by King Charles at Buckingham Palace.

The market noticed even if the headlines did not dwell on it. Per the Financial Markets list, UK government bond yields spiked on the transition, the bond market's way of pricing uncertainty about a new leader's fiscal plans. A change of prime minister without an election is the kind of event that moves gilts before it moves front pages, and the yield jump is the tell that investors are not sure what Burnham does next.

3. China stood up its own AI-governance body. The most strategic move of the weekend was institutional. Per AI Central, 29 non-Western nations signed the founding agreement for a World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization, the first major AI-governance body headquartered outside the West, with Xi Jinping giving his first in-person keynote at the Shanghai conference since it began.

The membership and the message are the story. The signatories span Africa, Asia, and Latin America plus Russia and Belarus, with no major Western democracy, and Xi urged the world to embrace open-source AI and resist overstretching national security, pledging 5,000 training slots for developing nations. Underneath the diplomacy is a capability flex, with Moonshot's 2.8-trillion-parameter Kimi K3 billed as the largest open-weight model ever and Huawei showing an 8,192-chip cluster built to run without US semiconductors. While Washington debates whether its industry can regulate itself, China is writing rules and recruiting the countries that will follow them.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • The US AI-safety chief quit. The head of the Trump administration's AI safety agency resigned, per the Polymarket list. The government's own AI oversight lost its leader the same week China stood one up.
  • Australia is making data centers pay their own way in power. Australia will require large AI data centers to add at least as much electricity to the grid as they consume, per the Polymarket list. It is the bring-your-own-power idea, written into law.
  • A food outbreak is still growing. Michigan's cyclosporiasis outbreak surpassed 6,000 cases with more than 1,100 added since Friday, per the Polymarket list. The contamination has not been contained.
