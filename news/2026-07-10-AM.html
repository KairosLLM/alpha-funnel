The US strikes on Iran appear to have stopped overnight, Trump says Iran called to make a deal, and the panic that spiked oil yesterday is unwinding

A senior US official says no strikes have happened in recent hours, gold and crude slid on fresh US-Iran optimism, and Japan's bond market reversed with the yen soaring. At home the Maine Senate primary imploded as Graham Platner moved to withdraw. Here is the overnight update.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Yesterday ended with Trump calling the ceasefire dead and the US resuming strikes. Overnight it turned the other way. The bombing appears to have paused, the language shifted to a deal, and the markets that priced a wider war started pricing a pause. At home, the messiest Senate primary in the country lost its front-runner. Here is the update.

1. The strikes paused and the tone flipped to a deal. The reversal was fast. Per the Polymarket list, a senior American official told Axios that no US military strikes have been conducted in recent hours, walking back yesterday afternoon's resumption. Per the Financial Markets list, the shift was strong enough to move prices overnight, with gold hesitating above $4,100 and heading for a weekly loss on less hawkish Fed minutes and fresh US-Iran optimism. The oil-shock story is being challenged directly, with analyst Anas Alhajji arguing the Strait of Hormuz supply loss was overstated once SPR releases and softer demand are netted out. The caution is that a pause is not a deal. Per the Kalshi list, traders still put only a 30 percent chance on a US-Iran nuclear agreement this year, near a record low.

2. The Maine Senate primary imploded overnight. The Democratic front-runner is out. Per the Kalshi list, Graham Platner is withdrawing, with his advisor saying he will file the paperwork and Axios reporting he told staff he will exit Monday, and the market puts an 87 percent chance he is gone before Tuesday. The race to replace him is now Troy Jackson at 49 percent, Shenna Bellows at 31 percent after officially announcing, and Nirav Shah at 14 percent, ahead of the July 31 primary. It is a clean reset of a seat that looked locked a week ago.

3. A Tea Party is brewing inside the Democratic primaries. The bigger political signal is nationwide. Per the Kalshi list, Harry Enten frames it as a new Tea Party for Democrats, with an 82 percent chance that six or more incumbent House Democrats lose their primaries. The named vulnerable seats are Missouri's Wesley Bell at a 38 percent chance of losing, Michigan's Shri Thanedar at 19 percent, and Connecticut's John Larson under 50 percent to hold. Separately, Rahm Emanuel is delivering remarks in Israel and carries an 80 percent chance he runs for president, and Pennsylvania's John Fetterman and Dave McCormick, a Democrat and a Republican, launched a joint fundraising committee called Common Ground PA.

4. Japan's bond market reversed, and it moved money. The macro tell came out of Tokyo. Per the Financial Markets list, the 10-year JGB yield fell 10 basis points to 2.775 percent and the 20-year fell 10 basis points to 3.765 percent, reversing yesterday's push to a 30-year high, as the yen soared on a pledge by Katayama to promote GPIF investment in Japanese assets. About $174 billion was added to the Japanese stock market at the open. After months of the JGB selloff being the global bond-stress story, an overnight reversal of that size is its own signal.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • Ukraine. Per the Polymarket list, Zelensky says the number of countries wanting Ukraine in NATO has increased, a counter to yesterday's read that Putin was hardening against talks.
  • Europe's energy squeeze. Per the Polymarket list, NATO Secretary General Mark Rutte warned that Europe is closing more refineries than it is opening, tying military readiness to fuel supply.
  • Canada. Per the Kalshi list, the economy shrank in Mark Carney's first year as prime minister, called the worst economic start for any Canadian PM in over 60 years.
  • The US consumer. Per the Kalshi list, consumer credit fell $182 million in May, the first monthly decline since November 2024, a small but real crack in the borrowing story.
