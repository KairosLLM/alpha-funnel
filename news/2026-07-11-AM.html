Apple sued OpenAI over stolen hardware secrets in the same 24 hours its president stepped back, and Iran's deadline to reopen Hormuz is today

The trade-secret complaint names names and reads like a spy novel, while OpenAI loses its number two and its head of safety in one reshuffle. Iran privately calls the tanker attacks a rogue mistake as Washington demands it say so in public, and the White House drafts an executive order because Chinese AI models just took over the usage charts.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The overnight story is OpenAI having the worst 24 hours of its corporate life, and it earned every piece of it. Below that, a live deadline in the Persian Gulf and a policy scramble over Chinese AI. Ranked.

1. Apple's lawsuit against OpenAI is specific, and OpenAI's leadership was wobbling the same day it landed. The complaint, filed July 10 and detailed on the Financial Markets list: Apple engineer Chang Liu left for OpenAI in January 2026, kept his laptop, exploited an authentication bug in Apple's network storage on February 9, and downloaded technical compilations running past 1,000 pages on manufacturing and testing. The suit says he then coached colleague Yu-Ting "Alyssa" Peng to exfiltrate her own trove before her OpenAI interview. Per the People list, the allegations climb to OpenAI's chief hardware officer, a former Apple VP accused of telling recruits to bring actual parts to interviews. The target of all this borrowed knowledge is the consumer device OpenAI is building to compete with the iPhone.

The same day, per the People list, OpenAI president of product Fidji Simo moved to part-time advisory for health reasons with her duties split among Greg Brockman, Sarah Friar, and Jason Kwon, and head of safety systems Johannes Heidecke is leaving as safety gets folded under a research VP. Add the 20-plus lawsuits over ChatGPT's role in suicide and homicide cases and the picture is a company fighting the world's most valuable company in court while its own bench thins. Yesterday the OpenAI story was a balance sheet. Today it is personnel and subpoenas.

2. Iran has until today to say the strait is open, and its private excuse is remarkable. Washington's demand, per the Market and Financial Markets lists: a public declaration that the Strait of Hormuz is open and toll-free, by Saturday, or else, with the added condition of no nuclear dust, no deal. The reporting behind the scenes is stranger. CBS's Jennifer Jacobs reports Iran privately told Trump advisers the commercial-ship attacks were a mistake by an errant IRGC hardliner faction trying to derail the negotiations, while talks continue in Oman under Vance, Kushner, Witkoff, and Rubio, and the White House wants that admission made in public.

Two complications arrived with it. Friday's Treasury action strains the no-new-sanctions clause of the June memorandum, and per the Polymarket list Iran restarted the Taleqan-2 site at Parchin, which may breach the same document. Markets are pricing a handshake anyway, the S&P sits half a percent from its high. A deadline day where both sides are already accusing each other of violations is a strange thing to shrug at.

3. The White House is drafting an executive order on open-source AI because the usage numbers flipped. Jacob Wendler's scoop, nine sources, per the Financial Markets list: an executive order on open-source AI is under consideration, driven by fear of Chinese dominance. The numbers driving the panic are stark. Twenty of the world's fifty most-used AI models are now Chinese, up 400 percent since 2025 while the US count fell from 33 to 28, and Chinese models processed 98 trillion tokens in June against 53 trillion for American ones. Per the FT via Kalshi, OpenAI and Google are themselves selling advanced models to Chinese giants while this happens.

The twist is that Beijing may solve Washington's problem for it. Dylan Patel reports multiple Chinese labs plan to stop open-sourcing and license instead, per the Hermes list. If both governments wall off their models at once, the era of free frontier AI ends by mutual accident.

4. Meta's kids problem now carries a price bigger than its market cap can absorb. Per Prof G Markets: four states are seeking $1.4 trillion in penalties, more than 90 percent of Meta's value, nearly 30 states have federal suits running, a New Mexico court already ordered $375 million, and more than 40 countries have banned or are moving to ban social media for children. The guest's framing is big tobacco, whistleblowers plus internal documents showing the company measured the harm to kids and proceeded. Wall Street spent the week bidding the stock up on its AI models and pricing the courtroom at zero. One of those two rooms is doing the math wrong.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • Ukraine. Ukrainian drones hit roughly 50 ships in 120 hours and Russia closed the Don-Azov shipping channel, per the Polymarket list. Russian gasoline output covers 65 percent of demand, and Lindsey Graham struck a deal with the administration on sanctioning Russian oil buyers. The drone war is now an energy-infrastructure war, and it is working.
  • China cut off helium. Export ban effective immediately, per the Financial Markets list, deepening the squeeze from the Qatar outage. Helium cools MRI machines and chip fabs, so this lands on hospitals and semiconductors alike.
  • The EU passed chat control. Private-message scanning is now law, and threadguy's warning is the obvious next step, AI chat logs join your searches and messages in the monitored pile. Given item 1's lawsuits, the logs already matter in court.
  • Venezuela. The earthquake toll passed 4,000, per the Market list.
  • Maine is a coin flip. Susan Collins and Troy Jackson are tied at 39 percent each on Kalshi, which still gives Republicans 57 percent to hold the Senate, per the Market list.
  • Japan. A card-payment provider is suspected of falsifying statements for 20 years, more than 60 billion yen, per the Financial Markets list.
  • A Waymo called the police on its own passengers, two 15-year-olds, per the Polymarket list. The robotaxi as hall monitor arrived faster than the robotaxi as commodity.
  • New York. A legionella-type outbreak has sickened 46, per the Polymarket list.
