The Supreme Court just loosened the campaign-money rules, and the president disclosed $2 billion

SCOTUS loosened the limits on party spending, Trump reported more than $2B in income with most of it from crypto, and the layoff data says AI is now the No. 1 reason Americans are losing jobs.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Two stories actually moved today, and both are about money and power flowing to the top. Everything else was noise or a market story wearing a news headline. Here is what mattered.

1. The Supreme Court loosened the limits on party spending. Per Prof G Markets, the Court struck down, on First Amendment grounds, the cap on coordinated party spending, the rule that stopped donors from routing money through the parties to candidates above the individual limit. The individual per-cycle contribution cap sits near $7,000, and the party channel now lets big donors blow past it legally. The clip is a reaction, not a ruling readout, so it names no case and no vote count, and I would hold the specifics loosely until the opinion is in hand. The direction is not in doubt though. This tilts toward large donors and away from the small-donor party. That shift in who funds American campaigns will outlast the cycle.

2. Trump disclosed more than $2 billion in income, most of it crypto. The annual financial disclosure shows Trump made over $2B in his first year back in office, up about 233% year over year, with roughly $1.2B of it from crypto, more than $500M from his World Liberty Financial venture and more than $600M from his Trumpcoin meme token. This is a different filing from the trading disclosure covered this morning (the one showing up to $1.4B in stock purchases across roughly 22,000 trades), so read them as two data points on the same theme rather than a double-count.

The kicker from the same segment: nearly half of Trump voters say he has not profited from the presidency at all. A sitting president clearing a billion dollars from his own coins is the rare financial story that is also a straight political one.

3. AI is now the single biggest stated reason Americans are losing their jobs. Buried under this morning's soft payrolls print is the more durable number, from the Challenger layoff data walked through on ClearValue Tax: AI was cited for 101,743 job cuts year to date, about 23% of all cuts and 31% of June's, up from the low single digits historically. Tech alone accounts for roughly a third of all cuts this year, 139,156 and up 83%. That is the story the 4.2% unemployment rate hides. The headline labor market looks fine while the machine quietly reprices who is employable, and that gap is going to define the politics of the next two years more than any single monthly print.

4. The government keeps buying its way into AI, and other governments are pushing back. The compute-geopolitics thread ran hard today. On Kalshi, OpenAI is in talks to hand the US government a 5% stake valued near $42.5B, which would make Washington a direct shareholder in the leading lab. Cutting the other way, Spain blacklisted Palantir on national-security grounds, and Anthropic moved to design its own chip with Samsung to reduce its US-hardware dependence. Frontier AI has become state business: bought into, blacklisted, or built in-house. This morning the policy writers were states and standards bodies. By the close they were treasuries and export-control offices. That is a faster and blunter kind of government. The product mechanics of the Fable relaunch are in AI & Tools.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • Russia hit Kyiv hard. The strike this morning's edition warned was coming landed: Polymarket tracked 74 missiles and 496 drones on the capital overnight with at least 13 killed. The massing was real, and the pattern of large mixed strikes on Kyiv is the escalation to watch.
  • The dollar's ownership base is thinning. Per Kalshi, foreign ownership of US dollars fell to its lowest share this century, with money rotating hard into precious metals. When the world quietly diversifies away from your currency, that is a slow-motion story that matters more than any single day's tape.
  • Michigan's Senate primary has a runaway. Abdul El-Sayed sits at 87% to win the Democratic nomination, one of the cleaner locks on the board and a marker of where the party's energy is heading.
  • The IPO window is cracking open. Jersey Mike's filed to go public, a small tell that the risk appetite that vanished this spring is testing the water again.
