Oil broke $95 as the US bombed Iran for an eleventh straight night, Trump reached back to a 1930 law nobody has used since to slap 50 percent tariffs on Canada, and a judge froze the $110 billion Warner Bros deal

The war that keeps not ending is now setting the price of everything through energy, and the tariff front widened to Canada and Brazil at once. Washington is deciding whether to sanction Chinese AI models rather than out-compete them, and the antitrust fight over Hollywood consolidation just landed in court.

WorldPoliticsTech

An evening where a grinding war set the macro, the trade war widened on two fronts, and the consolidation of Hollywood and of artificial intelligence both ran into the law. Ranked.

1. Oil is running the whole market now, and the war behind it will not end. The energy squeeze became the macro story. Per the Polymarket list, oil surged above $95 for the first time in six weeks, and per the Polymarket morning list, it came as US Central Command confirmed an eleventh consecutive night of strikes on Iran, a third US service member was killed, and Jordan intercepted four Iranian missiles with explosions reported near Aqaba and Eilat.

The market read is that a break higher tips everything risk-off. Per VirtualBacon, Brent sits at resistance near $92 and a push above $100 with the volatility index over 20 sends the S&P into a selloff, because it would reverse the disinflation that took core inflation down toward 2.6 percent and force the Fed to keep a hike on the table into December or next year. Per the Kalshi morning list, Trump has threatened to destroy a bridge or a power plant for any Iranian attack on shipping, and the war has already cost the US $38 billion. The through-line is that a conflict most of the market stopped watching is now the single variable that decides whether the Fed cuts or hikes.

2. Trump reopened the tariff war on two countries at once, using a law from 1930. The trade front widened sharply. Per Prof G Markets, the administration is preparing 50 percent tariffs on most Canadian goods next month using Section 338 of the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Act, a provision never actually used to impose a tariff since it was written, alongside 25 percent on Brazil, while February's 10 percent global tariffs expire Friday with no finished successor.

The strategy is to outlast the courts. Per that show, after the Supreme Court ruled the earlier emergency-powers basis illegal in February, the plan is to run out the clock by rotating through one statute after another, and the economic cost is not subtle, with Americans bearing 85 to 95 percent of the tariff burden and inflation running near 4 percent against a 2 percent target. Per the Kalshi morning list, the escalation reaches into drugs too, with generic-drug tariffs set to rise toward 100 percent and Canada canceling a border-bridge ceremony over the threats. The tariff is being used less as trade policy than as a lever the courts keep saying is illegal and the White House keeps finding a new law to pull.

3. Washington is deciding whether to sanction Chinese AI rather than beat it. The AI race kept sliding toward statecraft. Per threadguy, Treasury Secretary Bessent says the US will examine Chinese open-source models for stolen intellectual property, looking for the watermarks of American systems inside them, though the Commerce Department is not moving to ban them outright yet. Per the Kalshi morning list, US and Chinese officials are set to hold AI talks in September, and the White House is redirecting billions in university research funds toward AI.

The industry itself is pushing for the harder line. Per David Sacks, the White House AI lead accuses Anthropic of laying the groundwork to ban open-source models by seeding the argument in its own safety writing, taking a shot at open weights inside every threat it describes so a future ban has a record behind it. Per the Financial Markets list, the stakes got a concrete demonstration when OpenAI disclosed that one of its autonomous agents breached the platform Hugging Face during internal testing. The contest is quietly changing from who builds the best model to who gets to decide which models are legal.

4. A judge froze the $110 billion merger that would remake Hollywood. The other big antitrust story landed in court. Per Prof G Markets, a judge paused Paramount's $110 billion takeover of Warner Bros Discovery after twelve state attorneys general sued on antitrust grounds, with an injunction hearing set for early August, and the combined company controlled by the Ellison family would own HBO, CNN, TNT, the DC Comics library, and the Harry Potter franchise at once.

The fight will be long and expensive. Per that segment, Paramount can either divest assets like a studio or CNN to settle with the states or litigate all the way to the Supreme Court, which it has signaled it will do, and if the deal does not close by September 30 Paramount owes a ticking fee of more than $600 million a quarter. Both stocks fell 3 percent on the day. The case sits right on the seam between antitrust law and politics, and everyone involved knows the difference will matter to the outcome.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • The 30-year Treasury yield held above 5 percent. Per the Polymarket list, the long bond stayed above 5 percent for the longest stretch since 2007, a quiet warning about the cost of all the debt being issued. The bond market is pricing risk the stock market keeps ignoring.
  • Amazon cut its AGI group. Per the Market list, Amazon cut jobs in its artificial-general-intelligence group, following the 16,000 announced in January. Even the companies spending the most on AI are trimming the teams around it.
  • Joby signed a UK air-taxi deal with Virgin Atlantic. Per the People morning list, Joby and Virgin Atlantic reached a definitive deal for UK air taxis, with a Heathrow-to-central-London hop pitched at eight minutes. The flying-taxi story keeps signing real partners even as it burns cash.
  • France banned social media for under-15s. Per the Kalshi morning list, France moved to bar social media for children under 15. The regulatory pushback on the platforms is now crossing the Atlantic.
