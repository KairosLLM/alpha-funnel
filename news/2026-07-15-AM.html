Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz shut and the US put its warships back around it, so the war Trump kept switching off is on again

Another round of US strikes hit southern Iran overnight, the naval blockade is reinstated with more than 20 ships, and Kuwait spent the night knocking down missiles and drones. At home, the man who runs Google DeepMind called for a federal AI watchdog, New York froze its data centers, and California is trying to block the biggest merger in Hollywood.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

The on-again-off-again war turned back on overnight, and two of the biggest names in AI and media landed on opposite sides of the government. Ranked.

1. The strait is shut, the blockade is back, and the ceasefire cycle is over for now. Per the Market list, Iran's Revolutionary Guard declared the Strait of Hormuz closed until US attacks stop, and Washington reinstated its naval blockade with more than 20 warships. This is the hard reversal of Monday, when Trump had walked the 20 percent cargo toll back within a day and narrowed the blockade to Iranian ports. The switch flipped the other way overnight.

The fighting escalated with it. Per the Polymarket and Financial Markets lists, US Central Command ran another round of strikes on southern Iran, including Qeshm Island and Ahvaz, Kuwait intercepted a ballistic missile plus five cruise missiles and 33 drones with a Navy vessel taking a hit, and Trump told Fox the strikes continue until he says it is enough and would not rule out a ground campaign. On the same night the Senate's Democrats blocked a $1.15 trillion defense bill. A war being fought with blockades and drone swarms, switched on and off by tweet, is not a war anyone can price.

2. The head of Google DeepMind asked Washington to regulate his own industry. Per Diet TBPN, Demis Hassabis used a Financial Times piece and a post on X to call for a US frontier-AI watchdog, and the proposal is specific. He wants a beefed-up federal standards body, funded by the AI companies themselves, that defines what counts as a frontier model, requires labs to submit models for testing up to 30 days before release, and checks them for cyber, biological, nuclear, deception, and autonomy risks, with the power to coordinate a slowdown if the testing turns up something dangerous.

The politics of it are the interesting part. It follows the White House abruptly banning exports of Anthropic's most advanced models last month, and Hassabis wants the rules to apply to every frontier model deployed in the US, including foreign and open-source ones. That last piece is where it gets hard, because policing open models means going after the places they are hosted, and the head of a leading lab asking the government to build a gate that his competitors also have to pass through is never a purely neutral request.

3. New York froze its data centers and California is trying to block Hollywood's biggest merger, two states drawing lines against the tech buildout. Per TBPN, Governor Kathy Hochul signed the nation's first statewide moratorium on hyperscale AI data centers, a one-year pause while New York studies the energy and water demand, with her stated reason being that she refuses to let the power costs get passed on to residents. Ken Griffin's warning, that this pushes hundreds of billions of dollars of investment to other states, is the counterargument.

The California fight is over media, not power. Per TBPN, California's attorney general and 11 other states sued to block Paramount's $110 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery, the largest merger in Hollywood history, which would give the combined company about 27 percent of US film distribution. The federal Justice Department has already cleared the deal, so California is the lone blocker, and Paramount's David Ellison is now weighing moving the company out of the state in response. Two blue states, two industries, the same instinct to say no to consolidation the federal government waved through.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • China's economy slowed more than expected, with second-quarter GDP growth at 4.3 percent, its weakest since 2022, and apparent oil demand down 19.4 percent year over year, per the Financial Markets list. The country buying the least oil in years is a quiet cap on the very price spike the Gulf is driving.
  • Foreigners keep funding America, buying a net $132 billion of US securities in May, per the Polymarket list. The capital keeps flowing in even as the politics get louder.
  • IBM had its worst day since the 1960s, falling 25 percent, per the Kalshi list, a reminder that the AI boom is now producing blue-chip losers as well as winners.
