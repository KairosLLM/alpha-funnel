Iran rejected a US ceasefire and threatened to blow up every oil and gas facility in the Gulf, oil is holding above $100, and at one minute past midnight the tariff wall went back up on sixty countries

The war widened as the Houthis hit two more Saudi tankers and the Senate voted down an effort to rein the president in. A new wave of tariffs replaced the one the courts struck down, home prices set another record even as the market froze, and the 30-year yield has now sat above 5 percent longer than any year since 2007.

An evening when the war reached for the world's energy supply, the trade war reopened on sixty fronts, and the cost of money kept quietly reshaping ordinary life. Ranked.

1. Iran rejected the ceasefire and aimed its threat at the Gulf's oil, and the Senate stood down. The war escalated toward its most dangerous target. Per the Market list, Iran rejected a US ceasefire offer and widespread US strikes hit across the country overnight, while the Houthis fired on two more Saudi tankers. Per the Crypto list, Iran is now threatening to blow up all oil and gas infrastructure in the region if pushed, with the named targets on both sides being real energy chokepoints like Kharg Island and the South Pars field, the kind of strike that analysts warn could trigger a global depression.

Washington's own checks came up short. Per the Kalshi list, the Senate rejected an Iran war-powers resolution with Susan Collins the lone Republican in favor, even as four House Republicans crossed over on a separate vote, and Trump said damage to US ships would be paid for with seized Iranian funds. Per the Kalshi list, Brent crude is back above $100, up 42 percent in 20 days, and Iran's parliament speaker says the Strait of Hormuz will not return to its pre-war conditions. A war that is now openly targeting the plumbing of the global oil market is the single biggest risk on the board.

2. The tariff wall the courts struck down came back in a new form at midnight. The trade war reopened on a technicality of timing. Per the People list, the 10 percent global tariff expired and was replaced at 12:01 this morning by a new wave of forced-labor tariffs, 10 percent on countries with forced-labor bans and 12.5 percent on those without, covering roughly 60 economies, with goods in transit given until July 28 and exemptions for USMCA trade, oil and gas, and fertilizer.

The structure is designed to survive the courts. Per the People list, these are Section 301 forced-labor duties rather than the emergency-powers tariffs the Supreme Court already struck down, and India negotiated its rate from 12.5 down to 10 percent. Per the Financial Markets list, the administration is rebuilding the wall across 60 countries with Japan and China at 12.5 percent and adding new tariffs on Brazilian chemicals. The through-line is that the tariffs keep changing shape faster than importers can plan, and the uncertainty itself has become the policy.

3. Home prices set another record while the market froze, and the bond market is the reason. The quiet economic story is a housing market that has stopped moving. Per Arbitrage Andy, US home prices have risen for 36 straight months to a record median near $440,000, yet the 30-year mortgage sits around 6.55 to 6.77 percent, 46 percent of sellers are now offering concessions at an all-time high, and foreclosure filings are up 21 percent on the year.

The cause sits in the Treasury market. Per that letter, the 30-year yield has traded above 5 percent for 27 days this year, the most since 2007, and the 10-year pushed above 4.70 percent, so a $800,000 mortgage now costs about $5,190 a month against $3,370 at the old 3 percent rate, roughly $650,000 more in interest over its life. The result is not a crash in prices but a collapse in transactions, with owners locked into cheap pandemic-era mortgages unwilling to sell and buyers unable to afford the financing. It is what higher-for-longer actually feels like on the ground, a market where the number goes up and nobody can move.

  • The House banned its own members from trading stocks. Per Trader Mayne, the House passed a ban on members trading individual stocks by 234 to 198, sending it to the Senate. The reform only moved once incumbents felt the midterms closing in.
  • Stripe is in talks to buy OpenRouter for $10 billion. Per the Polymarket list, the payments giant is negotiating to acquire the AI-model marketplace OpenRouter. The plumbing of the AI economy is consolidating fast.
  • OpenAI put your medical records inside ChatGPT. Per the Hermes list, OpenAI is rolling out Health in ChatGPT for US users, connecting Apple Health and medical records. The chatbot is moving into the most sensitive data a person has.
