On the Fourth of July, the Supreme Court hands Trump a defeat on citizenship while his disclosure shows how much richer the office made him

The Court reportedly struck the birthright-citizenship order, a new filing puts the president's portfolio near $3.8B with 327 stock buys the day before he paused tariffs, and the data says the AI squeeze is landing hardest on people under 25.

PoliticsMarketsWorldCulture

The holiday is quiet but two American stories underneath it are not. The Supreme Court dealt the White House a loss on one of its signature orders, and a fresh financial disclosure shows just how well the presidency has paid. Then the labor data keeps saying the same uncomfortable thing about who AI is actually displacing.

1. The Supreme Court reportedly struck down Trump's birthright-citizenship order. Per the All-In show notes, the Court ruled against the executive order seeking to end birthright citizenship, leaving the Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee in place. This is the show's framing rather than a court transcript, so treat the reasoning as still to be read in the opinion itself. The politics are clean regardless of the legal detail. A conservative-majority Court drawing a line on one of the administration's defining orders is the kind of check that reshapes what the next two years of executive action can attempt.

2. Trump's own disclosure shows how much the office is worth. A new filing puts the president's portfolio near $3.8B, including more than $1.3B in crypto-related income, per the People-to-follow list. The sharper detail sits in the Stocks list: Kalshi and NBC reporting flags that Trump-linked investment accounts made more than 300 stock purchases the day before he paused his tariffs, a sequence critics frame as pocketing roughly $1.4B on the reversal.

The timing lands against a new policy launching the same weekend. Per the Crypto list, "Trump Accounts" formally launch on July 4, running about a million signups a month, with the Treasury set to accept donations of publicly traded stock into them. Whatever the merits, the optics of a president disclosing billions while launching a stock-funded savings program on Independence Day will define the week's coverage.

3. The AI jobs squeeze is landing on the young, and the numbers are getting hard to wave off. The clearest signal in the Crypto list is generational. A Stanford and ADP study finds workers aged 22 to 25 in AI-exposed jobs are down 13% since late 2022, recent-graduate unemployment sits at 5.6% against a 4.3% national rate, and 42.5% of graduates are underemployed. The counterweight is that firms adopting AI early are still adding people, with Ramp data showing AI-adopter headcount up 10.2% and entry-level roles up 12%.

Read together, the story is not "AI took the jobs" but "AI is reshaping who gets hired first." The bottom rung of the ladder is where the pressure shows up earliest, and that is a political problem long before it is an economic one.

Around the world, quickly.

  • California and Colorado, two Democratic story lines. Per the All-In show notes, Governor Newsom is touting a balanced California budget that critics say papers over real fiscal strain, while a democratic-socialist candidate, Melat Kiros, reportedly pulled a primary upset in Colorado. Both feed the 2028 jockeying already showing up in prediction markets.
  • Argentina. Kalshi bettors put Javier Milei's standing near 58% per the Kalshi list, keeping his reform experiment the one to watch in Latin America.
  • The wealth-tax line keeps moving down. Per the Financial Markets list, Representative Ro Khanna argued a wealth tax "must not stop at billionaires" and should reach fortunes as low as $50M, a marker for where the Democratic left wants the debate.
  • Gold quietly became the world's largest reserve asset. Central-bank buying pushed gold past US Treasuries as the top reserve holding, per the Kalshi list, the same week US federal debt topped 100% of GDP for the first time since 1946. Lyn Alden's read on the Stocks list is that the deficit now exceeds all private bank lending combined.
  • Washington cancelled its July 4 parade for extreme heat. A small but telling detail from the Financial Markets list as much of the country bakes.
  • Prediction markets are becoming an asset class, and the regulators noticed. Bernstein projects a $1T industry by 2030, per the Financial Markets list, even as Europe's ESMA warns event contracts could fall under its 2018 binary-options retail ban.
