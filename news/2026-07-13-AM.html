The war got louder overnight and the United States answered Iran's closure claim not with words but by escorting tankers through the water. Ranked.

1. Washington is calling Iran's closure bluff by force. Iran declared the Strait of Hormuz closed again over the weekend, and the US response was physical. Per the Market and Polymarket lists, roughly 20 commercial vessels transited the strait in coordination with the US military, some without escort, while CENTCOM insists Iran does not control the waterway. The message is that the strait is open because the US Navy says it is, and the tankers moving through are the proof it wants the market to see.

The fighting escalated to back it. Per the Financial Markets list, CENTCOM completed another wave of strikes on Iran, hitting dozens of air-defense, radar, missile, and drone sites and using one-way attack sea drones for the first time, with reported direct hits on the Fifth Fleet headquarters in Bahrain and US bases in Kuwait. Iran fired back with ballistic missiles and drones at those bases and claimed it killed three Americans in Kuwait, which CENTCOM says is false. Trump said he bombed the hell out of them and has a thousand missiles locked.

Two things sit underneath the noise. Iran's foreign minister says the US pressured Oman to block the Muscat talks, which would close the one de-escalation channel that was still open, per the Market list. And the markets are reading it as contained rather than catastrophic, with US crude up 4 to 5 percent but Brent still under $80 and recession odds sitting below 10 percent, the lowest since 2022, per the Kalshi list. A war being fought with sea drones and tanker escorts is a strange kind of open.

2. The country lowered its flags for Lindsey Graham. Per the Polymarket list, Trump ordered flags to half-staff for Senator Lindsey Graham, confirming the South Carolina Republican's sudden death over the weekend. In the same run of Senate news, Mitch McConnell is in rehabilitation after a fall. Two of the chamber's longest-serving and most recognizable figures are off the board at once, one permanently, in the middle of a war Graham spent his career pushing to fight.

3. The financial damage showed up first in Asia. The clearest read on how markets took the weekend is the overnight tape in Asia, per the Kalshi list. Japanese stocks shed about $170 billion and Korean stocks about $150 billion in a single hour, with Korea's exchange tripping a circuit breaker to slow the selling. The dollar's share of global reserves fell to a 21st-century low even as it rallied on the day, and foreign stocks have now beaten US stocks for 70 straight weeks. The war is the headline, but the structural story underneath is money quietly moving away from American assets.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • A grand jury is examining Shawn Fain, the United Auto Workers president, per the Polymarket list. A federal probe of the union's most prominent leader is a real escalation in the government's posture toward organized labor.
  • Hyundai workers struck for three days over robots, per the Polymarket list. The automation fight is moving from the op-ed page to the factory floor, and Korea is where it is landing first.
  • Kash Patel is a coin flip to keep his job, with prediction markets putting his ouster by year-end at 54 percent, per the Polymarket list.
  • The 2026 map is firming up on the odds boards. Republicans sit at 57 percent to hold the Senate while Democrats are 82 percent to take the House, and in Maine, Democrat Troy Jackson leads Susan Collins 45 to 39, per the Kalshi list.
