The world keeps funding America whether it wants to or not, and the one thing that breaks the arrangement is a move on Taiwan

China's trillion-dollar surplus has nowhere to go but US assets, foreigners now own a fifth of the American stock market, and the dollar still clears 88 percent of global currency trades. The single fault line under all of it is a chip factory on an island, and Washington is quietly building the instruments to trade the day it cracks.

A quiet news morning, so the piece worth your time is the structural one: why the money keeps flowing to America, and the single event that would reverse it. Two threads.

1. American dominance is a mechanical arrangement, not a mood. The clearest version of why US assets stay expensive, per Capital Flows: China runs roughly a $1 trillion goods surplus behind a closed capital account, which means all that money has almost nowhere to go except into deep, safe US markets. That recycling is a structural necessity, not a vote of confidence, and it is the reason a decade of predictions that the dollar would break have all failed. The dollar is 58 percent of world reserves and clears 88 percent of currency trades, and tariffs did not change the balance, they just rerouted supply chains through Mexico, Vietnam, and Malaysia while the deficit stayed negative.

The political cost is real and named. That same capital inflow strengthens the dollar and mechanically widens the trade deficit, which shows up as a hollowed-out middle class and stagnant real wages, the grievance that drives the politics of both parties. Per BowTied Bull, foreigners now own about 21.4 percent of American stocks and a quarter of US bonds, and the S&P 500 is quietly becoming the country's retirement system, with Trump Accounts making it the default place a new generation's savings go. The arrangement that frustrates voters is the same one that keeps the lights on.

2. The fault line under all of it is a single chip factory. For all the talk of American resilience, the whole structure runs through one physical choke point, per Capital Flows: TSMC in Taiwan, which makes the advanced chips the entire AI economy depends on. A move by China on Taiwan is the one event that would break the arrangement instantly, turning the semiconductor supply chain from a strength into a hostage. It is the geopolitical tail that markets price at close to zero right up until the morning it is not zero.

Washington and the exchanges are quietly preparing for that morning. The CME launches single-stock futures on Nvidia, AMD, and other chip names on July 27, which are precisely the instruments a trader would reach for to short the sector the instant a Taiwan headline crosses. The existence of those products is its own kind of news. The financial system is building the plumbing to trade a Taiwan crisis, which tells you the people who move real money consider it a when-not-if, even as everyone agrees it is not today.
