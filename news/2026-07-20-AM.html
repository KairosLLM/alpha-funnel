Korea's stock market is having the most volatile stretch in its recorded history, worse than the financial crisis, after ordinary people borrowed to bet on the same AI chip boom that shook Wall Street

The KOSPI is down 30 percent from a record it set last month and fell another 4 percent overnight, with the contagion already spreading to Taiwan. The Iran war passed its tenth day with oil back over $90, and Australia moved to become one of the first countries to mandate AI standards.

WorldMarketsTech

A financial crisis in one country, a shooting war in another, and a quiet regulatory first. Ranked.

1. Korea's market crisis is now a historic event. The story that should travel is in Seoul. Per the Financial Markets list, South Korea's equity volatility has reached the highest level in its recorded history, surpassing the global financial crisis, after retail investors borrowed heavily to chase the semiconductor boom and got caught when it reversed. The KOSPI fell another 4 percent overnight and is now down 30 percent from the all-time high it set only last month.

The damage is spreading. Per the People list, Taiwanese retail investors unwound their own leveraged bets on Friday at the fastest pace since April 2025, and China is stepping up efforts to stabilize its sagging stock market, so the forced selling that started with Korean chip leverage is becoming a regional event. This is the same AI-chip mania the US is running, but with more borrowed money and less cushion, and it is the clearest live picture of what happens when households lever into a crowded trade at the top.

2. The Iran war passed ten days and pushed oil back over $90. The conflict is grinding on with no off-ramp. Per the Market list, the US-Iran war entered its tenth day with Iran vowing the Strait of Hormuz stays blocked while what it calls US malice persists, Bahrain intercepting an Iranian attack, and Trump pushing to add Iran to the Russia sanctions bill.

The price is showing up in energy. Per the People list, Brent crude jumped back over $90 a barrel, the level that starts to matter for inflation and for a Federal Reserve meeting at the end of the month. A war that the market spent two weeks treating as background noise is now doing something concrete to the price of fuel, and the longer the strait stays contested, the harder it gets to keep calling the oil move temporary.

3. Australia moved to mandate AI standards. The quieter first of the weekend is regulatory. Per the Polymarket list, Australia is establishing a national Office of AI and moving toward mandatory AI standards, one of the earliest efforts by a national government to impose binding rules on the technology rather than voluntary guidelines.

The significance is the direction, not the detail. As the US debates whether the industry can regulate itself and China races to ship open models, a mid-sized democracy writing mandatory standards is a marker that the permission to deploy AI is becoming a government-granted asset rather than a purely technical race. It is the same shift showing up everywhere from New York's data-center moratorium to the fight over who gets to ship a frontier model, now with a dedicated agency attached.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Spain won the World Cup. Spain beat Argentina one to nil in extra time to take the title, closing a tournament that ran through the whole month, per the Market list. The prediction markets had favored Spain, and they were right.
  • The starter home is slipping out of reach. Only about four in ten people who do not already own a home can afford a $200,000 starter house, per the Polymarket list. The affordability gap that defined the last few years has not closed.
  • A Chinese giant is bouncing. Alibaba rallied 35 percent off an all-time-low momentum reading, per the Financial Markets list. Even in a shaky region, the most beaten-down names are finding buyers.
