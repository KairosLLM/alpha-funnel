Trump said he is close to a massive strike on Iran and Rubio answered a threat with "a head for an eye," oil broke back above $100, and the House voted a second time to try to halt the war

The Houthis hit two Saudi tankers and explosions rocked Iran's Qeshm Island as the campaign entered its twelfth straight night. Congress passed a $1.15 trillion defense bill that also bans its own members from trading stocks, and gold quietly overtook US Treasuries as the world's largest reserve asset.

An evening when the war escalated toward a decision, Congress pushed back on two fronts at once, and the world quietly moved its money into gold. Ranked.

1. The Iran war is nearing a decision point, and the rhetoric has stopped pretending otherwise. The escalation is now explicit. Per the Polymarket list, Trump said he is close to deciding on a large-scale strike on Iran and warned that further Houthi attacks would trigger major military punishment, while Secretary of State Rubio said an eye for an eye had become a head for an eye and refused to rule out hitting civilian infrastructure, as the campaign entered its twelfth consecutive night.

The war is spreading across the Gulf. Per that list, the Houthis struck two Saudi tankers in the Red Sea, explosions hit Iran's Qeshm Island, sirens sounded in Bahrain, and the IRGC claimed it struck US bases in Kuwait and Jordan while warning Britain that any base it uses is a legitimate target. Per the Market list, oil is pressing toward and above $100 with the Strait of Hormuz disrupted, and the market is pricing the risk of a wider war rather than a ceasefire. The language coming out of Washington is the language of a decision that has mostly been made.

2. Congress pushed back on the war and on itself in the same session. The legislative response was unusually pointed. Per the Polymarket list, the House passed a resolution to halt US action in Iran for the second time, with four Republicans joining Democrats, even as it passed a $1.15 trillion defense bill that adds $60 billion in military spending much of it tied to the Israel and Iran campaign.

The same bill did something Congress has resisted for years. Per the Kalshi list, it bans members of Congress from trading individual stocks, a reform that finally passed, with Nancy Pelosi among those voting against it. The two votes together capture a Congress trying to reclaim authority it has ceded, over when the country goes to war and over how its own members profit while serving, and the fact that both moved on the same day is a sign of how much the ground has shifted heading into the midterms.

3. Gold overtook US Treasuries as the world's largest reserve asset, and that is a verdict. The quiet macro story is the loudest one over time. Per the Kalshi list, gold has now surpassed US Treasuries as the largest reserve asset held around the world, a milestone that says central banks would rather hold the metal with no counterparty than the debt of the United States.

The backdrop explains the shift. Per the Financial Markets list, weekly jobless claims came in at 187,000, the lowest since 1969, which keeps the labor market too hot for the Fed to cut while oil above $100 threatens to reignite inflation, and the market is now pricing the chance of two rate hikes this year. A world that moves its reserves into gold while the largest economy runs $2 trillion deficits and fights a widening war is making a statement about trust, and it is doing it with its balance sheet rather than its words.

4. China is betting that time is on its side, and it may be right. The most clarifying geopolitical analysis of the day is about patience. Per a CaspianReport analysis, the widely cited 2027 deadline for a Chinese move on Taiwan traces to a single 2021 warning from Admiral Philip Davidson, and the more telling fact is that China shows none of the signs of an imminent invasion, no nationwide mobilization, no conversion of civilian industry, no invasion-scale logistics, because modernization is not mobilization.

The strategy is to win without fighting. Per that analysis, Beijing prefers a spectrum of gradual coercion, median-line crossings, naval drills, economic integration, and legal pressure that normalizes control while forcing Washington to decide when a slow squeeze justifies a response, betting the world accepts gradual change more easily than a sudden one. The counterweight is China's own clock, a slowing economy, a property crisis, and an aging population that could eventually convince Beijing that time is no longer its ally, and that is the real race underneath the headline about 2027.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • The EU fined Google $1.02 billion. Per the Polymarket list, Brussels fined Google over self-preferencing in Search and the Play Store, landing the same day the stock cratered on earnings. The regulatory bill keeps arriving on top of the market one.
  • Uber cut a tenth of its support staff to AI. Per the Kalshi list, Uber cut 10 percent of its customer-service workforce and moved the work to AI. The job displacement is no longer theoretical at the big platforms.
  • A Russian Su-57 crashed near Moscow. Per the Polymarket list, one of Russia's most advanced fighters went down near the capital. The loss is an embarrassment for a military already stretched.
  • The CLARITY Act's ethics deal bars officials from crypto. Per Blockworks, the deal that could pass the crypto bill would bar the president, vice president, and Congress from sponsoring a digital asset for profit, sunsetting in January 2029. The provision exists because the president reported more than $1 billion in crypto income last year.
