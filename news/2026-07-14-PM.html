Trump reinstated the Hormuz blockade and a 20 percent cargo toll in the morning and walked it back by the afternoon, and oil round-tripped with him

The on-again-off-again war added dead crews on two UAE tankers and an Iranian threat to treat Starlink as a military target. Inflation posted its biggest monthly drop since 2020 into a hawkish new Fed chair, IBM had its worst day since 1987, and New York became the first state to freeze data-center construction.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

A whipsaw day, in the Gulf and in the market. Ranked.

1. The Hormuz policy reversed inside a single day, and the oil tape reversed with it. In the morning, per the Financial Markets AM list, Trump moved to reinstate the Strait of Hormuz blockade and a 20 percent toll on all cargo, a fee that works out to roughly $30 million per loaded supertanker on a waterway carrying a fifth of the world's oil, and Brent spiked above $86. By the afternoon he had walked it back, narrowing the blockade to Iranian ports only and replacing the toll with promised Gulf trade deals, and US crude gave back the entire move to turn negative on the day, per the Financial Markets PM list. A policy that would reshape global energy markets came and went between breakfast and the close.

The fighting underneath it did not reverse. Overnight, per the Polymarket AM list, a five-hour round of US strikes hit six Iranian cities including Bushehr and Bandar Abbas, and Iranian cruise missiles struck two UAE tankers, the Mombasa B and the Bahia, killing an Indian crew member and wounding eight. Iran then declared Starlink a legitimate military target, which drags Elon Musk's satellite network into the war directly. The blockade may be a bargaining chip Trump can pick up and drop in a day, but the missiles and the dead crews are not.

2. Inflation cooled hard, right as the Fed chair signaled he will not chase it lower. June inflation posted its largest monthly drop since 2020, per the CPI breakdown, with headline at 3.5 percent and core at 2.6, driven by a 5.7 percent fall in energy. In a normal year that print buys rate cuts. This is not that year. New Fed chair Kevin Warsh used his first testimony to stay hawkish, saying inflation is a choice and that he will deliver price stability, per the Kalshi list, so the market debate is now whether the Fed hikes once more or holds, with no cut priced this year.

The political read is sharper than the market one. A cooling inflation number under a chair who refuses to declare victory is the setup for a fight between a White House that wants lower rates and a Fed that will not give them.

3. IBM cratered and New York moved to freeze the data centers, and both are the AI buildout meeting reality. IBM had its worst day since 1987, falling about 25 percent, per the Kalshi list, after its CEO said corporate clients are diverting their budgets into memory and hardware ahead of price hikes. It is the first blue-chip casualty of the memory squeeze that has minted fortunes elsewhere. On the policy side, per the Polymarket PM list, New York became the first US state to freeze large data-center construction for up to a year, with the industry warning it weakens the country against China. The AI boom is now colliding with the two things that always eventually check a boom, corporate earnings and local politics.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • The banks had a blowout quarter, with Goldman posting record equities-trading revenue of $7.42 billion, and JPMorgan, Citi, and Bank of America all beating, per the People AM list. Wall Street is thriving while IBM burns, a split screen of the same economy.
  • Warren Buffett is winding down, giving away about $6 billion of Berkshire stock, ending his donations to the Gates Foundation over its Epstein ties, and signaling he will dispose of his Berkshire holdings within roughly eight years, per the Polymarket PM list.
  • DeepSeek is heading for an IPO and reportedly raising at around a $71 billion valuation, per the Market AM list. The Chinese lab that erased half a trillion of Nvidia value last year is now going to the public market.
  • Nvidia has started shipping its H200 chips to China again, confirmed by a US official, per the Kalshi list. The export door that Washington spent a year building is quietly opening.
  • Cuba's power grid collapsed and ICE suspended vehicle stops, per the Market PM list, two reminders that the infrastructure and enforcement stories keep running under the market headlines.
