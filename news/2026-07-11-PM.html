The Hormuz deadline came and went, and instead of a handshake Trump threatened Iran with a thousand missiles

Iran refuses to talk until Washington retracts, its shipping traffic through the strait is dropping again, and back-channel talks in Oman are down to arguing over a median lane. Meanwhile the music industry moves to force AI labels onto Spotify, and the White House orders employers to fire hundreds of thousands of immigrant workers as their permits lapse.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Today's deadline was supposed to produce a public declaration that the Strait of Hormuz is open. It produced a missile threat instead. Below that, the record labels pick their fight with AI and the immigration crackdown gets a number. Ranked.

1. The strait deadline passed and the temperature went up, not down. This morning Washington wanted Iran to say in public, by today, that the Strait of Hormuz is open and toll-free. Per the Market list, what came instead was Trump threatening massive strikes if Iran targets him, claiming a thousand missiles are aimed and the orders are already in place, while Iran's Fars agency says there are no negotiations until the US retracts. Mojtaba Khamenei, the supreme leader's son, added a public revenge pledge.

The back channel has not fully closed, but it has narrowed to the geometry of a shipping lane. Axios reports the Oman talks are now down to arguing over a "median lane" through the strait, and Hormuz traffic is dropping sharply again, per the Kalshi list. Markets are still pricing a deal. Call it discipline or call it complacency, but a deadline day that ends with a thousand-missile threat is a strange thing to keep shrugging at.

2. The record labels are coming for AI music, and the pitch is a transparency label that is really a wedge. Per AI Central citing the Wall Street Journal, the RIAA and the international IFPI are leading a push to label AI-generated music across Spotify and Apple Music, backed by the Recording Academy, SAG-AFTRA, and the Human Artistry Campaign. Two tags are proposed, "AI-generated" for fully synthetic tracks and "AI-assisted" for the rest, with RIAA chief Mitch Glazier framing it as listener transparency.

The number that undercuts the whole effort is buried in the reporting. A Deezer study of 9,000 listeners found 97 percent could not tell an AI song from a human one, while 80 percent still want the AI tracks labeled. So the labels are demanding a disclosure for a difference almost nobody can hear, which tells you the real aim is not transparency but a foothold to eventually restrict the recordings that threaten their business. Watch whether the labeling stays voluntary or becomes a distribution requirement, because that is where the leverage lives.

3. The immigration crackdown got a scale today: employers told to fire hundreds of thousands. Per the Polymarket list, US employers are being told to fire hundreds of thousands of immigrant workers as their work permits expire, moving the policy from border enforcement into the middle of the labor market. This is the version of immigration policy that shows up in payrolls and in specific industries, not in cable-news footage.

The knock-on question is economic. Sectors that lean on permit-dependent labor, from agriculture to warehousing to construction, absorb this first, and it lands the same week the market is arguing about whether inflation is cooling. A supply shock to labor cuts the other way from a soft CPI, and both are live.

4. Two policy moves worth tracking, one crypto and one chips. A ban on a central-bank digital currency (a government-issued digital dollar) became law without Trump's signature, folded into a housing bill, per Scott Melker on the Crypto list. It is a quiet win for the crypto lobby that spent two years arguing a CBDC would be a surveillance tool, and it landed with almost no fanfare.

On the other side of the tech-policy ledger, Washington eased export limits on the United Arab Emirates, clearing Nvidia, AMD, and Cerebras to sell advanced AI chips into the Gulf, per the Polymarket list. That is the same government that spent this morning's news drafting an order to keep Chinese AI from winning on open models, now widening the export map for American silicon. The through-line is a US chip policy that says yes to allies and no to Beijing, and the UAE just moved into the yes column.

5. Around the world, quickly.

  • Ebola. A US citizen in the Democratic Republic of Congo has tested positive, per the CDC via the Polymarket list. One case is not an outbreak, but an American index case changes the attention it gets.
  • Typhoon Bavi is hitting Japan and Taiwan, per the Polymarket list, the second major storm to cross the region's supply-chain corridor this season.
  • Maine's Senate race has a Democratic nominee taking shape, with Troy Jackson at 67 percent to win the primary, per the Kalshi list. The general still leans Republican, but the challenger is now a real name.
  • Blue Origin is raising $10 billion and targeting a return to flight by year-end, per the Market list. The private-space capital race is not a two-horse field anymore.
  • OpenAI's bad week has a foreign-policy angle. Beyond the Apple trade-secret suit and the executive departures, the company was flagged for selling advanced models to Chinese firms even as Washington frets about Chinese AI, per the Financial Markets list. The lab is now a character in the US-China tech fight, not a bystander to it.
