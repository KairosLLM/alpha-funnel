The US Treasury is quietly drafting a warning that an AI crash could be worse than the dot-com bust, and the same day the market was told China could shut down the world's chip supply in three weeks without firing a shot

A leaked Treasury report says the AI boom is now so wired into banks, chips, and private credit that a downturn would spread everywhere the last one did not. Trump vowed the Iran campaign is far from over, oil hit a five-week high, and Anthropic paid $1.5 billion to settle with the authors it trained on.

WorldPoliticsTech

A government sizing up its own bubble, a blockade scenario that would dwarf a depression, and a war that keeps not ending. Ranked.

1. The Treasury is drafting the bubble warning nobody in the market wants to hear. The most consequential document of the day is an internal one. Per Lark Davis, a draft US Treasury report obtained by the outlet NOTUS warns that the AI boom is so deeply embedded across the economy that a sharp downturn could hit stocks, banks, chipmakers, cloud providers, data centers, and private credit all at once, making an AI bust potentially far broader than the dot-com crash.

The point is not that a crash is imminent, it is that it would not stay contained. The dot-com bust of 2000 mostly burned equity investors, but the report's argument is that today's AI is wired into the banking system and the private-credit market in a way that did not exist then, so a repricing would ripple through leverage the last one never touched. It is a rare thing for the Treasury to be modeling the downside of the trade driving the whole market, and the fact that it is doing so is itself the news.

2. China could cripple Taiwan in three weeks without invading it. The scenario that should worry every chip investor is a blockade, not a war. Per the All-In Podcast, Taiwan holds less than three weeks of energy reserves, so China could brown out the island simply by cutting off its oil and gas imports with a quarantine, no shot required, and once a chip fab loses power it does not come back for about 90 days.

The stakes are hard to overstate. The argument is that a Taiwan brownout would do more economic damage than the Great Depression, because the world's most advanced chips all flow through one island that imports nearly all of its energy, and China has already blockaded the Taiwan Strait seven times in the last four years as a demonstration of intent. That is the quiet risk under the entire AI trade, that the physical chokepoint is not a war anyone would declare but a siege that starves the grid.

3. Trump says the Iran campaign is far from finished. The war kept escalating even as talk of mediation surfaced. Per the Market list, Trump said in the afternoon that the US is not finished at all with Iran, that Iran has not seen anything yet, and that the US will soon hit the fortified Pickaxe Mountain nuclear site very heavily while the naval blockade of Iran's ports continues.

The market felt it in energy. Per the Financial Markets list, oil hit a five-week high with Brent back over $91 on the renewed strikes and Houthi threats to blockade Saudi shipping, and Goldman warned crude could top $120 if the Gulf chokepoint crisis deepens, with the US strategic reserve already at a 40-year low. There were faint signs of an off-ramp, with an Iranian official reported to be meeting mediators in Pakistan, but a president promising to hit a mountain very heavily is not the language of a ceasefire.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Anthropic paid $1.5 billion to the authors it trained on. Anthropic reached a $1.5 billion settlement with authors over using their books to train its models, the largest of its kind, per the Financial Markets list. The bill for scraping the world's writing is now a real number.
  • The Fed is being priced to hike rather than cut. The odds of a Federal Reserve rate hike this year jumped to 62 percent even as the beaten-down chips rallied, per the Financial Markets list. The market is bracing for tighter money rather than easier.
  • Trump opened another tariff front. A new wave of tariffs on dozens of countries is set to land as the 10 percent global tariff expires Friday, per the Polymarket list. The trade war keeps widening even as the shooting war does.
