The US and Iran spent the week turning the world's most important oil chokepoint on and off like a switch, and the strangest fact is that after a blockade, a toll, and six nights of strikes, crude barely moved

Washington went from calling Iran's closure a bluff to declaring itself the guardian of the Strait of Hormuz, taxing every ship through it, then bombing Iran's ports for six straight nights. The market's refusal to price the war is either wisdom or the setup for the shock nobody is hedging.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

One story ran every News edition this week, and it kept escalating in a way that should have moved oil far more than it did. Here is the full arc and what it turns on next.

How the week escalated. It began with Iran declaring the Strait of Hormuz closed and Washington answering by force. The US sent tankers through the water Iran said was shut, then went further, with Trump declaring the United States the guardian of the strait and putting a 20 percent toll on everything that sails through it, a move that turned a military standoff into a tax on global trade.

Then came the whipsaw. The blockade and the toll were reinstated one morning and walked back by the afternoon, and the oil tape round-tripped with each statement, which taught traders to fade the headlines faster than they arrived. The ceasefire cycle finally broke for good, the US warships came back, and the campaign turned sustained. Per the Financial Markets list, Central Command ran a sixth consecutive night of strikes while Kuwait intercepted 32 Iranian drones by dawn and the retaliation spread across the Gulf. The targeting then shifted from ships to infrastructure, with the US hitting the bridges around Bandar Abbas to strangle the port itself, per the Polymarket list, and Iran widening its own list to a Saudi base and a Kuwaiti desalination plant, per the Market list.

By the weekend the last framework collapsed. Per the Financial Markets list, Iran formally declared the 14-point understanding dead and said it would no longer honor its obligations, which removes the thin diplomatic floor the whole thing had been resting on.

The fact that does not fit. For all of that, oil would not break out. Even with active strikes on the most important energy chokepoint on earth, crude sat near $84 for two full weeks and refused to spike, per the Market list. That refusal is the single most consequential thing that happened, because cheap oil is what gave the Federal Reserve cover to leave rates alone and kept the disinflation trade alive, and a market that shrugs off a shut strait is quietly deciding this war is a series of spikes to fade.

There are only two readings of that calm, and they cannot both be right. Either traders are correct that this ends in a settlement and every escalation is noise, or they have simply gone numb to a war that switches on and off by tweet, and the numbness is itself the risk. The tell that the numbness may be cracking is at the pump, where the average price of US diesel has crossed $5 a gallon and regular gasoline is heading back toward $4, per the Financial Markets list. The war has not moved the crude benchmark, but it is finally reaching the American driveway.

What to watch next. The first thing is whether oil finally breaks. A benchmark that held $84 through a blockade has a lot of stored energy, and the pump prices suggest the disconnect between the futures screen and the real economy is starting to close. If crude follows diesel higher, the Fed's cover disappears and the disinflation trade that carried the whole market unwinds with it.

The second is geography. Iran has already told the Houthis to be ready to close the Bab el-Mandeb strait if the US hits its power grid, which would open a second chokepoint on the Red Sea while the first stays contested, and the collapse of the 14-point understanding removes the last reason for either side to hold back. The third is the shape of the endgame, because a war fought over bridges, desalination plants, and a Saudi base is no longer aimed at Iran's military, it is aimed at a region's ability to function, and that is the kind of escalation that does not fade on a tweet. The market has bet all week that this stays contained. The coming week is when that bet either pays or gets called.
