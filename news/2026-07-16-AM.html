The US ran a second wave of strikes on Iran overnight, and OpenAI's answer to the smartphone turned out to be a talking speaker with a camera and moving parts

Kuwait spent the night shooting down two dozen Iranian missiles and drones while Trump swung from threats to talk of a settlement in a single day. OpenAI's first device, designed with Jony Ive, is a screenless home companion that watches the room, and a new argument says America's factory revival is why interest rates will not fall.

A restarted war, a strange new gadget, and a case that the American economy is being physically rebuilt. Ranked.

1. The Iran war turned back on, and the US and Iran are back to trading strikes and mixed signals. Per the Market list, US Central Command ran a second wave of strikes Wednesday afternoon, with explosions reported at Chabahar and Bandar Abbas, and Kuwait intercepted four cruise missiles and 21 drones fired from Iran overnight. Trump ran the full range in a day, warning Iran to behave and predicting it would be defeated soon, then saying Iran wants to meet and settle, and separately noting Iran had released a US citizen detained since December. US military planners were even reported to have examined a possible raid on Cuba.

The market read is the striking part. Despite active strikes on the world's most important oil chokepoint, crude sat near $84 and did not spike, the same refusal to break out that has held for two weeks. Either traders are convinced this ends in a deal, or they have simply gotten numb to a war that switches on and off by tweet, and both explanations should worry anyone pricing the tail.

2. OpenAI's first device is a speaker that watches you, and it lands in the middle of a lawsuit about how it was built. Per Diet TBPN, Bloomberg reports OpenAI's first consumer device is a screenless AI-companion speaker, movable and battery-powered, with a camera and sensors so it can understand the room and recognize when you walk in, plus mechanical parts that move on their own to make it feel alive. It runs on GPT Live, learns from your personal data down to your email, and Jony Ive is helping design it, with about five devices in the pipeline and the first unveiling this year for a 2027 release.

The context is what makes it news rather than a gadget rumor. This is the exact product Apple is suing OpenAI over, alleging its engineers walked out with the hardware secrets to build it, so a talking speaker that watches your living room is arriving as a courtroom fight over who owns the idea. A camera-equipped device that learns your habits from your inbox is also going to be its own privacy argument the moment it ships.

3. The case that America's factory revival is why rates will not fall. The macro argument worth reading, per Capital Flows: the analysts at New Vista Capital argue the re-industrialization of the United States is the reason interest rates are stuck higher, because the country is rebuilding hardware, defense, and energy at the same moment AI is rebuilding software, and doing both at once keeps money expensive.

Their named winners are the vertically integrated defense-tech companies that broke the old contractor mold, Anduril, Palantir, and SpaceX, alongside automation stories like using physics-based AI to raise output of artillery shells at General Dynamics. The warning underneath it is that China out-produces the US in shipbuilding by roughly 200 times and controls more than 90 percent of critical-mineral processing, so the industrial base is now a national-security asset, not just an economic one.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Congress may finally move on its own stock trading. Per the Market list, Representative Bryan Steil's bill to ban members of Congress from buying individual stocks hits the House floor next week, though it exempts the President. The perennial promise gets another vote.
  • The banks are booming. Jamie Dimon called it close to as good as it gets, on high rates and a wave of dealmaking led by the SpaceX IPO, per Prof G Markets. Wall Street is having its best stretch in years while the factory-floor economy is the thing everyone says needs rebuilding.
