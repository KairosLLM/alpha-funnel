Iran fired missiles at US bases across the Gulf, the US answered with a second round of strikes, and by 1am Trump said Iran wants a deal

A night that started with rockets hitting American forces in Bahrain and Kuwait ended with the President floating negotiations, the sharpest whipsaw yet in a war that keeps being declared over. At home, the 2026 Senate map came into focus and Maine finally resolved. Here is what moved overnight.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The overnight news was war, and then, abruptly, maybe peace. Iran hit American bases across the Gulf, the US struck back harder, and within hours Trump was talking about a deal. Underneath the whiplash, the shape of the 2026 Senate election got clearer and a marquee race that looked like chaos suddenly had an answer. Here is the update.

1. Iran hit US bases, the US struck back, and then Trump floated a deal. The escalation was real before the reversal was. Per the Financial Markets list, Iran launched missiles at US bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar, with two striking the US forces headquarters in Bahrain and Kuwait's army intercepting incoming fire. Per the Polymarket list, US Central Command answered with strikes on Chabahar, Bandar Abbas, and Qeshm Island, knocking out power in Chabahar, while Iran's parliament speaker warned the Strait of Hormuz only opens on Iranian terms. Then, per the Market list, Trump said around 1am that Iran had called and wanted a deal, and the whole risk-off move reversed. Whether that is a genuine off-ramp or a pause between rounds is the question the region wakes up to.

2. The 2026 Senate map came into focus, and it favors Republicans. With the midterms sixteen months out, the picture is hardening. Per Election Time's race-by-race map, the projection is Republicans holding the Senate 53 to 47, with the real battlegrounds in Texas, where Ken Paxton leads Democrat James Talarico by under two points, Ohio, where John Husted edges Sherrod Brown, and North Carolina, where Roy Cooper is up nearly eight over Michael Whatley in what the map calls the most likely flip. Georgia's Jon Ossoff, a 2028 hopeful, leads by six. The map that decides control runs through a handful of states, and right now it leans red.

3. Maine finally resolved after weeks of chaos. The messiest race got an answer overnight. Per the Kalshi list, Democrat Graham Platner officially suspended his campaign as his exit odds hit 99% on a video release, ending the sexual-misconduct saga that had whipsawed the market for a week. Former state senate president Troy Jackson immediately announced and is now the front-runner for the July 31 Democratic nomination at 47%, with incumbent Republican Susan Collins leading the overall race at 40% but Democrats favored to hold the seat at 64%, the highest since May. One trader reportedly cleared $173,769 on the Platner exit market alone.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Iraq and Syria. Per the Polymarket list, Iraq agreed to stop dollar flows to Iran-backed militias, and Trump rescinded Syria's state-sponsor-of-terrorism designation, two moves that reshuffle the region's alignments around the Iran fight.
  • South Korea. Per the Financial Markets list, the Kospi is now down 25% from the record it set just three weeks ago, a full bear market driven by the unwind in its two chip giants, even as the Bank of Korea signals a rate hike may be needed.
  • China. Per the Kalshi list, factory-gate inflation came in at its highest in about four years, a tentative sign the long deflation may be turning.
  • The AI jobs question. Per the Kalshi list, a Forbes read of the latest data notes that firms are now spending more on AI than they save from the employees it replaces, a warning that the productivity payoff still trails the bill.
