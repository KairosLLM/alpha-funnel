The US ran a fourth wave of strikes on Iran, then Trump said Iran wants a deal and added $300 billion to the stock market before Tehran denied it

The blockade held, the strait stayed shut, and the on-off war added another turn of the dial. Meanwhile the man who ran Intel warned that Taiwan has less than three weeks of power and one blockade could brown out the world's chips, Wall Street posted a blowout quarter, and the machinery that settles US stocks started moving on-chain.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

Two war stories today, one in the Gulf and one that has not started yet in the Taiwan Strait. Ranked.

1. The Iran war took another swing between escalation and a deal that may not exist. Per the Polymarket list, US Central Command ran a fourth wave of strikes on Iran overnight, hitting Bandar Abbas, Chabahar, Shiraz, and Bushehr, and Iran retaliated across Jordan, Kuwait, and Bahrain, with a Shahed drone hitting a Kuwaiti oil facility and Iran's death toll reaching 37. The naval blockade held and two vessels were turned back. Then the dial swung the other way. Per the Kalshi list, Trump said Iran had called and wanted to make a deal, a line that added about $300 billion to US stocks in minutes, before Iran's foreign minister flatly denied there were any plans to negotiate.

The pattern is now the story. This is the same on-again, off-again cycle that has run for weeks, a strike wave followed by a peace headline followed by a denial, and the market has learned to buy the headline anyway. The one thing that did not move was oil, which stayed below its June average even with the strait shut, a sign that traders have decided this war is a series of spikes to fade rather than a supply shock to price.

2. The man who ran Intel says Taiwan is three weeks of electricity away from a global depression. The sharpest geopolitical warning of the day came from a business podcast, not a briefing room. Per Pat Gelsinger on All-In, the former Intel chief executive said Taiwan holds under three weeks of energy reserves, so a Chinese blockade would not need to fire a shot to brown out the island, and the chip fabs there take 90 days to restart once they go dark. His estimate of the economic hit is blunt, greater than the Great Depression, and he noted China has already run blockade drills near the strait seven times in four years.

It is the same chokepoint logic as Hormuz, one island making the chips the entire AI economy runs on, and it is the tail risk sitting under every semiconductor story in the other channel.

3. Wall Street had a blowout quarter, and the plumbing of the market started going on-chain. The banks printed huge numbers, per Mark Zandi's breakdown: Goldman Sachs profit rose 80 percent and the stock popped 9 percent, with trading and investment-banking fees up 30 to 90 percent, including $500 million in fees from the SpaceX IPO alone and the OpenAI and Anthropic public offerings flagged as the next tailwinds.

On the structural side, per the Stocks list, the Depository Trust and Clearing Corporation, which settles nearly every US stock trade, launched a pilot with JPMorgan, BlackRock, Goldman, and Vanguard to tokenize stocks and Treasurys, while BlackRock crossed $15 trillion in assets for the first time. The crypto idea of putting securities on a blockchain is now a project run by the institutions that own the securities.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Warren Buffett cut off the Gates Foundation, ending his donations following the revelations about Bill Gates and Jeffrey Epstein, per the Financial Markets list. One of the largest philanthropic relationships in history ended over the association.
  • Anthropic is heading for the public market, with Bloomberg reporting IPO investor meetings in the coming weeks and prediction markets putting an offering this year at 75 percent, per the People list. The AI lab race is about to have a public scoreboard.
  • Europe cleared the Wegovy pill, granting marketing authorization for Novo Nordisk's oral semaglutide, per the People list. The weight-loss drug war moves from injections to tablets.
