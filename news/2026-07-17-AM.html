The US hit Iran for a sixth straight night and Kuwait shot down 32 drones by dawn, while New York became the first state to slam the brakes on AI data centers

Trump told the nation the fruits of the Iran campaign are coming very shortly as the strikes rolled into a sixth night and the retaliation spread across the Gulf. New York paused permits for the biggest AI data centers for a year, and Washington aired a contested claim that China grabbed 220 million American voter files.

A war that will not stop escalating, a state saying no to the AI buildout, and a spy claim that deserves a second look before anyone panics. Ranked.

1. The Iran campaign hit six straight nights and the retaliation is now hitting the whole Gulf. The war is widening by the day. Per the Financial Markets list, US Central Command completed its latest major strike wave on Iran at 9:40 pm Eastern, the sixth consecutive night, with jets, drones, and warships in play and more than 50,000 US troops in the region. Overnight the return fire spread, with Kuwait intercepting 32 Iranian drones since dawn and explosions reported around Bahrain and other Gulf allies.

The words from both capitals stayed maximalist. Iran's armed forces said they will never back down from the Strait of Hormuz and demanded a US withdrawal, per the Polymarket list, while Trump used a single line of an address to the nation to say the US is winning big in Iran and the country will see the fruits of that labor very, very shortly. Oil is firmer on supply-disruption fears, and a war that switches chokepoints on and off is now being fought over the most important one in the world.

2. New York just told the AI buildout to wait a year. The backlash against data centers found its first hard line. Per Prof G Markets, New York issued the country's first statewide data center moratorium, pausing state permits for the largest AI facilities for a year while it writes rules on the grid, water, and who pays the electricity bill. The pressure is national, with nearly $130 billion of projects already delayed or blocked in the first quarter alone.

The grievance is specific and local. In northern Virginia and Georgia, data centers plugged straight into the grid have pushed residents' power bills up 30 to 40 percent, which turns an abstract AI story into a kitchen-table one and produces the line politicians now hear, why am I subsidizing Sam Altman. The proposed fixes are that the centers bring their own power and use closed-loop cooling so they stop drawing down local water. This is the moment the physical cost of AI stopped being the industry's problem and became the public's.

3. Washington floated a big China voter-data claim, and it deserves the skeptical read. The alarming headline of the morning needs its own footnote. Per the People list, the White House and intelligence figures including CIA director Ratcliffe claim China acquired 220 million American voter files, calling it the largest election-data breach ever. That is the kind of number that moves a news cycle before anyone checks it.

The counterpoint is the part that matters. A 2021 intelligence assessment found no foreign actor altered a single vote, and much of the voter-file data in question is already public and legally available in many states, per the Polymarket list. A breach of public records is a very different thing from a threat to the count, and the gap between those two framings is exactly where a reader should slow down.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Google stumbled and got told to share. Google delayed its Gemini launch after the model fell short of internal targets, and European regulators moved to force Google to give OpenAI and Anthropic access to Android features and search data, per the Polymarket list. The gatekeeper is being pried open just as its own model slips.
  • The smoke came back. Canadian wildfire smoke pushed Detroit to the worst air quality in the world, with Minneapolis and New York rated hazardous, per the Polymarket list. The summer's recurring haze is a headline again.
  • A crypto firm got a bank charter. Circle won approval to establish a national trust bank for digital-asset custody, per the Financial Markets list. The stablecoin business keeps walking further into the regulated core of finance.
