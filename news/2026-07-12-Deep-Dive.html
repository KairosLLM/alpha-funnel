A war Washington called over in June ran every headline this week, from a missile in a Qatari gas tanker to a thousand-missile threat on Saturday

Iran put missiles back on the Strait of Hormuz, the US answered with more than eighty targets in a night, and the ceasefire died on camera the same afternoon Trump floated it at 1am. Ali Khamenei was buried in Mashhad, his son took over and pledged revenge, and by the weekend deadline to declare the strait open there was no handshake, just an ultimatum. Here is the full arc and what is still unresolved.

WorldPoliticsMarkets

The dailies chased this war hour by hour all week, and hour by hour it kept reversing on them. Stepping back, the shape is clear and worth telling as one story, because the on-again-off-again ceasefire was the whole point. Here is how the week actually moved.

The chronology, because it changed almost every session. It started quiet. The weekend editions led on other things while Saudi Arabia pushed crude exports to a record and cut its Asian price, so oil was soft and traders expected a calm summer. That ended Tuesday morning, when Iran fired at least two missiles at ships in the Strait of Hormuz, the narrow chokepoint that carries a fifth of the world's oil, per the note on the first strike. Tuesday evening it got specific and worse, with a missile hitting a Qatari liquefied-natural-gas carrier, which pulled gas into a premium that had been about oil.

Wednesday the ceasefire broke in full. The US hit more than eighty targets inside Iran overnight after Iran attacked three commercial vessels, the M/T Al Rekayyat, the M/T Wedyan, and the M/T Cyprus Prosperity, and crude jumped about 5 percent, per the eighty-targets breakdown. By Wednesday night Trump declared the June memorandum of understanding dead, said the US had struck Kharg Island (which handles most of Iran's oil exports) and might take it over, and Brent pushed past $80. Iran fired back, and Kuwait and Bahrain were intercepting missiles and drones.

Thursday was the whipsaw. Iran launched missiles at US bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar, two of them striking the US forces headquarters in Bahrain, and US Central Command answered with a second round on Chabahar, Bandar Abbas, and Qeshm Island, per the account of the bases strike and the second-round note. Then, around 1:15am, Trump posted that Iran had called and wanted a deal, and the risk-off move reversed on a single sentence, per the overnight-reversal note. That was the head-fake of the week. By Thursday afternoon, asked on camera if the ceasefire was over, Trump said he thought it was and did not want to deal with them anymore, and the bombing resumed while Iran was burying Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in Mashhad, per the Week 20 note.

Friday the tone flipped again toward a deal, with a senior US official saying no strikes had been conducted in recent hours and oil unwinding its panic. By Saturday the demand was explicit, a public declaration that the Strait of Hormuz is open and toll-free with no nuclear activity, per the Market list. The strangest reporting came with it, that Iran privately told Trump advisers the ship attacks were a mistake by a rogue IRGC hardliner faction trying to wreck the talks, while negotiations continued in Oman under Vance, Kushner, Witkoff, and Rubio.

The deadline came and went. Instead of a declaration, Trump threatened massive strikes and claimed a thousand missiles were aimed and ready, Iran's Fars agency said there would be no talks until the US retracts, and Khamenei's son Mojtaba, now the presumptive successor, publicly pledged revenge, per the Saturday-evening Market list.

The oil throughline, because it is why this is a markets story too. Oil ran the arc in miniature, up 5 percent Wednesday, Brent past $80 Wednesday night, up 7 percent toward $80 Thursday to a two-week high, then a full reversal Friday as gold slipped below $4,100 on the deal optimism. Kalshi priced a 38 percent chance oil finishes July above $74.99, sitting oddly next to the frothiest options sentiment since December 2020, per the Kalshi list.

Strait traffic fell from more than a hundred ships a day toward a handful and was dropping sharply again into the weekend, per the Saturday Kalshi list. The equity market mostly refused to care, adding roughly $500 billion in half an hour into Wednesday's bombing, on the theory that the war premium was already in the price.

The contrarian read that aged best. Against the Friday relief, a Prof G Markets guest argued the opposite, that an enormous amount of Middle East risk is simply not priced into oil. His three steps were that traders are doing best-case scenario planning, that the region is not structurally stable no matter what anyone wishes, and that the usual shock absorber is missing because there is no functioning traditional diplomacy, only an unpredictable US president meeting an unpredictable region. By Saturday's thousand-missile threat that read looked a lot better than the morning's handshake pricing.

What is still unresolved, and it is almost everything. The strait was not declared open by the deadline, traffic is still falling, and the back channel is down to arguing over a single median shipping lane. Iran restarted the Taleqan-2 site at Parchin, which may breach the same June memorandum both sides now accuse each other of violating, per the Polymarket list. The nuclear question is open, the sanctions question is open, and the succession question is brand new, with Ali Khamenei dead and buried, his son installed and pledging revenge, and US sanctions already aimed at the son's money.

What to watch next. The first thing is whether any credible declaration on Hormuz actually appears, because the entire market calm rests on the war premium staying in the price rather than in the tape. The structural piece to keep in mind is that the Gulf's pipeline bypasses, the Saudi East-West line to Yanbu and the UAE's Fujairah route, can help those states survive a closure but cannot replace Hormuz volume, and Iran struck the Saudi East-West pipeline during this very conflict. The one variable that would change everything is a real, sustained diplomatic channel, because the guest who called the mispricing was right about the mechanism: the only thing that compresses this risk is credible diplomacy, and a pause in strikes is a data point, not a peace.
