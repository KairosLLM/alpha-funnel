The 1am ceasefire is dead, Trump said so on camera, and the US went back to bombing Iran while Khamenei was being buried

This morning's deal talk lasted hours. By afternoon Trump called it over and called Iran scum, oil ripped 7 percent toward $80, Jordan and Kuwait were intercepting Iranian missiles, and Maine's Senate seat flipped hard to the Democrats. Here is what moved since the open.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The morning ended with Trump saying Iran wanted a deal. The afternoon ended with Trump saying the war was over because he was done talking to them. In between, the US struck Iran again, missiles flew at three more countries, and Iran buried its supreme leader. At home, the messiest Senate race in the country swung to the other party. Here is the update.

1. Trump killed the ceasefire on the record and the bombing resumed. The reversal was his own. Per Prof G Markets, asked directly if the ceasefire was over, Trump said, to me I think it's over, I don't want to deal with them anymore, they're scum, and if they had a nuclear weapon they'd use it. The US launched another round of strikes after. Brent oil soared more than 7 percent toward $80, the highest in over two weeks. Brian Katulis of the Middle East Institute put the frame on it, that the war Trump said would take four or five weeks is now in week 20, with no answer yet on who rules Iran, who controls the Strait of Hormuz, or where the nuclear program stands.

2. Khamenei is being buried, and the strikes are widening. The hard facts moved all day. Per the Polymarket list, Iran is reportedly burying Ayatollah Ali Khamenei today in Mashhad as the ceasefire unravels, and Prof G's guests noted top Iranian leaders were killed in the first day of the war. Iran reported 14 killed and 78 wounded in the overnight US strikes across five provinces. US Central Command says it hit about 90 targets on July 8, following 80 the day before, and named new sites including the Bushehr naval base, Chabahar port again, and a bridge on Iran's strategic corridor to China and Russia. The stated trigger was Iran attacking three commercial vessels in the Strait of Hormuz.

3. The war is spilling into the neighbors. The interceptions tell you how wide this got. Per the Polymarket list, Jordan intercepted eight Iranian ballistic missiles fired at its territory, Kuwait shot down three ballistic missiles, a cruise missile, and ten drones at dawn with one injury and debris damage, and Bahrain's US forces headquarters appears to have taken two missile hits overnight with sirens nationwide. Per the Kalshi list, Iran is now formally accusing the US of war crimes. One detail on the American side, the Secret Service made Trump fly the older Air Force One out of Turkey when the fighting resumed and had the press lower the window blinds.

4. Maine flipped to the Democrats, and it flipped fast. The race that resolved this morning kept moving. Per the Kalshi list, Maine's Senate seat went from Republican 52 to Democrat 48 on Monday to Democrat 63 and Republican 37 today, a 15-point swing in the party's favor after Graham Platner's exit cleared the field. Nirav Shah has now officially entered the Democratic primary at about a 15 percent chance of the nomination. Looking to 2028, Citadel's Ken Griffin said he would back Marco Rubio over JD Vance, per Axios.

5. The market ignored all of it, and that silence is its own story. Stocks rose into the bombing. Per the Kalshi list, US equities added roughly $500 billion in half an hour, the Magnificent Seven sits at its cheapest versus the S&P 500 in over a decade even as the top ten names now make up 43 percent of the index, and traders are the most bullish on the dollar in more than ten years. The catch is under the surface. Rate-hike odds before year end climbed to 56 percent, the NY Fed survey shows one-year inflation expectations up another 3.7 percent, and Japan's 10-year government bond yield hit its highest level in 30 years. A market this calm about a widening war and a hotter inflation read is either right or asleep.

6. Around the world, quickly.

  • Ukraine. Per the Polymarket list, three Kremlin sources say Putin is rejecting peace talks and is likely to escalate as long as Ukraine keeps striking Russian territory.
  • Europe. Per the Kalshi list, the European Parliament passed the Chat Control proposal that allows private messages to be monitored, a major surveillance shift for the bloc.
  • The Gulf's long game. Per CaspianReport, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Oman are racing to build pipelines that bypass the Strait of Hormuz, but even at full capacity they cannot replace the volume that moves through it. The point is to survive a closure, not to defeat one.
