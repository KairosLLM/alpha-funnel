Trump wants cheaper gas he can't deliver, and Iran is the reason

Crude cracked a four-month low as the war premium bled out, David Sacks says the DSA now runs the Democratic base, and the prediction markets moved.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

Oil is the thread running through the whole day. Crude cracked a four-month low as the Iran war premium bled out, Trump tried to jawbone gas prices he cannot actually move, and the prediction markets swung toward Republicans holding the Senate. Here is what actually moved.

1. The gas-price theater. President Trump went on Truth Social today demanding that gas retailers cut prices because they are "too high," which Prof G Markets correctly filed under break-something-then-blame-someone-else economics. Here is the actual chain of events. Gas is elevated because of the Iran conflict, and the "memorandum of understanding" the administration claimed had settled it is not holding. The US and Iran traded strikes over the weekend, each side accusing the other of breaking the deal, and the Strait of Hormuz risk is unresolved. The irony is that the market already did the work Trump is demanding: WTI crude slid under $68.50 today, a four-month low, and traders say the entire war premium has now bled out, with crude flirting with contango (near-term barrels priced below later-dated ones, a sign of ample supply) per Javier Blas. Prices will not fall on a social-media post. They will fall when the conflict actually ends.

2. David Sacks says the socialists already won the Democratic base. On All-In, David Sacks argued that the Democratic Socialists of America have effectively taken over the Democratic Party, controlling the base and "where all the energy is." He read out what he says is the DSA platform, abolish the Senate, subordinate the courts to Congress, public ownership of major corporations, and defund the "Department of War," and quoted a DSA co-chair saying the quiet part out loud: "We're using the Democratic Party as a ballot-access vehicle, not because we share its goals. We see the democratic establishment as an obstacle, not a home." Take Sacks' framing with the salt it deserves, he is an interested party. But "public ownership of major corporations" gaining a foothold in a major party's platform is exactly the kind of tail risk investors ignore until it is priced, so file it.

3. The prediction markets moved. From the Kalshi and Polymarket lists: Republicans are now 59% to hold the Senate, the highest since March 1. In the New Jersey seventh, Democrat Rebecca Bennett sits at 79% versus Kean at 21%. The Colorado GOP governor primary is a coin flip, Victor Marx around 57% against Kirkmeyer. On monetary policy, only about 20% odds that Kevin Warsh's Fed hikes at the next meeting, though Bank of America's Brian Moynihan is the loud dissent calling for three hikes. Trump is on CNBC tomorrow at 5PM and said he wants to "declassify almost everything." Watch the Warsh-hike odds, because a hawkish surprise is the one thing that would spoil the disinflation story the market is currently leaning on.

4. The rare-earth choke point is the story under all the AI stories. Capital Flows, with guest Adam Rossi, made the case that the physical world is back at the center of markets. COVID and now AI pulled capital into hardware, materials, and physical supply chains, and the US position in critical minerals and rare-earth magnets is fragile. Rare earths sit under both defense systems and AI hardware, which means a single policy headline can ripple straight through the metal stocks and the whole space-and-defense industrial buildout that rests on top of energy and metals. One export restriction out of Beijing reprices the entire chain.

5. Around the world, quickly. Sweden ordered Google to pay Klarna $1.97B. Binance is running monthly net outflows above $1.8B and is suspending services in the EU as the MiCA deadline hits, pushing crypto founders toward Dubai. NATO is pushing a 5%-of-GDP defense-spending target and the UK added roughly $20B in defense. Japan is planning a domestic AI model and a stated goal of 10 million AI robots by 2040, and Sony said PlayStation goes digital-only from 2028. Small items on their own, but they rhyme: rearmament, sovereignty over compute, and a hard pivot to the physical and strategic.
