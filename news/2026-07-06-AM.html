A joint rebel-jihadist offensive hit Mali's army and its Russian mercenaries on the Fourth of July, and America's 2028 race is already taking shape a year early

Sahel militants coordinated an attack that killed around 20 in Sevare, JD Vance pulled clear of the Republican field, and Britain's politics flipped as Labour edged back ahead of Reform. Here is what moved over the holiday.

PoliticsWorldMarkets

The holiday weekend was quiet at home and busy at the edges. A coordinated offensive in the Sahel put Russia's African footprint back in the headlines, the American 2028 field started to harden a full year before anyone votes, and Britain's two-party fight swung again. Here is what happened.

1. Militants hit Mali's army and its Russian mercenaries in a coordinated Fourth of July offensive. The Sahel escalation is the real news of the weekend. Per the Polymarket list, a separatist force and the al-Qaeda-linked JNIM launched a joint offensive on July 4 against Mali's ruling junta and the Africa Corps mercenaries who prop it up, with around 20 people reported killed in the central town of Sevare. The detail that matters is the coordination. A Tuareg separatist movement and a jihadist group are ideological rivals, and when they attack the same target together it points at a junta losing control of its own center, not just its periphery. It also drags Moscow in, because the Africa Corps presence is the thing keeping this government standing.

2. The 2028 American field is already hardening, and Vance is pulling clear. A year out, the prediction markets are starting to converge. Per the Kalshi list, JD Vance now sits at 41% to be the 2028 Republican nominee, up from 32% a month ago, the kind of move that signals a field consolidating around a front-runner rather than splintering. The down-ballot action is in Michigan, where the same market has the Democratic primary reshuffling hard: Abdul El-Sayed slipped from 90% to 80%, Haley Stevens doubled from 11% to 22%, and the odds of Republicans taking the Senate seat at all climbed from 19% to 30%. Early markets are noisy, but a 10-point swing in a month is a real shift in who the serious money thinks wins.

3. Britain's politics flipped back, with Labour edging ahead of Reform. The UK story is a reversal. Per the Kalshi list, Labour has climbed to 37% in the next-general-election market and overtaken Nigel Farage's Reform at 33%, unwinding the Reform lead that had defined British polling for months. It is a market read rather than a ballot, so treat it as sentiment, but the direction is the point. The insurgent-right surge that looked inevitable through the spring has stalled, and the governing party is clawing back the lead it was supposed to have lost for good.

4. Around the world, quickly.

  • Hungary. Per the Polymarket list, a bill to remove President Tamas Sulyok, an Orban ally, has the market at 68% for an ouster this month, a rare and serious move against the ruling bloc.
  • Taiwan. Per the same list, Taipei is flagging a jump in Chinese naval activity around the island alongside joint China-Russia drills, the kind of tempo increase that gets watched for a pattern rather than a one-off.
  • Ukraine. Per the Financial Markets list, Ukrainian long-range strikes have now disrupted an estimated 15 to 30% of Russia's refining capacity, a slow attritional campaign aimed at the fuel behind the front.
  • Oil. Per the same list, Saudi Arabia pushed crude exports to a record near 5 million barrels a day as OPEC+ lifts output, and prices fell on the extra supply.
  • South Korea. Per the Polymarket list, Seoul is beginning 24-hour trading in the won, a structural step toward treating its currency like a global one.
  • United States. Per the Financial Markets list, the first joint New York Stock Exchange and Nasdaq bell-ringing from the Oval Office is set for Monday morning to mark the launch of Trump Accounts.
